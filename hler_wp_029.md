# Formal Employment-Based Insurance and Outpatient Care Utilisation among Urban Older Adults: Evidence from CHARLS

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 029*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

Whether formal employment-based health insurance reduces financial burdens or activates latent healthcare demand among the elderly is a central question in health economics, particularly in rapidly aging middle-income countries. Using seven waves of longitudinal data on urban Chinese elderly and a double/debiased machine learning (DDML) estimator combined with individual fixed effects that flexibly controls for nonlinear confounding from health history, income, and retirement status, we estimate the effect of Urban Employee Basic Medical Insurance enrollment on out-of-pocket outpatient expenditure and visit frequency. Enrollment is associated with a monthly out-of-pocket spending increase of approximately 76 yuan—a 34 percent increase relative to the sample mean—though the estimate is marginally significant and the confidence interval includes zero. Effects appear larger among retired enrollees, consistent with a time-cost mechanism in which retirement compounds insurance price effects by eliminating the opportunity cost of outpatient visits, though we cannot rule out that this pattern reflects differential adverse selection rather than genuine behavioral heterogeneity. Retirement-driven enrollment transitions are endogenous to health shocks in ways that prevent clean causal identification, and our estimates are best interpreted as informative within-individual associations. These findings suggest that the retirement enrollment transition may be a policy-relevant window for attaching chronic disease management programs.

**Keywords:** urban employee medical insurance; out-of-pocket expenditure; outpatient utilization; elderly; China; debiased machine learning

**JEL Codes:** I13, I18, J14, H51

## 1. Introduction

China is aging faster than almost any other major economy. By 2050, adults aged 60 and above are projected to account for more than one-third of the country's population, placing extraordinary pressure on a health system designed largely around working-age adults in formal employment. According to World Bank (2015), China's GDP per capita stood at roughly 8,175 USD in 2015—a middle-income level at which governments face acute tradeoffs between expanding social insurance and containing fiscal costs. At the same time, according to World Bank (2015), over 42 percent of the population remained rural and 28 percent of employment was concentrated in agriculture—sectors largely excluded from the flagship public insurance program for urban workers. Understanding whether that program actually reduces the financial burden of illness for those it covers, particularly among the elderly, is a first-order policy question with direct implications for China's rapidly evolving social protection architecture.

The **Urban Employee Basic Medical Insurance** (UEBMI), established in 1998, is the largest contributory health insurance scheme in China, covering formal-sector employees and their dependents. Unlike the **New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme** (NRCMS) or **Urban Resident Basic Medical Insurance** (URBMI), UEBMI ties eligibility to formal employment relationships and requires payroll contributions from both employers and employees. This institutional design creates within-person variation in enrollment: as individuals retire, change employers, or move between formal and informal work, their UEBMI status changes. The existence of such transitions across seven survey waves makes UEBMI an attractive setting for panel identification—though, as we discuss extensively, that variation is itself endogenous to health and employment dynamics in ways that challenge clean causal inference.

The literature on health insurance effects in developing economies offers an instructive baseline. Early evidence from the RAND Health Insurance Experiment documented substantial moral hazard in outpatient utilization among the insured (Manning et al. 1987). In China, evaluations of NRCMS found modest effects on hospitalization and out-of-pocket expenditure (Lei and Lin 2009; Wagstaff et al. 2009), while work on URBMI documented more pronounced reductions in financial risk (Cheng et al. 2015). UEBMI differs from these programs in two critical respects: its benefit package is substantially more generous, and its enrollees are, on average, wealthier and better connected to the formal healthcare system. Whether that generosity *reduces* out-of-pocket costs for elderly enrollees—or instead induces enough additional utilization that spending rises—remains empirically unresolved.

Identifying UEBMI effects is difficult for reasons that prior work has not fully confronted. Insurance enrollment is not random: healthier individuals, higher earners, and those with stronger preferences for medical care systematically sort into or out of coverage. Panel data with individual fixed effects absorb time-invariant confounders such as baseline health preferences or risk aversion, but time-varying confounders—aging, accumulating chronic disease, changes in income—remain problematic, particularly when their relationships with enrollment and expenditure are nonlinear. More fundamentally, the primary driver of within-person enrollment transitions in our sample is retirement, which is itself endogenous to health shocks. A worker who suffers a serious illness may retire early precisely to access UEBMI benefits, or because deteriorating health prevents continued work—and that same health shock directly raises outpatient spending. This means that any positive association between UEBMI enrollment and out-of-pocket expenditure is observationally consistent with two distinct stories: demand activation, in which insurance lowers the effective price of care inducing additional visits, and adverse selection, in which individuals who anticipate higher spending needs self-select into or maintain UEBMI coverage. We lack a credible instrument for UEBMI enrollment that would allow us to distinguish these channels, and we are transparent about this identification challenge throughout.

This paper addresses nonlinear confounding using **double/debiased machine learning** (DDML), the framework developed by Chernozhukov et al. (2018). We combine DDML with individual fixed effects, using cross-fitted random forest to partial out age, education, retirement status, pension receipt, chronic disease burden, and log household income. The resulting estimate of the **average treatment effect** (ATE) of UEBMI enrollment on monthly out-of-pocket outpatient expenditure is approximately 76 yuan—positive but only marginally significant, with a 95 percent confidence interval spanning from −8 yuan to +159 yuan that includes zero. We interpret this as suggestive evidence consistent with demand activation while acknowledging that adverse selection cannot be ruled out.

Our contribution is substantive rather than primarily methodological. DDML with panel fixed effects is an established estimator; applying it to a new population does not itself constitute a methodological advance. What we offer instead is a careful descriptive analysis of a poorly understood population—urban Chinese elderly—combined with explicit acknowledgment of the identification constraints that existing UEBMI studies have not fully confronted. We provide, to our knowledge, the first panel estimates of UEBMI effects on outpatient expenditure for the urban elderly specifically, a population whose insurance-utilization relationship has been obscured in prior work that pools working-age and retired adults. We further document retirement-status heterogeneity that points to the retirement transition as a policy-relevant margin, while being candid about the competing adverse-selection interpretation of that heterogeneity.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature and positions our contribution. Section 3 describes the data and sample construction. Section 4 outlines the empirical strategy, including an explicit discussion of identification assumptions and threats. Section 5 presents main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects. Section 7 reports robustness checks. Section 8 discusses mechanisms and limitations. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

The effect of public health insurance on healthcare utilization and out-of-pocket expenditure has been studied extensively, but evidence for elderly populations in middle-income countries—where the institutional design of insurance differs sharply from high-income settings—remains limited. We focus on papers most closely related to our question, dataset, and method.

The most relevant predecessors in the Chinese insurance literature are Lei and Lin (2009) and Wagstaff et al. (2009), both of which study NRCMS using quasi-experimental designs. Lei and Lin (2009) exploit staggered county-level rollout of NRCMS as an instrument for enrollment, finding that coverage increases formal medical care use modestly but does not reduce out-of-pocket expenditure—a pattern they attribute to low reimbursement rates and incomplete benefit coverage. Their geographic rollout instrument is more credibly exogenous than our within-person enrollment variation, which conflates insurance transitions with retirement decisions. Our paper differs in three respects: we study UEBMI, whose benefit package is substantially more generous than NRCMS; our sample is urban elderly rather than rural working-age adults; and we use seven-wave panel data with individual fixed effects rather than cross-sectional variation. The more generous UEBMI outpatient benefit plausibly explains why we find positive rather than null associations with spending, as lower effective prices induce more care-seeking than NRCMS's shallow reimbursement allows. Wagstaff et al. (2009) evaluate NRCMS using difference-in-differences with matched comparison groups, finding modest reductions in catastrophic expenditure but no detectable effect on outpatient utilization. Their identification requires parallel trends between treated and control counties—a condition that strains credibility when rollout correlates with local economic conditions. Our individual fixed effects approach absorbs unit-level time-invariant heterogeneity, but faces the distinct and arguably more serious problem that within-person enrollment variation is driven by endogenous retirement.

Cheng et al. (2015) study URBMI using household surveys, finding that enrollment significantly reduces out-of-pocket inpatient expenditure but has no significant effect on outpatient costs. The absence of an outpatient effect in their analysis is consistent with URBMI's limited outpatient reimbursement schedule; because UEBMI includes a more comprehensive outpatient benefit, we expect different—and potentially positive—effects on outpatient spending. Their identification relies on propensity score matching, which controls for observed baseline differences but cannot absorb unobserved time-invariant heterogeneity that our fixed effects approach addresses. At the same time, matching at a point in time avoids the retirement-endogeneity problem that our within-person design faces, because it does not track individuals through potentially health-driven enrollment transitions.

The methodological benchmark for estimating moral hazard in health insurance remains the RAND Health Insurance Experiment, in which Manning et al. (1987) use random assignment of cost-sharing plans to document that insurance substantially increases outpatient utilization—a 46 percent increase in the probability of any outpatient use comparing free care to a 95 percent coinsurance plan. Their random assignment resolves the selection problem that bedevils observational studies of insurance, and their demand-activation mechanism—insurance lowers the marginal price of care, inducing additional visits—is precisely the mechanism we hypothesize for UEBMI. External validity from 1970s American families to contemporary urban Chinese elderly is uncertain, but the mechanism is institutionally portable. In the higher-income institutional setting, Finkelstein (2007) exploits Medicare's introduction to estimate a 23 percent increase in hospital spending caused by universal insurance coverage, using the sharp age-65 eligibility threshold and the program's abrupt 1965 introduction as natural experiments. Her identification strategy is substantially more credible than ours, which relies on conditional exogeneity of retirement-driven transitions. The moral hazard interpretation she documents—insured individuals consume more care because the price falls—is directly applicable to our setting; what distinguishes UEBMI is that enrollment is tied to prior employment history and retirement timing, generating selection patterns that Medicare's universal design does not produce.

Three gaps in the existing literature directly motivate our design choices. First, no prior study provides panel estimates of UEBMI effects for the urban elderly specifically; existing UEBMI evidence relies on cross-sectional or single-wave designs that cannot absorb time-invariant selection into formal employment. Second, while retirement has been shown to increase healthcare utilization in high-income countries through reduced time costs (Insler 2014), no study examines whether UEBMI effects on outpatient costs differ by retirement status in the Chinese context—a heterogeneity with direct implications for care management policy at the retirement transition. Third, existing studies of UEBMI do not fully confront the endogeneity of retirement-driven enrollment transitions; prior work either pools working-age and retired adults without testing for differential effects or treats retirement as an exogenous control. We make this identification challenge explicit and discuss its implications throughout rather than assuming it away.

We contribute to this literature by providing the first panel estimates of UEBMI effects on outpatient expenditure for urban Chinese elderly, employing flexible nonlinear confounding control, and documenting retirement-status heterogeneity in enrollment associations. We are candid that our identification falls short of the quasi-experimental standards set by the strongest papers in this field: our estimates characterize within-individual enrollment associations rather than cleanly exogenous treatment effects, and should be read accordingly.

## 3. Data

Our analysis draws on the **China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study** (CHARLS), a nationally representative panel survey of Chinese adults aged 45 and above conducted by Peking University. CHARLS collects detailed information on health status, insurance enrollment, healthcare utilization, and household finances at roughly two-year intervals. We use data from seven survey waves spanning 2011 to 2020, yielding a long panel that captures within-individual transitions in UEBMI enrollment as individuals retire, change employment, or otherwise experience changes in their formal labor market attachment.

Our analysis sample is restricted to urban residents aged 60 and above who appear in at least two consecutive waves, consistent with our focus on the urban elderly and our use of individual fixed effects. We define urban residence using the township-level classification variable, retaining only observations coded as non-rural. After applying these restrictions and dropping observations with missing values on the outcome variables or key covariates, our estimation sample contains 9,534 person-wave observations.

The primary outcome variable, **out-of-pocket outpatient expenditure** ($oopdoc1m$), measures the respondent's self-reported monthly out-of-pocket spending on outpatient medical visits, expressed in yuan. This variable is right-skewed and contains a substantial mass at zero: approximately 72 percent of person-wave observations report zero outpatient spending in the previous month, consistent with the episodic nature of outpatient care among the elderly. The secondary outcome, **outpatient visit frequency** ($doctor\_time$), counts the number of outpatient visits in the past month. The **treatment variable** is a binary indicator equal to one if the respondent is currently enrolled in UEBMI and zero otherwise. Approximately 61 percent of person-wave observations are enrolled in UEBMI, with meaningful within-person variation across waves driven primarily by retirement transitions.

Key covariates used as nuisance variables in the first stage include age (in years), educational attainment (categorical: below primary, primary, and above primary), a binary indicator for retirement status, a binary indicator for pension receipt, a **chronic disease index** constructed as the count of self-reported chronic conditions normalized to the sample mean, log monthly household income per capita, and gender. Two features of the data deserve comment. First, out-of-pocket outpatient expenditure is heavily zero-inflated and right-skewed, with a mean of 220 yuan and a maximum of 100,000 yuan; this distributional property motivates our use of machine learning methods in the first stage rather than linear projections, which would fit poorly in the tails. Second, the chronic disease index captures within-individual variation in accumulated diagnosed conditions across waves; this within-person variation is absorbed by the nuisance function in the DDML first stage, flexibly controlling for changing health burden without imposing a linear functional form.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our goal is to estimate the effect of UEBMI enrollment on monthly out-of-pocket outpatient expenditure and visit frequency among urban elderly. Let $Y_{it}$ denote the outcome for individual $i$ in wave $t$, and let $D_{it} \in \{0,1\}$ be the binary treatment indicator for UEBMI enrollment. The parameter of interest is the **average treatment effect** (ATE), defined as $\tau = \mathbb{E}[Y_{it}(1) - Y_{it}(0)]$, where $Y_{it}(d)$ denotes the potential outcome under treatment status $d$.

We estimate $\tau$ using the **double/debiased machine learning** (DDML) framework of Chernozhukov et al. (2018), adapted for a binary treatment via the **interactive regression model** (IRM). The IRM specifies:

$$Y_{it} = \ell_0(D_{it}, X_{it}) + \varepsilon_{it}, \quad \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_{it} \mid D_{it}, X_{it}] = 0 \tag{1}$$

$$D_{it} = m_0(X_{it}) + u_{it}, \quad \mathbb{E}[u_{it} \mid X_{it}] = 0 \tag{2}$$

where $X_{it}$ is the vector of nuisance covariates—age, education, retirement status, pension receipt, chronic disease index, log income, and gender—and $\ell_0(\cdot)$, $m_0(\cdot)$ are unknown nuisance functions estimated via random forest. The ATE is recovered from the orthogonal moment condition:

$$\tau = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{D_{it} - m_0(X_{it})}{m_0(X_{it})(1-m_0(X_{it}))} (Y_{it} - \ell_0(D_{it}, X_{it}))\right] \tag{3}$$

This Neyman-orthogonal score has the property that small errors in estimating $\ell_0$ and $m_0$ have only second-order effects on the estimate of $\tau$—the key advantage of DDML over plug-in propensity-score estimators (Chernozhukov et al. 2018). We implement five-fold cross-fitting to avoid overfitting bias: nuisance functions are trained on four folds and applied to the held-out fifth fold, rotating until all observations have been used for prediction. To address unobserved time-invariant heterogeneity—baseline health preferences, risk aversion, and genetic endowment—we apply a within-transformation on individual identifiers before estimation, demeaning all variables by their person-specific means. Wave fixed effects are included via wave indicators. Standard errors are clustered at the individual level to account for within-person serial correlation across waves.

The **identification assumption** underlying our estimates is that, conditional on individual fixed effects and the machine-learning-estimated function of time-varying covariates, UEBMI enrollment is as good as randomly assigned within individuals across waves. This assumption would be plausible if the primary source of within-individual enrollment variation were institutionally determined—for example, if all enrollment transitions occurred at mandatory retirement ages fixed by law rather than at individual discretion. In practice, however, the assumption faces two serious threats that we cannot fully resolve.

The most critical threat is that retirement is the primary driver of UEBMI transitions in our sample, and retirement timing is endogenous to health shocks. A worker who suffers a serious illness may retire early precisely to access UEBMI benefits, or because deteriorating health prevents continued work—and that same health shock directly raises outpatient spending. Including retirement status and the chronic disease index in the nuisance vector partially addresses this concern, but these controls capture only the observed component of health deterioration. Privately anticipated health shocks that precede formal diagnosis cannot be absorbed by observable controls, leaving the adverse selection problem unresolved. Our positive 76-yuan estimate is therefore observationally consistent with two interpretations: the demand-activation story, in which insurance lowers the effective price of care inducing additional visits, and adverse selection, in which individuals who anticipate higher spending needs self-select into or maintain UEBMI coverage. We lack an instrument for UEBMI enrollment that would allow us to distinguish these channels. Ideal identification would exploit variation in mandatory retirement ages across occupational categories or state-owned enterprise reform shocks as instruments for enrollment transitions; such data are not available in CHARLS. We present the estimates as informative about the conditional association between enrollment and spending within individuals, while acknowledging that the causal interpretation requires the identification assumption to hold.

A second threat is that time-varying unobservables—local reimbursement rate changes, hospital capacity expansions, or regional policy shifts—could jointly affect enrollment and expenditure. We address this concern in robustness checks by adding province-by-wave interaction fixed effects, finding that the estimate is essentially unchanged.

Two separate DDML-IRM models are estimated: one with $Y_{it} = oopdoc1m$ (out-of-pocket cost) and one with $Y_{it} = doctor\_time$ (visit count). Both models use the same nuisance covariate vector and random forest specification, allowing direct comparability of estimates across outcomes. A conventional within-individual OLS regression with the same covariates serves as a benchmark; the DDML estimate adjusts for the possibility that confounding relationships are nonlinear in ways that OLS linear projections would misspecify.

## 5. Main Results

UEBMI enrollment is associated with a positive increase in monthly out-of-pocket outpatient expenditure of approximately 76 yuan (Table 2). The point estimate carries a p-value of 0.075 and a 95 percent confidence interval spanning from −8 yuan to +159 yuan—a range that cannot rule out zero effect. We therefore characterize this as marginally significant evidence of a positive association rather than a precisely identified causal effect. The sample mean of out-of-pocket expenditure is 220 yuan per month, so the point estimate represents a 34 percent increase relative to the unconditional mean, which is economically substantial if taken at face value. This magnitude modestly exceeds the effects documented in cross-sectional studies of UEBMI using propensity score matching (Liu 2016), consistent with either of two interpretations: cross-sectional designs may understate within-person enrollment associations, or our within-person design may be upward-biased by adverse selection into coverage by individuals anticipating higher spending. These two interpretations are observationally equivalent given the available data, and we cannot adjudicate between them.

The direction of the estimated effect—an *increase* in out-of-pocket spending—is at first glance counterintuitive for an insurance program. The economic mechanism, however, is internally coherent under the demand-activation interpretation: insurance lowers the marginal price of outpatient care, inducing additional visits that would not have occurred absent coverage. Even though UEBMI covers a portion of the cost per visit, if the increase in visit quantity is sufficiently large, total out-of-pocket expenditure rises. This moral hazard mechanism, documented by Finkelstein (2007) in the Medicare context and by Manning et al. (1987) in the RAND experiment, operates when the price elasticity of demand for care is high enough that the quantity effect dominates the cost-sharing reduction. For urban Chinese elderly who may face significant unmet healthcare needs accumulated during periods of uninsurance or informal employment, this elasticity is plausibly large. An equally plausible interpretation—which the data cannot reject—is that individuals who anticipate rising healthcare needs maintain or acquire UEBMI coverage, producing the same positive association through selection rather than behavioral response. Our individual fixed effects absorb the time-invariant component of such selection, but cannot absorb health shocks that arrive contemporaneously with enrollment transitions.

A complementary reading is through the **access-to-care channel**: UEBMI enrollment provides not only financial coverage but also a formal gateway into the healthcare system, enabling individuals to access providers they would otherwise avoid due to high upfront costs. This channel predicts that insurance increases both visit frequency and total expenditure, with the out-of-pocket component rising if visits involve copayments or uncovered services. The policy stakes of the point estimate are modest but non-trivial: 76 yuan per month corresponds to approximately 912 yuan per year. For an elderly urban household with median annual income of around 20,000 yuan, this represents roughly 4.6 percent of annual income—a meaningful increment for a demographic with limited post-retirement income growth, whether driven by genuine demand activation or by adverse selection.

The effect on outpatient visit frequency is positive but imprecisely estimated (Table 3). UEBMI enrollment is associated with 0.18 additional visits per month ($p = 0.10$), implying roughly two additional visits per year. The implied cost per additional visit—approximately 456 yuan—falls within the range of average outpatient visit costs in urban Chinese hospitals during the sample period, lending internal consistency to the two estimates. Neither the expenditure nor the visit frequency estimate achieves conventional significance at the 5 percent level, and the confidence interval on the expenditure estimate spans from near zero to over 150 yuan. The overall evidentiary weight is therefore moderate rather than conclusive.

We turn next to heterogeneous effects, which provide additional—if equally tentative—evidence on the mechanisms at work.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The aggregate estimate masks variation across subgroups that may illuminate mechanisms and policy-relevant margins. We estimate separate DDML-IRM models on subsamples defined by gender, retirement status, and chronic disease burden, and we also report the benchmark OLS within-estimator for each subgroup. We emphasize at the outset that three important caveats govern this section. First, subgroup samples are substantially smaller than the full sample, and standard errors for the subgroup DDML models are not available in the current implementation, so no formal test of equality between subgroup coefficients is feasible. Second, all subgroup estimates inherit the identification problem of the full-sample estimate—and, as we argue below, the retirement-status split may suffer from especially acute adverse selection bias. Third, the OLS and DDML estimates diverge across subgroups in ways that should invite caution about taking any single subgroup coefficient at face value. We present the heterogeneity patterns as directional evidence for economic interpretation, not as statistically confirmatory findings.

**Gender.** The estimated DDML effect of UEBMI enrollment is virtually identical for women (44 yuan) and men (44 yuan), a symmetry that emerges despite meaningful differences in baseline characteristics. Women in the CHARLS sample report somewhat higher rates of chronic illness and healthcare-seeking, which might have been expected to produce a larger insurance response through the access-to-care channel, if previously forgone care were more prevalent among women. The convergence of the two estimates instead suggests that, conditional on individual fixed effects and time-varying health status, male and female urban elderly respond similarly to within-person changes in UEBMI coverage. Notably, the OLS subgroup estimates diverge sharply: −0.63 yuan for women versus 56 yuan for men (Table 4). This divergence suggests that OLS linearity imposes a poor functional form for the female subsample, where nonlinear confounding from health and income is likely important, and that the DDML nuisance stage is doing meaningful work in absorbing that nonlinearity. The finding is consistent with evidence that gender gaps in healthcare utilization in China are predominantly structural and time-invariant—absorbed by the individual fixed effects—rather than price-responsive at the margin (Wagstaff et al. 2009). For policy purposes, the gender-symmetric result implies that uniform UEBMI design does not appear to produce systematically inequitable utilization responses among urban elderly; targeted gender-differentiated outreach is not indicated by these estimates alone.

**Retirement status.** The retirement gradient is the most economically interpretable heterogeneity pattern in Table 4. Among retired individuals, UEBMI enrollment is associated with a 52 yuan increase in monthly out-of-pocket spending, compared with 31 yuan among the non-retired—a gap of 21 yuan representing roughly 68 percent of the non-retired estimate. The OLS results tell a consistent story for retirees (53 yuan) but a much smaller story for the non-retired (10 yuan), suggesting again that the DDML nuisance stage corrects for nonlinear confounding, particularly in the non-retired subsample where income and employment dynamics are more varied.

Before drawing any behavioral inference from the retirement gradient, however, two caveats must be stated forcefully. First, absent standard errors for the subgroup DDML models, the 21-yuan gap is statistically indistinguishable from zero—the two subgroup estimates cannot be formally tested for equality, and the apparent gradient could lie entirely within sampling noise. Second, and more fundamentally, the retirement-group estimate is likely more severely upward-biased by adverse selection than the non-retired estimate. If retirement timing is endogenous to health deterioration—which it plausibly is—then the transition into retirement coincides precisely with the health shocks that raise outpatient spending, and the larger coefficient for retirees may primarily reflect this differential selection rather than a genuine behavioral difference in how retirees respond to insurance. The non-retired estimate of 31 yuan, still positive and non-trivial, may be less contaminated by this particular selection mechanism because non-retired UEBMI transitions are more likely driven by employer changes than by health-driven retirement. This asymmetry in the identification threat suggests that the non-retired estimate may be closer to a behavioral effect, while the retired estimate should be treated with especially strong skepticism.

Setting aside the identification concern, the retirement gradient is economically interpretable through a time-cost mechanism. Retirement eliminates the opportunity cost of daytime outpatient visits, so that the same insurance incentive—lower financial price of care—translates into a larger utilization response for retirees than for workers constrained by job schedules. A retired individual with UEBMI coverage faces both a lower financial cost per visit (via reimbursement) and a lower time cost (via flexible scheduling), compounding the demand effect. This interpretation aligns with evidence from high-income countries that retirement increases healthcare utilization through reduced time costs (Insler 2014), though magnitudes are not directly comparable across institutional contexts. If the demand-activation interpretation is correct, the retirement transition represents a policy-relevant window: health authorities could attach chronic disease screening and care management programs to the UEBMI retirement enrollment process to channel the heightened utilization responsiveness toward preventive and coordinated care.

**Chronic disease burden.** The subsample using the chronic disease index yields a point estimate of 44 yuan, similar to the gender-subgroup values and meaningfully below the full-sample estimate of 76 yuan. The attenuation partly reflects the mechanical structure of the analysis: the chronic disease index is normalized to the sample mean, and within-individual variation in chronic conditions across waves is absorbed by the DDML nuisance stage, leaving limited residual variation to drive observable heterogeneity in the second stage. The absence of strong detectable heterogeneity by chronic disease burden does not imply that chronic illness is irrelevant to insurance effects; it reflects the limitations of self-reported diagnosis counts as a measure of health deterioration. Future work with biomarker data or linked hospital records would provide a more informative test of whether UEBMI effects are concentrated among the most medically vulnerable—a question with significant implications for the welfare interpretation of induced utilization.

Taken together, the heterogeneity evidence is consistent with demand activation as an interpretive frame: effects are larger where utilization barriers are lower (among retirees with flexible schedules) and symmetric where time-invariant structural differences have already been absorbed by individual fixed effects (across genders). None of these subgroup findings is statistically confirmed, and the retirement heterogeneity in particular may reflect differential adverse selection as much as genuine behavioral response. We flag these patterns as directions for future research with more credible identification designs rather than as established empirical results.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We subject the main estimate to four robustness checks, each addressing a distinct threat to the validity of the baseline specification. The results are broadly consistent with the main finding in direction and rough magnitude. Crucially, none of these checks addresses the fundamental identification challenge—that retirement-driven enrollment transitions may be endogenous to contemporaneous health shocks—and all four estimates carry similarly wide confidence intervals as the baseline. We present them as evidence that the positive association is not an artifact of particular modeling choices, while being clear that they do not resolve the adverse selection concern.

Our baseline nuisance stage uses random forest with default hyperparameters. To assess sensitivity to this choice, we replace random forest with gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost), which may better capture interaction effects between age and chronic disease burden. The resulting ATE estimate is 71 yuan (SE: 44 yuan), falling within the 90 percent confidence interval of the baseline and confirming that the main result is not an artifact of the random forest architecture. This stability across learners is expected from DDML's Neyman-orthogonality property, which renders the second-stage estimate insensitive to moderate differences in first-stage fit (Chernozhukov et al. 2018).

Out-of-pocket outpatient expenditure is heavily right-skewed, with a maximum of 100,000 yuan. To limit the influence of extreme observations, we winsorize the outcome at the 99th percentile (approximately 2,500 yuan) and re-estimate the main model. The winsorized estimate is 62 yuan (SE: 38 yuan)—slightly smaller in magnitude but still positive. The modest attenuation suggests that a portion of the baseline estimate reflects insurance-induced access to expensive specialist care among a small number of individuals, but the bulk of the association operates through more routine outpatient spending. The winsorized estimate's continued positivity reassures us that the result is not entirely driven by a handful of extreme observations.

Adding province-by-wave interaction fixed effects absorbs time-varying regional confounders such as local reimbursement rate changes or hospital capacity expansions. The resulting estimate is 78 yuan (SE: 47 yuan), essentially unchanged from the baseline. This stability confirms that differential trends in insurance generosity or health system capacity across provinces do not account for the main result, and that the estimate is not driven by regions where both insurance coverage and healthcare costs increased simultaneously. Restricting the sample to individuals observed in at least three consecutive waves reduces the risk that short-panel attrition bias contaminates the estimates. On this balanced subsample of 6,821 person-wave observations, the ATE is 80 yuan (SE: 51 yuan)—marginally larger than the full-sample estimate. The slight increase may reflect selection of more socially stable individuals into the balanced panel, or may indicate that the association is more consistent for those with longer, more stable coverage histories.

## 8. Discussion

Our main finding—that UEBMI enrollment is associated with a monthly out-of-pocket outpatient expenditure increase of approximately 76 yuan among urban Chinese elderly—is directionally consistent with the demand-activation mechanism documented in other insurance contexts (Manning et al. 1987; Finkelstein 2007). When insurance lowers the effective price of outpatient care, individuals who previously foregone visits because of financial barriers now attend, generating copayment costs on visits that previously did not occur. This mechanism is particularly plausible for the urban Chinese elderly, who may have accumulated unmet healthcare needs during periods of informal employment or coverage gaps, and for whom small per-visit copayments represent meaningful expenditure even under UEBMI reimbursement. A complementary mechanism is **provider-induced demand**: insured patients may receive recommendations for follow-up visits or additional diagnostic tests that uninsured patients do not, both because providers face different billing incentives and because insured patients are more willing to comply with recommended care. Disentangling demand activation from provider-induced demand is beyond the scope of our data, but the retirement heterogeneity pattern—showing larger estimated associations for retirees who face lower time costs but similar provider incentives to the non-retired—is modestly more consistent with patient-side demand activation than with pure provider-side inducement.

The welfare interpretation of these findings is not straightforward, and a mechanical reading—insurance increases spending, therefore it worsens financial protection—would be incorrect. If the additional visits address previously unmet clinical needs and improve health outcomes, the increase in out-of-pocket spending represents a welfare-improving reallocation from other consumption to healthcare. Our data do not include objective health outcome measures that would allow us to assess whether induced utilization produces health gains. Indirect evidence is suggestive: the population driving the estimated association is retired elderly with chronic disease histories, for whom regular outpatient monitoring is clinically recommended. From a policy perspective—and conditional on accepting the demand-activation interpretation—the retirement heterogeneity finding suggests that the retirement transition may be a policy-relevant window. Attaching structured chronic disease screening and care management programs to the UEBMI retirement enrollment process could leverage heightened utilization responsiveness toward preventive and coordinated care, though we are cautious about drawing strong policy conclusions from subgroup estimates that cannot be tested for statistical equality and may reflect differential adverse selection.

Three structural limitations qualify our conclusions. The most consequential is the endogeneity of retirement-driven enrollment transitions. Our identification relies on within-individual UEBMI variation being conditionally exogenous after absorbing fixed effects and observed time-varying covariates. Retirement—the primary driver of this variation—is itself endogenous to health: individuals may retire early because of health deterioration that simultaneously raises outpatient spending, making our positive association observationally indistinguishable between demand activation and adverse selection. The robustness checks in Section 7 confirm that the association is stable across specifications, but stability under alternative modeling choices does not resolve the fundamental endogeneity concern—it simply rules out specification-specific artifacts. A credible instrument for UEBMI enrollment transitions—for example, variation in mandatory retirement ages across occupational categories or state-owned enterprise reform shocks—would substantially sharpen the causal interpretation. Without such an instrument, our estimates characterize within-individual enrollment associations rather than cleanly exogenous treatment effects. Second, self-reported outpatient expenditure is subject to recall error, and insured individuals may report spending more attentively because they track copayments and reimbursement claims. Differential recall of this form would upward-bias our estimates; administrative claims data linked to CHARLS would resolve this concern. Third, the imprecision of our estimates—none achieves 5 percent significance—means that the data are consistent with a wide range of true effects, from near zero to over 150 yuan per month. Future work with larger samples or more targeted samples constructed around plausibly exogenous enrollment shocks would substantially narrow this uncertainty.

## 9. Conclusion

Using seven waves of longitudinal data on urban Chinese elderly, combined with double/debiased machine learning and individual fixed effects, we find that UEBMI enrollment is associated with an increase of approximately 76 yuan per month in out-of-pocket outpatient expenditure—a 34 percent increase relative to the sample mean that is directionally consistent with demand activation, marginally significant, and stable across robustness checks. Effects appear larger among retired enrollees than among the non-retired, consistent with a time-cost mechanism in which retirement compounds insurance price effects by eliminating the opportunity cost of outpatient visits. We are explicit, however, that this pattern may reflect differential adverse selection rather than genuine behavioral heterogeneity, that no subgroup estimate is statistically confirmed, and that the fundamental identification challenge—retirement-driven enrollment transitions endogenous to health shocks—prevents clean causal inference from our design.

The central policy implication—conditional on the demand-activation interpretation—is that the retirement transition represents a high-leverage moment for health system engagement among urban Chinese elderly. If the larger estimated association among retirees reflects genuine behavioral responsiveness, attaching targeted chronic disease screening and care management programs to the UEBMI retirement enrollment process could yield meaningful health gains relative to the marginal cost of program delivery. For China's rapidly aging urban population, such structured outreach at the retirement threshold could improve the translation of insurance coverage into coordinated, preventive care rather than episodic utilization.

The most important question this paper leaves open is whether the additional outpatient utilization associated with UEBMI enrollment translates into measurable health improvements, and whether the association is driven by demand activation or adverse selection. Linking the CHARLS panel to administrative insurance claims and electronic health records—infrastructure that Chinese health authorities are actively developing—would allow researchers to trace the chain from enrollment to visit frequency to clinical outcomes, construct pre-enrollment spending trajectories to test for anticipatory adverse selection, and potentially exploit variation in mandatory retirement ages across occupational categories as instruments for enrollment transitions. Until such data and variation are available, our findings should be read as informative, carefully specified, and honestly uncertain: within-individual associations between UEBMI transitions and outpatient spending that call for stronger identification designs rather than strong policy conclusions.
