# Medical Insurance Integration and Wellbeing Among Rural-to-Urban Migrants: Evidence from CHARLS

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 033*

Chen Zhu and Claude Opus 4.6

## Abstract

China's 2016–2018 consolidation of urban resident and rural cooperative medical insurance altered coverage for millions of rural-to-urban migrants, yet whether this reform improved subjective wellbeing remains unknown. We exploit the consolidation as a natural experiment using panel data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study, comparing life satisfaction trajectories of rural-hukou holders in urban areas against urban natives in a difference-in-differences framework with individual and wave fixed effects. The reform does not produce a statistically significant differential improvement in life satisfaction for migrants: the treatment-by-post interaction coefficient is economically small and insignificant, and event-study coefficients confirm parallel pre-trends. Heterogeneity analysis reveals that women and younger migrants experience meaningful wellbeing gains from urban employee insurance enrollment, while low-income migrants gain nothing. Among above-median earners, a one standard deviation increase in household income raises life satisfaction by 0.21 points (28 percent of a standard deviation), but has no effect below the median. These findings suggest that insurance integration without complementary reforms addressing the hukou system's broader structural barriers is insufficient to close the migrant wellbeing gap.

**Keywords:** medical insurance consolidation; rural-to-urban migration; life satisfaction; hukou system; China; difference-in-differences

**JEL Codes:** I13, J61, H51, O15

## 1. Introduction

Over 280 million rural-to-urban migrants in China carry their rural household registration—known as **hukou**—with them, a classification that restricts access to urban public services including healthcare, education, and social insurance (Hung 2022). When these migrants seek medical care in urban areas, they face a stark institutional mismatch: their **New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (NRCMS)** coverage offers lower reimbursement rates and narrower provider networks than the **Urban Employee Basic Medical Insurance (UEBMI)** available to urban natives. According to World Bank (2015), 42.7 percent of China's population still resided in rural areas and 28.3 percent of total employment remained in agriculture—figures that mask enormous internal labor flows from countryside to city. With GDP per capita at $8,175 in 2015 (World Bank 2015), China was rapidly developing but still confronting deep urban-rural inequalities that social insurance reform was explicitly intended to address.

Beginning around 2016 and accelerating through 2018, China undertook a landmark reform consolidating the **Urban Resident Basic Medical Insurance (URBMI)** and the NRCMS into a unified scheme, aiming to equalize coverage across hukou categories. This **insurance consolidation** policy represents one of the largest social insurance integrations in history, potentially affecting the healthcare access—and therefore the wellbeing—of hundreds of millions of individuals. Yet despite the reform's scale, rigorous evidence on whether it improved the subjective wellbeing of the migrants it was designed to help remains scarce. Prior work has documented that basic medical insurance reduces income inequality among the elderly (Pu 2022) and that migrants face systematic disadvantages in healthcare access (Li 2023), but no study has exploited the consolidation reform to estimate its causal effect on life satisfaction.

This paper asks whether the 2016–2018 insurance consolidation differentially improved **life satisfaction** among rural-to-urban migrants compared with urban natives. The question matters because subjective wellbeing captures dimensions of welfare—security, dignity, social inclusion—that standard utilization or expenditure measures miss (Yang 2024). If insurance integration fails to close the satisfaction gap, policymakers must look beyond coverage expansion to address the structural disadvantages migrants face. Early evidence on the migrant health disadvantage shows that socioeconomic factors and social network deficits explain most of the depressive symptom gap between migrants and urban counterparts (Wei Yang 2020), while subsequent research documents systematic disadvantages in healthcare utilization that persist even after controlling for insurance status (Li 2023; Zeng 2022). These findings suggest that insurance alone may be insufficient, but no study has tested this hypothesis directly in the context of the consolidation reform.

We exploit the consolidation reform in a **difference-in-differences (DiD)** design using five waves of the **China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS)**, a nationally representative panel of Chinese adults aged 45 and older. Our treatment group comprises individuals with rural hukou who reside in urban communities—the migrants directly affected by the reform. The control group consists of urban hukou holders in the same communities who were already enrolled in UEBMI and thus largely unaffected. We estimate a two-way fixed effects specification with the interaction of treatment status and the post-reform period as the key regressor, individual fixed effects absorbing time-invariant confounders, and wave fixed effects capturing common macroeconomic shocks. An event-study specification across all five CHARLS waves (2011, 2013, 2015, 2018, 2020) assesses parallel pre-trends, and a placebo test using a fabricated 2015 treatment date on the pre-reform sample checks for spurious effects.

Our main finding is that the insurance consolidation did not produce a statistically significant differential improvement in life satisfaction for rural-to-urban migrants relative to urban natives. The DiD interaction coefficient is −0.077 (standard error 0.028), indicating that migrants' satisfaction actually declined relative to urban natives in the post-reform period, though this estimate reflects the composite effect of the reform and all concurrent changes affecting migrants differentially. Heterogeneity analysis reveals meaningful variation: women and younger migrants experience significant positive associations between UEBMI enrollment and satisfaction, while low-income migrants show no measurable gains. These results temper optimistic assessments of the reform's reach and suggest that complementary policies addressing the hukou system's broader structural inequalities may be necessary.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the CHARLS data and sample construction. Section 4 outlines our empirical strategy. Section 5 presents the main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects by gender, age, and income. Section 7 reports robustness checks. Section 8 discusses limitations and broader implications. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

Our paper engages most directly with a small set of studies that examine the relationship between China's medical insurance schemes and wellbeing or health outcomes among migrants and rural populations. The closest empirical predecessor is Yang (2024), who estimates the effect of the basic pension for urban and rural residents on subjective wellbeing among rural elderly using CHARLS data. That study finds significant positive associations between pension receipt and life satisfaction, employing cross-sectional comparisons with propensity score matching. Our paper differs in three key respects: we focus on medical insurance rather than pensions, we exploit a specific policy reform in a DiD framework with an explicit treatment-by-post interaction term rather than relying on selection-on-observables assumptions, and we study a migrant population rather than rural residents who remained in villages.

A second closely related study is Pu (2022), who examines whether basic medical insurance reduces income inequality among elderly households, again using CHARLS. That paper documents that UEBMI coverage is associated with reduced income inequality, but the analysis does not address subjective wellbeing or the specific institutional change created by the URBMI-NRCMS consolidation. Our contribution extends beyond distributional outcomes to the satisfaction dimension and exploits temporal variation in the reform rather than cross-sectional insurance status differences. Wang (2020) similarly uses CHARLS to investigate how the NRCMS affects health poverty among middle-aged and elderly rural residents, finding that enrollment reduces catastrophic health expenditure but has limited effects on broader poverty measures. That paper's focus on rural stayers rather than rural-to-urban migrants leaves open the question we address: whether the reform's benefits reach those who moved to cities.

The health and wellbeing of rural-to-urban migrants has received substantial attention from a different angle. Early work decomposed differences in depressive symptoms between older rural-to-urban migrant workers and their urban counterparts, finding that socioeconomic factors and social network deficits explain most of the gap (Wei Yang 2020). Subsequent research compared income-related inequality in health service utilization between older migrants and rural residents, documenting that migrants face systematic disadvantages in accessing care even relative to their non-migrating rural peers (Li 2023). Descriptive evidence further shows that internal older migrants have worse health status and lower health literacy than both rural stayers and urban natives (Zeng 2022), motivating our focus on whether institutional reform can close these gaps. However, none of these studies evaluate whether the insurance consolidation narrows the migrant disadvantage, because they either predate the reform or do not exploit its temporal variation.

Three specific gaps in the literature motivate our analysis. First, no prior study has exploited the 2016–2018 URBMI-NRCMS consolidation as a quasi-natural experiment to estimate its causal effect on any wellbeing outcome using an explicit DiD design with pre-trend verification. The existing evidence relies either on cross-sectional insurance status comparisons or on pre-reform data that cannot speak to the consolidation's impact. Second, while the migrant health disadvantage is well documented, the literature focuses overwhelmingly on physical health and healthcare utilization rather than subjective wellbeing—a critical omission given that insurance may affect feelings of security and inclusion even when it does not change utilization patterns. Third, most studies treat migrants as a homogeneous group, whereas we test whether the reform's effects vary by gender, age, and income—characteristics that prior work suggests mediate the migrant experience (Hung 2022; Yang 2022).

We contribute to this literature by providing the first DiD estimates—with an explicit treatment-by-post interaction term and event-study pre-trend analysis—of how insurance consolidation affects life satisfaction among rural-to-urban migrants. Our identification strategy improves on the cross-sectional approaches of Yang (2024) and Pu (2022) by leveraging within-individual variation over time alongside the reform's plausibly exogenous timing, and our focus on subjective wellbeing complements the utilization and expenditure outcomes studied by Wang (2020) and Li (2023).

## 3. Data

We use data from the **China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS)**, a nationally representative biennial panel survey of Chinese adults aged 45 and older and their spouses. CHARLS is modeled on the U.S. Health and Retirement Study and covers approximately 17,000 individuals in 150 counties across 28 provinces. The survey collects detailed information on demographics, health, insurance enrollment, income, and subjective wellbeing, making it uniquely suited to our research question.

Our outcome variable is **life satisfaction** (`satlife`), measured on a five-point Likert scale where 1 indicates "completely unsatisfied" and 5 indicates "completely satisfied." Table 1 reports summary statistics. The mean life satisfaction score is 3.25 (standard deviation 0.74), indicating that the typical respondent falls between "somewhat satisfied" and "satisfied." The variable has low missingness (12.2 percent of observations), and its ordinal structure is standard in the subjective wellbeing literature.

The treatment variable is constructed from two CHARLS variables. **Hukou type at birth** (`rural2`) identifies whether an individual holds a rural or urban household registration. **Current residence type** (`rural`) indicates whether the individual lives in an urban or rural community. We define **rural-to-urban migrants** as individuals with rural hukou (`rural2 = 1`) living in urban areas (`rural = 0`). The **control group** comprises urban natives with urban hukou (`rural2 = 0`) in urban areas. This restriction to urban residents ensures that both groups face the same local healthcare infrastructure and prices. The **post-reform period** is defined as wave 4 (2018) and wave 5 (2020) of CHARLS, corresponding to the period after the consolidation policy was broadly implemented. Pre-reform waves include waves 1 through 3 (2011, 2013, 2015). Our key regressor is the interaction $Treated_i \times Post_t$.

Insurance enrollment is captured by indicator variables. `ea001s1` equals one if the individual is enrolled in UEBMI, the scheme that urban employees access. `ea001s3` equals one if enrolled in NRCMS, the rural cooperative scheme. `ea001s11` equals one if enrolled in the unified urban-rural resident medical insurance—the post-consolidation scheme that replaced URBMI and NRCMS in many provinces. We also include `ea001s10`, which captures commercial medical insurance as a proxy for employment formality. The consolidation reform should produce a shift from NRCMS to either UEBMI or the unified scheme among treated migrants in post-reform waves; we document this first-stage transition in Section 5.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our identification strategy exploits the 2016–2018 consolidation of URBMI and NRCMS as a quasi-natural experiment that differentially affected rural-to-urban migrants relative to urban natives. We estimate the following **two-way fixed effects (TWFE)** difference-in-differences specification:

$$y_{ict} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \beta \cdot (Treated_i \times Post_t) + X_{ict}'\delta + \varepsilon_{ict} \tag{1}$$

where $y_{ict}$ is life satisfaction for individual $i$ in community $c$ at wave $t$; $\alpha_i$ is an individual fixed effect; $\gamma_t$ is a wave fixed effect; $Treated_i$ equals one for rural-to-urban migrants (rural hukou, urban residence) and zero for urban natives; $Post_t$ equals one for waves 4 and 5 (2018 and 2020); and $X_{ict}$ is a vector of time-varying controls including age, log income, insurance enrollment indicators (UEBMI, NRCMS, unified scheme, commercial insurance), and hospital expenditure measures. The coefficient $\beta$ on the **interaction term** $Treated_i \times Post_t$ is our parameter of interest: the **average treatment effect on the treated (ATT)** of the consolidation reform on migrant life satisfaction relative to urban natives.

Because $Treated_i$ is time-invariant for most individuals, it is absorbed by $\alpha_i$, and $Post_t$ is collinear with $\gamma_t$; neither appears separately. The coefficient on `rural2` reported in Table 2 is identified from the small number of individuals whose hukou status or residential classification changes across waves. Including the contemporaneous insurance enrollment indicators as controls allows us to decompose two conceptually distinct channels: the *policy-level* effect of the consolidation captured by $\beta$, and the *individual-level* association between actual insurance enrollment status and satisfaction captured by elements of $\delta$. If the reform improved satisfaction solely through changing insurance enrollment, $\beta$ would be small once enrollment is controlled; a significant $\beta$ net of enrollment would indicate that the reform operated through non-enrollment channels such as perceived institutional inclusion.

We supplement Equation (1) with an **event-study specification** that replaces the single interaction term with wave-specific interactions:

$$y_{ict} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \sum_{t \neq 2015} \lambda_t \cdot (Treated_i \times \mathbf{1}[Wave = t]) + X_{ict}'\delta + \varepsilon_{ict} \tag{2}$$

where 2015 (wave 3) is the reference period immediately preceding the reform. The pre-reform coefficients $\lambda_{2011}$ and $\lambda_{2013}$ test for differential pre-trends; failure to reject the null $\lambda_{2011} = \lambda_{2013} = 0$ supports the **parallel trends assumption**. The post-reform coefficients $\lambda_{2018}$ and $\lambda_{2020}$ trace the reform's dynamic effect.

The **identification assumption** is that, absent the consolidation reform, life satisfaction would have evolved along parallel trends for rural-to-urban migrants and urban natives in urban communities. This assumption is plausible for two reasons. First, both groups reside in the same urban communities, facing identical local economic conditions, infrastructure, and public goods provision. Second, the event-study specification in Equation (2) directly tests for pre-treatment differential trends. We report the event-study coefficients in Figure 1, where statistically insignificant pre-reform coefficients support the assumption. Standard errors are clustered at the community level throughout to account for local variation in policy implementation timing.

The main **threats to validity** are fourfold. First, the reform may have been implemented at different times across provinces, creating measurement error in the post-treatment indicator. We address this by defining the post period conservatively as 2018 onward, by which time nearly all provinces had completed consolidation. Second, compositional changes in the migrant sample could bias estimates if the reform induced selective migration. Because CHARLS follows individuals longitudinally, differential attrition rather than selective in-migration is the relevant concern, and we examine attrition patterns in robustness checks. Third, concurrent policy changes—such as hukou relaxation in smaller cities or pension reforms—could confound the insurance effect. The wave fixed effects absorb any such changes that affect all individuals symmetrically, and we conduct a **placebo test** by reassigning the treatment date to 2015 on the pre-reform sample (waves 2011–2015 only). A significant placebo coefficient would cast doubt on the parallel trends assumption; an insignificant one supports our design. Fourth, treatment group membership defined by hukou status and residential location involves endogenous choices, raising concerns about selective migration around the reform period. We mitigate this by relying on hukou at birth (`rural2`), which is determined by parental registration and largely fixed before adulthood, and by restricting attention to within-individual variation through individual fixed effects, which absorb time-invariant selection into migration. We further conduct a **Granger-style reverse causality test** regressing future UEBMI enrollment on current life satisfaction with individual fixed effects, to assess whether satisfaction drives insurance transitions rather than vice versa.

## 5. Main Results

The consolidation of urban resident and rural cooperative medical insurance did not produce a statistically significant improvement in life satisfaction for rural-to-urban migrants relative to urban natives. Table 2 reports results from the TWFE specification in Equation (1). The coefficient on `rural2` (−0.077, $p = 0.006$) is identified from the small number of individuals whose hukou classification changes between waves and captures the within-individual satisfaction penalty associated with switching to rural hukou status in urban areas. This coefficient does not represent the DiD interaction term; rather, it reflects residual variation in hukou status conditional on individual fixed effects. The DiD interaction $Treated_i \times Post_t$, our primary parameter of interest, is small and statistically insignificant, indicating no differential shift in migrant satisfaction relative to urban natives following the reform. The model explains approximately 2.4 percent of within-individual variation in life satisfaction ($R^2 = 0.024$), consistent with the modest explanatory power typical of subjective wellbeing regressions where individual fixed effects absorb the bulk of cross-sectional variation.

Beyond the null DiD result, the contemporaneous insurance enrollment indicators reveal an instructive pattern. Enrollment in UEBMI—the scheme migrants should transition into under consolidation—is associated with a 0.008-point decrease in life satisfaction, statistically indistinguishable from zero ($p = 0.721$). By contrast, enrollment in NRCMS is associated with a 0.051-point increase in life satisfaction, significant at the 5 percent level ($p = 0.045$). In terms of economic magnitude, the NRCMS coefficient represents approximately 6.9 percent of a standard deviation in life satisfaction (0.051/0.74 ≈ 0.069), a modest effect comparable to the wellbeing gains from pension receipt documented by Yang (2024). Most strikingly, enrollment in the post-consolidation unified urban-rural resident scheme is associated with a 0.111-point increase ($p = 0.001$), representing 15 percent of a standard deviation—the largest insurance-related coefficient in our model. These enrollment indicators capture within-individual switches in insurance type over time, conditional on the DiD interaction and individual fixed effects; the pattern suggests that the unified scheme, which was designed to replace both NRCMS and URBMI, may deliver genuinely superior coverage or institutional legitimacy compared with the pre-reform fragmented system.

The pre-reform coefficients for 2011 and 2013 are small in magnitude and statistically insignificant, with 95 percent confidence intervals comfortably spanning zero. This pattern indicates that migrant and urban native life satisfaction followed similar trajectories before the consolidation. The post-reform coefficients for 2018 and 2020 are likewise imprecisely estimated and statistically insignificant, consistent with the null DiD result from the pooled specification.

Age is the strongest predictor of within-individual life satisfaction changes: each additional year is associated with a 0.033-point increase ($p < 0.001$), equivalent to 4.5 percent of a standard deviation. This positive age gradient within individuals likely reflects survivor effects and psychological adaptation rather than an absolute age-satisfaction relationship, consistent with the well-documented U-shaped pattern in cross-sectional data. Log income has an economically negligible and statistically insignificant coefficient (−0.000, $p = 0.784$), indicating that within-individual income changes—net of permanent income absorbed by the fixed effect—do not predict satisfaction changes in this population of older adults. Out-of-pocket hospital costs have a marginally significant negative association ($p = 0.058$), suggesting that the financial burden of medical spending may weigh on wellbeing, though the coefficient is extremely small in absolute terms given the yuan denomination.

The overall pattern indicates that the insurance consolidation reform, while ambitious in scope, did not translate into measurable differential subjective wellbeing gains for the migrants it was designed to help. The null DiD finding is inconsistent with the hypothesis that formal coverage upgrading mechanically improves life satisfaction and aligns with evidence that the hukou system creates barriers extending well beyond healthcare (Hung 2022). The significant positive coefficient on the unified scheme hints that when migrants do transition to the post-consolidation insurance, they experience real satisfaction gains—but this transition was far from universal by 2020, attenuating the population-level reform effect. The marginally positive NRCMS coefficient suggests that the scheme's embeddedness in migrants' home-community social networks may generate psychosocial benefits that formal urban schemes cannot replicate, an interpretation consistent with Wang (2022), who documents the importance of neighborhood social capital for migrant mental health.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The aggregate null result on the insurance consolidation's effect on life satisfaction may mask important variation across subpopulations. If the reform benefits some migrants while leaving others unaffected or worse off, targeted policy responses rather than uniform coverage expansion would be warranted. We examine heterogeneity along three dimensions that prior literature identifies as key moderators of the migrant experience in urban China: gender, age, and income. For each dimension, we estimate split-sample regressions of life satisfaction on the full set of controls with individual and wave fixed effects, reporting coefficients on insurance enrollment, income, and demographic variables across subgroups. Table 3 summarizes key results. These split-sample regressions estimate within-individual associations between insurance enrollment type and satisfaction, conditional on the same fixed effects structure as Equation (1); they capture how the correlates of life satisfaction differ across subpopulations rather than subgroup-specific DiD interaction effects.

The gender split reveals a striking divergence in the association between UEBMI enrollment and life satisfaction. For women, enrollment in the urban employee insurance scheme is associated with a 0.082-point increase in life satisfaction ($p < 0.01$), equivalent to approximately 11.1 percent of a standard deviation (0.082/0.74)—an economically meaningful magnitude roughly double the pension-wellbeing effects documented by Yang (2024). For men, the UEBMI coefficient is positive and marginally significant but substantially smaller (0.032, $p < 0.10$), representing about 4.3 percent of a standard deviation. The near-tripling of the UEBMI association for women relative to men suggests that female migrants derive disproportionate wellbeing gains from transitioning to urban insurance coverage. This pattern is consistent with evidence that women face greater barriers to healthcare access under the hukou system (Wei Yang 2020) and that older women have higher rates of chronic conditions requiring regular outpatient care, which UEBMI covers more generously than NRCMS. The income-satisfaction gradient is also modestly steeper for women (0.014 versus 0.010), indicating that financial security is a relatively more important correlate of wellbeing for female migrants who may have fewer independent economic resources.

The age-based heterogeneity is equally revealing. We split the sample at the median age (approximately 60 years) into younger and older cohorts. The UEBMI enrollment coefficient is more than twice as large for younger migrants (0.075, $p < 0.01$) as for older migrants (0.032, $p < 0.05$). In economic terms, a within-individual transition to UEBMI enrollment is associated with a 10.1 percent of a standard deviation increase in life satisfaction for the younger cohort, compared with 4.3 percent for the older cohort. This pattern makes economic sense: younger migrants are more likely to be actively employed in formal-sector jobs that provide UEBMI access, and they interact more frequently with the urban healthcare system for both themselves and their dependents. Older migrants, many of whom have retired or shifted to informal work, may use the insurance system less intensively, muting any wellbeing effects of coverage type. Notably, the age coefficient itself is insignificant for the younger cohort (0.002, $p > 0.10$) but strongly positive for the older group (0.007, $p < 0.01$), consistent with the within-person adaptation effects discussed in Section 5 being concentrated at older ages. The gender coefficient (`gender_男性`) is large and significant for the younger cohort (0.044, $p < 0.01$) but insignificant for the older group (0.013, $p > 0.10$), suggesting that the male satisfaction advantage attenuates with age.

The income-based split produces the starkest heterogeneity. For below-median income individuals, UEBMI enrollment is associated with a positive but statistically insignificant 0.026-point change ($p > 0.10$), while for above-median income individuals the coefficient is essentially zero (−0.004, $p > 0.10$). The fact that neither income subgroup shows a significant UEBMI-satisfaction association contrasts with the significant results in the gender and age splits, suggesting that income level alone is not the primary moderator of the insurance-wellbeing relationship but rather correlates with other characteristics—such as employment formality and healthcare literacy—that drive the gender and age patterns. However, the log income coefficient itself diverges dramatically across groups: essentially zero for the below-median group (−0.003, $p > 0.10$) but 0.080 for the above-median group ($p < 0.001$). This twenty-seven-fold difference in the income-satisfaction gradient indicates that income gains translate into wellbeing improvements primarily for individuals who have already surpassed a threshold of basic economic security. A one standard deviation increase in log income (approximately 2.59 points) is associated with a 0.21-point increase in life satisfaction for above-median earners—28 percent of a standard deviation—but virtually no change for below-median earners. Below this threshold, additional income may be absorbed by competing demands—remittances to rural family members, urban housing costs, and children's education—leaving little margin for satisfaction improvement, consistent with evidence that social support and poverty interact in shaping elderly wellbeing (Liao 2023).

The rural hukou coefficient displays noteworthy variation across income groups. Among above-median earners, holding rural hukou is associated with a statistically significant 0.040-point decrease in satisfaction ($p < 0.01$), representing 5.4 percent of a standard deviation. Among below-median earners, the coefficient is essentially zero. This divergence suggests that the stigma or institutional disadvantage of rural hukou status is most salient for higher-income migrants who have achieved economic parity with urban natives but remain institutionally excluded—a pattern consistent with relative deprivation theory and the broader literature on the hukou system's psychosocial costs (Hung 2022; Yang 2022).

Taken together, the heterogeneity results suggest three implications for policy targeting. First, the consolidation reform's potential wellbeing benefits from urban employee insurance are concentrated among women and younger migrants, indicating that outreach and enrollment assistance should prioritize these groups. Second, the insignificant UEBMI coefficient for low-income migrants implies that premium subsidies or copayment reductions must accompany formal coverage transitions to ensure the poorest migrants can access care under the new system. Third, the negligible income-satisfaction association for below-median earners underscores that income transfer programs alone cannot substitute for structural reforms in healthcare access and social inclusion, a finding consistent with the evidence that income inequality constrains healthcare utilization among older adults (Chi Zhang 2024).

## 7. Robustness Checks

We subject our main findings to several alternative specifications to assess their sensitivity. First, we conduct a **placebo test** by reassigning the treatment date to 2015 and restricting the sample to pre-reform waves only (2011, 2013, 2015). If our DiD design captures genuine reform effects rather than preexisting differential trends, the placebo interaction $Treated_i \times Post^{placebo}_t$ (where $Post^{placebo}_t = 1$ for wave 2015) should be insignificant. The placebo coefficient is close to zero and statistically insignificant, supporting the interpretation that our main null finding reflects the absence of a reform effect rather than a misspecified pre-trend.

Second, we document the reform's **first stage** by tabulating the proportion of treated migrants enrolled in NRCMS versus UEBMI across CHARLS waves. Among rural-to-urban migrants, the share enrolled in NRCMS declines modestly in post-2018 waves while enrollment in the unified urban-rural resident scheme (`ea001s11`) increases, confirming that the consolidation reform altered the insurance composition of the treated group. Among urban natives, insurance enrollment patterns remain stable. This asymmetric shift validates the reform's relevance for the treated group, though the modest magnitude of the transition suggests that many migrants had not yet fully shifted to the consolidated scheme by 2020—potentially explaining the attenuated DiD estimate. The significant positive coefficient on unified scheme enrollment in Table 2 (0.111) suggests that when migrants do complete the transition, the satisfaction gains are substantial.

Third, we re-estimate Equation (1) using community fixed effects rather than individual fixed effects. This less demanding specification absorbs community-level time-invariant confounders—such as local healthcare infrastructure quality and regional policy environments—while allowing between-individual variation to contribute to identification. The DiD interaction term remains statistically insignificant, and the NRCMS enrollment coefficient retains its significant positive sign, with the point estimate increasing slightly. The consistency across specifications suggests that our findings are not driven by the particular source of identifying variation.

Fourth, we address concerns about the ordinal nature of our dependent variable by estimating an ordered probit model. Life satisfaction is measured on a five-point scale, and treating it as continuous in a linear model imposes a cardinality assumption. The ordered probit marginal effects at the mean are consistent with our linear estimates: the DiD interaction does not significantly shift the probability of reporting higher satisfaction categories, while NRCMS enrollment increases the probability of reporting "somewhat satisfied" or higher by approximately 2 percentage points.

Fifth, we conduct a **Granger-style reverse causality test** regressing future UEBMI enrollment on current life satisfaction with individual fixed effects. If higher life satisfaction causes individuals to seek formal employment conferring UEBMI rather than the reverse, our enrollment coefficients in Table 3 would reflect reverse causation. The test yields an insignificant coefficient on current satisfaction, suggesting that the UEBMI-satisfaction association is not primarily driven by satisfaction causing enrollment. Sixth, we restrict the sample to individuals observed in at least three waves to mitigate concerns about selective attrition, obtaining point estimates within 15 percent of the baseline with no change in significance levels. Seventh, we exclude the 2020 wave to address the possibility that COVID-19 pandemic disruptions confound the post-reform estimates, producing qualitatively similar results with modestly reduced statistical power.

## 8. Discussion

Our results paint a sobering picture of China's medical insurance consolidation reform. The reform's architects anticipated that merging URBMI and NRCMS would level the playing field for rural-to-urban migrants, granting them access to the same coverage urban natives enjoy. Our difference-in-differences evidence—supported by parallel pre-trends in the event-study specification, an insignificant placebo test, and first-stage documentation of insurance transitions—suggests that this formal equalization has not translated into measurable differential subjective wellbeing gains at the population level. The null DiD result, combined with the heterogeneity findings, challenges the assumption that coverage upgrading automatically improves welfare.

Several mechanisms may explain why formal insurance integration falls short. The hukou system creates barriers that extend well beyond healthcare: housing restrictions, children's educational access, and social discrimination all contribute to the migrant wellbeing gap (Hung 2022). Insurance consolidation addresses only one dimension of this multifaceted disadvantage. Moreover, the first-stage evidence shows that many migrants had not yet transitioned from NRCMS to UEBMI or the unified scheme by 2020, suggesting that administrative frictions and incomplete implementation may have attenuated the reform's potential impact. The transition itself may impose costs—higher premium contributions, unfamiliar administrative procedures, loss of designated rural providers—that offset the benefits of nominally superior coverage. The significant positive coefficient on the unified scheme (0.111) and the positive NRCMS coefficient (0.051) together suggest that scheme design matters: migrants benefit most from insurance arrangements that are either purpose-built for the unified system or embedded in their home-community social networks, rather than from simply being added to the existing urban employee scheme. This interpretation aligns with Wang (2022), who documents the importance of neighborhood social capital for migrant mental health, and with Hu (2018), who finds that unmet care needs and social isolation jointly predict depression in rural elderly populations.

The heterogeneity findings sharpen the policy implications. Women and younger migrants show significant positive associations between UEBMI enrollment and life satisfaction, suggesting that these groups face the greatest unmet healthcare needs under the pre-reform system and therefore stand to gain most from effective integration. The null effects for low-income migrants are particularly concerning, as these individuals are precisely the population the reform was designed to protect. Premium burden and information barriers may prevent the poorest migrants from fully utilizing their new coverage, echoing findings by Pu (2022) that insurance's equalizing potential depends on effective implementation.

Three structural limitations of our analysis warrant discussion. First, our identification relies on the assumption that no concurrent policy differentially affected migrant satisfaction in the post-2018 period. While the event-study pre-trends are flat and the placebo test is insignificant, province-level hukou liberalization measures implemented during the same period could partially confound our estimates. The direction of this bias is ambiguous—hukou relaxation could independently improve migrant wellbeing, implying that our null DiD estimate may understate the insurance reform's negative relative effect—and our community-level clustering accounts for some local policy variation. Future work linking CHARLS to province-level policy variation databases could disentangle these channels. Second, our post-reform window of two waves (2018 and 2020) may be insufficient to capture the reform's full impact, particularly given COVID-19 disruptions in 2020 and the incomplete first-stage transition. The insurance enrollment coefficients in Table 2 suggest that migrants who do complete the transition to the unified scheme experience substantial gains; longer follow-up using future CHARLS waves would reveal whether population-level effects materialize as more migrants transition. Third, our sample is restricted to adults aged 45 and older, reflecting CHARLS's design. The heterogeneity results demonstrate that younger respondents within this range show stronger insurance-satisfaction associations, suggesting that effects may be larger still among prime-working-age migrants outside our sample frame. Extrapolation to younger cohorts requires complementary evidence from surveys covering the full adult age distribution.

## 9. Conclusion

China's consolidation of urban resident and rural cooperative medical insurance schemes did not produce a statistically significant differential improvement in life satisfaction among rural-to-urban migrants relative to urban natives. This null finding, estimated in a difference-in-differences design with individual and wave fixed effects on over 38,000 person-wave observations from CHARLS, is supported by flat pre-reform event-study coefficients, an insignificant placebo test, and robustness to alternative specifications including community fixed effects, ordered probit estimation, and sample restrictions. The heterogeneity analysis reveals that within-individual transitions to urban employee insurance are associated with meaningful satisfaction gains for women (11 percent of a standard deviation) and younger migrants (10 percent of a standard deviation), while low-income migrants—the reform's primary intended beneficiaries—show no measurable gains.

The central policy implication is that insurance integration must be accompanied by complementary reforms to close the migrant wellbeing gap. Premium subsidies for low-income migrants, targeted enrollment assistance for women and younger cohorts, and provisions ensuring continuity of provider networks could help translate formal coverage equality into genuine wellbeing improvements. The significant positive coefficient on the unified urban-rural scheme suggests that the post-consolidation institutional design holds promise—the challenge lies in accelerating the transition and ensuring equitable access. More fundamentally, our results reinforce the growing consensus that the hukou system's effects on migrant welfare extend far beyond healthcare access, requiring systemic institutional reform rather than piecemeal insurance adjustments.

Future research should exploit the staggered provincial implementation of the consolidation reform to estimate heterogeneous treatment effects across policy environments and extend the analysis to younger migrant cohorts using complementary data sources. As China continues to urbanize and its agricultural labor force contracts, understanding whether institutional reforms can close the urban-rural wellbeing gap remains among the most consequential questions in development economics.
