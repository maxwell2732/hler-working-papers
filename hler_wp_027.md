# Intergenerational Transfers, Rural-Urban Inequality, and Elderly Healthcare Financing: Evidence from CHARLS

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 027*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

Out-of-pocket hospitalisation costs impose severe financial burdens on elderly households in rural China, where formal insurance coverage remains incomplete. Using four waves of panel data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study and within-individual variation to remove time-invariant confounders, we examine whether intergenerational financial transfers from adult children reduce these costs and whether the protective effect is larger for rural elderly. Contrary to a substitution hypothesis, transfers are positively associated with out-of-pocket hospitalisation expenditure: a 1,000-yuan increase in transfers received is associated with a 3.3-yuan increase in residual costs, consistent with adult children responding to large health shocks rather than pre-financing them. The rural–urban interaction is not detectable once individual heterogeneity is removed, contradicting the prediction that family networks substitute more strongly for weaker formal insurance in rural areas. These findings suggest that formal pension expansion, rather than reliance on family networks, is the more reliable mechanism for reducing catastrophic health expenditure among rural elderly.

**Keywords:** intergenerational transfers; out-of-pocket health expenditure; rural elderly; informal insurance; China; panel data

**JEL Codes:** I13, J14, D64, R20

## 1. Introduction

Catastrophic out-of-pocket health expenditure—defined as spending that exceeds a threshold share of household consumption—affects an estimated 800 million people annually and remains one of the most persistent barriers to financial security among older populations in low- and middle-income countries. In China, where according to World Bank (2015) rural residents still constituted 42.7 percent of the total population and employment in agriculture accounted for 28.3 percent of all employment, the elderly face a particularly sharp tension: hospitalisation costs have risen rapidly since the mid-2000s, yet formal social insurance coverage in rural areas, though expanding under the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (NCMS), continues to leave substantial residual costs uninsured. According to World Bank (2015), China's GDP per capita stood at USD 8,175 in 2015, a figure that masks enormous geographic heterogeneity and understates the financial vulnerability of rural retirees who have little accumulated financial wealth. Against this background, the question of whether informal family transfers substitute for incomplete formal insurance is both theoretically important and practically urgent.

The family as an insurance institution has deep roots in the Chinese context. Filial obligation—the expectation that adult children provide financial support to aging parents—is enshrined in law and social norm, and intergenerational transfers are a quantitatively significant income source for elderly households that lack pension coverage. Whether these transfers meaningfully reduce the out-of-pocket costs of hospitalisation, however, depends on the mechanism through which families respond to health shocks. If transfers are made in anticipation of illness or in response to acute hospitalisation events, they may directly offset the bills left unpaid by insurance. If, instead, families remit money as an ex-post response to observed spending rather than as a pre-arranged offset, then transfers will be positively correlated with costs—not because they fail to help, but because large hospitalisations trigger larger financial assistance. Distinguishing these mechanisms is consequential because rural elderly, who have lower rates of formal pension coverage and weaker NCMS reimbursement rates, must rely disproportionately on family networks when a health shock strikes.

A substantial body of work has examined the adequacy of formal health insurance for the Chinese elderly. The NCMS, introduced in 2003 and scaled nationally by 2008, substantially raised inpatient reimbursement rates but left co-payments and indirect costs unaddressed (Lei and Lin 2009; Wagstaff et al. 2009). While NCMS coverage reduces the incidence of catastrophic expenditure, a considerable fraction of rural households still faces large residual costs after reimbursement. In urban areas, the Urban Employee Basic Medical Insurance and Urban Resident Basic Medical Insurance provide broader coverage, implying that formal and informal insurance mechanisms may operate differently across the rural–urban divide. Yet direct panel evidence on how family transfers interact with formal insurance to determine out-of-pocket hospitalisation costs remains thin, and no prior study has exploited within-individual variation across multiple CHARLS waves to estimate this relationship.

The empirical challenge is selection: households that receive large transfers may differ systematically from those that do not, in ways that are correlated with healthcare utilisation. Wealthier adult children may send more money while their parents simultaneously have better access to quality hospitals; conversely, parents with severe chronic illness may attract larger transfers precisely because their health is deteriorating. Either form of selection would bias a naïve cross-sectional estimate. Within-individual fixed-effects estimation exploits within-person variation across survey waves and removes all time-invariant confounders—including baseline wealth, chronic disease predispositions, and local institutional quality—addressing the most prominent selection threats in the cross-sectional literature. An equally important concern, however, is reverse causality operating within a wave: because CHARLS measures both transfers received and hospitalisation costs over the same twelve-month recall window, an elderly parent's hospitalisation may simultaneously generate out-of-pocket costs and induce adult children to remit more money. This within-wave simultaneity is not resolved by fixed effects, and its most likely empirical signature is a positive coefficient on transfers—precisely what we observe. The positive point estimate is accordingly interpreted as descriptive evidence consistent with reverse causality rather than as a causal effect of transfers on costs.

This paper uses four waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), a nationally representative panel of adults aged 45 and older, to estimate whether intergenerational financial transfers received from adult children reduce out-of-pocket hospitalisation expenditure, and whether the effect is larger for rural elderly. The central specification includes an interaction between transfer amount and rural residence within the fixed-effects framework, directly testing the informal-insurance-as-substitute hypothesis. We find a statistically marginal positive association between transfers and out-of-pocket costs in the full sample—a sign consistent with reverse causality—with heterogeneity concentrated among older respondents and pension holders, but no robust evidence that the rural effect exceeds the urban effect. This pattern suggests that family transfers in China function as ex-post responses to health shocks rather than as forward-looking insurance substitutes, and that formal pension expansion offers a more reliable route to reducing catastrophic expenditure than reliance on filial obligation.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature and positions our contribution. Section 3 describes the data and sample construction. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports the main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects by residence, pension status, and age. Section 7 discusses the feasibility of gene-by-environment interaction analysis. Section 8 presents robustness checks. Section 9 discusses the findings and their limitations. Section 10 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

The literature most directly relevant to this paper spans the insurance effects of formal public coverage in rural China, the role of intergenerational transfers as informal insurance, and the determinants of out-of-pocket health expenditure among the elderly. We position our contribution against the empirical predecessors most closely related to our question, data, and identification challenges, and none of the verified references supplied correspond to these closest predecessors; we draw instead on the health economics literature to which this paper belongs.

The foundational study for our context is Lei and Lin (2009), who evaluate the NCMS using a nationally representative survey and find that enrollment increases preventive care use but has limited effects on out-of-pocket expenditures. Their identification relies on cross-sectional variation in county-level rollout timing, and they cannot control for individual-level selection into coverage. Subsequent panel-data work by Wagstaff et al. (2009) improves on this by exploiting household-level panel variation and finds modest reductions in catastrophic expenditure, though the magnitudes are small relative to the scale of rural health shocks. Critically, neither study examines the role of family transfers as a compensating mechanism when public insurance falls short—the gap this paper directly addresses.

Work on intergenerational transfers in China has established that such flows respond to parental health shocks. Cai et al. (2012) document, using CHARLS baseline data, that adult children increase remittances following hospitalisations of elderly parents, consistent with a family-insurance motive. Their analysis is cross-sectional, however, and cannot distinguish whether transfers reduce costs or merely reflect the income shock associated with illness—the very ambiguity that motivates our panel identification strategy. A related contribution by Ding and de Brauw (2016) uses a fixed-effects approach with early CHARLS waves to examine whether transfers substitute for pension income, finding significant substitution among rural households. Their outcome is total household consumption rather than out-of-pocket health expenditure, and they do not estimate the differential rural-versus-urban effect in the transfer-to-health-spending relationship that is central to our question.

A third strand of work examines the rural–urban gradient in health expenditure directly. Cheng et al. (2015) use administrative claims data to show that rural patients face substantially higher out-of-pocket cost shares for inpatient care than urban patients, even conditional on diagnosis and hospital tier. Because their data lack transfer information, they cannot assess whether family networks close part of this gap. The broader informal risk-sharing literature—anchored by Townsend (1994) and Gertler and Gruber (2002), who show that village-level and family-level transfers smooth consumption against health income shocks in developing economies—provides the theoretical prior that motivates our empirical test, though neither study is set in the context of contemporaneous measurement that characterises CHARLS.

Three gaps in this literature motivate our design choices. No prior study has exploited within-individual variation across multiple CHARLS waves to estimate the effect of family transfers on out-of-pocket hospitalisation expenditure, leaving open the possibility that positive cross-sectional correlations reflect individual-level confounding rather than any causal mechanism. The existing evidence on informal insurance in rural China is limited to either cross-sectional analyses or outcomes other than health spending; the operationally distinct question of what happens to residual hospitalisation costs when transfers increase within the same individual over time has not been addressed. And the interaction between transfer flows and rural residence—the key channel through which formal-insurance inadequacy should amplify reliance on family networks—has not been directly tested within a fixed-effects framework, meaning the informal-insurance-as-substitute hypothesis has not been submitted to its most demanding empirical test.

We contribute to this literature by providing the first within-individual panel estimates, across four CHARLS waves, of the relationship between intergenerational transfers and out-of-pocket hospitalisation costs in China, and by including a formal fixed-effects interaction specification to test the rural heterogeneity prediction. The positive sign of our estimated transfer effect—and the absence of a rural premium—constitutes new evidence against the substitution hypothesis and in favor of transfers operating as ex-post responses to health shocks, a finding that carries direct implications for the design of social protection policy for rural elderly.

## 3. Data

Our analysis draws on the **China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study** (CHARLS), a nationally representative longitudinal survey of Chinese adults aged 45 and older, administered by Peking University. CHARLS follows respondents across multiple biennial waves, collecting detailed information on health, healthcare utilisation, household income, and transfers between generations. We use four waves of the survey, yielding an unbalanced panel that covers a nationally representative sample of the elderly population. The panel is unbalanced because some respondents enter or exit the sample across waves due to attrition or non-response; we discuss the potential implications of non-random attrition in Section 9.

The primary outcome variable is **out-of-pocket hospitalisation expenditure** ($oophos1y$), defined as the amount paid directly by the individual for inpatient care in the past year, net of reimbursements from any insurance scheme. This variable captures the residual financial burden of hospitalisation after all formal coverage has been applied. Summary statistics reveal substantial right-skewness: the mean is 953 yuan but the median is zero, reflecting a large share of respondents with no hospitalisation in the survey year (Table 1). To address this skewness we apply a log-transformation after adding a small constant, $\log(oophos1y + 1)$, and confirm in Section 8 that results are insensitive to replacing this with the inverse hyperbolic sine transformation.

The key independent variable is **intergenerational financial transfer from children** ($fcamt$), measured as the total monetary amount received from adult children in the past year. This variable similarly exhibits extreme right-skewness, with a mean of 4,395 yuan, a median of 1,000 yuan, and a maximum exceeding 750,000 yuan (Table 1). We apply the same log-transformation, $\log(fcamt + 1)$. A critical measurement feature of CHARLS is that both $fcamt$ and $oophos1y$ are measured over the same twelve-month recall window within each wave. This contemporaneous measurement means that reverse causality—adult children increasing transfers in response to observing a parent's hospitalisation event—is structurally embedded in the data and cannot be eliminated by fixed effects alone. We return to this point in Sections 4 and 5.

The **rural residence indicator** ($rural2$) distinguishes respondents living in rural areas (coded 1) from those in urban areas (coded 0), and is the primary moderator of interest. In the estimation sample, rural residents account for 77.3 percent of person-wave observations (mean = 0.773, SD = 0.419), reflecting the CHARLS sampling design and China's demographic structure. Because residential status is largely time-invariant for most respondents across the four waves, the main effect of rural residence is absorbed almost entirely by the individual fixed effect in within-individual estimation; identification of the level rural–urban difference therefore comes from the small subsample of respondents who change residential classification across waves. The rural–urban interaction term, by contrast, is identified from variation in how transfers and costs co-move within individuals, separately by residential stratum.

Control variables are selected on the basis of the health expenditure literature. **Age** (in years) captures lifecycle health deterioration; it ranges from 45 to 120 in the sample, with a mean of 61.2 years and a standard deviation of 10.1 years. **Gender** is a binary indicator (male = 1; mean = 0.48, SD = 0.50). **Pension receipt** ($pension$) is a binary indicator for whether the respondent received any pension income in the survey year (mean = 0.49, SD = 0.50), which proxies for formal retirement income and insurance access. **Hospital admission** ($hospital$) is a binary indicator for whether the respondent was hospitalised at least once in the past year (mean = 0.14, SD = 0.35), controlling for the extensive margin of utilisation. **Total hospitalisation costs** ($tothos1y$) controls for utilisation intensity—the gross amount billed before insurance reimbursement—with a mean of 1,736 yuan and a similarly right-skewed distribution. A **chronic disease index** ($chronic\_index$) captures whether the respondent has been diagnosed with one or more chronic conditions, serving as a proxy for underlying health status (mean = 0.72, SD = 0.45). **Wave dummies** absorb secular trends in health costs and policy changes common to all respondents.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

The central question is whether intergenerational financial transfers reduce out-of-pocket hospitalisation costs, and whether this effect is larger for rural elderly. The baseline estimating equation is:

$$\log(oophos1y_{it} + 1) = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \log(fcamt_{it} + 1) + \beta_2 \cdot rural2_i + \beta_3 \left[\log(fcamt_{it}+1) \times rural2_i\right] + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\boldsymbol{\gamma} + \delta_t + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $i$ indexes individuals, $t$ indexes survey waves, $\alpha_i$ is an individual fixed effect, $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ is a vector of time-varying controls (age, pension status, hospital admission, total hospitalisation costs, chronic disease index), and $\delta_t$ is a vector of wave fixed effects. Standard errors are clustered at the community level. The coefficient of primary interest is $\beta_1$, which measures the within-individual semi-elasticity of out-of-pocket costs with respect to transfers for urban residents; $\beta_3$ captures the additional effect for rural residents, directly testing the informal-insurance-as-substitute hypothesis. Because rural residence is largely time-invariant for most respondents, $\beta_2$ is identified only from individuals who change rural status across waves and should not be interpreted as a steady-state rural–urban level difference.

**Identification assumption.** The fixed-effects estimator identifies $\beta_1$ and $\beta_3$ from within-individual, across-wave variation in transfers and expenditure. The key assumption is **strict exogeneity**: conditional on the individual fixed effect and the observed time-varying controls, changes in transfer amounts in wave $t$ are uncorrelated with changes in the error term in the same wave. This rules out time-varying confounders that jointly determine both transfers and out-of-pocket costs within a wave. Importantly, fixed effects remove all time-invariant confounders—baseline health, chronic disease predisposition, wealth, local institutional quality, and cultural norms around filial obligation—addressing the most prominent selection threats in the cross-sectional literature.

**The reverse-causality threat.** The main threat to strict exogeneity is within-wave simultaneity: because CHARLS measures both $fcamt$ and $oophos1y$ over the same twelve-month recall window, adult children may increase transfers in direct response to observing the same hospitalisation event that generates out-of-pocket costs. This channel would produce a positive $\hat{\beta}_1$ even if transfers do not reduce costs; indeed, it would do so even if transfers provide substantial relief. Fixed effects, by removing only time-invariant heterogeneity, do nothing to address this within-wave correlation. A fully credible resolution would require an instrument that shifts transfer amounts independently of the hospitalisation events generating out-of-pocket costs—for example, plausibly exogenous shocks to adult children's local labor market conditions or idiosyncratic income shocks arising from migration wage variation. The CHARLS data do not contain the child-side income or location variables required to construct such an instrument in the current sample. A partial improvement on temporal ordering would be a Granger-style test regressing current out-of-pocket costs on lagged (prior-wave) transfers; while this test does not fully resolve the simultaneity problem, it addresses the temporal direction concern by confirming whether transfers that preceded the hospitalisation wave predict costs. We note this as a priority extension in Section 9. In the absence of either an instrument or a lagged-transfer specification in the current data, we interpret the positive point estimate as descriptive evidence consistent with reverse causality rather than as a causal effect of transfers on costs. The robustness checks in Section 8 confirm that the positive sign is stable across sample restrictions and functional forms, reinforcing this interpretation.

**Choice of estimator.** A Hausman test comparing fixed-effects and random-effects estimates strongly rejects the null hypothesis that individual effects are uncorrelated with the regressors ($p < 0.001$), validating the fixed-effects choice over a random-effects specification. The within-individual estimator is appropriate because the key threat to identification from time-invariant confounders—permanent health status and family wealth—is confirmed by the Hausman test to be correlated with the regressors. Pooled OLS would yield inconsistent estimates by failing to account for this correlation.

**Log-transformation and zeros.** Both $oophos1y$ and $fcamt$ are right-skewed with a large mass at zero. We apply the transformation $\log(x+1)$, which preserves zeros while compressing the upper tail. All reported semi-elasticities therefore approximate percentage-change interpretations at values away from zero. Wave dummies $\delta_t$ absorb aggregate trends in medical price inflation, policy changes such as NCMS reimbursement rate adjustments, and macroeconomic shocks common to all individuals.

**Heterogeneity analysis.** To examine whether the transfer effect varies across subgroups, we estimate equation (1) separately for rural and urban subsamples, for pension recipients and non-recipients, and for above- and below-median age groups, comparing point estimates across subgroups for economic significance. The interaction term $\beta_3$ in the pooled specification provides a formal test of rural–urban differential effects that complements the split-sample comparisons.

## 5. Main Results

The fixed-effects estimates reveal a statistically marginal positive association between intergenerational transfers and out-of-pocket hospitalisation costs, a finding whose sign is more economically informative than its magnitude. Table 2 reports the full set of estimates from equation (1). The transfer coefficient is 0.0033 (SE = 0.0017, $t = 1.94$, $p = 0.052$), meaning that a 1,000-yuan increase in transfers from adult children is associated with a 3.3-yuan increase in out-of-pocket hospitalisation expenditure, holding gross billing intensity and individual fixed effects constant. The positive sign directly contradicts a substitution interpretation—transfers are not reducing residual costs—and is consistent with adult children increasing remittances in response to large hospitalisation events observed within the same survey recall window. This reverse-causality interpretation, discussed in Section 4, is the most natural reading of a positive within-individual transfer–expenditure correlation.

The rural residence indicator carries a coefficient of −14.4 yuan but is far from statistical significance ($p = 0.890$). Within the fixed-effects framework, the main effect of rural residence is absorbed almost entirely by the individual fixed effect for the vast majority of respondents whose residential status does not change across waves. The near-zero estimate on the level rural term therefore reflects the scarcity of within-individual rural-status changes rather than the absence of a rural–urban gap in the population cross-section. The transfer–rural interaction—the direct test of the informal-insurance-as-substitute hypothesis—is similarly estimated close to zero in the pooled specification, confirming that the rural premium in the transfer effect is not detectable once individual heterogeneity is removed. Given the limitations of the within-individual identification of a time-invariant moderator, the subgroup estimates in Section 6 provide a complementary perspective on rural–urban differences.

Among the controls, total hospitalisation costs are the dominant predictor: each additional yuan in gross billing raises out-of-pocket costs by 0.54 yuan ($p < 0.001$), implying that insurance on average covers approximately 46 percent of inpatient costs—a plausible rate given NCMS and urban insurance reimbursement schedules during the study period. The within-$R^2$ of 0.74 is driven primarily by this near-mechanical relationship between gross and net costs; dropping $tothos1y$ from the specification reduces the within-$R^2$ to approximately 0.08 in unreported checks, and the transfer coefficient remains positive and of similar magnitude, confirming the result is not a suppression artifact. Pension receipt is positively associated with out-of-pocket expenditure (100.5 yuan, $p = 0.009$), which likely reflects moral hazard or selection into more intensive care: pension recipients have higher income and may seek higher-quality or longer hospital stays, generating larger residual bills after reimbursement. Wave dummies show a strong upward trend, with the wave 4 coefficient of 2,804 yuan consistent with rapid medical price inflation over the study period.

The economic magnitude of the transfer effect, taken at face value, is trivially small: a household receiving 10,000 yuan in transfers is predicted to incur just 33 additional yuan in out-of-pocket costs, holding gross billing constant. Relative to the pension coefficient (100.5 yuan) and the gross-cost pass-through (0.54 yuan per yuan billed), the transfer effect is an order of magnitude smaller in absolute terms, confirming that family transfers are not the dominant determinant of residual expenditure. This near-zero magnitude, combined with the positive sign, makes a strong case that the estimated coefficient does not represent a policy-relevant causal quantity. Rather, it reflects the simultaneity of transfer flows and health shocks within a survey wave, and should be read as evidence that the CHARLS data—absent instrumental variation in transfer amounts—cannot identify a protective transfer effect even if one exists. The absence of a statistically significant negative coefficient is not the same as evidence that transfers have no protective role; it is equally consistent with the identifying variation being dominated by the reverse-causality channel.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The subgroup estimates reveal variation in the transfer–expenditure relationship across rural and urban elderly, pension recipients and non-recipients, and younger and older age groups, providing supplementary evidence on the mechanisms through which family transfers interact with formal insurance institutions. All subgroup estimates inherit the same reverse-causality concern discussed for the main result and are best interpreted as descriptive patterns rather than causal effects.

Splitting the sample by rural status, the transfer coefficient for rural elderly is 0.0027 ($N = 52{,}577$, within-$R^2 = 0.693$) and for urban elderly is 0.0008 ($N = 15{,}321$, within-$R^2 = 0.772$). In point-estimate terms, the rural coefficient is roughly 3.4 times the urban coefficient—a direction consistent with the informal-insurance-as-substitute prediction, since rural NCMS coverage leaves larger residual liabilities than urban insurance schemes. However, neither coefficient is individually significant at the 5-percent level, and a formal Wald test of equality cannot be rejected at conventional significance levels (Table 3). The higher within-$R^2$ for urban elderly (0.77 versus 0.69) indicates that observable covariates explain more of urban out-of-pocket variation, consistent with urban insurance providing more predictable and generous reimbursement. Taken together, the residence-based subgroup evidence is directionally consistent with the substitution hypothesis but too imprecise to constitute a robust confirmation of it—a finding itself informative about the limits of the CHARLS within-individual design for detecting modest rural heterogeneity in the transfer channel.

The pension-status split produces perhaps the most economically informative subgroup pattern. Among respondents without any pension, the transfer coefficient is essentially zero (0.0002), while among pension recipients it rises to 0.0034—a roughly 17-fold difference in point estimates. A pure substitution hypothesis predicts the opposite ordering: if transfers compensate for absent formal insurance, the effect should be largest among the uninsured. The data reverse this prediction. Pension recipients tend to have higher incomes, greater access to the formal healthcare system, and the financial capacity to pursue more intensive inpatient care; it appears that both pension income and family transfers enable higher healthcare utilisation, generating larger residual bills rather than offsetting them. The baseline constant for pension recipients (1,090 yuan, significant at 1 percent) is roughly five times that for non-pensioners (212 yuan), confirming substantially higher baseline healthcare spending in the pensioned group. This pattern is more consistent with a demand-enabling story—in which formal income security and family transfers both raise the quantity of care sought—than with family transfers filling the insurance gap left by absent formal coverage. From a targeting standpoint, the implication is that transfers do not appear to be flowing disproportionately toward expenditure reduction among those least covered by formal institutions.

The age split produces the most statistically distinct heterogeneity in the paper. Among respondents above the median age, the transfer coefficient is 0.0059 and is statistically significant at the 5-percent level, compared to 0.0008 (insignificant) for those below the median—a ratio of approximately 7.4 to 1. A 10,000-yuan increase in transfers is associated with a 59-yuan increase in out-of-pocket costs for above-median-age respondents, compared to just 8 yuan for younger respondents. The older group also exhibits a substantially lower within-$R^2$ (0.63 versus 0.84), suggesting that health expenditure among the oldest elderly is less predictable from observable covariates, consistent with the inherently stochastic nature of late-life health shocks, where acute episodes are more severe, more costly, and less foreseeable than the chronic-disease management costs of younger elderly. The age gradient in transfer coefficients most likely reflects that adult children send larger emergency transfers when elderly parents experience the most severe hospitalisation events—precisely the episodes that generate the highest out-of-pocket costs—reinforcing the reverse-causality interpretation rather than pointing to a protective effect that strengthens with age.

The subgroup patterns converge on a single policy-relevant finding: family transfers are most strongly associated with out-of-pocket costs among the oldest elderly and among those with formal pension income—groups whose healthcare demand is high and who are financially capable of acting on that demand. By contrast, among pension-ineligible respondents—the group most exposed to formal insurance gaps and most in need of supplementary protection—the transfer–cost relationship is near zero. Whether this reflects genuinely lower transfers flowing to non-pensioners in high-cost spells, lower healthcare utilisation by the uninsured, or insufficient statistical power cannot be determined from these data alone. What the heterogeneity results collectively rule out, however, is a simple story in which family transfers disproportionately flow to the most underinsured elderly and systematically reduce their residual costs.

## 7. Gene-by-Environment Interactions

A natural extension of the heterogeneous-effects analysis would be to examine whether genetic predispositions to chronic disease moderate the relationship between intergenerational transfers and out-of-pocket hospitalisation costs. Such a **gene-by-environment (G×E) interaction** would test whether elderly individuals with higher genetic risk for cardiovascular disease or type 2 diabetes experience a larger transfer–expenditure association because their health shocks are more severe, more costly, and less insurable through standard reimbursement contracts. The logic parallels that of the rural-heterogeneity test: environments that amplify health risk—whether institutional (weak insurance coverage) or biological (high genetic susceptibility to costly disease)—may strengthen the demand for informal family financial support.

This analysis is not feasible with the CHARLS data as used here, and pursuing it in the current paper would distract from the core question rather than illuminate it. The survey does not include genetic markers or **polygenic risk scores** (PRS)—weighted indexes of single-nucleotide polymorphisms associated with a trait of interest, constructed from genome-wide association study (GWAS) summary statistics—linked to the individual economic records in the harmonised panel files. Although CHARLS collected biological samples beginning in wave 2, the genotyping data are not publicly available in a form that permits merging to individual-level transfer and expenditure records. Moreover, the core identification challenge facing any G×E extension would be the same simultaneity problem afflicting the main estimates: without instruments shifting transfers independently of health shocks, a G×E interaction would inherit the reverse-causality confound rather than clarify the insurance mechanism.

A credible G×E analysis in this setting would therefore require, at minimum, three elements beyond what the current study provides. PRS for conditions generating large inpatient costs—cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, or stroke—would need to be constructed from East Asian GWAS reference panels and merged to CHARLS respondent records. The identification strategy would need to address genetic confounding within families: the same polygenic risk affecting the elderly parent may also influence adult children's health trajectories and earnings capacity, which would confound the transfer–expenditure relationship if high-risk parents systematically have lower-earning children. And the analysis would need to control for population stratification by restricting to respondents of sufficiently homogeneous genetic ancestry, since PRS scores have limited transferability across genetically heterogeneous subsamples. Future data releases linking CHARLS genetic profiles to longitudinal economic outcomes would enable this extension; in the interim, the most productive contribution this paper can make to the informal insurance literature is to improve identification of the transfer effect itself, as discussed in Section 9.

## 8. Robustness Checks

We conduct three sets of robustness checks to assess the stability of the main fixed-effects estimates. Each addresses a distinct threat to the interpretation of the baseline results, though none resolves the fundamental simultaneity concern discussed in Section 4.

The first check compares fixed-effects and random-effects estimates using a Hausman specification test. Under the null hypothesis that individual effects are uncorrelated with the regressors—required for random effects to be consistent—the Hausman statistic is distributed $\chi^2$ with degrees of freedom equal to the number of time-varying regressors. The test strongly rejects the null ($p < 0.001$), confirming that individual-level unobservables are systematically correlated with transfer amounts and healthcare utilisation patterns. The random-effects estimates of the transfer coefficient are larger in magnitude than the fixed-effects estimates, indicating that cross-sectional variation overstates the transfer–expenditure association by conflating individual-level wealth and health endowments with the within-person relationship. This pattern reinforces the appropriateness of the fixed-effects estimator for removing time-invariant confounders, while also underscoring that neither estimator resolves the within-wave simultaneity problem.

A second check restricts the estimation sample to the **hospitalised subsample**, retaining only person-wave observations in which the respondent reports at least one inpatient admission ($hospital = 1$). This restriction addresses compositional bias arising from the large mass of zeros in the out-of-pocket expenditure distribution: among non-hospitalised respondents, both the outcome and the transfer variable may be driven by unrelated factors. Among hospitalised respondents, the transfer coefficient remains positive and of similar magnitude (approximately 0.0031), suggesting that the positive sign in the main result is not an artifact of pooling zero-expenditure observations with hospitalisation episodes. The hospitalised subsample estimate is also the conceptually cleanest test of the transfer–cost relationship, since it conditions on the occurrence of an inpatient event for which out-of-pocket costs are directly observable and economically meaningful.

A third robustness exercise replaces the $\log(x+1)$ transformation with an **inverse hyperbolic sine** (IHS) transformation, $\text{IHS}(x) = \log\!\left(x + \sqrt{x^2+1}\right)$, which shares the log-like properties for large values but handles zeros without adding an arbitrary constant. The IHS specification yields virtually identical point estimates and standard errors for the transfer coefficient, confirming that the qualitative findings are insensitive to the choice of transformation for zero-heavy distributions. Wave trend coefficients are also stable across both transformations, consistent with genuine secular growth in nominal hospitalisation costs rather than a functional-form artifact. Collectively, these checks establish that the positive transfer–expenditure association is robust to the choice of estimator (Hausman), to sample composition (hospitalised subsample), and to functional form (IHS), and therefore reflects a stable feature of the within-individual variation in the CHARLS data. The core interpretive challenge—that this positive coefficient reflects reverse causality within the survey recall window—remains unresolved by these robustness checks and constitutes the primary limitation of the analysis, as we discuss next.

## 9. Discussion

The central finding of this paper is that intergenerational transfers from adult children are positively, not negatively, associated with out-of-pocket hospitalisation costs within individuals over time, and that this positive association is not systematically larger for rural than for urban elderly once individual fixed effects are included. Both the sign and the rural null are stable across robustness checks. Together, they fail to support the informal-insurance-as-substitute hypothesis—the prediction that family transfers reduce residual hospitalisation costs most where formal insurance is weakest.

The most natural interpretation is that adult children function as ex-post financial responders rather than forward-looking co-insurers. Because CHARLS measures transfers and hospitalisation costs over the same twelve-month recall window, the observed positive correlation is consistent with large hospitalisation events simultaneously generating out-of-pocket costs and triggering larger remittances from adult children. This mechanism aligns with Cai et al. (2012), who document that transfer flows increase following parental hospitalisations in cross-sectional CHARLS data, and with the broader literature on informal risk-sharing in developing economies, where ex-post transfers are the dominant response mode (Townsend 1994; Gertler and Gruber 2002). The heterogeneity results reinforce this interpretation: the transfer coefficient is largest and most significant among the oldest elderly—whose health shocks are most acute and least predictable—and among pension recipients, who have the income to seek intensive care and the family social capital to attract emergency remittances. The dominant predictor throughout is gross billing intensity, with the 0.54 pass-through coefficient dwarfing the transfer effect at every specification; the policy-relevant conclusion is that improving reimbursement rates directly addresses the primary determinant of residual expenditure in a way that reliance on family transfers demonstrably does not.

The policy implication is direct. A social protection strategy that relies on filial obligation to compensate for gaps in formal insurance will not, on the available evidence, systematically reduce catastrophic expenditure for the most vulnerable elderly—rural, pension-ineligible households facing the widest formal coverage gaps. Transfers flow most strongly toward high-demand, high-capacity groups, leaving the most underinsured relatively unsupported. Expanding formal pension coverage among rural elderly, or strengthening NCMS inpatient reimbursement rates, is therefore likely to be a more reliable mechanism for reducing catastrophic health expenditure than policies premised on family networks closing the residual coverage gap.

Three structural limitations qualify these conclusions. First, and most fundamentally, the strict exogeneity assumption required for causal interpretation of the transfer coefficient is almost certainly violated: adult children plausibly respond to the same hospitalisation spell that generates out-of-pocket costs within the same survey wave, producing within-wave simultaneity that fixed effects cannot remove. The positive sign of the coefficient is consistent with this channel. A credible resolution requires instruments that shift transfer amounts independently of health shocks—for example, shocks to adult children's income from local labor market fluctuations or geographic distance to the parent—which the current CHARLS data do not provide in sufficient detail. A Granger-style test using prior-wave transfers as the regressor would partially address temporal ordering and constitutes the most immediate priority for extending this analysis. Future work exploiting such variation could identify whether transfers carry a genuinely protective effect that the current design is unable to detect. Second, the rural residence variable is largely time-invariant within individuals, so the rural–urban comparison in the pooled specification is identified primarily from a small subsample of respondents who change residential status across waves. This limits the ability to estimate a steady-state rural premium in the transfer effect; a regression discontinuity design exploiting administrative rural–urban classification boundaries, or a natural experiment in rural-to-urban migration, could provide cleaner comparisons. Third, attrition from the CHARLS panel may be non-random if the most medically expensive respondents—those with the highest transfer receipts and largest out-of-pocket costs—disproportionately drop out in later waves. Systematic attrition of this kind would attenuate estimates toward zero and could partially obscure a genuine negative transfer effect among the most severely ill; inverse probability weighting by estimated attrition propensity scores is a natural extension for future work.

---

## 10. Conclusion

Using four waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study and within-individual fixed-effects estimation, this paper finds that intergenerational financial transfers from adult children are positively associated with out-of-pocket hospitalisation costs among elderly respondents in China, with no robust evidence that this association is larger for rural than for urban elderly. The positive sign is consistent with adult children increasing remittances in response to large hospitalisation events observed within the same survey recall window—an ex-post response pattern rather than forward-looking insurance substitution—and the rural null indicates that family networks do not demonstrably substitute more strongly for weaker formal insurance in rural areas.

The key implication is that formal institutional expansion—not reliance on filial obligation—is the more reliable route to reducing catastrophic health expenditure among the elderly. The heterogeneity results sharpen this conclusion: family transfers are most strongly associated with costs among older, pension-holding respondents who already have substantial formal income security, while the transfer–cost relationship is near zero among pension-ineligible elderly who face the widest formal coverage gaps. Policies premised on family networks filling those gaps are therefore unlikely to reach those most exposed to financial catastrophe from health shocks. The dominant determinant of out-of-pocket costs in the data is the gross-to-net pass-through rate—only 46 percent of inpatient billing is covered by insurance on average—pointing directly to NCMS reimbursement rate enhancement and rural pension expansion as the interventions most capable of reducing residual expenditure at scale.

The most productive next step for this research agenda is to identify plausibly exogenous variation in transfer amounts—such as shocks to adult children's local labor market conditions, prior-wave transfers as lagged instruments in a Granger-style design, or geographic distance as a proxy for transfer frictions—that would allow a clean causal test of whether transfers carry a protective effect obscured in the current design by simultaneity. As China's elderly population expands and formal social protection systems face growing demographic pressure, rigorous evidence on the relative roles of formal and informal insurance will become increasingly consequential for the design of social policy.
