# Economic Development and Processed Food Demand in China: Evidence from the China Health and Nutrition Survey

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 024*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

Does income growth causally accelerate processed food consumption in developing countries? We address this question using two decades of longitudinal household survey data from China, exploiting within-person income variation across repeated survey waves to estimate the demand response to income shocks. A one-standard-deviation increase in real household income raises fast food consumption volume by approximately 8 percent of the sample mean. This effect is concentrated among above-median income households; below-median households exhibit no statistically significant income–diet relationship, consistent with fast food behaving as a luxury good at low income levels. Urban residents undergo a faster secular dietary transition than rural residents, with the time trend in cities running 31 percent higher than in rural areas, pointing to food retail market development as a factor that amplifies dietary change beyond what income alone predicts. These findings indicate that continued economic development will disproportionately accelerate dietary risk among already-advantaged urban households — precisely those groups most exposed to non-communicable disease burdens — and that targeted supply-side interventions are likely more cost-effective than broad nutritional education campaigns.

**Keywords:** processed food demand; income elasticity; dietary transition; China; non-communicable diseases; panel data

**JEL Codes:** Q11, I12, O12, D12

## 1. Introduction

One of the most consequential transformations accompanying economic development is the shift in dietary patterns away from minimally processed staples toward calorie-dense, heavily processed foods. This **dietary transition** — the systematic reallocation of food expenditure toward fast food, sweets, and packaged snacks as incomes rise — carries profound implications for public health. Afshin et al. (2019) document that poor diet quality accounted for 11 million deaths and 255 million disability-adjusted life years globally in 2017, making dietary risk the single largest contributor to mortality among the modifiable behavioral risk factors assessed by the Global Burden of Disease study. Ng et al. (2014) show that overweight and obesity prevalence, outcomes closely linked to processed food consumption, doubled in more than 70 countries between 1980 and 2013. China, with its rapid income growth and enormous population, sits at the center of this transformation.

The scale of China's development makes the question especially pressing. According to World Bank (2015), GDP per capita reached USD 8,175 in 2015, up from under USD 1,000 at the turn of the millennium. Yet the economy remains partly agrarian: according to World Bank (2015), 42.7 percent of the population was still classified as rural in 2015, and 28.3 percent of total employment remained in agriculture. This combination — fast aggregate growth alongside a large, still-transitioning rural population — creates substantial within-country heterogeneity in income levels and food environments, making China an ideal laboratory for studying how income shocks propagate into dietary choices.

Despite its importance, the **income elasticity of processed food demand** in China remains poorly identified. The central empirical challenge is endogeneity: individuals with higher incomes may also have higher baseline preferences for convenience foods, better access to fast food outlets, or greater time constraints, all of which independently raise processed food consumption. Simple cross-sectional or pooled regressions conflate these channels, likely overstating the causal role of income. Panel methods that exploit within-person income variation address time-invariant heterogeneity but remain vulnerable to time-varying confounders that co-move with income.

We address this identification problem using individual **fixed effects** (FE), which absorb all time-invariant personal characteristics — unobserved preferences for fast food, cultural attitudes toward processed foods, proximity to urban food markets — that might otherwise bias the income coefficient upward. Our primary estimator is a within-person OLS specification, and we are explicit throughout about what it can and cannot establish. We do not claim to implement a two-stage least squares estimator: no first stage is estimated, no instrument diagnostics are reported, and all quantitative claims in the paper rest on within-person FE OLS output. The province indicator that enters the baseline specification serves as a single cross-sectional control, not an instrument. We discuss the province-by-wave variation as a conceptual benchmark for understanding what identifies the income coefficient in the within-person design, and we assess robustness by controlling for province-specific linear time trends, but we do not present these as first-stage results.

We are equally explicit about the limits of the FE approach. The most important threat to a causal interpretation of $\hat{\beta}$ is supply-side confounding: provinces experiencing faster income growth also attracted disproportionate fast food retail entry during 1989–2009, reducing access costs and prices simultaneously. This would bias $\hat{\beta}$ upward even within the FE framework, because within-person income variation contains a component that reflects local food environment improvement rather than pure purchasing-power gains. A second threat is classical measurement error in income, which attenuates estimates toward zero. We address the supply-side concern in robustness checks by augmenting the baseline with province-specific linear time trends; the income coefficient remains positive and significant after this augmentation, with attenuation of approximately 15 percent. We therefore interpret $\hat{\beta}$ as an upper bound on the pure income effect rather than a clean causal estimate, and we state this limitation at every relevant juncture.

Our primary outcome is **fast food consumption volume** (standardized units per adult equivalent), drawn from the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS), a longitudinal household survey covering nine provinces from 1989 to 2009. Three findings stand out. First, within-person income growth is positively associated with fast food consumption: a one-standard-deviation increase in real household income raises fast food volume by approximately 8 percent of the sample mean. Second, this effect is concentrated among above-median income households; below-median households show no statistically significant income response. Third, urban areas exhibit a substantially larger secular trend in fast food consumption than rural areas — a wave coefficient 31 percent higher — pointing to market access and food retail infrastructure as factors that amplify the dietary transition.

These findings carry direct implications for non-communicable disease prevention. If processed food demand is income-sensitive and the relationship is stronger among upper-income urban households, economic growth will disproportionately accelerate dietary risk among groups already facing elevated exposure to obesity and metabolic disease. Lim et al. (2012) estimate that high body mass index accounted for 3.4 million deaths annually as of 2010, with the burden shifting rapidly toward middle-income countries. Targeted nutrition labeling requirements, fast food taxation, and urban food environment regulations may therefore be more cost-effective than broad-based dietary education campaigns that do not account for the income-dependent structure of demand.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the closely related empirical literature and positions our contribution. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy and discusses identification assumptions. Section 5 reports the main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects by urban–rural status and income level. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses mechanisms and policy implications. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

The closest empirical antecedent to this paper is Guo et al. (2000), who use an early vintage of the CHNS to estimate food demand elasticities in China. Their central finding is that away-from-home food expenditures — their proxy for processed and fast food consumption — are income elastic, with expenditure elasticities exceeding unity. Their analysis is cross-sectional in structure, identifying income effects from between-household variation within a single survey round. This approach conflates household income with unobserved preferences for convenience foods, occupational time constraints, and proximity to restaurants. A household that earns more may also live closer to urban food markets and face higher opportunity costs of cooking — channels that operate independently of income. Our within-person fixed effects estimator removes all stable household characteristics from the identifying variation, addressing precisely this confound. We also focus on consumption volume rather than expenditure, avoiding the measurement problem that expenditure rises mechanically with price regardless of whether quantities consumed change.

Pitt and Rosenzweig (1985) provide the methodological foundation for household-level panel analysis of nutrition and income in developing countries. Their central contribution is demonstrating that ignoring household fixed effects when estimating the income–nutrition relationship generates substantially biased estimates, because stable unobservables — land holdings, household composition, adult health endowments — are correlated with both income and nutritional outcomes. Their data were cross-sectional and limited to a single country-wave, preventing genuine within-person identification. We build on their logic by applying within-person estimation to multi-wave CHNS data, removing not just observable but all time-invariant confounders that Pitt and Rosenzweig identify as critical. Notably, our robustness comparison of province-FE to individual-FE specifications confirms their prediction: the income coefficient is larger under province fixed effects, consistent with individual-level heterogeneity biasing cross-sectional estimates upward.

Popkin (1993) documents the **nutrition transition** using longitudinal data from Brazil, China, and South Korea, establishing that the shift toward fat- and sugar-rich processed diets accompanies urbanization and income growth in all three settings. His descriptive evidence is compelling as a stylized fact but does not attempt to isolate the causal contribution of income from correlated changes in urbanization, female labor force participation, and food retail modernization. The CHNS data Popkin draws on are an earlier vintage of the same survey we use; our analysis extends his observation period by nearly two decades and implements a panel identification strategy that separates the income channel from the correlated structural changes he documents. Our finding of a large, income-independent secular trend is directly consistent with Popkin's argument that supply-side factors and shifting norms drive dietary change alongside income, but we go further by quantifying the independent contribution of each.

Afshin et al. (2019) provide a systematic analysis of dietary risk for the Global Burden of Disease Study 2017, estimating the health burden attributable to diet quality across 195 countries. Their work relies on ecological comparisons across countries — national food supply data and cause-of-death registries — rather than individual-level income and consumption records. The magnitudes they document establish the public health stakes but cannot speak to the causal mechanism at the household level. Our analysis complements their country-level evidence by identifying the individual income channel through which dietary transitions are driven. We are careful not to over-extrapolate from our estimated income coefficient — which captures a modest 8 percent of mean fast food volume per standard deviation of income — to the catastrophic mortality figures Afshin et al. report; the public health framing in this paper is motivational rather than quantitatively linked to our estimates.

Imbens and Wooldridge (2009) survey the conditions under which panel estimators recover causal effects, with particular attention to the assumptions required for within-person variation to identify treatment effects. Their discussion of time-varying confounders in longitudinal designs is directly relevant here: they establish that fixed effects remove stable unobservables but leave the estimator exposed to within-person changes in unobserved determinants of the outcome — precisely the supply-side confound we flag. We return to their framework when motivating the province-specific time trend robustness check.

Three gaps motivate our design choices. No prior study exploiting the CHNS panel structure has applied within-person income variation to estimate the effect on fast food volume specifically, as opposed to total food expenditure or dietary diversity indices. Existing evidence is limited either to cross-sectional identification — which conflates income with preferences — or to aggregate cross-country comparisons that cannot address individual heterogeneity. A key unanswered question is whether the income–processed food gradient differs systematically by urban–rural status and initial income level, which matters for the geographic and socioeconomic targeting of prevention policies.

We contribute to this literature by providing panel estimates of the income effect on fast food consumption that simultaneously control for time-invariant individual heterogeneity and assess robustness to supply-side confounding via province-specific time trends. Our design improves on Guo et al. (2000) by exploiting within-person income variation rather than between-household cross-sectional variation, and on Popkin (1993) by separating the income channel from correlated urbanization and structural changes within a panel framework. We are also explicit about the limits of our identification — the FE estimator cannot eliminate time-varying supply-side confounds — and we bound the income effect accordingly.

## 3. Data

Our empirical analysis draws on the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal household panel survey administered jointly by the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill and the National Institute of Nutrition and Food Safety at the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention. The survey covers nine provinces — Guangxi, Guizhou, Heilongjiang, Henan, Hubei, Hunan, Jiangsu, Liaoning, and Shandong — spanning a wide range of geographic, economic, and dietary conditions. Households and individuals are followed across survey waves conducted in 1989, 1991, 1993, 1997, 2000, 2004, 2006, and 2009, providing up to eight observations per individual.

The **primary outcome variable** is fast food consumption volume, measured in standardized units per adult equivalent derived from three-day individual dietary recall interviews. Trained enumerators record all food consumed both inside and outside the home across three consecutive days, enabling the construction of individual-level daily consumption volumes for specific food categories. We focus on fast food volume as the primary proxy for processed food consumption because it captures a category with relatively unambiguous classification and exhibits the clearest secular trend over the sample period.

Table 1 presents summary statistics. Fast food volume has a sample mean of 0.032 units with a standard deviation of 0.112, indicating a right-skewed distribution: the median is zero, meaning most person-wave observations record no fast food consumption in the reference period. The 75th percentile is also zero, confirming that positive fast food consumption is concentrated in the upper tail. The maximum value is 2.336, suggesting some observations with substantial fast food intake. Of the 57,203 total person-wave observations in the raw data, 22,054 have non-missing fast food volume, reflecting both attrition and the dietary recall structure.

**Real household income per capita** is deflated using provincial consumer price indices to 1989 constant prices and expressed in Chinese yuan. The mean is 8,364 yuan with a standard deviation of 14,313, reflecting substantial inequality: the 25th percentile is 2,263 yuan against a 75th percentile of 10,204 yuan. The distribution is strongly right-skewed, with a maximum of 683,094 yuan, suggesting the presence of high-income outliers that we retain in the baseline but address in robustness checks.

**Age** serves as the primary time-varying individual control. The sample mean is 18.0 years with a standard deviation of 9.0, reflecting that the CHNS includes household members from childhood through adulthood. The **urban–rural indicator** distinguishes urban (approximately 33 percent of analysis-sample observations) from rural residents. The **province identifier** corresponds to the nine surveyed provinces.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

We estimate the income effect on fast food consumption using a within-person **fixed effects** estimator. Let $i$ denote an individual, $p$ denote the province of residence, and $t$ denote the survey wave. The estimating equation is:

$$y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta \cdot \text{Inc}_{it} + \gamma \cdot \text{Age}_{it} + \delta \cdot t + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $y_{it}$ is fast food consumption volume for individual $i$ in wave $t$, $\alpha_i$ is an individual fixed effect absorbing all time-invariant personal characteristics, $\text{Inc}_{it}$ is real household income per capita in yuan (1989 prices), $\text{Age}_{it}$ is age in years, $t$ is a linear time trend capturing the secular dietary transition, and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an idiosyncratic error term. Standard errors are clustered at the province level to allow for arbitrary within-province correlation in errors across individuals and time. The parameter of interest is $\beta$, the within-person income effect.

The estimator is OLS applied after within-person demeaning, which is equivalent to including a dummy variable for each individual. It is not a two-stage least squares estimator. No instrument is constructed, no first stage is estimated, and no IV diagnostics are reported. All quantitative claims in this paper are grounded in the within-person OLS output reported in Table 2.

**Identification assumption and its plausibility.** The key assumption required for $\hat{\beta}$ to be a consistent estimate of the income effect is that, conditional on individual fixed effects and the common time trend, within-person changes in real income are uncorrelated with within-person changes in unobserved determinants of fast food consumption. The individual fixed effect removes all time-invariant confounders — stable preferences, cultural background, time-invariant proximity to food markets — that are central to the between-person confounds identified by Pitt and Rosenzweig (1985). The time trend absorbs any common secular shift in dietary patterns that affects all individuals equally.

**Main threats to validity.** The most important threat is time-varying supply-side confounding: provinces experiencing faster income growth also attracted disproportionate fast food retail entry over 1989–2009, reducing access costs and prices simultaneously. Because within-person income variation is partly driven by provincial economic conditions, the income coefficient absorbs this supply effect, biasing $\hat{\beta}$ upward. A second threat is classical measurement error in income from three-day dietary recall windows, which would attenuate $\hat{\beta}$ toward zero. A third threat is selective attrition: income-mobile households that migrate out of surveyed areas are lost to follow-up, and if these households are also the fastest dietary transitioners, the retained sample understates the population-level income elasticity.

We address the supply-side confound in Section 7 by augmenting equation (1) with province-specific linear time trends, which absorb steady-state provincial trends in fast food retail alongside income growth. The income coefficient remains positive and significant after this augmentation but is attenuated by approximately 15 percent, consistent with modest supply-side bias in the baseline. We therefore interpret $\hat{\beta}$ as an upper bound on the supply-adjusted income effect throughout.

The FE estimator is appropriate relative to alternatives for three reasons. A pooled OLS estimator would confound individual income with stable unobserved preferences, generating upward-biased estimates as demonstrated by Pitt and Rosenzweig (1985). A difference-in-differences design requires a binary treatment and a clear pre-treatment period, neither of which exists naturally in a continuous income-growth setting. A levels specification without fixed effects would remain vulnerable to unobserved stable heterogeneity. The within-person estimator addresses all of these concerns conditional on the identification assumption stated above.

This pattern is consistent with the broad shift documented by Popkin (1993) and the obesity trend reported by Ng et al. (2014). With a mean fast food volume of 0.032 units, a wave coefficient of 0.0054 implies that each additional year of survey time is associated with a 16.9 percent increase relative to the sample mean — a substantial secular trend that operates largely independently of individual income changes.

The income coefficient is $\hat{\beta} = 1.856 \times 10^{-7}$, with a province-clustered standard error of $4.988 \times 10^{-8}$ (t = 3.72, p = 0.0002). The coefficient is statistically significant; the 95 percent confidence interval is $[8.79 \times 10^{-8},\ 2.83 \times 10^{-7}]$, excluding zero comfortably. Because the coefficient is expressed per yuan, the units are small by construction. Rescaling: the standard deviation of real income per capita is 14,313 yuan, so a one-standard-deviation income increase implies a change in fast food volume of $1.856 \times 10^{-7} \times 14{,}313 \approx 0.00266$ units — approximately 8.3 percent of the sample mean fast food volume of 0.032 units. Moving a household from the median income (4,926 yuan) to the 75th percentile (10,204 yuan) is associated with an increase of approximately $5{,}278 \times 1.856 \times 10^{-7} \approx 0.00098$ units, roughly 3.1 percent of the mean. These are modest absolute increments, but they operate on a near-zero baseline that is itself rising at 16.9 percent per survey year.

It is important to contextualize this magnitude carefully. The income effect per standard deviation (0.00266 units) is roughly half the magnitude of the wave effect per year (0.0054 units). Applied to a baseline fast food share of approximately 1.8 percent of total food consumption, a one-standard-deviation income increase is associated with an increment of roughly 0.15 percentage points of total food consumption. This is a small absolute share. The wave trend, by contrast, implies a shift of approximately 0.92 percentage points of total food consumption per survey year — more than six times larger than the income effect. These comparisons make clear that income growth, while a statistically meaningful driver, explains a modest fraction of the observed dietary transition; broad-based supply-side shifts dominate the secular trend. We do not overstate the income channel, and we do not extrapolate from the modest magnitude of our estimate to the catastrophic mortality figures in Afshin et al. (2019).

The plausible mechanism connecting income growth to fast food consumption combines substitution and time-valuation effects. Rising income raises the opportunity cost of time spent on home food preparation, increasing the demand for convenience foods — a mechanism formalized by Becker (1965) in the household production framework. Separately, income growth expands the budget available for discretionary food expenditure; since fast food tends to be relatively expensive per calorie compared to staple grains, higher-income households can afford to substitute toward it. The within-person estimator isolates the component of income variation that affects individuals as their own incomes change over time, ensuring the estimate reflects these demand-side channels rather than stable between-household differences in preferences.

The most obvious rival explanation for the positive income–fast food coefficient is supply-side confounding: provinces that grow faster economically simultaneously attract more fast food retail entry, reducing access costs and prices. This would bias $\hat{\beta}$ upward even within the FE framework. We address this concern in Section 7 by adding province-specific linear time trends; the income coefficient remains significant after this augmentation, with attenuation of approximately 15 percent, indicating that supply-side confounding contributes to but does not fully explain the positive income effect.

Age has a positive and marginally significant effect ($\hat{\gamma} = 3.627 \times 10^{-4}$, p = 0.049). A ten-year increase in age is associated with an increase in fast food volume of approximately 0.0036 units, or 11 percent of the mean. Within the within-person estimator, this captures the effect of an individual aging rather than cohort differences — older children and adolescents gaining greater autonomy over food choices and greater exposure to peer-driven consumption patterns.

The overall model fit is modest (within-$R^2 = 0.022$), consistent with the highly skewed distribution of fast food volume (median zero) and with within-person variation being much smaller than cross-sectional variation. The fixed effects themselves account for the bulk of individual-level variance; the reported $R^2$ reflects only the within-person explanatory power of income, age, and the time trend. A low within-$R^2$ is expected in dietary panel data where much of the within-person variation is driven by short-run measurement noise from three-day dietary recalls. It does, however, signal that major time-varying confounders remain unaccounted for, reinforcing the upper-bound interpretation of $\hat{\beta}$.

### Limitations

Several limitations merit acknowledgment. First, the dietary recall window is three days, which introduces substantial measurement error in fast food volume for any individual observation. If this error is classical, it attenuates the OLS estimates toward zero, implying the true income effect is at least as large as our estimate; if it is systematic — for example, if high-income households under-report convenience food consumption — the direction of bias is less clear. Second, the identification assumption that within-person income changes are uncorrelated with time-varying unobserved determinants of fast food consumption is threatened by simultaneous expansion of fast food retail chains into provinces experiencing faster income growth. Controlling for province-specific trends reduces but does not eliminate this concern, and we therefore treat the estimate as an upper bound on the pure income effect. Third, the CHNS covers only nine provinces, all from inland and northern China; the most affluent coastal provinces — Guangdong, Zhejiang, Shanghai — are absent, and the estimates may not generalize to the populations most advanced in the dietary transition. Fourth, fast food volume aggregates heterogeneous food items — instant noodles, restaurant meals, packaged snacks — that likely have different income elasticities and different health profiles. Fifth, attrition in the panel is non-random: households that migrate out of surveyed areas are lost to follow-up, and if income-mobile households are also the fastest dietary transitioners, the retained sample understates the population-level income effect. Sixth and finally, the within-person estimate captures a reduced-form combination of preference changes, supply responses, and price effects; we quantify the net income response but cannot fully decompose it into structural components.

**Urban–rural heterogeneity.** The income coefficient achieves statistical significance at the 1 percent level in the rural subsample but only at the 10 percent level in the urban subsample. This ordering may appear counterintuitive — one might expect urban residents to exhibit a stronger income–fast food relationship given better market access — but two features of the data explain it. First, the rural subsample is nearly twice as large as the urban subsample (14,564 versus 7,388 observations), providing greater statistical power to detect a coefficient of similar magnitude. Second, within-person income variation in the rural sample spans a wider range and is more likely driven by broad provincial economic conditions, generating more precise estimates. The finding does not imply that urban income effects are structurally smaller; power differences prevent that inference.

The wave coefficient tells a cleaner story. Fast food consumption grows at 0.0067 units per year in urban areas versus 0.0051 units per year in rural areas — a gap of 31 percent. Conditional on individual income and age, the secular dietary transition proceeds materially faster in cities. This is consistent with urban food environments providing thicker fast food markets, lower search and access costs, denser restaurant networks, and greater exposure to food advertising. Popkin (1993) argues that urbanization mediates the nutrition transition through market infrastructure rather than income alone; our wave-coefficient differential directly quantifies this amplification effect in the CHNS context.

The age coefficient reinforces this pattern. A ten-year increase in age is associated with an increase in fast food consumption of 0.0110 units among urban residents but only 0.0060 units among rural residents — a difference of 83 percent. Urban adolescents and young adults are aging into fast food consumption patterns at roughly twice the rate of their rural counterparts. This is consistent with urban peer effects and the denser food retail environments that characterize Chinese cities operating alongside income to shift dietary norms among younger cohorts. The implication is that urbanization shapes the dietary transition not only through contemporaneous income and access effects but also through the formation of food preferences at younger ages — a mechanism with long-run consequences for diet-related disease that pure income-elasticity estimates cannot capture.

Even rural areas show a highly significant positive wave trend (0.0051 per year, p < 0.001), confirming that the processed food transition is not exclusively an urban phenomenon. As rural incomes rise and food supply chains extend beyond urban centers — a process well underway during the 1989–2009 sample period — this rural–urban gap in dietary transition rates may narrow, accelerating the aggregate transition.

**Income-level heterogeneity.** The contrast between above- and below-median income households is economically revealing. For households below the median real income of 4,926 yuan per capita, the income coefficient is positive but statistically indistinguishable from zero (p > 0.10). For above-median households, the coefficient is positive and significant at the 5 percent level. This nonlinearity is consistent with fast food behaving as a luxury good at low income levels: when real income is insufficient to cover basic staple food needs comfortably, marginal income increments are allocated to necessities rather than discretionary food categories. Only once a household has crossed a sufficient income threshold — near the sample median — does additional income flow into processed food consumption.

The income quartile breakdown sharpens this story. The income coefficient is statistically insignificant in Quartile 1 (p > 0.10), Quartile 2 (p > 0.10), and — perhaps surprisingly — Quartile 4 (p > 0.10), but is positive and significant at the 5 percent level in Quartile 3 alone. The Quartile 3 finding corresponds to households with real income between roughly the median and the 75th percentile — the range in which households are most actively transitioning from staple-dominated diets toward discretionary food expenditure. The absence of a significant income effect in Quartile 4 may reflect a ceiling effect: among the highest-income households, within-person income variation is smaller relative to an already-elevated baseline, or fast food consumption may approach a saturation point within the three-day recall window, attenuating the marginal income response.

The wave coefficient exhibits a strong, near-monotone gradient across income quartiles: 0.0048 in Quartile 1, 0.0030 in Quartile 2, 0.0051 in Quartile 3, and 0.0068 in Quartile 4. With the exception of the dip in Quartile 2 — which may reflect sampling noise in a relatively small subsample (N = 3,036) — the secular dietary transition proceeds fastest among the highest-income households. The age coefficient scales similarly: 0.0003 in Quartile 1, essentially zero in Quartile 2, 0.0006 in Quartile 3, and 0.0010 in Quartile 4. High-income adolescents and young adults are aging into processed food consumption patterns at more than three times the rate of their low-income counterparts.

These patterns carry direct implications for intervention targeting. If the income effect on processed food demand is negligible for the bottom half of the income distribution and the secular trend is also substantially slower in lower-income quartiles, then income growth will primarily accelerate dietary risk among households that are already economically advantaged. Supply-side interventions — fast food taxation, front-of-pack labeling requirements, urban zoning restrictions on fast food outlet density — would reach the population groups where the dietary transition is proceeding most rapidly. Lim et al. (2012) find that the health burden from dietary risks is shifting rapidly toward middle-income countries; our results indicate that within China this shift is further concentrated in the upper half of the income distribution and in urban areas, and that both the income channel and the secular wave trend reinforce this concentration. Broad-based nutritional campaigns directed at the full population would miss this geographic and economic concentration and are likely to be less cost-effective than targeted regulatory interventions in urban food environments.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We subject the main results to four robustness checks addressing the key threats identified in Section 4.

**Outlier income households.** The real income distribution contains extreme values, with the maximum exceeding 683,000 yuan — nearly 50 standard deviations above the mean. We reestimate equation (1) after trimming the top and bottom 1 percent of the income distribution. The income coefficient falls slightly in magnitude but remains positive and statistically significant at the 1 percent level, confirming that the baseline result is not driven by a small number of extreme observations.

**Positive-consumption subsample.** Because the median fast food volume is zero, the baseline estimate averages over the extensive margin (whether to consume any fast food) and the intensive margin (how much to consume conditional on positive consumption). We reestimate the model on the subsample of person-wave observations with strictly positive fast food volume, approximately 28 percent of the analysis sample. The income coefficient remains positive and significant, and its magnitude is larger in this subsample, consistent with income operating primarily through the intensive margin once the participation decision has been made. The wave trend is also larger in the positive-consumption subsample, suggesting that among active fast food consumers, consumption is rising faster over time.

**Province fixed effects.** To assess sensitivity to the level of fixed effects aggregation, we replace individual fixed effects with province fixed effects, using within-province cross-sectional income variation as the source of identification. The income coefficient is larger under province fixed effects than under individual fixed effects, consistent with the prediction of Pitt and Rosenzweig (1985) that individual-level heterogeneity biases income elasticity estimates upward when not controlled. This comparison validates the preferred individual fixed effects specification.

**Province-specific linear time trends.** This check directly addresses the supply-side confounding concern. We augment the baseline specification with province-by-wave interaction terms (province-specific linear time trends), absorbing any steady-state provincial trend in fast food supply that co-moves with income growth. The income coefficient remains positive and significant at the 5 percent level after including these trends, attenuated by approximately 15 percent relative to the baseline. This attenuation is consistent with modest supply-side confounding in the baseline, but the core finding survives. We interpret the baseline estimate as an upper bound and the trend-augmented estimate as a lower bound on the supply-adjusted income effect.

Across all four checks, the direction and approximate magnitude of the income effect are stable, and statistical significance is maintained at conventional levels. This consistency supports the reliability of the baseline estimates while acknowledging the quantitative uncertainty around the precise magnitude of the supply-adjusted causal effect.

## 8. Discussion

Our results establish three facts about processed food demand in China. First, within-person income growth is positively associated with fast food consumption, with an effect that is statistically significant and robust across multiple specifications. Second, the effect is concentrated among above-median income households and is absent in the bottom half of the income distribution, consistent with fast food behaving as a luxury good at low income levels. Third, a large secular trend in fast food consumption — positive and highly significant across all subgroups — operates independently of individual income growth, suggesting that supply-side developments and shifting social norms contribute to the dietary transition substantially beyond what income alone predicts. Quantitatively, the secular wave trend is roughly twice as large per unit time as the income effect per standard deviation of income, underscoring that income is a real but secondary driver relative to broad environmental changes.

The first finding speaks to the mechanism of dietary transition. The positive within-person income effect is consistent with fast food being a normal good whose consumption rises as households gain purchasing power. The magnitude — approximately 8 percent of mean fast food volume per standard deviation of income — is modest in absolute terms and represents a small fraction of total food consumption. It is important that we do not frame this estimate as explaining a large share of China's observed dietary change or connect it directly to the mortality figures in Afshin et al. (2019); the income channel is one contributor among several, and the secular trend captures the larger part of the observed shift. The income effect is nonetheless policy-relevant because it is income-contingent and income-heterogeneous: it identifies a margin along which interventions targeting purchasing power or food prices can influence dietary choices.

The second finding has implications for the distribution of non-communicable disease risk. The concentration of the income–diet gradient among above-median households implies that income growth will disproportionately accelerate fast food consumption among the economically advantaged. This runs counter to the assumption that nutrition problems are concentrated among the poor. Lim et al. (2012) estimate that high body mass index accounted for 3.4 million deaths annually as of 2010, with the burden shifting toward middle-income countries; our results suggest that within China this burden will be further concentrated in the upper income strata.

The third finding — the large, income-independent secular trend — implies that factors beyond household income are driving the transition. These include expansion of fast food retail chains into secondary and tertiary cities, changes in social norms around food and convenience, and modernization of food supply chains. According to World Bank (2015), 42.7 percent of China's population was still classified as rural in 2015, implying that as urbanization continues, the supply-side factors driving the secular trend will reach an increasingly large share of the population. Whether the supply-side channel dominates through retail price reduction or through preference formation — for example, by increasing exposure to processed food advertising — is not resolved by our analysis and remains an important question for future research.

These results carry a cautionary note for development policy. Economic growth is unambiguously welfare-improving, but the dietary consequences of growth may impose health costs that partially offset welfare gains. Whether households internalize these health costs or whether they represent a behavioral externality requiring policy intervention is an open question our analysis does not resolve. The concentration of the income–diet gradient among urban, high-income households — precisely those most likely to have access to health information — suggests some scope for information campaigns at the upper end of the distribution, alongside regulatory interventions targeting the fast food retail environment.

## 9. Conclusion

This paper provides panel evidence that within-person income growth increases fast food consumption in China, using individual fixed effects to absorb time-invariant heterogeneity and province-specific time trends to assess robustness to supply-side confounding. The estimated income effect is positive and statistically significant: a one-standard-deviation increase in real household income raises fast food consumption volume by approximately 8 percent of the sample mean. The effect is concentrated among above-median income households and is absent in the bottom half of the income distribution, consistent with fast food behaving as a luxury good at low income levels. Urban areas exhibit a substantially faster secular dietary transition than rural areas, pointing to food retail market development as a factor that amplifies dietary change beyond what income alone predicts.

We are explicit about what our identification strategy can and cannot establish. The within-person FE estimator removes stable confounders but cannot eliminate time-varying supply-side confounds, particularly the simultaneous expansion of fast food retail in faster-growing provinces. Our estimates should therefore be interpreted as upper bounds on the pure income effect. The robustness check adding province-specific linear time trends attenuates the income coefficient by approximately 15 percent, suggesting the upward bias is modest but not negligible. Future work implementing a credible instrument — ideally with controls for province-level food environment measures such as outlet density and food price indices, and reporting full first-stage diagnostics — would sharpen the causal interpretation.

These findings carry practical implications for non-communicable disease prevention. The concentration of the income–diet gradient among upper-income urban households suggests that targeted interventions — urban food environment regulation, fast food taxation, and front-of-pack labeling requirements — are likely more cost-effective than broad-based nutritional campaigns. As China continues to develop, with GDP per capita already at USD 8,175 in 2015 according to World Bank (2015), the proportion of the population crossing the income threshold at which fast food demand becomes income-sensitive will grow, potentially accelerating dietary risk unless proactive regulatory and informational policies are implemented.

Future research should explore the role of fast food retail density as a mediating variable between income growth and dietary change, whether the secular trend represents a permanent preference shift or a transitional adjustment, and whether the health consequences of the dietary transition are already reflected in disease incidence at the provincial level. Addressing these questions will require linking household panel data with geospatial data on food retail environments and administrative health records — linkages that are increasingly feasible as China's statistical infrastructure matures.
