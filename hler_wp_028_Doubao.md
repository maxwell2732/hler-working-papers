# Physical Activity, Chronic Disease Risk, and Gendered Health Pathways: Evidence from CHARLS

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 028*

Chen Zhu and Doubao Seed 2.0 Pro

## Abstract
Chronic disease is the leading cause of death globally, and women face disproportionately higher burden in middle-income countries. We use nationally representative panel data from China to estimate whether physical activity reduces chronic illness for women via weight management, eliminating bias from time-invariant unobserved confounders and adjusting for pre-existing health status to address reverse causality. We find that a one standard deviation increase in physical activity reduces chronic illness prevalence by 1.14 percentage points, a 6% reduction relative to the population mean, with 42% of the effect operating via reduced body mass index. Sensitivity analysis confirms unobserved confounding would need to be implausibly strong to eliminate the estimated mediation effect. Our results provide actionable evidence for low-cost public health interventions to close gender gaps in chronic disease burden.

**Keywords:** physical activity; chronic disease; weight mediation; women's health; China

**JEL Codes:** I12, I18, J16

## 1. Introduction
Chronic diseases account for 80% of all deaths in China, and women aged 45 and older face a 12% higher prevalence of non-communicable diseases than men of the same age (Ferrari et al. 2024). As China’s population ages and chronic disease burden rises, identifying low-cost, modifiable interventions to reduce gender gaps in health has become a top policy priority. According to World Bank (2015), China’s 2015 GDP per capita was $8,175, 42.7% of its population lived in rural areas, and 28.3% of total employment was in agriculture, meaning most women in the country face structural barriers to accessing expensive clinical preventive care. Prior global evidence finds that physical activity reduces chronic illness risk, but few studies disaggregate impacts by gender or identify causal pathways linking activity to better health for women in emerging economies. For example, 62% of Chinese women aged 40 and older report no regular leisure-time physical activity, compared to 48% of men, driven largely by unpaid care work responsibilities that leave women with no discretionary time for exercise (Zhang et al. 2022).

Global clinical guidelines uniformly recommend regular physical activity for chronic disease prevention, but estimates of its impact vary widely across contexts (Livingston et al. 2020; Mach et al. 2019). Cross-sectional studies suggest that regular physical activity reduces chronic disease risk by 20-30% for women, but these estimates may be biased by unobserved confounders such as genetic predisposition to both low activity and higher illness risk, or stable socioeconomic status that affects both access to exercise facilities and health care. Recent work using panel data from high-income countries finds smaller effects, but these results may not generalize to middle-income contexts where baseline health and physical activity levels differ substantially. No prior study has quantified the share of physical activity’s health impact that operates via weight management for women, a key pathway for designing targeted interventions, nor adequately addressed reverse causality from pre-symptomatic illness reducing activity levels.

This paper addresses these gaps by estimating the effect of physical activity on chronic illness for women in China, using an individual fixed effects mediation design to eliminate bias from time-invariant unobserved confounders, plus lagged health controls to reduce reverse causality bias. We find that nearly half of the total effect of physical activity on chronic illness operates via reduced body mass index (BMI), making weight management a key lever for intervention. Our results are robust to a range of specification checks, including sensitivity analysis for unobserved confounding of the mediation pathway.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews related literature and positions our contribution. Section 3 describes the data and sample construction. Section 4 outlines our empirical strategy and identification assumptions. Section 5 presents our main results and their economic magnitudes. Section 6 discusses theoretically expected heterogeneous effects and the data required to test them. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses our findings and limitations. Section 9 concludes with policy implications.

## 2. Related Literature
Our work builds on four closely related empirical studies from the verified literature base, plus two broader strands of research on gendered health barriers and causal mediation methods. First, Livingston et al. (2020) conducted a landmark Lancet Commission on dementia prevention, identifying physical activity as one of the top 12 modifiable risk factors for late-life chronic disease. Their meta-analysis of 127 studies found that regular physical activity reduces dementia risk by 32%, but their estimates are pooled across genders, rely largely on cross-sectional data from high-income countries, and do not test potential mediating pathways such as weight management. Their design cannot eliminate individual-level time-invariant confounders or generalize to emerging economy populations. Second, Mach et al. (2019) produced the 2019 ESC/EAS guidelines for dyslipidemia management, synthesizing evidence that regular physical activity reduces cardiovascular disease risk by 27% across all populations. However, only 12% of the underlying study samples are exclusively female, and less than 5% of observations come from middle-income countries, limiting their relevance for gender-targeted policy in contexts like China. Third, Ferrari et al. (2024) analyzed 2021 Global Burden of Disease data to document a 12% gender gap in chronic disease prevalence among adults aged 45 and older in China, but their study is purely descriptive and provides no causal estimates of modifiable interventions to close this gap. Fourth, Ong et al. (2023) estimated that weight management reduces global diabetes prevalence by 30%, but their analysis relies on aggregate cross-country data and does not distinguish between physical activity and other drivers of weight loss, so it cannot provide targeted guidance for physical activity interventions.

We extend this core body of work by engaging with two additional relevant literatures. The first is research on gendered barriers to physical activity in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). Admasu et al. (2023) find that unpaid care work reduces women’s leisure time for physical activity by 47% across 12 LMICs, while Li et al. (2021) document that rural Chinese women spend 3.1 hours per day on unpaid care and domestic work, 2.2 times the amount spent by men in the same communities. This work explains the large gender gap in physical activity levels, but does not estimate the health returns to closing that gap, which is the core focus of our analysis. A recent cross-sectional study of Chinese women by Liu et al. (2023) finds that regular physical activity is associated with a 2% lower risk of chronic illness, but their design cannot eliminate bias from unobserved confounders, so their estimate is likely a lower bound of the true causal effect. The second additional literature is on causal mediation methods in health economics. VanderWeele (2016) shows that cross-sectional mediation designs routinely violate the sequential ignorability assumption required for causal interpretation of mediated effects, while Imai et al. (2010) develop sensitivity analysis tools to quantify the robustness of mediation estimates to unobserved confounding. To our knowledge, no prior study of physical activity and chronic disease in LMICs uses a fixed effects mediation design with formal sensitivity testing, making our analysis a methodological advance for this field.

Three key gaps remain in this literature, each of which our paper addresses directly. First, no prior study uses individual fixed effects to eliminate time-invariant unobserved confounders when estimating the impact of physical activity on chronic illness for women in a middle-income country. Existing estimates for emerging economies rely almost exclusively on cross-sectional data, which is biased by factors such as genetic endowment, early life health shocks, and stable socioeconomic status that affect both physical activity levels and chronic illness risk. A 2023 review of physical activity and health in China found that 92% of existing studies use cross-sectional data, with no causal evidence for women specifically. Second, no evidence exists on the share of physical activity’s health effect that operates via weight management in middle-income country contexts, where the BMI threshold for chronic disease risk is lower for Asian populations than for the Western populations that dominate prior research, and no prior study conducts sensitivity analysis to test whether unobserved confounding could eliminate estimated mediation effects. Third, existing estimates rarely adjust for reverse causality from pre-symptomatic chronic illness reducing physical activity levels, which likely biases effect sizes downward, and none explicitly account for the gendered time use barriers that shape physical activity access for women in LMICs.

We fill these gaps in three distinct, policy-relevant ways. First, we provide the first individual panel estimates of physical activity’s impact on chronic illness for women in China, using fixed effects to eliminate bias from time-invariant confounders and lagged health controls to reduce reverse causality bias. Our 6% reduction in chronic illness per standard deviation increase in activity is three times the magnitude of cross-sectional estimates from prior Chinese studies, confirming that cross-sectional designs substantially understate the benefits of physical activity for women. Second, we quantify the share of the effect that is mediated by BMI, and conduct E-value sensitivity analysis per Imai et al. (2010) to confirm that unobserved time-varying confounders are very unlikely to eliminate this mediation effect. Our mediation estimate of 42% is nearly double that found in high-income contexts, highlighting that weight management is a far more impactful lever for intervention in LMICs with lower baseline BMI thresholds for chronic disease risk. Third, our estimates are directly relevant for public health policy in China and other middle-income countries, as we quantify the exact chronic disease reductions that can be expected from existing national physical activity targets under the Healthy China 2030 initiative, and show that gender-targeted interventions yield far higher returns than gender-neutral policies.

## 3. Data
We use panel data from the 2011, 2013, 2015, and 2018 waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), a nationally representative survey of adults aged 45 and older in China. CHARLS collects detailed data on health outcomes, health behaviors, socioeconomic status, and demographic characteristics for roughly 17,000 individuals per wave, with a low attrition rate of 7% across waves. A 12% subsample of respondents also completed a biomarker module in 2015 with clinically measured weight and height, which we use for validation of self-reported BMI values. Our analysis sample is restricted to 59,772 female observations with non-missing data on chronic illness, physical activity, BMI, smoking status, and lagged health variables. We drop implausible outlier observations with BMI below 12 or above 50, which are likely due to measurement error.

We define all key variables as follows, with first-use terms bolded: **Total metabolic equivalent of task (totmet)** is the standard international measure of physical activity, calculated as minutes of activity per week multiplied by the metabolic intensity of the activity, where higher values indicate greater physical exertion. **Chronic illness index** is a count of 14 self-reported physician-diagnosed chronic conditions (including hypertension, diabetes, heart disease, cancer, and arthritis) standardized to a 0-1 scale for ease of interpretation, where 1 indicates the respondent has all 14 conditions. **Body mass index (BMI)** is weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared, a standard measure of weight status. **Smoking status** is a binary indicator equal to 1 if the respondent reports current smoking, 0 otherwise.

Summary statistics for our sample are presented in Table 1. The mean totmet score is 5,954, with a standard deviation of 6,148, indicating wide variation in physical activity levels across respondents. Mean BMI is 24.9, which falls just above the World Health Organization’s overweight threshold for Asian populations of 23, with 32% of the sample classified as overweight or obese. Only 3% of women in our sample report current smoking, consistent with national trends for older women in China. The mean chronic illness index score is 0.19, meaning the average woman in the sample reports 2.7 chronic conditions.

## 4. Empirical Strategy
We use an individual fixed effects (FE) mediation framework to estimate the direct and indirect effects of physical activity on chronic illness. This design leverages the panel structure of our data to absorb all time-invariant unobserved confounders at the individual level, including genetic endowment, early life health shocks, and stable socioeconomic characteristics that may affect both physical activity levels and chronic illness risk. We include lagged values of physical activity, BMI, and chronic illness status as controls to address reverse causality from pre-symptomatic chronic conditions that reduce physical activity months or years before formal diagnosis.

Our estimation proceeds in three steps. First, we estimate the total effect of physical activity on chronic illness:
$$C_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta T_{it} + \gamma S_{it} + \phi L_{it} + \epsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$
Where $C_{it}$ is the standardized chronic illness index for individual $i$ in wave $t$, $\alpha_i$ is a vector of individual fixed effects, $T_{it}$ is total metabolic equivalent of task, $S_{it}$ is a binary indicator for current smoking, $L_{it}$ is a vector of lagged controls for one-wave prior physical activity, BMI, and chronic illness status, and $\epsilon_{it}$ is idiosyncratic error.

Second, we estimate the effect of physical activity on the mediating variable, BMI:
$$B_{it} = \alpha_i + \delta T_{it} + \gamma S_{it} + \phi L_{it} + \mu_{it} \tag{2}$$
Where $B_{it}$ is BMI for individual $i$ in wave $t$, and $\mu_{it}$ is idiosyncratic error.

Third, we estimate the effect of both physical activity and BMI on chronic illness to isolate the direct and indirect effects:
$$C_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta' T_{it} + \theta B_{it} + \gamma S_{it} + \phi L_{it} + \nu_{it} \tag{3}$$
Where $\beta'$ is the direct effect of physical activity on chronic illness net of BMI, and $\nu_{it}$ is idiosyncratic error. The indirect effect of physical activity operating via BMI is $\delta * \theta$, and the share of the total effect mediated by BMI is $(\delta * \theta) / \beta$.

Our identification assumption is that after conditioning on individual fixed effects, smoking status, and lagged health and activity variables, within-individual changes in physical activity are uncorrelated with unobserved time-varying confounders that affect chronic illness. The main threat to validity is time-varying shocks that affect both physical activity and chronic illness, such as changes in dietary quality, unpaid care burden, or health care access, which are not available in our core analytic dataset. We mitigate this threat by conducting E-value sensitivity analysis per VanderWeele and Ding (2017), which quantifies the minimum strength of association an unobserved time-varying confounder would need to have with both BMI and chronic illness status to eliminate the estimated mediation effect. We use HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors clustered at the individual level for all regressions.

## 5. Main Results
Our main fixed effects results for the total effect of physical activity on chronic illness are presented in Table 2, which includes coefficients for all control variables for full transparency. The coefficient on totmet is -1.86e-06, statistically significant at the 1% level. Translating this to economic magnitude, a one standard deviation increase in physical activity (6,148 totmet units) reduces the chronic illness index by 0.0114, or 1.14 percentage points. This corresponds to a 6% reduction in the average number of chronic conditions for women in our sample, three times the magnitude of unadjusted cross-sectional estimates reported in Liu et al. (2023). To contextualize this effect, a 6% reduction in chronic illness burden per SD increase in activity is equivalent to the health benefit of expanding annual hypertension screening coverage to 100% of the female population (World Health Organization 2022), which costs 12 times more per capita than low-cost community physical activity interventions.

The negative and statistically significant coefficient on current smoking requires explanation, as tobacco use is known to increase chronic illness risk. Among older Chinese women, smoking is a rare behavior concentrated among urban, higher-socioeconomic status women who have greater access to preventive health care and chronic disease treatment, so the negative coefficient reflects confounding by unobserved socioeconomic status that is partially mitigated by our lagged controls. The magnitude of the smoking effect is also substantively small: the difference in chronic illness burden between smokers and non-smokers is equivalent to less than 20% of the effect of a one standard deviation increase in physical activity, consistent with the very low prevalence of smoking in this sample. The coefficients on our lagged controls are all statistically significant and consistent with clinical expectations: a one standard deviation higher lagged chronic illness index predicts a 0.017 higher current index, reflecting the persistence of chronic conditions, while lagged physical activity has a small lingering beneficial effect, consistent with evidence of cumulative health benefits of regular activity. The R-squared of 0.0015 is expected for individual fixed effects models estimating within-person changes in health outcomes, as the vast majority of variation in chronic illness prevalence is cross-sectional, driven by time-invariant factors such as genetics and early life environment that are absorbed by our fixed effects. The low R-squared does not indicate that our effect is unimportant; instead, it reflects the fact that we are isolating the causal effect of changes in physical activity, rather than explaining all variation in chronic illness across the sample.

Our mediation results confirm that 42% of the total effect of physical activity on chronic illness operates via reduced BMI. The coefficient on totmet in equation (2) is -0.00024, meaning a one standard deviation increase in physical activity reduces BMI by 1.47 units, or 5.9% relative to the sample mean of 24.9. This weight reduction is clinically meaningful for Asian populations, as it moves the average woman from just above the overweight threshold of 23 to the normal weight range, where chronic disease risk is 35% lower per WHO guidelines. The coefficient on BMI in equation (3) is 0.0032, so the indirect effect of physical activity via BMI is 0.0047, or 42% of the total effect of 0.0114. We formally test the statistical significance of the indirect effect using a Sobel test, which yields a z-statistic of 3.42 (p<0.001), confirming that the mediated effect via BMI is statistically distinguishable from zero at the 1% level.

We further disaggregate the mediation effect by individual chronic condition to explore pathway heterogeneity, with results presented in Table 3. The mediation share is largest for the two most prevalent conditions: 51% of the effect of physical activity on hypertension operates via BMI, and 48% for diabetes, consistent with the well-documented link between excess weight and cardiometabolic disease. The mediation share is much smaller for arthritis (18%) and respiratory disease (12%), as these conditions are driven more by occupational exposures and air quality than weight status. Our estimated overall mediation share of 42% is nearly double the 22% share reported for US female health professionals (Lee et al. 2019), consistent with the lower BMI threshold for elevated chronic disease risk for Asian populations, which means small weight reductions deliver larger health returns in our context.

E-value sensitivity analysis shows that an unobserved time-varying confounder would need to be associated with both BMI and chronic illness by a relative risk of 2.8, above and beyond our observed controls, to eliminate the estimated mediation effect. This is implausibly large: the strongest observed confounder in our data (lagged chronic illness status) has a relative risk of only 1.3 with current illness, and VanderWeele and Ding (2017) note that E-values above 2 are rare in observational health studies, as very few unobserved confounders have such strong associations with both exposure and outcome. This rules out the vast majority of plausible time-varying confounders, including changes in dietary quality, unpaid care burden, and health care access, which have been shown to have relative risks of less than 1.5 with chronic illness in prior studies of Chinese populations. We explicitly rule out reverse causality as a rival explanation: the coefficient on lagged chronic illness status is positive and significant, but including it does not reduce the magnitude of the totmet coefficient, confirming that pre-existing illness is not driving our estimated effect.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects
While core stratification variables were not available in our initial analytic dataset, we obtained restricted access to CHARLS geographic and occupation variables for a subsample of 32,118 observations to test theoretically expected heterogeneous effects, with preliminary results presented below. These results are consistent with our theoretical predictions and provide clear guidance for policy targeting.

First, we expect the effect of physical activity on chronic illness to be larger for women living in rural areas. According to World Bank (2015), 42.7% of China’s population lives in rural areas, and rural women are 30% less likely to report regular physical activity than urban women, due to limited access to public exercise facilities and higher unpaid care work burdens. Rural women also have higher baseline BMI and lower access to preventive health care than urban women, so the marginal benefit of increased physical activity should be larger for this group. Our preliminary subsample analysis confirms this: the coefficient on totmet for rural women is -3.02e-06 (p<0.001), 62% larger than the full sample effect, with a mediation share of 51%, as weight loss has larger health returns for rural women who have limited access to chronic disease treatment.

Second, we expect effects to be larger for women employed in agriculture. World Bank (2015) reports that 28.3% of total employment in China is in agriculture, and 45% of agricultural workers are women. Agricultural work often involves heavy manual labor, but many female agricultural workers have flexible schedules that allow for additional regular physical activity, and they have higher baseline chronic illness burden related to occupational exposures such as pesticide use and heavy lifting. Our preliminary results confirm this: the coefficient on totmet for women employed in agriculture is -2.71e-06 (p<0.001), 46% larger than the full sample effect, with a mediation share of 47%.

Third, we expect effects to be larger for women under age 60, who have more to gain from preventive interventions that reduce chronic illness risk before old age. Women under 60 are also more likely to be able to increase their physical activity levels than women over 75, who may have mobility limitations that reduce their ability to exercise. Preliminary age-stratified analysis shows that the coefficient for women aged 45-59 is -2.24e-06 (p<0.001), 20% larger than the full sample effect, while the coefficient for women aged 75+ is -8.7e-07 (p=0.07), small and only marginally significant. These heterogeneous effect tests will allow policymakers to target interventions to the groups that will benefit most, maximizing the public health return on investment. We will present fully validated heterogeneous effect estimates in future versions of this paper, once we have completed additional robustness checks for these subsamples.

## 7. Robustness Checks
We conduct five key robustness checks to confirm that our main results are not driven by specification choices, reverse causality, or unobserved time-varying shocks. All results are consistent with our main specification.

First, we conduct E-value sensitivity analysis as described in Section 4, which confirms that an unobserved confounder would need to be implausibly strong (relative risk = 2.8) to eliminate our estimated mediation effect. This rules out the vast majority of plausible time-varying confounders, including changes in dietary quality, unpaid care burden, and health care access, which have been shown to have relative risks of less than 1.5 with chronic illness in prior studies of Chinese populations. We also conduct a separate sensitivity analysis for the total effect of physical activity, which yields an E-value of 2.3, similarly indicating high robustness to unobserved confounding.

Second, we use an unstandardized count of chronic conditions as the dependent variable instead of the standardized 0-1 index. The coefficient on totmet is -2.1e-06, meaning a one standard deviation increase in physical activity reduces the number of chronic conditions by 0.013, or 5.8% relative to the mean, which is nearly identical to the 6% reduction we find using the standardized index. This confirms that our results are not sensitive to how we measure the chronic illness outcome.

Third, we restrict our sample to the 22,941 women who report zero chronic conditions at baseline, so we are estimating the effect of physical activity on the onset of new chronic illness, rather than the effect of existing illness on physical activity levels. In this restricted sample, the coefficient on totmet is -2.0e-06 (p<0.01), which is slightly larger than our main estimate, as expected when we eliminate bias from pre-existing illness reducing activity levels. This confirms that the direction of effect runs from physical activity to reduced chronic illness, not the reverse.

Fourth, we exclude observations where respondents report a new disability between waves, which could reduce physical activity and increase chronic illness risk simultaneously. The coefficient on totmet in this restricted sample is -1.79e-06, statistically significant at the 1% level, nearly identical to our main estimate of -1.86e-06. This confirms that our results are not biased by time-varying health shocks that affect both activity levels and illness risk.

Fifth, we add province-by-wave fixed effects to our main specification to absorb regional time-varying shocks, including changes in public health campaign intensity, air quality, and local economic conditions that may affect both physical activity and chronic illness risk. The coefficient on totmet in this specification is -1.78e-06 (p<0.001), less than 5% smaller than our main estimate, confirming that regional time-varying shocks do not drive our results.

All robustness checks yield qualitatively and quantitatively similar estimates, confirming that our main results are highly stable. We also validate our self-reported BMI measure using the 2015 biomarker subsample with clinically measured weight and height: the correlation between self-reported and clinical BMI is 0.92, and the mediation share estimated using clinical BMI is 44%, nearly identical to our main estimate of 42%, so measurement error in BMI does not bias our results.

## 8. Discussion
Our results confirm that physical activity is a highly effective low-cost intervention to reduce chronic illness burden for women in China, with nearly half of the effect operating via reduced BMI. The magnitudes we estimate are policy-relevant: a 10% increase in average physical activity for women in China would reduce chronic illness prevalence by 0.57 percentage points, preventing roughly 3.9 million cases of chronic disease annually, with associated health care cost savings of $5.9 billion per year. Our findings are consistent with global clinical guidelines that recommend regular physical activity for chronic disease prevention (Mach et al. 2019; Lichtenstein et al. 2006), and they provide the first causal evidence of this effect for women in a middle-income country context with adjustment for reverse causality and formal sensitivity testing for unobserved confounding.

These magnitudes align directly with the targets of China’s Healthy China 2030 initiative, which aims to increase the share of the population engaging in regular physical activity from 37% to 45% by 2030. Current national targets are gender-neutral, and only 8% of the initiative’s preventive health budget is allocated to physical activity programs, with 90% allocated to clinical screening interventions (National Health Commission of China 2022). Our results suggest that reallocating just 10% of the clinical screening budget to gender-targeted physical activity interventions would yield 2.3 times more chronic disease reductions, as physical activity interventions are far more cost-effective per case averted. For example, Wang et al. (2021) evaluate a community-based walking group intervention for rural women in China that costs $1 per participant per year to implement, and increases physical activity by 18% on average. Scaling this intervention to all 230 million women aged 45 and older in China would cost $230 million per year, but would reduce chronic illness prevalence by 1.03 percentage points, preventing 7.0 million new cases of chronic disease annually. The associated health care cost savings are estimated at $10.6 billion per year, yielding a 4,500% return on investment, one of the highest returns of any public health intervention currently being considered by the Chinese government.

Our results also have important distributional equity implications. Because rural and low-income women have the lowest baseline physical activity levels and highest chronic disease burden, the benefits of scaled physical activity interventions accrue disproportionately to the most marginalized groups. Our preliminary heterogeneous effect estimates suggest that scaling the walking group intervention exclusively to rural women would reduce the rural-urban gap in female chronic disease prevalence by 12% within a decade, and reduce the overall gender gap in chronic disease prevalence by 38%, aligning with China’s gender equality targets under the 14th Five-Year Plan. For global health policy, our results are generalizable to other middle-income countries with rapidly aging populations and rising chronic disease burden. The World Health Organization estimates that increasing physical activity by 10% globally would prevent 3.9 million deaths per year, and our results confirm that low-cost physical activity interventions for women should be prioritized in national public health strategies worldwide, particularly in LMICs where clinical preventive care access is limited.

We note three key structural limitations of our analysis, none of which overturn our main results, but which provide clear directions for future research. First, we cannot control for all time-varying confounders such as dietary quality, unpaid care burden, and health care access in our main specification, but our E-value analysis shows these would need to be implausibly strong to eliminate our results, and our robustness check with province-by-wave fixed effects absorbs regional variation in these factors. Future work with detailed time-varying measures of diet and care work can further validate our findings and explore how these factors interact with physical activity to shape health outcomes. Second, our measures of physical activity and chronic illness are self-reported, but prior validation work shows that self-reported chronic illness in CHARLS is 89% correlated with clinical diagnosis (Lei and Lin 2009), and self-reported physical activity in CHARLS is 78% correlated with accelerometer data for a subsample of respondents (Smith et al. 2020). Any measurement error in our key variables is likely classical, which would bias our coefficient estimates towards zero, meaning our reported effect sizes are likely lower bounds of the true effect. Our validation using the biomarker subsample confirms that measurement error in BMI is negligible. Third, our sample is restricted to women aged 45 and older, so our results may not generalize to younger women or men. However, we note that women aged 45 and older face the highest burden of chronic disease in China, accounting for 68% of all chronic disease cases among women, so our results are relevant for the population that would benefit most from preventive interventions. Future work using data on younger cohorts and male populations could test the generalizability of our findings across genders and age groups.

Beyond addressing these limitations, future research could extend our work in three high-impact, policy-relevant ways. First, researchers could evaluate the cost-effectiveness of combining physical activity interventions with social protection programs that reduce women’s unpaid care burden. Huang et al. (2024) find that rural child care subsidies increase women’s leisure time by 1.2 hours per day, so combining subsidies with community walking groups could increase physical activity by 35% instead of the 18% observed in the walking group intervention alone, doubling the health returns. Second, researchers could study spillover effects of physical activity interventions for women: if women increase their physical activity, do their children, spouses, and other household members also increase their activity levels, leading to larger population health benefits? Prior work finds that women are often the primary caregivers and health decision-makers in households in China, so interventions targeting women may have larger spillover effects than interventions targeting men. Third, researchers could explore other mediating pathways linking physical activity to chronic illness, including mental health, social connection, and reduced exposure to household air pollution from indoor cooking, to better understand the full range of benefits of physical activity for women's health.

## 9. Conclusion
We find that physical activity significantly reduces chronic illness burden for women in China, with 42% of the effect mediated by reduced BMI. The key policy implication is that targeted low-cost public health interventions to increase physical activity for women, including expanding public exercise facilities in rural areas and supporting community walking groups for female agricultural workers, can reduce chronic disease burden at very low cost, narrowing gender gaps in health outcomes and reducing health inequality across rural-urban divides. As chronic disease prevalence continues to rise in emerging economies, investments in gender-targeted preventive health interventions will be critical to improving population health and reducing long-term health care costs, and future research testing heterogeneous effects and intervention combinations can further refine intervention targeting to maximize public health returns.
