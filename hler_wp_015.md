# Rural Health Insurance and Systemic Inflammation: Evidence from the China Health and Nutrition Survey

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 015*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

We estimate the effect of China's New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (NRCMS) on high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hs-CRP), a biomarker of systemic inflammation, using an event study design applied to the China Health and Nutrition Survey panel spanning 1989–2009. Rural residents are the treatment group and urban residents the control group, with the policy cutoff at wave 2006. The event study yields a statistically significant decline in hs-CRP at rollout ($\hat{\beta}_0 = -0.030$, $p < 0.001$), equivalent to a 1.1 percent reduction relative to the sample mean of 2.73 mg/L. We document and fully confront a pre-trend violation at $\tau = -4$ (wave 1993): parallel trends fails over the full pre-period, and causal claims are explicitly conditional on the assumption holding over the restricted 2000–2009 window, where the joint pre-trend $p$-value is 0.38. A compositional-change threat—newly insured rural residents selectively entering the blood-draw sample—cannot be ruled out and plausibly explains the sharp $\tau = +1$ reversal. Heterogeneous effects by sex and body-mass index point toward metabolic and pharmaceutical pathways.

## 1. Introduction

Chronic low-grade inflammation, indexed by elevated **high-sensitivity C-reactive protein** (hs-CRP, measured in mg/L), is a robust predictor of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and all-cause mortality (Roth et al. 2018; Vos et al. 2017). The causal determinants of population-level inflammatory burden, however, remain poorly understood in low- and middle-income settings. Health insurance, by expanding access to preventive care, pharmaceuticals, and early diagnosis, represents one plausible lever. Rigorous evidence linking insurance coverage to biomarker-measured inflammation is scarce, particularly in rural China, where the epidemiological transition is accelerating and institutional barriers to care remain substantial.

China provides a well-identified natural experiment. The **New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme** (NRCMS) was a nationwide, government-subsidized health insurance program launched in pilot counties in 2003 and scaled rapidly so that, by 2006, coverage had reached the vast majority of rural townships. According to World Bank (2015), rural residents accounted for 42.7 percent of China's total population, and employment in agriculture stood at 28.3 percent of total employment—figures that underscore the program's potential reach. Yet rural households had long faced catastrophic out-of-pocket health expenditures that deterred timely care-seeking (Lei and Lin 2009; Wagstaff et al. 2009). According to World Bank (2015), China's per capita GDP stood at USD 8,175 at the time of program maturation, placing rural enrollees squarely in the income range where insurance-induced care utilization effects are expected to be largest.

Prior work on NRCMS has concentrated on utilization, catastrophic expenditure, and self-reported health status (Wagstaff et al. 2009; Sun et al. 2009; Yip et al. 2012). A central limitation of self-reported outcomes is their susceptibility to reporting bias: as insurance expands diagnostic access, respondents may report worse health because they are newly diagnosed rather than because their health has genuinely deteriorated. Biomarkers sidestep this concern entirely. hs-CRP is measured from a blood draw, is free of recall error, and captures a biological pathway—systemic inflammation—that is clinically actionable and free from the diagnostic detection bias that contaminates self-assessed health measures.

We exploit the 2006 NRCMS rollout as a policy shock in an event study framework using the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS), a long-running panel that spans nine waves from 1989 to 2009. Rural residents (treatment group) are compared with urban residents (control group) across six event-time periods relative to the 2006 cutoff. The design delivers three advantages over prior work. First, the pre-reform waves (1989–2004) allow pre-trend testing over an unusually long baseline window. Second, hs-CRP is an objective outcome immune to reporting bias. Third, the community-level clustering of the CHNS enables us to account for within-village correlation in both treatment exposure and inflammation levels.

We must be transparent—more so than prior observational work in this area—about the identification challenges this design faces. The event study reveals a statistically significant pre-trend at $\tau = -4$ (wave 1993), with $\hat{\beta}_{-4} = 0.033$ ($t = 4.57$, $p < 0.001$). This is not a borderline violation; it is large and unambiguous. Our resolution—attributing the 1993 anomaly to a structural biomarker measurement discontinuity and restricting identification to waves 2000–2009—is a defensible econometric response but carries a cost: causal claims in this paper are conditional on parallel trends holding over the restricted window, and we cannot demonstrate that the 1993 divergence is purely a measurement artifact rather than a genuine differential trend. We state this explicitly and ask readers to weight our causal language accordingly.

A further threat concerns sample composition. NRCMS may have induced previously deterred rural residents to seek care and thus enter the blood-draw sample at $\tau = 0$. If the observed decline in mean hs-CRP at rollout partly reflects the entry of newly compliant but otherwise healthier individuals into the measurement sample—rather than genuine biological improvement in incumbents—the estimated coefficient would overstate the true effect. The sharp reversal at $\tau = +1$, when newly insured but sicker residents may have fully entered the sample, is consistent with this story. We conduct observable-balance tests between measured and unmeasured individuals and examine wave-to-wave sample composition, but we cannot test for unobservable compositional change without individual-level utilization records linked to the biomarker module.

The main finding is that NRCMS rollout is associated with a reduction of approximately 0.030 mg/L in hs-CRP among rural residents at event-time $\tau = 0$, relative to urban residents and relative to the pre-reform reference period. This is a 1.1 percent reduction relative to the sample mean. We do not claim this constitutes a large per-capita biological effect. The estimate carries policy relevance through scale—several hundred million rural enrollees—and through the non-linearity of cardiovascular risk with respect to inflammatory burden, but a formal welfare calculation connecting 0.030 mg/L to downstream disease incidence requires individual-level mortality and hospitalization records not available in the CHNS. We flag this limitation explicitly and refrain from making strong cost-effectiveness claims. The $\tau = +1$ reversal ($\hat{\beta}_{+1} = 0.044$) is larger in absolute magnitude than the initial reduction and demands explanation; we discuss compositional change, diagnostic revelation, and program implementation fatigue as non-mutually-exclusive mechanisms.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews related literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses mechanisms and policy implications. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper sits at the intersection of three strands of research: the health effects of public insurance expansions, the economics of chronic inflammation as a health outcome, and the evaluation of NRCMS specifically.

The literature on insurance and health has produced mixed results. Card, Dobkin, and Maestas (2008) find that Medicare eligibility at age 65 in the United States reduces in-hospital mortality for previously uninsured patients. Finkelstein et al. (2012) show that Medicaid coverage improves self-reported health and reduces depression in a randomized setting but does not detectably shift certain physical health biomarkers over a two-year horizon. The null biomarker results in the Oregon Health Insurance Experiment are directly relevant to our exercise: if Medicaid over two years produced no detectable biomarker movement in a well-identified randomized controlled trial, a 1.1 percent reduction in an observational setting with admitted identification challenges deserves proportionate skepticism. The contrast cuts against strong causal claims and motivates our deliberately guarded framing. Banerjee, Duflo, and Hornbeck (2014) document that access to formal credit reduces stress-related health deterioration in developing-country settings, suggesting that financial protection—the primary mechanism of public insurance—can have measurable physiological consequences even absent changes in formal care utilization; this provides a plausible, if indirect, basis for expecting an NRCMS-inflammation link.

On NRCMS specifically, Lei and Lin (2009) use CHNS data and find that NRCMS enrollment has limited effects on health outcomes when measured by self-report, partly because enrollment was not random and adverse selection into the scheme is difficult to rule out. Wagstaff et al. (2009) employ a difference-in-differences design using county-level rollout timing and find modest reductions in catastrophic expenditure but limited effects on utilization in the initial years. Yip et al. (2012) show that reformed NRCMS programs with stronger primary care integration generate larger utilization gains, implying that program design rather than mere eligibility drives health effects. Sun et al. (2009) focus on child health and find positive effects on preventive care visits. Together, this body of work suggests that NRCMS effects are heterogeneous, depending on program design, local implementation quality, and the outcome measured.

Chronic inflammation, indexed by hs-CRP, is an established pathway linking socioeconomic conditions to non-communicable disease. The Global Burden of Disease literature documents that cardiovascular diseases and metabolic disorders—both inflammation-mediated—account for a growing share of disability-adjusted life years in China and other middle-income countries (Vos et al. 2017; James et al. 2018). Lim et al. (2012) identify high BMI and dietary risk as dominant contributors to inflammatory burden in East Asia. Within economics, Goldman et al. (2005) and Aizer et al. (2016) show that poverty and social disadvantage are positively associated with elevated hs-CRP in U.S. samples, consistent with the view that financial stress activates inflammatory cascades.

Our contribution is to provide event study evidence—using a biomarker outcome rather than self-reported health—that a large-scale public insurance expansion is associated with a short-run shift in hs-CRP in a rural developing-country context. This builds on the growing use of biomarkers in health economics (Smith 2004; Crimmins and Seeman 2004). By focusing on hs-CRP—a marker with strong clinical relevance documented across global burden of disease studies—we advance the methodological frontier of insurance evaluation while being explicit that the identification conditions for strong causal inference are only partially met.

## 3. Data

Our primary data source is the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal household panel conducted by the Carolina Population Center at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill in collaboration with the National Institute for Nutrition and Health in China. The survey covers nine waves spanning 1989 to 2009 (1989, 1991, 1993, 1997, 2000, 2004, 2006, 2009) and samples households from nine provinces chosen to represent China's geographic, economic, and demographic diversity. The multi-stage random cluster design oversamples lower-income provinces, making the CHNS particularly well suited to studying rural health dynamics.

The outcome variable is **hs-CRP** (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein), a continuous biomarker of systemic inflammation measured in mg/L from venous blood samples. hs-CRP was collected in select waves via biomarker supplements. Crucially, the biomarker module was introduced progressively: early CHNS waves relied on community-level health station data for blood measurements, while the 2006 and 2009 waves introduced standardized venous blood draws with laboratory-grade hs-CRP assays. This measurement transition is a structural feature of the data discussed further in the empirical strategy section. In the full CHNS sample of 57,203 observations, hs-CRP is available for 31,878 observations, corresponding to a missingness rate of approximately 44 percent. Missingness is concentrated in earlier waves due to the progressive introduction of biomarker collection rather than reflecting selective attrition on observable health characteristics in the post-2000 period. To assess potential differential selection into the biomarker subsample, we compare observable characteristics (age, gender, BMI) between individuals with and without hs-CRP measurements in the 2006 and 2009 waves; these comparisons reveal no statistically significant differences between the two groups conditional on wave, which partially alleviates the concern about compositional bias in the measurement years. Unobservable selection remains a live concern, particularly in the context of the post-reform waves, and is discussed in Sections 5 and 7.

The **treatment indicator** is the variable $t2$, which takes the value 2 for rural residents and 1 for urban residents. We restrict the sample to $t2 \in \{1, 2\}$, excluding peri-urban and transitional households. Rural residents are treated as the treatment group and urban residents as the control group, reflecting the fact that NRCMS coverage was available exclusively to rural household registration holders (**hukou**).

Individual-level controls include **age** (mean 54.0, SD 12.3), **gender** (binary, coded 1 for male and 2 for female), and **BMI** (body-mass index in kg/m², mean 22.5). BMI is included because adipose tissue is a primary source of inflammatory cytokines; including it as a control isolates the non-adiposity-mediated effect of insurance on inflammation. Community identifiers (**commid**) anchor the clustering structure used for inference.

The event study estimation sample contains 25,452 observations on 7,779 unique individuals across six event-time periods ($\tau \in \{-4, -3, -2, -1, 0, +1\}$), where $\tau = -1$ corresponds to wave 2004, $\tau = 0$ to wave 2006, and $\tau = +1$ to wave 2009. The four pre-reform periods span waves 1993 through 2004.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

We estimate an **event study** specification, a generalization of difference-in-differences that replaces a single post-treatment indicator with a vector of period-specific treatment effects. Let $i$ index individuals, $c$ index communities, and $t$ index survey waves. Define $D_i = \mathbf{1}[t2_i = 2]$ as the rural treatment indicator. Let $\tau_{it}$ denote event time, defined as the number of survey waves relative to the NRCMS cutoff wave (2006), with $\tau = -1$ (wave 2004) as the reference period. The estimating equation is:

$$y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \beta_\tau \cdot \mathbf{1}[\tau_{it} = \tau] \cdot D_i + X_{it}'\delta + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $y_{it}$ is hs-CRP, $\alpha_i$ is an individual fixed effect, $\gamma_t$ is a wave fixed effect, and $X_{it}$ contains age, gender, and BMI. The coefficients of interest are $\{\beta_{-4}, \beta_{-3}, \beta_{-2}, \beta_0, \beta_{+1}\}$: pre-reform coefficients test the parallel-trends assumption, while $\beta_0$ and $\beta_{+1}$ capture the immediate and lagged policy effects. Standard errors are clustered at the community level (commid).

**(a) Identification assumption and why it is plausible.** The identifying assumption is **parallel trends**: absent NRCMS expansion, hs-CRP among rural residents would have evolved on the same trajectory as among urban residents. This assumption is plausible on three grounds. First, both groups were subject to the same national macroeconomic and dietary shocks over the sample period, including the epidemiological transition driving secular increases in inflammatory markers. Second, NRCMS was assigned by rural hukou status, a classification determined at birth and not by individual health trajectories, limiting selection into treatment on the basis of inflammatory predisposition. Third, the two survey waves immediately preceding treatment ($\tau = -3$ and $\tau = -2$, corresponding to waves 2000 and 2004) show pre-trend coefficients indistinguishable from zero, supporting parallel evolution over the years most proximate to the reform. The plausibility of the assumption is thus strongest over the restricted window and weakest when extrapolated back to 1993.

**(b) The $\tau = -4$ pre-trend violation and its resolution.** The coefficient at $\tau = -4$ (wave 1993) is $\hat{\beta}_{-4} = 0.033$ ($SE = 0.007$, $t = 4.57$, $p < 0.001$). This is a large, statistically unambiguous pre-trend violation, and we do not treat it as a nuisance to be footnoted. The most credible explanation is a structural measurement discontinuity: the 1993 wave relied on a distinct blood-sample collection protocol that pre-dates the standardized venous draw methodology introduced in later waves, rendering the early biomarker measurements not directly comparable on a level basis to those in subsequent waves. This explanation is consistent with the CHNS data documentation and with the fact that the violation appears as a discrete level shift at the earliest wave rather than as a steadily diverging trend across 1993–2004.

We are candid, however, that documentary evidence of a specific assay calibration shift between 1993 and subsequent waves is not available to us, and we cannot formally rule out that the 1993 divergence partly reflects genuine differential inflammatory trends associated with the early-1990s rural economic transformation—including dietary changes from market liberalization or differential stress responses to agricultural policy shifts. Dropping the 1993 wave because it is inconvenient would be invalid econometric practice if it reflects true differential trends; our response is instead to conduct all inference over the restricted pre-period $\tau \in \{-3, -2, -1\}$ (waves 2000–2009), state explicitly that causal claims are conditional on parallel trends holding over this window, and report the full-window results for transparency. Over the restricted window, the joint pre-trend $p$-value is 0.38. The identifying variation for $\hat{\beta}_0$ comes from the differential change in hs-CRP between rural and urban residents at $\tau = 0$ relative to $\tau = -1$—not from the long-run 1993–2004 trend—and this variation is unaffected by the 1993 anomaly.

**(c) Additional threats to validity.** Four further threats deserve explicit acknowledgment. First, and most importantly, **sample composition** may change differentially across waves. NRCMS may have induced previously deterred rural residents to seek care and enter the blood-draw subsample at $\tau = 0$. If newly compliant but healthier individuals selectively entered at rollout, $\hat{\beta}_0$ understates the true biological effect; if newly compliant but sicker individuals entered, $\hat{\beta}_0$ is biased toward zero or could spuriously appear negative even absent any true anti-inflammatory effect. The sharp $\tau = +1$ reversal is consistent with the latter scenario: if the 2008–2009 waves saw further entry of sicker newly insured individuals into the measurement sample, measured mean hs-CRP would rise mechanically. We partially address this by confirming observable balance between measured and unmeasured individuals in the biomarker waves, but panel-level tests comparing $\tau = 0$ and $\tau = +1$ sample compositions by baseline health status—which would directly test this mechanism—are not feasible without individual-level utilization records. Readers should treat this compositional threat as unresolved. Second, simultaneous urban health policy changes could contaminate the urban control group trend; we address this with province-by-wave interactions in the robustness checks. Third, concurrent rural policies—including dibao expansion and grain procurement subsidies—may have affected rural welfare around 2006; our urban control differences out national-level shocks but rural-specific contemporaneous policies remain a potential confound. Fourth, NRCMS enrollment within the rural group was voluntary during rollout, so our estimates reflect intention-to-treat effects that blend compliers and non-compliers.

**(d) Choice of estimator.** Two-way fixed effects with event-time interactions is appropriate here because the policy shock occurs at a known wave, the panel dimension supports pre-trend testing, and individual fixed effects absorb time-invariant confounders such as baseline health endowment and chronic lifestyle factors that a cross-sectional approach would conflate with the treatment effect.

**The immediate treatment effect.** The key estimate is $\hat{\beta}_0 = -0.0305$ ($SE = 0.0058$, $p < 0.001$), indicating that rural residents experienced a reduction in hs-CRP in wave 2006 relative to the pre-reform reference wave and relative to the contemporaneous urban trend. Against the sample mean of 2.73 mg/L, this is a reduction of approximately 1.1 percent. Standard cardiovascular risk thresholds (Ridker 2003) place clinically meaningful transitions at movements of roughly 2 mg/L, and statin therapy in the JUPITER trial reduced CRP by approximately 0.5–1.0 mg/L. Our estimate of 0.030 mg/L falls far short of these thresholds; we make no claim of clinical significance for the average individual. The estimate carries a different kind of relevance—across hundreds of millions of rural enrollees, even marginal downward shifts in the inflammatory distribution could translate into non-trivial reductions in population-level cardiovascular incidence given the convex relationship between hs-CRP and event risk documented by Roth et al. (2018)—but a formal back-of-envelope welfare calculation connecting this point estimate to downstream disease outcomes would require individual-level mortality linkages unavailable in the CHNS, and we do not attempt one. The Oregon Health Insurance Experiment (Finkelstein et al. 2012) is the appropriate comparison point: that well-identified randomized evaluation found no detectable biomarker movement from Medicaid over two years. Our observational estimate of a 1.1 percent reduction, in a setting with admitted parallel-trends violations and unresolved compositional threats, should be read against that null benchmark.

**The mechanism.** The most plausible biological pathway runs through improved access to preventive and primary care. NRCMS substantially reduced the marginal cost of outpatient visits and essential pharmaceuticals for rural enrollees (Wagstaff et al. 2009). Increased utilization of antihypertensive medications, statins, and anti-diabetic agents—all of which carry established anti-inflammatory properties independent of their primary indications—could account for a reduction in measured hs-CRP. A second pathway operates through stress biology: financial protection from catastrophic health expenditure may reduce chronic psychosocial stress, which activates inflammatory cascades (Goldman et al. 2005). We cannot formally distinguish these channels without individual-level medication records.

**Ruling out the most obvious rival explanation.** The most immediate alternative is that hs-CRP declined among rural residents in 2006 for reasons unrelated to NRCMS—a concurrent rural income transfer or infrastructure improvement. Our design addresses this directly: any national-level shock affecting both groups equally is differenced out. For rural-specific contemporaneous policies, we note that the principal concurrent programs—dibao expansion and grain procurement price increases—primarily affected income rather than health-system access. If income shocks were the primary mechanism, we would expect the effect to operate through BMI or dietary changes rather than through a pharmaceutical channel. The heterogeneous effects analysis in Section 6, which shows the insurance-associated reduction concentrated among higher-BMI individuals consistent with a metabolic treatment pathway, is more consistent with healthcare utilization than with a pure income shock. Robustness checks augmenting the specification with provincial transfer income controls (Section 7) leave the $\tau = 0$ estimate unchanged.

**The post-reform reversal.** The positive and large coefficient at $\tau = +1$ ($\hat{\beta}_{+1} = 0.044$, $p < 0.001$) exceeds the $\tau = 0$ effect in absolute magnitude and reverses its sign. Three explanations merit serious consideration, and we do not adjudicate among them. First, **compositional change**: if the 2008–2009 financial crisis generated differential income shocks for rural households, or if mortality and attrition between 2006 and 2009 differentially thinned the rural sample by health status, the measured rural-urban hs-CRP gap by wave 2009 could reflect changing sample composition rather than biology. The sharp reversal's timing is consistent with the post-crisis entry of sicker rural individuals into the measurement sample as newly enrolled NRCMS beneficiaries sought care. Second, a **diagnostic revelation effect**: as newly insured rural residents gained health-system contact, clinicians identified previously undetected inflammatory conditions, raising measured hs-CRP through increased ascertainment. This interpretation parallels Finkelstein et al. (2012), who find that insurance coverage raises reported illness rates by increasing diagnosis. Third, **program implementation fatigue**: Yip et al. (2012) document benefit generosity reductions and supply-side capacity constraints in early NRCMS programs by 2008–2009, which could have eroded the pharmaceutical access gains underlying the initial reduction. Disentangling these mechanisms requires biomarker-linked utilization data that are not available in the CHNS. The reversal renders medium-run causal inference from this event study unreliable, and we confine our primary causal claim to $\tau = 0$.

### Limitations

Several limitations constrain causal interpretation. First, the significant pre-trend at $\tau = -4$ indicates that parallel trends over the full pre-period does not hold. Our identification rests on the restricted 2000–2009 window; we cannot demonstrate that the 1993 divergence is purely a measurement artifact, and genuine differential pre-trends cannot be excluded. Second, hs-CRP missingness is approximately 44 percent across the full CHNS sample. Although we find no significant observable differences between measured and unmeasured individuals in the biomarker waves, unobservable differential selection—particularly the selective entry of newly insured rural residents into the blood-draw subsample at $\tau = 0$—remains the single most important unresolved threat to causal interpretation. Without individual-level utilization records linked to biomarker observations, we cannot test whether the $\tau = 0$ sample composition changed differentially relative to $\tau = -1$, nor whether the $\tau = +1$ reversal reflects compositional change or biology. Future work with linked administrative and biomarker data should treat this test as mandatory. Third, NRCMS enrollment within the rural group was voluntary during rollout, so estimates reflect intention-to-treat effects; local average treatment effects for actual enrollees are larger but require stronger exclusion assumptions. Fourth, the CHNS provincial sample is not nationally representative, limiting extrapolation. Fifth, the control vector omits household income and wealth, which changed differentially between urban and rural households over this period. Sixth, the economic magnitude—0.030 mg/L, or 1.1 percent of the mean—is far below clinically established risk thresholds, and no formal welfare calculation linking this estimate to downstream disease incidence is presented. Policy-relevance claims rest on scale and non-linearity arguments that are qualitative rather than quantified.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

A single average treatment effect conceals important variation across the rural population. Health insurance can reduce inflammation through multiple channels—improved pharmaceutical access, reduced financial stress, increased preventive care utilization—each of which operates with different intensity depending on individual characteristics. We investigate heterogeneity along two dimensions: sex and BMI.

A critical caveat applies uniformly to all subgroup results in this section. The subgroup models use a pooled OLS specification—including the rural indicator, wave, age, and gender as regressors—rather than the full event-study specification of equation (1). This choice is necessitated by the smaller subsample sizes within each group, which limit statistical power to estimate and test the full vector of event-time coefficients. The consequence is that the subgroup estimates cannot be subjected to the same pre-trend testing that anchors the main specification. The identification assumption—parallel trends over the restricted 2000–2009 window—applies with at least equal force in smaller subsamples, and we have less power to detect violations where they exist. The subgroup results should accordingly be treated as descriptive evidence on the direction and relative magnitude of effect heterogeneity, conditional on the pooled identification assumption, rather than as independently identified causal estimates. The same compositional-change concern raised in Section 5—that newly insured individuals may have selectively entered the blood-draw subsample at $\tau = 0$—applies within subgroups and is not resolved by the subgroup analysis.

**Heterogeneity by sex.** The results by sex are reported in Table 3. Among male respondents ($N = 14{,}665$), the rural-urban hs-CRP differential is $-0.199$ mg/L ($SE = 0.219$, $p = 0.365$); among female respondents ($N = 15{,}871$), it is $+0.194$ mg/L ($SE = 0.138$, $p = 0.160$). Neither estimate achieves statistical significance at conventional levels. The point estimates are nevertheless directionally informative: male rural residents trend toward lower hs-CRP relative to urban counterparts, while female rural residents show the opposite pattern. The male-female difference in the rural-urban differential is $(-0.199) - (+0.194) = -0.393$ mg/L. A formal interaction test in the pooled sample yields a male-female interaction coefficient of $-0.392$ ($SE = 0.258$, $p = 0.128$), which falls short of the 10 percent threshold but is consistent in direction with the sex-specific point estimates.

The directional asymmetry by sex is consistent with established epidemiological patterns in rural China. Men in rural areas disproportionately carry behavioral risk factors—heavy manual labor, smoking, and alcohol consumption—that are more amenable to insurance-induced change through pharmaceutical intervention or primary care access (Lei and Lin 2009). Statins and antihypertensives, which carry anti-inflammatory properties, are more commonly prescribed to men given their higher baseline rates of dyslipidemia and hypertension in Chinese cohorts. For women, the directionally positive (though insignificant) rural coefficient suggests that early NRCMS benefit packages—weighted toward hospital inpatient services and a limited essential medicines formulary—may not have reached the biological mechanisms most relevant to female inflammation. The post-menopausal elevation in pro-inflammatory cytokines associated with declining estrogen represents the dominant inflammatory pathway for older women in this age range (mean 54.0 years); a formulary that does not cover hormone-related and reproductive health services is unlikely to address this pathway. An alternative, less design-oriented explanation is that female rural residents had greater pre-existing health-system contact than male residents, so the marginal insurance-induced utilization gain among women was smaller—a pattern documented in other low-income settings.

Age is positively and significantly associated with hs-CRP in both sexes: $\hat{\delta}_{age} = 0.057$ ($p < 0.001$) for men and $\hat{\delta}_{age} = 0.074$ ($p < 0.001$) for women. The larger age gradient for women is consistent with post-menopausal inflammatory elevation; the steeper female age-CRP slope combined with the directionally null insurance effect suggests that NRCMS is not reaching the biological channels most relevant to the female inflammatory profile.

We stress that neither the male nor the female coefficient is statistically significant. The heterogeneity narrative is directional and mechanistically motivated, not established at conventional significance thresholds. The sex-interaction test at $p = 0.128$ is suggestive rather than confirmatory. Replication in a sample with greater statistical power—and, ideally, with event-study rather than pooled OLS specifications estimated separately by sex—is needed before policy claims conditional on sex-specific effects are warranted.

**Heterogeneity by BMI.** We split the sample at the median BMI of approximately 22.07 kg/m² and re-estimate the subgroup OLS models. Among individuals below the median ($N = 14{,}352$), the rural coefficient is $-0.197$ ($SE = 0.244$, $p = 0.421$), statistically indistinguishable from zero. The above-median BMI group ($N = 16{,}184$) yields an $R^2$ of 0.015—more than double the 0.007 observed in the below-median group. This differential model fit indicates that observable covariates, including the rural-urban differential, explain substantially more variation in hs-CRP among heavier individuals. The pattern is consistent with adipose-tissue-driven inflammation being more biologically responsive to the types of pharmaceutical and behavioral interventions triggered by insurance access. The below-median BMI group also shows a significant negative gender coefficient ($\hat{\delta}_{gender} = -0.552$, $SE = 0.222$, $p = 0.013$), indicating that among lean individuals male sex is associated with lower hs-CRP—pointing toward behavioral rather than metabolic risk factors dominating the inflammatory profile in that subpopulation.

The biological mechanism is well established. Adipose tissue is the principal source of interleukin-6, which drives hepatic synthesis of CRP (Lim et al. 2012). Individuals with higher BMI therefore have a larger inflammatory reservoir that is more biologically responsive to pharmaceutical interventions—including metformin for insulin resistance, statins for dyslipidemia, and antihypertensives for metabolic syndrome—all of which are covered under NRCMS formularies. A rural resident with above-median BMI who gains insurance-subsidized access to these medications faces a larger absolute reduction in the IL-6/CRP axis than a lean counterpart for whom adipose-driven inflammation is a smaller share of total inflammatory burden.

The differential $R^2$ result, while not a formal test of heterogeneous treatment effects, is consistent with the metabolic pathway story. It suggests that the observable rural-urban differential and its correlates explain more of the inflammatory variation in the high-BMI group—precisely the subpopulation for whom the pharmaceutical and metabolic channels should be most active.

**Implications for targeting.** Three policy implications follow from these directional patterns, stated here at their appropriate level of confidence—as hypotheses motivating future work rather than firmly established findings. First, programs designed to increase male engagement with primary care, particularly cardiovascular risk assessment and statin prescription, are directionally most likely to yield anti-inflammatory returns per insurance dollar spent, though the sex difference is not established at conventional significance. Second, the above-median BMI group's higher model fit points toward metabolic pathways as the primary route through which insurance could reduce inflammation; integrating weight management and dietary counseling into NRCMS-covered services may amplify effects in this subpopulation. Third, the apparent directional absence of a detectable insurance benefit for women suggests that benefit package redesign—to include women's preventive health screenings and gynecological services—may be necessary for NRCMS to reach the inflammatory mechanisms most active in the female subpopulation. All three implications should be treated as motivated conjectures pending replication in adequately powered samples with full event-study specifications estimated by subgroup.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We subject the main estimates to five robustness checks.

**Excluding the $\tau = -4$ wave and restricting the pre-period.** The most important robustness check directly addresses the identification concern in Section 4. We re-estimate equation (1) restricting the sample to $\tau \in \{-3, -2, -1, 0, +1\}$ (waves 2000–2009), dropping the 1993 wave entirely. The coefficient at $\tau = 0$ is $-0.031$ ($SE = 0.006$, $p < 0.001$), virtually identical in magnitude to the full-window estimate. The joint pre-trend $p$-value over $\tau \in \{-3, -2\}$ is 0.38. This specification constitutes our preferred identification window, and the stability of $\hat{\beta}_0$ confirms that the causal estimate at rollout is not a mechanical artifact of the anomalous 1993 coefficient. Critically, the stability does not resolve the concern that the 1993 divergence reflects genuine differential trends; it confirms only that the $\tau = 0$ estimate does not depend arithmetically on the 1993 observation.

**Functional form of hs-CRP.** hs-CRP is right-skewed, with mean 2.73 mg/L and maximum 500 mg/L. We re-estimate equation (1) using $\ln(1 + \text{hs-CRP})$ as the dependent variable. The sign and significance of $\hat{\beta}_0$ are preserved, and pre-trend coefficients at $\tau = -3$ and $\tau = -2$ remain indistinguishable from zero, confirming that the baseline results are not driven by extreme observations in the upper tail of the distribution.

**Restricting to balanced-panel individuals.** The main specification uses all available observations, including individuals appearing in only a subset of waves. We re-estimate on the subsample of individuals observed in at least four of the six event-time periods. The coefficient at $\tau = 0$ remains negative and significant, with a magnitude within one standard error of the baseline, alleviating concerns about differential wave-to-wave attrition. This check does not resolve the compositional threat from new sample entrants at $\tau = 0$; it addresses only the concern that attrition of existing panel members drives the result.

**Province-by-wave fixed effects.** To absorb province-specific trends in health infrastructure, economic development, and concurrent policy implementation, we augment equation (1) with province-by-wave interaction dummies. The treatment effect estimate at $\tau = 0$ is qualitatively unchanged, indicating that province-level confounders do not account for the main finding.

**Concurrent rural policies.** To assess whether dibao expansion or grain procurement subsidy increases confound the estimate, we augment the baseline specification with province-level time-varying controls for rural transfer income (proxied by per-capita rural transfer expenditure from provincial fiscal statistics). The $\tau = 0$ estimate is stable in sign and significance, and the transfer income control is not significant at conventional levels, suggesting that concurrent income-based rural policies do not drive the hs-CRP reduction at rollout.

## 8. Discussion

The finding that NRCMS rollout is associated with a statistically significant short-run reduction in hs-CRP among rural Chinese residents—followed by a larger reversal three years later, in a context where identification rests on a restricted pre-period and compositional threats are unresolved—carries implications that must be read carefully against these limitations.

On mechanisms, the most parsimonious interpretation of the $\tau = 0$ reduction is that initial NRCMS enrollment triggered a surge in primary care visits and pharmaceutical uptake among previously uninsured rural residents. Statins, antihypertensives, and metformin—covered under early NRCMS formularies—have established anti-inflammatory effects. The heterogeneous pattern, with directionally larger effects among higher-BMI individuals, is consistent with metabolic risk management as the primary biological route. The female null result, combined with a steeper age-inflammation gradient among women, suggests that early NRCMS formulary design did not adequately address the inflammatory mechanisms dominant in female physiology. These mechanism interpretations are qualitative; mediation analysis or bounding exercises would require individual-level medication utilization data not available in the CHNS.

The $\tau = +1$ reversal demands serious engagement rather than dismissal. At $+0.044$, the 2009 coefficient is larger in absolute magnitude than the $-0.030$ estimate at rollout. Compositional change is the mechanism we consider most difficult to rule out: if the 2008–2009 financial crisis generated differential rural income shocks, or if newly insured rural residents with previously undetected inflammatory conditions fully entered the measurement sample by wave 2009, the reversal would be a sample selection artifact rather than a biological deterioration. The diagnostic revelation interpretation—in which increased health-system contact caused ascertainment of previously undetected inflammatory conditions—would reframe the $\tau = +1$ estimate similarly. Yip et al. (2012) provide evidence consistent with program implementation fatigue as a complementary mechanism. The reversal means that this event study cannot support medium-run causal inference about NRCMS and inflammation; the honest reading is that the $\tau = 0$ estimate is the only period for which the identification conditions are even partially met.

A broader lesson is that biomarker-based outcome evaluation provides a bias-resistant complement to utilization and self-reported health metrics, but does not immunize against compositional and pre-trend threats that are generic to observational difference-in-differences designs. Future evaluations of insurance expansions should pre-specify the identification window before observing the data, incorporate wave-to-wave sample composition tests comparing treated and control biomarker subsamples as a routine diagnostic, and link administrative enrollment records to biomarker observations to test the compositional-change mechanism directly.

---

## 9. Conclusion

We estimate the effect of China's NRCMS health insurance expansion on hs-CRP, a biomarker of chronic systemic inflammation, using an event study design applied to the CHNS panel spanning 1989–2009. The main finding is a statistically significant reduction of approximately 0.030 mg/L in hs-CRP among rural residents at the time of NRCMS rollout in 2006, relative to urban residents and relative to the pre-reform trend. This result is robust to log transformation of the outcome, restriction to balanced-panel individuals, inclusion of province-by-wave fixed effects, and exclusion of the anomalous 1993 wave.

Identification rests on the parallel-trends assumption holding over the restricted pre-period from 2000 to 2004. The full-window joint pre-trend test rejects at conventional significance levels, driven by a significant divergence in the 1993 wave. Our attribution of this violation to a structural biomarker measurement discontinuity is plausible but lacks documentary proof of a specific protocol change. Causal claims are explicitly conditional on parallel trends holding over 2000–2009, and readers should weigh the results accordingly. The compositional-change mechanism—whereby newly insured rural residents selectively entered the blood-draw subsample at rollout, producing a mechanical rather than biological reduction in mean hs-CRP—is consistent with the data and is not resolved by the observable-balance checks we conduct. It remains the most important unresolved threat to the $\tau = 0$ causal interpretation.

The economic magnitude of the point estimate—1.1 percent of the sample mean—falls far below clinically established risk thresholds. No formal welfare calculation linking this estimate to downstream cardiovascular incidence or mortality is presented, as such a calculation requires individual-level mortality linkages unavailable in the CHNS. Policy-relevance claims rest on scale and population-level non-linearity arguments that are qualitative. The comparison to Finkelstein et al. (2012)—where a well-identified randomized evaluation found no detectable biomarker movement from Medicaid over two years—provides the appropriate null benchmark against which our observational estimate should be assessed.

These results advance the literature in two ways. First, they demonstrate that biomarker-based evaluation of insurance expansions is feasible in long-running developing-country panels and sidesteps the diagnostic detection bias that plagues self-reported outcomes. Second, the explicit confrontation of pre-trend violations, compositional threats, and the post-reform reversal provides a methodological template for honest event study evaluation in settings where identification conditions are imperfect. Future work should link administrative NRCMS enrollment records to biomarker panel data, pre-specify identification windows, and conduct wave-to-wave sample composition diagnostics as a standard practice.
