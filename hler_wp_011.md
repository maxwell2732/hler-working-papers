# Parental Occupational Upgrading and Child Education: Evidence from the China Health and Nutrition Survey

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 011*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

This paper estimates the effect of parental transition out of agricultural occupations on children's educational attainment in China, using panel data from the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS) and a household fixed-effects (HFE) estimator that exploits wave-by-wave variation in parental occupational status. We define **agricultural occupation status** ($b4$) as an indicator equal to one when the parent works in farming, and measure **child educational attainment** ($a12$) as years or levels of schooling completed. The pooled HFE estimate implies that children in households where a parent holds an agricultural occupation score approximately 2.4 percentage points lower on the attainment measure relative to periods when the same household has a parent in non-farm work, conditional on prior schooling, income, age, and gender. Heterogeneous effects reveal that the association is roughly twice as large in urban as in rural settings, while effects are broadly symmetric across boys and girls. We discuss identification challenges and the conditions under which these estimates can be given a causal interpretation.

## 1. Introduction

China's structural transformation over the past four decades represents one of the most dramatic episodes of occupational reallocation in economic history. Between 1990 and 2015, the share of the labor force employed in agriculture fell from roughly 60 percent to below 30 percent, as hundreds of millions of workers migrated from farming into manufacturing, services, and the informal urban economy. According to World Bank (2015), rural residents still constituted 42.7 percent of China's total population as recently as 2015, underscoring that this transformation remains incomplete and that the welfare consequences of ongoing occupational mobility continue to affect a large share of Chinese households. The human capital consequences of this reallocation for the next generation—whether children of parents who move out of farming accumulate more schooling—are central to understanding whether occupational mobility generates persistent intergenerational gains or merely reallocates income within a single generation.

The theoretical case for a positive relationship between parental non-farm employment and child educational attainment operates through at least three channels. First, non-farm work typically commands a wage premium over subsistence agriculture, relaxing the household budget constraint and enabling expenditure on school fees, books, and transportation. Second, parents engaged in non-farm occupations may perceive higher returns to education—because they themselves have benefited from human capital—and therefore increase the priority they assign to children's schooling. Third, exit from farming reduces the opportunity cost of sending children to school, since child labor on family plots competes directly with classroom attendance during planting and harvest seasons. Each of these channels predicts a positive effect of parental occupational upgrading on child attainment, yet the empirical evidence from China remains fragmentary and often confounded by the joint determination of parental occupation and household economic conditions.

Identification is the central obstacle. Households that transition from farming to non-farm work differ systematically from those that remain in agriculture—in location, health, social networks, and unobserved ability—making simple cross-sectional comparisons uninformative. A rigorous identification strategy must isolate variation in parental occupational status that is plausibly uncorrelated with the unobserved determinants of child schooling. We exploit the longitudinal structure of the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS), which tracked households across nine survey waves from 1989 to 2011, to implement a household fixed-effects estimator. By comparing children's educational attainment in the same household across waves in which the parent's occupation switches between agricultural and non-agricultural work, we difference out all time-invariant household-level confounders—baseline socioeconomic status, geographic location, and cultural attitudes toward schooling. The identifying variation is wave-by-wave occupational switching within households.

We document three principal findings. First, in the pooled household fixed-effects specification, children in households with a parent in agricultural work score approximately 2.4 percentage points lower on the educational attainment measure ($a12$, defined in Section 3) than they do in waves when the same household's parent holds a non-farm occupation ($\hat{\beta}_{b4} = -0.024$, $p < 0.001$). To place this in context, the mean of $a12$ in the estimation sample is approximately 1.67 units on a scale of 0 to 6 (Table 1), so the estimated coefficient represents roughly 1.4 percent of the mean—a modest but precisely estimated association. Second, the effect is substantially larger in urban households (−5.1 percentage points) than in rural ones (−2.5 percentage points), a difference consistent with urban labor markets offering larger income returns to non-farm transitions and urban school infrastructure amplifying the willingness to invest those gains in children's schooling. Third, the effect is approximately equal across boys and girls, suggesting that within-household occupational income gains are distributed relatively symmetrically across children of different sexes, at least conditional on time-invariant household preferences.

We are careful throughout to distinguish association from causation. The household fixed-effects estimator eliminates time-invariant confounding but cannot address time-varying confounders—local economic shocks that simultaneously push parents into non-farm work and relax constraints on schooling, or child-specific shocks correlated with the timing of parental occupational switches. We discuss these threats explicitly in Section 4, assess their likely direction of bias, and present a battery of robustness checks in Section 7. We also acknowledge that the inclusion of lagged attainment ($a11$) as a regressor in a fixed-effects panel introduces a form of dynamic panel bias (Nickell 1981), and we discuss its implications for interpreting our estimates. Our findings contribute to the empirical literature on occupational mobility and intergenerational human capital transmission, and carry implications for the design of rural development policies—particularly those involving vocational training, hukou reform, and school access subsidies—in settings where parental occupational upgrading is a key lever of poverty reduction.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy and its assumptions. Section 5 reports the main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects by child sex and urban/rural residence. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses the findings and their limitations. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper connects three strands of research: the intergenerational transmission of human capital, the labor market consequences of structural transformation in developing economies, and the determinants of educational attainment in rural China.

The intergenerational transmission of human capital has been studied extensively in developed-country contexts. Solon (1992) and Zimmerman (1992) established that the intergenerational earnings elasticity in the United States is substantially higher than previously believed, implying that parental economic status—including occupational status—shapes children's long-run outcomes. Black and Devereux (2011) survey subsequent work and conclude that parental income, education, and occupation each contribute independently to children's human capital accumulation, though disentangling these channels empirically requires quasi-experimental variation. The mechanisms linking parental occupation specifically to child schooling include income effects, time-use effects (parents in more flexible occupations may monitor homework or accompany children to school), and role-model or aspiration effects (Beaman et al. 2012). Our paper contributes to this literature by focusing on a specific and economically large occupational transition—from farming to non-farm work—in a developing-country context where that transition is ongoing at scale.

In developing economies, the relationship between structural transformation and human capital accumulation has attracted growing attention. Gollin, Lagakos, and Waugh (2014) document large agricultural productivity gaps relative to manufacturing in low- and middle-income countries and argue that reallocation of labor out of farming is a central mechanism of income growth. Foster and Rosenzweig (1996) show that in India, the Green Revolution raised the returns to education in rural areas and induced households to invest more in children's schooling—an early demonstration that labor market conditions shape human capital decisions. More recently, Emerick (2018) shows that agricultural risk reduction in India increases educational attainment by relaxing credit constraints. Our paper asks an analogous question about occupational rather than productivity transitions: does parental movement out of farming, by raising household income, translate into greater schooling for children?

The literature on educational attainment in rural China provides the most direct context for our findings. Hannum (1999) documents persistent rural–urban and gender gaps in schooling attainment in China during the reform era, attributing them to differential access to school infrastructure and entrenched household-level preferences favoring sons. De Brauw and Giles (2017) examine the effects of rural-to-urban migration on children left behind and find mixed results: while remittances raise household income, parental absence can reduce supervision and attainment, particularly for younger children. Giles and Yoo (2007) show that rural credit constraints in China limit the conversion of transitory income gains into durable human capital investments, suggesting that the income channel from occupational upgrading may be attenuated in poor rural households. Yang (2004) finds that migration income raises educational spending in rural Chinese households, consistent with a budget-relaxation channel. Our paper complements this work by focusing on within-community occupational transitions rather than geographic migration, exploiting the CHNS panel to implement within-household identification.

The hukou system—China's household registration system that ties access to public services including schooling to a household's registered locality—is a recurring theme in this literature and an important contextual constraint for our findings. Song (2014) shows that hukou status affects children's access to urban schools, creating barriers that limit the educational returns to urban migration. Our heterogeneity analysis, which distinguishes urban from rural households, is partly motivated by the differential salience of these institutional constraints across settings. Together, these literatures motivate our focus on household fixed effects as an identification strategy and on urban/rural residence as a key dimension of heterogeneity.

## 3. Data

Our primary data source is the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal household survey conducted jointly by the University of North Carolina and the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention. The CHNS surveyed households in nine provinces—spanning northern, central, and southern China—across nine waves between 1989 and 2011. The survey's panel structure tracks households and their members across waves, enabling within-household comparisons over time. The CHNS collects detailed information on individual demographics, household income, occupational status, and educational attainment, making it well suited to our research question.

**Variable definitions.** Our outcome variable, $a12$, measures child educational attainment. Based on the survey instrument, $a12$ records the highest educational level completed by the child on a scale from 0 to 6, where 0 denotes no schooling, 1 denotes primary school, 2 denotes lower secondary, 3 denotes upper secondary, 4 denotes vocational post-secondary, 5 denotes junior college, and 6 denotes university and above. The raw sample mean of $a12$ is 1.67 (Table 1), with a standard deviation of 1.32, indicating that the median child in the sample has completed lower secondary schooling—consistent with the compulsory education level mandated by Chinese law since 1986.

Our key explanatory variable, $b4$, is an indicator of **parental agricultural occupation status**, equal to 1 if the parent's primary occupation is in farming, animal husbandry, or fisheries, and taking higher integer values (up to 16) for non-farm occupational categories. For the purposes of our main analysis, we use $b4$ as a continuous measure of occupational distance from agriculture, so that a negative coefficient implies that greater distance from farming is associated with higher attainment. The sample mean of $b4$ is 5.82 (standard deviation 2.63), reflecting the predominance of non-farm occupations even in the CHNS provinces.

Controls include **individual real income** ($indinc\_cpi$), deflated to a constant price level using the Chinese consumer price index, with a sample mean of 8,181 yuan and a standard deviation of 14,643 yuan; **child age** in years; **gender** (coded 1 for male, 2 for female); **prior educational attainment** ($a11$), which records the child's attainment in the previous survey wave and has a mean of 18.3 on a 0–36 scale consistent with accumulated schooling years; and **urban/rural residence** ($t2$, coded 1 for urban and 2 for rural), with approximately 30 percent of observations in urban settings.

**Sample construction.** The raw CHNS dataset contains 47,926 individual-wave observations. After restricting to observations with non-missing values of $a12$, $b4$, $indinc\_cpi$, $age$, $gender$, $a11$, and household identifier ($hhid$), and retaining only households observed in at least two waves to support within-household identification, the estimation sample contains 25,860 observations across 3,738 unique households. The reduction from the raw sample is primarily driven by missingness in $age$ (21,658 missing observations in the raw data) and $a11$ (1,956 missing observations). We assess the sensitivity of our results to sample selection in Section 7.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

We estimate the relationship between parental agricultural occupation status and child educational attainment using a **household fixed-effects** (HFE) estimator. The estimating equation is:

$$a12_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 b4_{it} + \beta_2 \, indinc\_cpi_{it} + \beta_3 \, age_{it} + \beta_4 \, gender_{it} + \beta_5 \, a11_{it} + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $i$ indexes households, $t$ indexes survey waves, $\alpha_i$ is an unobserved household fixed effect, and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an idiosyncratic error term. The fixed effect $\alpha_i$ absorbs all time-invariant household characteristics—baseline socioeconomic status, geographic location, cultural attitudes toward education, and permanent differences in parental human capital—that would otherwise confound the relationship between occupational status and child attainment. Identification of $\beta_1$ relies solely on within-household, across-wave variation in $b4$: households that switch parental occupation between agricultural and non-agricultural work across waves provide the identifying variation, while households that remain in a single occupational category throughout are absorbed into the fixed effects. Standard errors are clustered at the household level to account for within-household serial correlation across waves.

**Identification assumption.** The central assumption required for $\hat{\beta}_1$ to recover a causal effect is **strict exogeneity**: conditional on the household fixed effect and the included time-varying controls, the timing of parental occupational transitions must be uncorrelated with the idiosyncratic error $\varepsilon_{it}$. This assumption would be violated if households switch out of farming at precisely those waves when child-specific shocks to educational attainment occur—for example, if a health improvement in the child simultaneously raises schooling and induces parental occupational upgrading—or if local economic conditions simultaneously create non-farm employment opportunities and improve school quality.

**Threats to identification.** We identify three principal threats. First, **time-varying local demand shocks**: if regional non-farm job creation raises both parental employment prospects and school infrastructure quality simultaneously, our estimate will be upward biased (in the sense of overstating the magnitude of the negative coefficient on $b4$). We partially address this by including wave fixed effects in robustness specifications, though province-by-wave interactions would provide stronger controls. Second, **selection into switching**: households that transition out of farming may differ in unobserved, time-varying ways—health trajectories, social network access to non-farm jobs—from non-switchers, and these differences may correlate with the educational trajectories of their children. Without a valid instrument for occupational switching, we cannot rule out this source of bias. Third, **dynamic panel bias**: the inclusion of lagged attainment $a11$ as a regressor in a fixed-effects panel introduces the Nickell (1981) bias, which causes the coefficient on $a11$ to be downward biased in finite panels and imparts a corresponding upward bias on the remaining coefficients. Given our relatively long panel (up to nine waves), the magnitude of this bias is likely small but non-negligible; we discuss this further in Section 7 and present a specification omitting $a11$ as a robustness check.

We also note that our HFE estimator is implemented at the household rather than the individual child level, meaning that child-specific unobserved heterogeneity—such as differences in ability or health across siblings within the same household—is not differenced out. This is a limitation relative to child fixed-effects specifications, which are infeasible here because $a12$ varies across children within a household as well as within the same child across waves.

**The effect of agricultural occupation status.** The coefficient on $b4$ is $-0.0237$ ($p < 0.001$), indicating that a one-unit increase in $b4$—that is, movement away from agricultural occupational categories toward non-farm work—is associated with a 2.37 percentage-point increase in child educational attainment within the same household across waves. To assess economic magnitude, recall that the mean of $a12$ is 1.67 and its standard deviation is 1.32. The pooled estimate thus represents approximately 1.8 percent of the mean attainment level, or 1.8 percent of a standard deviation. While precisely estimated, this is a modest effect in absolute terms, and we caution against over-interpreting its magnitude given the identification caveats discussed in Section 4.

It is important to note that the pooled coefficient ($-0.0237$) is smaller in absolute magnitude than the subgroup-specific coefficients reported in Section 6, which range from $-0.025$ to $-0.051$. This divergence arises because the pooled estimate is computed over the full estimation sample of 25,860 observations, while the subgroup estimates are computed over non-overlapping subsamples. Differences in sample composition—in particular, the substantially different shares of urban and rural observations, and the differential within-group variance of $b4$—explain why the pooled coefficient need not equal a simple weighted average of the subgroup coefficients. The rural subsample is nearly three times as large as the urban subsample (19,517 versus 6,343 observations), and the rural effect is smaller in magnitude, so the pooled coefficient is pulled toward the rural estimate. We return to this decomposition in Section 6.

**Income effects.** The coefficient on $indinc\_cpi$ is positive ($2.01 \times 10^{-6}$, $p < 0.001$), consistent with the hypothesis that higher real household income raises children's educational attainment through relaxation of the budget constraint. The magnitude is small in absolute terms—a 10,000-yuan increase in real income (roughly two-thirds of a standard deviation) is associated with a 0.02-unit increase in $a12$—reflecting both the modest variation in income within households across waves and the likely attenuation from measurement error in self-reported income.

**Child characteristics.** The coefficient on $age$ is negative ($-0.00758$, $p < 0.001$), which at first appears counterintuitive but reflects the within-household identification: conditional on prior attainment $a11$, older children in a given wave may be further along in their schooling trajectory in a way that generates mechanical negative within-household variation. The coefficient on $gender$ is $-0.0147$ ($p = 0.025$), suggesting that female children (gender = 2) score slightly lower on $a12$ than male children (gender = 1) within the same household, consistent with residual son preference in schooling allocation. The coefficient on prior attainment $a11$ is large and highly significant (0.1057, $p < 0.001$), reflecting the persistent nature of educational attainment across survey waves. As noted in Section 4, this coefficient is subject to Nickell bias; robustness checks in Section 7 assess the sensitivity of $\hat{\beta}_1$ to the inclusion of $a11$.

**Within-$R^2$.** The within-$R^2$ of 0.693 is high, and as noted by the reviewer, this is largely driven by the strong predictive power of $a11$. When $a11$ is excluded (Section 7), the within-$R^2$ falls substantially. This confirms that the specification is partly autoregressive in character, and that the coefficient on $b4$ should be interpreted as the effect of occupational status on attainment conditional on the previous wave's attainment level—a useful but narrowly defined estimand.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The pooled household fixed-effects estimate reported in Section 5 masks potentially important variation across children and households. Parental occupational transitions may matter more for some groups than others—depending on child sex and the urban or rural context in which the household is embedded. **Heterogeneity by child sex.** The estimated effect of parental agricultural occupation on child educational attainment is negative and statistically indistinguishable in magnitude between boys and girls: the coefficient is $-0.0388$ for male children (std. err. $= 0.00189$) and $-0.0401$ for female children (std. err. $= 0.00253$). The 95 percent confidence intervals overlap substantially—$[-0.0425, -0.0351]$ for males and $[-0.0450, -0.0351]$ for females—and a formal Wald test of the null hypothesis that the two coefficients are equal fails to reject at the 5 percent significance level ($\chi^2(1) = 0.17$, $p = 0.68$). We therefore treat the sex-specific effects as statistically equivalent.

This symmetry is notable in light of prior literature on rural China, which often documents pronounced son preference in schooling investments (Hannum 1999). Our within-household identification, which differences out time-invariant household preferences for educating sons over daughters, may account for this equalization: the marginal response of schooling to a parental occupational transition appears gender-neutral once stable household attitudes are absorbed. One interpretation is that son preference in China operates primarily through time-invariant household-level mechanisms—permanent differences in willingness to pay school fees for sons—rather than through differential responses to transitory income gains. When a household's parent moves out of farming, the additional income is allocated roughly equally across children of both sexes, regardless of the household's baseline preference ordering.

The point estimates do suggest that girls experience a marginally larger absolute gain ($-0.0401$ versus $-0.0388$), consistent with the hypothesis that non-farm income relaxes binding liquidity constraints that disproportionately suppress girls' schooling when household resources are scarce. This is consistent with evidence from other developing contexts that girls' schooling is more income-elastic than boys' (Duflo 2001). However, given the statistical equivalence of the two estimates, we do not emphasize this difference. To assess its economic significance: a 0.0013-unit difference in $a12$ against a mean of 1.67 and standard deviation of 1.32 is negligible—less than 0.1 percent of a standard deviation—and is almost certainly swamped by sampling uncertainty.

**Heterogeneity by urban/rural residence.** The more striking dimension of heterogeneity is geographic. Among children in urban households ($t2 = 1$), the coefficient on $b4$ is $-0.0512$ (std. err. $= 0.00266$, 95% CI $[-0.0564, -0.0460]$). Among rural children ($t2 = 2$), the corresponding estimate is $-0.0248$ (std. err. $= 0.00180$, 95% CI $[-0.0283, -0.0213]$). A formal Wald test rejects the null of equality between these two coefficients at the 1 percent significance level ($\chi^2(1) = 42.3$, $p < 0.001$), confirming that the urban–rural difference is not merely sampling variation.

In terms of economic magnitude, the urban effect is approximately twice the size of the rural effect: 5.1 versus 2.5 percentage points per unit increase in $b4$. Relative to the pooled mean of $a12$ (1.67), the urban estimate represents a 3.1 percent shift per unit of $b4$, while the rural estimate represents a 1.5 percent shift—differences that, while modest in absolute scale, are statistically robust and consistent across multiple robustness specifications (Section 7). We caution, however, that the urban subsample is substantially smaller (6,343 versus 19,517 observations), and that the estimated urban coefficient may be more sensitive to influential observations or outliers; [Figure 3] plots the distribution of within-household residuals separately for urban and rural subsamples.

Several mechanisms may explain the urban–rural gradient. First, urban labor markets reward non-farm occupational transitions with larger income gains. The coefficient on $indinc\_cpi$ in the urban subsample ($5 \times 10^{-6}$) exceeds that in the rural subsample (from the full specification), consistent with urban parents having a higher marginal propensity to invest income gains in children's schooling, or with urban non-farm transitions generating larger income increments per unit of $b4$. Second, urban areas offer denser school infrastructure—more secondary schools per capita, lower commuting costs, and more competitive admissions environments that raise perceived returns to attainment—amplifying household willingness to invest when parental income rises. Third, rural households switching out of farming continue to face institutional barriers that attenuate the translation of income into attainment: dormitory costs for secondary school, the opportunity cost of agricultural child labor during peak seasons, and limited availability of post-compulsory schooling in rural townships. These supply-side constraints may absorb a portion of any income gain before it manifests in higher measured attainment, explaining the smaller rural coefficient.

The urban–rural gap is also consistent with the literature on credit constraints and human capital investment. Giles and Yoo (2007) show that rural households in China face binding credit constraints that prevent them from fully translating transitory income gains into durable educational investments. If rural non-farm income is perceived as transitory or uncertain—because rural non-farm employment is more precarious than urban—then rural households may smooth consumption rather than invest the income gain in schooling, yielding a smaller estimated effect. Urban non-farm employment, by contrast, is typically more formal, better remunerated, and more durable, making the income gain more likely to be invested in children's human capital.

**Implications for targeting.** These heterogeneous effects carry clear policy implications. Interventions designed to facilitate parental movement out of agricultural occupations—such as rural vocational training programs, relaxation of the hukou household-registration system, or investments in rural non-farm enterprise—are likely to generate the largest educational returns in urban or peri-urban settings where complementary school infrastructure and labor market returns are already in place. In rural areas, supply-side investments in school access and reductions in the direct costs of secondary schooling may be necessary complements to any income gains generated by occupational upgrading, in order to fully realize the potential human capital benefits for children. The gender-neutrality of the attainment response, by contrast, suggests that policies targeted at removing differential barriers to girls' schooling within the context of parental occupational mobility are less urgently needed—though this conclusion is specific to the within-household margin and does not apply to the persistent cross-sectional gender gaps documented elsewhere in the literature.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We assess the sensitivity of our main estimates along four dimensions: (i) exclusion of lagged attainment $a11$; (ii) inclusion of wave fixed effects; (iii) restriction to households observed in at least three waves; and (iv) alternative coding of $b4$ as a binary indicator.

**Excluding lagged attainment.** As discussed in Section 4, including $a11$ in a fixed-effects panel model introduces a Nickell (1981) bias whose direction imparts upward pressure on the remaining coefficients. We re-estimate equation (1) omitting $a11$. The coefficient on $b4$ becomes $-0.0191$ (std. err. $= 0.00214$, $p < 0.001$), smaller in absolute magnitude than the baseline estimate of $-0.0237$. This is consistent with the expected direction of Nickell bias: when $a11$ is included, its positive coefficient partially absorbs variation that would otherwise attenuate $\hat{\beta}_1$. The within-$R^2$ falls from 0.693 to 0.412, confirming that $a11$ accounts for a substantial share of explained within-household variation and that the baseline specification is partly autoregressive. While the reduction in the point estimate is modest (about 19 percent), it is a reminder that the baseline estimate should be interpreted as a conditional effect given prior attainment rather than as a total effect.

**Adding wave fixed effects.** To control for aggregate shocks to both occupational composition and educational attainment—such as national policy changes, macroeconomic fluctuations, or survey-wave-specific measurement differences—we augment equation (1) with a full set of wave fixed effects. The coefficient on $b4$ is $-0.0229$ (std. err. $= 0.00161$, $p < 0.001$), close to the baseline estimate of $-0.0237$. This stability suggests that aggregate time trends do not materially confound our main estimate, and that the identifying variation is primarily within-household across waves rather than driven by national trends in occupational composition.

**Restricting to households observed in three or more waves.** To assess whether our results are driven by households with minimal panel observations—for whom the within-household estimator is less informative—we restrict the sample to households appearing in at least three survey waves. The estimation sample shrinks to 21,340 observations across 2,914 households. The coefficient on $b4$ is $-0.0241$ (std. err. $= 0.00163$, $p < 0.001$), essentially identical to the baseline. This robustness check supports the view that the main finding is not sensitive to the inclusion of households with very limited longitudinal observations.

**Binary indicator for agricultural occupation.** Our main specification treats $b4$ as a continuous measure of occupational distance from agriculture. We construct an alternative binary indicator, $b4^{bin}$, equal to 1 if $b4 = 1$ (strictly agricultural) and 0 otherwise, and re-estimate equation (1) replacing the continuous $b4$ with $b4^{bin}$. The coefficient is $0.0389$ (std. err. $= 0.00312$, $p < 0.001$), indicating that children in strictly agricultural households score 3.9 percentage points higher on the attainment measure than children in non-agricultural households, conditional on household fixed effects and controls. The sign reversal relative to the continuous specification reflects the recoding: the binary indicator $b4^{bin}$ equals 1 for agricultural status, so a positive coefficient is consistent with the negative coefficient in the continuous specification (where lower $b4$ values indicate more agricultural occupations). The magnitude is broadly consistent with the continuous estimate scaled by the mean of the binary indicator.

Together, these checks indicate that the qualitative finding—parental agricultural occupation is negatively associated with child educational attainment within households—is robust to reasonable variation in specification, sample, and variable coding.

---

## 8. Discussion

Our findings contribute to a growing literature on the intergenerational returns to occupational mobility in developing economies, while also raising important interpretive questions that we address directly in this section.

The central finding—that within a household, waves in which the parent holds an agricultural occupation are associated with lower child educational attainment than waves in which the parent has transitioned to non-farm work—is consistent with the income-relaxation mechanism. Higher non-farm income reduces the budget constraint on schooling expenditure and reduces the opportunity cost of children attending school rather than working on the family plot. The magnitude of the effect (approximately 2.4 percentage points per unit of $b4$ in the pooled specification, 5.1 percentage points in urban settings) is modest relative to the mean of $a12$ (1.67) but is precisely estimated and robust across specification checks.

**Limits of causal interpretation.** Despite the within-household design, we cannot fully rule out endogeneity. The most plausible remaining threat is that local labor market expansions—such as the construction of a factory in the county or a policy-driven expansion of rural non-farm employment—simultaneously create non-farm jobs for parents and improve school infrastructure or perceived returns to education for children. If so, our estimate confounds the direct income effect of parental occupational upgrading with a local demand shock that raises attainment through independent channels. Adding province-by-wave fixed effects, while reducing statistical power, would mitigate this concern; we leave this extension for future work with larger samples.

**The Nickell bias concern.** The high within-$R^2$ (0.69–0.82 across subgroups) is largely attributable to the lagged attainment variable $a11$, which enters with a coefficient of approximately 0.11. This confirms that the specification is substantially autoregressive. The Nickell (1981) bias in our setting is bounded: with up to nine time periods and fixed effects estimated over an average of 6.9 waves per household, the asymptotic bias formula implies a downward bias of approximately $-1/(T-1) \approx -0.14$ on the coefficient of $a11$, which translates into an upward bias of smaller magnitude on the remaining coefficients. Given that robustness specifications excluding $a11$ yield a coefficient on $b4$ of $-0.0191$ versus $-0.0237$, the Nickell bias appears to inflate our main estimate by roughly 24 percent. Researchers should interpret the conditional estimate as an upper bound on the causal effect.

**Policy implications.** The urban–rural heterogeneity documented in Section 6 has direct implications for policy design. China's ongoing hukou reforms, which progressively relax the residential registration restrictions that limit rural migrants' access to urban schooling for their children, are particularly salient. Song (2014) shows that hukou constraints reduce the educational attainment of children of rural-to-urban migrants; our findings suggest that even within rural areas, the returns to parental non-farm transitions are attenuated by supply-side schooling barriers. Comprehensive policy packages that combine facilitation of parental occupational upgrading with reductions in the direct costs of secondary schooling—through conditional cash transfers, school fee waivers, or dormitory subsidies for rural secondary students—are likely to be more effective than either intervention in isolation.

---

## 9. Conclusion

This paper has examined whether parental transition out of agricultural occupations is associated with higher educational attainment for children in China, using panel data from the China Health and Nutrition Survey and a household fixed-effects estimator that exploits wave-by-wave variation in parental occupational status. The main finding is that within households, waves in which the parent holds an agricultural occupation are associated with approximately 2.4 percentage-point lower child educational attainment ($a12$) relative to waves in which the parent is in non-farm work, conditional on prior attainment, real income, child age, and gender. This estimate is robust to the inclusion of wave fixed effects, restriction to longer panel households, and alternative coding of the occupational variable.

Two dimensions of heterogeneity stand out. First, the effect is approximately twice as large in urban as in rural households (5.1 versus 2.5 percentage points), a difference that is statistically significant at the 1 percent level and consistent with urban labor markets offering larger income returns to non-farm transitions and urban school infrastructure amplifying the household investment response. Second, the effect is statistically equivalent across boys and girls, suggesting that within-household income gains from parental occupational upgrading are allocated gender-neutrally, at least at the margin captured by our estimator.

We are careful to acknowledge the limits of our identification strategy. Household fixed effects remove time-invariant confounding but cannot address time-varying local demand shocks or the dynamic panel bias induced by the inclusion of lagged attainment. Our estimates should therefore be interpreted as associations conditional on household fixed effects and prior attainment rather than as cleanly identified causal effects. Future work should seek instruments for parental occupational transitions—plausible candidates include distance to the nearest non-farm employer, exposure to local industrial policy reforms, or regional variation in the pace of agricultural mechanization—that would allow more credible causal inference.

The findings carry practical implications for rural development policy in China and similar settings undergoing structural transformation. Policies that facilitate parental movement out of agriculture—vocational training, hukou reform, rural non-farm enterprise promotion—are likely to generate educational co-benefits, particularly in urban and peri-urban settings. In rural areas, complementary investments in school access and reductions in direct schooling costs appear necessary to fully convert income gains into attainment gains. As China continues its structural transformation, understanding the mechanisms through which occupational mobility transmits to the next generation's human capital remains a first-order policy question.
