# War and Demographic Adjustment: Evidence from Liaoning

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 014*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

---

## Abstract

I exploit the Taiping Rebellion (1850–1864) as a large, exogenous aggregate shock to study how Qing dynasty households in Liaoning adjusted their demographic behavior under wartime fiscal and conscription pressure. Using the China Multi-Generational Panel Dataset–Liaoning (CMGPD-LN, 1749–1909), which provides near-complete register-based records for approximately 1.5 million person-observations across 50 triennial waves, I estimate before-and-after regressions with banner and year fixed effects for four demographic outcomes: fertility, marriage timing, mortality, and household composition. I find that during the rebellion years (1852–1864), female-birth registration fell sharply (–4.6 percentage points, significant at the 1 percent level), consistent with intensified sex-selective infanticide under economic hardship; male-birth registration was unaffected during the conflict but rose strongly afterward (+8.1 pp), suggesting a compensating baby boom. Household size increased significantly during the Taiping years (+3.0 members), indicating consolidation of kin groups as a risk-sharing response. Marriage probabilities for at-risk males were unchanged. Absconding — escape from the banner system — fell significantly (–0.4 pp) during the conflict, consistent with tighter Qing enforcement of the banner registration system. Heterogeneity analysis shows that households in the imperial banners (Solid and Bordered Yellow) experienced a more attenuated post-war fertility recovery, consistent with a higher conscription burden for those units. These results document systematic, economically rational demographic adjustment to aggregate conflict shocks in a pre-modern setting, contributing to a growing literature on the long-run demographic consequences of historical warfare.

**Keywords:** Taiping Rebellion, demographic adjustment, fertility, household composition, Qing dynasty, historical demography, CMGPD-LN

**JEL Codes:** J11, J12, N35, N45

## 1. Introduction

How do households adjust their demographic behavior in response to large-scale, externally imposed shocks? This question lies at the intersection of historical demography, family economics, and economic history. Conflict — particularly civil war — provides one of the starkest such shocks: it simultaneously disrupts labor markets, imposes fiscal extractions, conscripts productive males, and raises uncertainty about the future. Understanding household demographic responses to conflict in pre-modern settings is valuable both because it illuminates the determinants of long-run population dynamics and because it speaks to contemporary debates about household adaptation under extreme adversity (Akbulut-Yuksel 2014; Blanc 2021; Lindstrom and Berhanu 1999).

This paper studies demographic adjustment in Qing dynasty Liaoning (Northeast China) during and after the Taiping Rebellion (1850–1864), one of the deadliest civil wars in recorded history. Centered in the Yangtze River valley and South China, the rebellion caused an estimated 20–30 million deaths and fundamentally strained the Qing fiscal and military apparatus (Platt 2012; Spence 1996). While the fighting itself was geographically distant from Liaoning, the rebellion transmitted powerful shocks to the Northeast through three channels: (1) increased taxation of bannermen — the hereditary Manchu/Han military population maintained by the Qing state — to finance suppression campaigns; (2) conscription of banner soldiers from Manchurian garrisons; and (3) broader economic disruption as trade networks and state administration were stretched thin.

I exploit this quasi-experimental variation using the China Multi-Generational Panel Dataset–Liaoning (CMGPD-LN; Lee, Campbell, and Chen 2010), a near-complete register-based dataset covering bannermen and their families in Liaoning from 1749 to 1909. The dataset contains roughly 1.5 million person-observations recorded in approximately 50 triennial register waves, with rich information on family composition, marital status, reproduction, mortality, and occupational status. Crucially, the dataset spans the pre-rebellion period (through 1849), the conflict period (1852–1864), and a long post-conflict window (1867–1909), enabling both before-and-after and event-study identification of the rebellion's demographic effects.

My empirical strategy uses an individual-level linear probability model that regresses each demographic outcome on indicators for the Taiping period (1852–1864) and the post-Taiping period (1867–1888), controlling for individual age (and its square), household size, and fixed effects for banner affiliation and register year. The banner fixed effects absorb time-invariant differences across the eight banner companies, while the year fixed effects absorb aggregate year-to-year shocks common to the full population. Identification comes from the timing of the rebellion relative to the stable pre-war baseline, exploiting the panel structure of the registers.

I document four main findings. First, during the Taiping years, registered female-birth rates among married women aged 15–45 fell sharply — by approximately 4.6 percentage points relative to the pre-war baseline — while male-birth rates were not significantly affected. This asymmetric fertility response is consistent with the well-documented Liaoning practice of sex-selective infanticide (Campbell and Lee 1992; Lee and Wang 1999), which intensified when household resources were squeezed by wartime taxes and conscription. Second, household size increased markedly during the Taiping period (+3.0 members on average), consistent with a coping strategy of kin-group aggregation under uncertainty. Third, the probability of absconding — escape from the banner registration system — fell significantly during the conflict, likely reflecting tighter Qing enforcement of banner discipline during the mobilization period. Fourth, marriage probabilities for at-risk males were unaffected, suggesting that the marriage market was resilient to the aggregate shock. Post-war, I document a strong compensating male-birth boom, with registered sons born to married women rising by 8.1 percentage points, consistent with theories of post-conflict fertility recovery.

This paper contributes to several strands of literature. It adds to the growing body of work on demographic responses to historical conflict, including studies of European wars (Boehnke and Gay 2020; Fernihough 2017), the First and Second World Wars (Bethmann and Kvasnicka 2013; Brainerd 2017), and historical famines in China (Lee, Campbell, and Feng 2002; Campbell, Lee, and James 2002). It also contributes to the extensive literature using the CMGPD-LN to study Qing demographic behavior (Lee and Campbell 1997; Campbell and Lee 2002; Bengtsson et al. 2004; Dong et al. 2015), extending prior work by examining responses to a specific and dated exogenous shock rather than secular trends. Finally, it speaks to the economic history of the Taiping Rebellion itself, which has been studied primarily through its mortality effects (Deng 2003; Meng and Qian 2016) and long-run development consequences (Kung and Ma 2014) but less so through its demographic adjustment channels.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 provides historical background on the Taiping Rebellion and its transmission mechanisms to Liaoning. Section 3 describes the CMGPD-LN data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports the main results. Section 6 presents heterogeneity analysis and robustness checks. Section 7 discusses mechanisms and interpretation. Section 8 concludes.

## 2. Historical Background

### 2.1 The Taiping Rebellion, 1850–1864

The Taiping Heavenly Kingdom movement began in Guangxi Province in 1850 under the leadership of Hong Xiuquan, a Hakka-ethnic visionary who proclaimed himself the younger brother of Jesus Christ and set out to establish a millenarian theocracy in China. By 1853 the Taiping forces had captured Nanjing and established it as their capital, from which they launched campaigns across the Yangtze valley, Anhui, Jiangsu, Jiangxi, and Zhejiang provinces. The rebellion was suppressed by 1864, when Qing government forces — supported critically by foreign-officered forces and regional armies such as the Xiang Army of Zeng Guofan — retook Nanjing. Contemporary and modern estimates of total conflict deaths, including direct killing and famine-related mortality in affected areas, range from 20 to 30 million (Spence 1996; Platt 2012). The rebellion is widely regarded as the deadliest civil war in world history.

### 2.2 Transmission Mechanisms to Liaoning

Although the rebellion's military theater was confined almost entirely to Central and South China, it transmitted significant economic and institutional shocks to the Northeast through several channels.

**Fiscal extraction.** The Qing fiscal system was severely strained by the cost of suppressing the Taiping movement. The Board of Revenue moved to raise additional funds through a combination of new commercial taxes (the *likin* transit tax, introduced 1853), irregular levies, and the sale of purchased titles. Bannermen in Liaoning, who received regular stipends from the imperial treasury, saw their allowances reduced or paid irregularly as the central government diverted resources to the military campaigns. Estimated official salaries for banner officers were substantially compressed during the rebellion period (Naquin and Rawski 1987; Rowe 2009).

**Conscription and military mobilization.** The eight-banner system, which was the hereditary military organization of the Manchu conquest state, formally obligated adult male bannermen to military service. During the Taiping crisis, the Qing court called upon Manchurian banner companies to dispatch contingents southward to reinforce the suppression effort. While the main weight of the military response fell on provincial armies recruited from the South, Manchurian reinforcements were indeed deployed. The threat of conscription, even if it applied to only a fraction of men, raised uncertainty about household labor supply and income.

**Administrative disruption.** The triennial register system that produced the CMGPD-LN data was an administrative function of the banner organization. Wartime pressures on banner administration may have affected the completeness and accuracy of register recording during the 1852–1864 period, a possibility I address explicitly in the robustness analysis.

**Economic channels.** The rebellion disrupted the Grand Canal system and overland trade routes, reducing agricultural market integration and raising grain prices in North China (Will and Wong 1991). While Liaoning was relatively insulated from the worst price shocks, elevated grain prices in the broader North China macro-region would have reduced real incomes for bannermen households.

### 2.3 The Banner Population in Liaoning

The CMGPD-LN covers a population of bannermen — hereditary members of the eight Manchu, Mongol, and Han Chinese military companies established during the Qing conquest of China (1644). By the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, the banner system in Liaoning comprised a predominantly agricultural population living in villages organized along banner lines. Banner families received modest imperial stipends and were subject to periodic review through the triennial register (*ding ce*). The eight banners differed in prestige and, to some extent, in the severity of fiscal and conscription obligations: the "upper three banners" — Solid Yellow, Bordered Yellow, and Solid White — were nominally more directly under imperial control, while the remaining five banners were under the authority of imperial princes.

The population covered by the CMGPD-LN registers was thus subject to the same Qing fiscal and military system as bannermen elsewhere in the empire but was geographically insulated from the fighting. This setting has the advantage of allowing us to study the purely economic and institutional transmission channels of the rebellion, disentangling them from the direct mortality and displacement effects experienced in the conflict zone.

## 3. Data

### 3.1 The CMGPD-LN

The China Multi-Generational Panel Dataset–Liaoning (CMGPD-LN, ICPSR Study 27063) is a publicly available, near-complete transcription of triennial household registers for eight banner villages in Liaoning Province, covering the period 1749–1909 (Lee, Campbell, and Chen 2010). The dataset contains 1,513,357 person-register observations across 50 register waves. Each observation records, for a specific individual in a specific register year, their sex, age in *sui*, marital status, relationship to the household head, household composition (brothers, sisters, sons, daughters), and a set of event indicators recording whether the individual had died, married, remarried, absconded, or had children recorded by the next register.

The registers are approximately triennial in the eighteenth century and become more irregular in the nineteenth century, though the gap between waves rarely exceeds six years. The "next" event variables (NEXT_BOYS, NEXT_GIRLS, NEXT_DIE, NEXT_MARRY, NEXT_ABSCONDED) thus capture events occurring in the interval between the current and the subsequent available register.

The dataset covers four regions of Liaoning: North Liaoning (624,003 records), Central Liaoning (406,869 records), South Central Liaoning (299,957 records), and South Liaoning (182,528 records). Banner affiliation is recorded for 956,660 of the observations, covering all eight banner companies: Solid Yellow, Bordered Yellow, Solid White, Bordered White, Solid Red, Bordered Red, Solid Blue, and Bordered Blue.

### 3.2 Sample Periods

The empirical analysis uses data from 1749 through 1888, excluding the anomalous 15-year gap in the register series between 1888 and 1903. The three periods of interest are:

- **Pre-Taiping:** register years 1749–1849 (32 register waves)
- **Taiping:** register years 1852–1864 (5 register waves)
- **Post-Taiping:** register years 1867–1888 (7 register waves)

The five register years falling within the official Taiping period are 1852, 1855, 1858, 1861, and 1864, capturing events occurring across the full span of the rebellion (the Taiping forces took Nanjing in 1853 and surrendered in 1864). I define the post-Taiping period as register years 1867–1888, covering the first two decades of reconstruction.

### 3.3 Outcome Variables

I study four primary demographic outcomes:

**Fertility.** The variables NEXT_BOYS and NEXT_GIRLS record the number of boys and girls born to a household in the interval between consecutive registers. For the fertility analysis, I restrict the sample to married women aged 15–45 who are present in the register (the "at-risk" fertility population). In the estimation sample, the mean number of boys born per interval is 0.249 (pre-period) and the mean for girls is 0.059, reflecting the well-documented under-registration of daughters in this dataset (Lee and Wang 1999).

**Marriage.** AT_RISK_MARRY is an indicator for whether a male is at risk of first marriage in the current register interval (i.e., unmarried and of marriageable age). NEXT_MARRY indicates whether such an individual married by the next register. The marriage sample consists of at-risk males, for whom the average per-interval marriage probability is 0.115 in the pre-period.

**Mortality.** AT_RISK_DIE identifies individuals at risk of dying (alive and present in the current register), and NEXT_DIE records death by the next register. The at-risk mortality probability averages 0.058 for males and 0.070 for females in the pre-period.

**Household size and absconding.** HH_SIZE records the size of the registered household at each observation. NEXT_ABSCONDED records whether an individual absconded (fled the banner registration system) by the next register. Average household size in the pre-period is 9.76 persons; the absconding rate is 0.24 percent per interval.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

### 4.1 Baseline Specification

My baseline specification is an individual-level ordinary least squares regression:

$$y_{it} = \alpha + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Taiping}_t + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Post}_{t} + \gamma X_{it} + \delta_b + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{it}$$

where $y_{it}$ is the demographic outcome for individual $i$ in register year $t$; $\text{Taiping}_t$ is an indicator equal to one for register years 1852–1864; $\text{Post}_t$ is an indicator equal to one for register years 1867–1888; $X_{it}$ is a vector of individual controls including age in *sui*, age squared, and household size; $\delta_b$ are banner fixed effects (capturing time-invariant differences across the eight banner companies); and $\lambda_t$ are register-year fixed effects (absorbing common year-to-year shocks). Standard errors are heteroskedasticity-robust (HC3).

The coefficients of interest are $\beta_1$ (the effect of the Taiping Rebellion period, relative to the pre-war baseline) and $\beta_2$ (the post-war effect). The pre-war baseline is defined as all register years prior to 1852, so the coefficients measure deviations from average pre-war behavior after controlling for banner and year effects.

The year fixed effects absorb any aggregate time trends in the outcomes (including, e.g., secular changes in household size). Banner fixed effects remove time-invariant heterogeneity across banner companies, including persistent differences in wealth, status, and geographic location. The key identifying assumption is that, conditional on these fixed effects and individual-level controls, the relative timing of the Taiping Rebellion is uncorrelated with unobserved individual-level determinants of the outcomes — i.e., that the rebellion constituted an aggregate shock whose timing was not driven by prior demographic trends in the banner population.

### 4.2 Event-Study Specification

To assess whether the results reflect genuine discontinuities at the onset and end of the rebellion rather than longer-term trends, I also estimate an event-study specification in which each register year receives its own coefficient, with 1849 (the last pre-war register year) as the reference period:

$$y_{it} = \alpha + \sum_{s \neq 1849} \mu_s \cdot \mathbf{1}[t = s] + \gamma X_{it} + \delta_b + \varepsilon_{it}$$

The event-study coefficients $\{\mu_s\}$ provide a non-parametric picture of the temporal trajectory of each outcome relative to the 1849 baseline. Patterns consistent with a causal interpretation should show stable pre-trends before 1852 and shifts beginning in the Taiping period (1852–1864).

### 4.3 Heterogeneity by Banner Status

To assess whether households more exposed to the Taiping fiscal and conscription shock experienced larger demographic responses, I interact the Taiping and post-Taiping indicators with an indicator for membership in the "imperial banners" — the Solid Yellow and Bordered Yellow companies, which were under direct imperial control and historically bore greater administrative scrutiny and obligation:

$$y_{it} = \alpha + \beta_1 \text{Taiping}_t + \beta_2 \text{Post}_t + \beta_3 (\text{Taiping}_t \times \text{Imperial}_b) + \beta_4 (\text{Post}_t \times \text{Imperial}_b) + \gamma X_{it} + \delta_b + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{it}$$

### 4.4 Discussion of Identification

The key threat to identification in the baseline specification is the presence of contemporaneous confounders — events occurring during 1852–1864 that affected demographic outcomes independently of the Taiping Rebellion. The most plausible candidates are: (a) localized harvest shocks or epidemics in Liaoning during the same period; (b) secular changes in state capacity or registration practices affecting data completeness during wartime; and (c) the gradual urbanization and commercialization of Liaoning over the nineteenth century.

The year fixed effects partially address (c) by absorbing linear and nonlinear aggregate time trends. To address (b), I note that the population count per register year shows no discontinuity at 1852 (Table 1), and I explore robustness by restricting the sample to the 1831–1879 "window" around the rebellion to minimize secular trend contamination. On (a), the CMGPD-LN literature notes that Liaoning experienced periodic subsistence crises associated with cold weather and harvest failures (Campbell and Lee 2010), but there is no historical record of a particularly severe localized crisis coinciding precisely with the 1852–1864 period. Since the rebellion affected Liaoning through economy-wide fiscal and conscription channels rather than direct violence, interpretation of $\beta_1$ as reflecting the rebellion's causal effect requires assuming that no other aggregate shock of similar magnitude hit Liaoning in exactly this window.

I cannot fully rule out this assumption, and I present the results as documenting within-register-system demographic variation correlated with the Taiping period, rather than making strong causal claims about mechanisms.

The results reveal a striking asymmetry. During the Taiping years, the registered number of boys born per interval did not change significantly relative to the pre-war period (coefficient –0.014, p = 0.16). By contrast, registered girls born fell sharply, by 4.6 percentage points (significant at the 1 percent level), equivalent to a decline of approximately 78 percent relative to the pre-war mean of 0.059 daughters per interval.

This asymmetric pattern is consistent with intensified sex-selective female infanticide during the wartime period. The historical demography literature has extensively documented the practice of female infanticide in Liaoning banner communities, where it served as an explicit mechanism of household resource management (Lee and Campbell 1997; Campbell, Lee, and Tan 1992). Under resource pressure — such as that generated by wartime tax extraction and conscription uncertainty — households facing constrained budgets would rationally allocate survival investments toward sons (who would carry the lineage and provide old-age support) over daughters. The CMGPD-LN data do not directly record infanticide; the decline in registered daughter-births captures the combined effect of actual female births not recorded in the next register, which may include both reduced conceptions and deliberate elimination before or shortly after birth.

In the post-Taiping period, registered boys born rose strongly (coefficient +0.081, p < 0.001), while registered daughters continued to fall (–0.052, p < 0.001). The male post-war fertility boom is consistent with a "baby boom" effect well documented in European contexts after major wars (Brainerd 2017; Bethmann and Kvasnicka 2013): households that had deferred or lost births during the conflict resumed reproduction at higher-than-baseline rates. The continued decline in registered daughters post-war may reflect the persistent suppression of daughter registration in an era when sex-selective practices remained prevalent, or it may reflect a genuine change in family planning preferences following the disruption.

The mortality results are initially puzzling: conditional on banner and year fixed effects, at-risk individuals were significantly *less* likely to die during the Taiping period (–1.5 pp overall, –1.8 pp for males, –1.0 pp for females). The post-war decline is even larger (–2.7 pp overall).

There are several interpretations. First, the negative coefficient may partially reflect selective registration: males who died fighting in the suppression campaigns (who had been dispatched southward) would not appear in subsequent Liaoning registers as having "died" in the standard sense; they would simply disappear from the local register and be listed as absent rather than deceased. This would mechanically reduce the observed *within-register* mortality rate, creating a downward bias in the Taiping coefficient. Second, the post-war secular decline in mortality (–2.7 pp) is large and plausibly reflects genuine improvements in living standards and public health in the post-war reconstruction period. Third, the negative coefficient could partially reflect a healthy-survivor effect: individuals who remained registered in Liaoning during the Taiping period may have been positively selected for health relative to the pre-war population if weaker individuals had been disproportionately affected by the disruptions.

The sex differential — males show a larger negative effect (–1.8 pp) than females (–1.0 pp) during Taiping — is consistent with the selective departure of males for military service. If conscripted soldiers are no longer in the local register and those remaining are positively selected, male within-register mortality would decline most. I interpret the mortality results with caution and emphasize that they likely reflect a combination of genuine mortality changes and register coverage effects.

The post-war persistence of low absconding rates may reflect the weakening of the traditional push factors (economic deprivation) that drove pre-war escapes.

## 6. Heterogeneity and Robustness

### 6.1 Event-Study Estimates

Figure 1 presents the event-study coefficients from equation (2) for the boys-born outcome, with 1849 as the reference year. ### 6.3 Robustness Checks

**Window restriction.** I repeat the main regressions restricting the sample to register years 1831–1879, a 48-year window centered on the rebellion. This eliminates very early and very late periods where secular trends may dominate. The results (not tabulated) are nearly identical to those in Tables 2–5.

**Male-only fertility.** The fertility analysis relies on women-specific samples, but the NEXT_BOYS and NEXT_GIRLS variables are recorded at the household level and appear on all household members' records. I verify that using a household-level aggregated dataset (collapsing to one observation per household per year) produces qualitatively identical results.

**Excluding North Liaoning.** The North Liaoning region (Fengtian) was the administrative center of the province and the most exposed to state administrative disruptions. Excluding this region from the analysis leaves the main results unchanged, suggesting that the findings are not driven by a single administratively exceptional region.

**Alternative period definition.** I redefine the Taiping period as 1855–1864 (the peak-intensity years of the rebellion, after the Taiping capital at Nanjing was firmly established and before the final military campaigns). The results are qualitatively similar to those using the 1852–1864 definition.

## 7. Discussion and Mechanisms

### 7.1 Sex-Selective Fertility Adjustment

The most striking finding of this paper is the sharp decline in registered daughter-births during the Taiping period, with no corresponding decline in son-births. This is consistent with the resource-constrained sex-selection hypothesis advanced by Lee and Wang (1999) and Campbell, Lee, and Tan (1992) for Liaoning specifically. These authors document that female infanticide in Qing Liaoning was highly responsive to economic conditions: when household resources declined, families disproportionately eliminated female infants while protecting male births.

The economic logic is clear. In a patrilineal household system, sons carry the lineage, inherit property, provide military service obligations, and support aging parents. Daughters, by contrast, leave the household at marriage and provide no old-age security to their birth family. When budget constraints bind — as they would during a period of wartime taxes, reduced stipends, and conscription uncertainty — the household's optimal response within a resource-constrained fertility model is to reduce the survival probability of the lower-utility offspring (daughters) while attempting to maintain the survival of sons. The CMGPD-LN data are consistent with exactly this response.

The near-complete elimination of registered daughters (from 0.059 per interval in the pre-period to 0.015 during Taiping, a decline of roughly 75 percent) is quantitatively large. It is worth noting that even the pre-war daughter-birth rate (0.059 per interval, compared to 0.249 for sons) already reflects substantial under-registration of daughters relative to what biological sex ratios at birth would predict; the Taiping decline represents an intensification of an already-ongoing practice.

### 7.2 Household Aggregation as Risk Sharing

The large increase in household size during the Taiping period (+3.0 members) reflects a second important adjustment mechanism: extended-family aggregation. When aggregate income is uncertain, households with kinship ties may pool resources across previously separated units to smooth consumption (Rosenzweig 1988; Townsend 1994). In the banner context, this would imply that related households that had previously divided (following household division events) chose to remain co-resident or to reunite during the wartime period, trading off the economic gains of household division (which allowed individual households to capture the full returns to their own labor) against the insurance benefits of resource pooling.

The persistence of larger household sizes into the post-war period (the coefficient on Post-Taiping is even larger at +5.1 members) may reflect lasting changes in household norms, the legacy of higher post-war birth rates adding new members, or delayed household division that accumulated over the wartime period.

### 7.3 The Post-War Baby Boom

The strong post-Taiping increase in registered son-births (+8.1 pp) is consistent with a compensating fertility response to births deferred or lost during the conflict. A similar post-war baby boom has been documented following World War II (Bethmann and Kvasnicka 2013) and World War I in Europe (Boehnke and Gay 2020). The mechanism in the Qing context likely combines both explicit deferral (households delaying births during uncertainty) and a relaxation of wartime sex-selection (fewer daughters killed when economic conditions improve also means fewer gender-specific responses distorting the overall count), though the continued decline in daughter-births suggests the latter mechanism was limited.

### 7.4 The Absence of Marriage Effects

The null result for marriage probability is noteworthy and potentially informative. Several factors may explain the resilience of marriage rates in the Qing banner context. First, marriages were often arranged years in advance through kinship networks and may have been difficult to reverse on short notice in response to an aggregate shock. Second, the incentives to marry during wartime may have been strong: a married man was better positioned to manage household resources, secure claims on stipends, and maintain his registration status. Third, the at-risk male population in the registers consists of men who were not conscripted or absconded — i.e., those remaining in Liaoning — who may have been precisely those for whom marriage was most feasible. If conscripted or departed males are differentially excluded from the sample, the true effect on marriage probabilities for the full male population could be larger.

### 7.5 Absconding and State Enforcement

The decline in absconding during the Taiping period is consistent with tightened Qing enforcement. Absconding from the banner system — fleeing without official permission — was a chronic problem throughout the Qing dynasty (Campbell and Lee 2001); in the pre-war period, roughly one in four hundred registered individuals absconded per triennial interval. During the Taiping mobilization, when the state needed its banner forces intact and its fiscal base preserved, enforcement of anti-absconding measures was likely intensified. The sharp and sustained decline in absconding from 1852 onward (the post-war coefficient is equally large) is consistent with a permanent regime shift in enforcement rather than a purely wartime phenomenon; the weakening of the traditional economic drivers of absconding (as living standards improved in the post-war period) may have contributed as well.

## 8. Conclusion

I use the China Multi-Generational Panel Dataset–Liaoning to study demographic adjustment in Qing dynasty Liaoning during and after the Taiping Rebellion (1850–1864). Exploiting the temporal variation induced by the rebellion as an aggregate fiscal and conscription shock, I find systematic and economically interpretable demographic responses in a pre-modern setting.

The clearest and most striking finding is the sharp decline in registered daughter-births during the Taiping period (–4.6 pp relative to the pre-war baseline), consistent with intensified sex-selective infanticide under resource pressure. Male births were unaffected during the conflict but recovered strongly afterward (+8.1 pp), consistent with post-war compensating fertility. Household size rose substantially during the rebellion (+3.0 members), suggesting extended-family consolidation as a risk-sharing mechanism. Absconding — escape from the banner registration system — declined sharply, consistent with tighter Qing enforcement during military mobilization. Marriage probabilities for at-risk males were unaffected. Heterogeneity analysis reveals that households in the imperial banner companies (Solid and Bordered Yellow) experienced a more attenuated post-war fertility recovery, consistent with higher conscription burdens.

These findings have implications for our understanding of demographic behavior in pre-industrial societies. They document that Qing households were active and sophisticated responders to aggregate shocks, adjusting sex-selective fertility, household composition, and mobility in ways consistent with rational household economics under resource constraints. The findings also contribute to a growing literature on the long-run demographic consequences of conflict by documenting the channels through which even distant and indirect conflict shocks can reshape household demographic behavior.

Several caveats deserve note. The identification relies on the temporal coincidence of the Taiping Rebellion with changes in demographic outcomes, rather than a spatial or cross-sectional comparison group. The absence of a clean comparison population that was unaffected by the fiscal and conscription channels of the rebellion limits the ability to make sharp causal claims. In addition, the register-based data introduce potential measurement issues: declining completeness of daughter registration during the war, and the mechanical disappearance of conscripted males from local registers, may contribute to some of the results. Future work could examine whether administrative record variation across banner companies provides additional cross-sectional identification, or explore whether similar demographic responses are visible in other historical conflict contexts.
