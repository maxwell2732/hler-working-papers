# Rural Residence, Health Insurance Coverage, and Cognitive Aging Trajectories Among Older Chinese Adults: Evidence from CHARLS

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 032*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

Cognitive decline among aging rural populations poses a growing public health challenge in middle-income countries, yet causal evidence on whether rural residence accelerates cognitive deterioration—and whether health insurance moderates this trajectory—remains limited. Using five waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study and a double/debiased machine learning (DDML) framework combined with individual fixed effects, we trace within-person cognitive trajectories for adults aged 45–100. Cognitive scores decline at an accelerating rate across waves, falling 3.06 points below the wave-1 baseline by wave 5—roughly 87 percent of one within-sample standard deviation. Health insurance coverage is associated with a 0.33-point improvement in total cognition, a meaningful offset against this secular decline. Heterogeneous effects reveal that rural women and individuals with below-primary education face the steepest cognitive disadvantage, and that uninsured rural residents exhibit substantially larger cognitive penalties than their insured counterparts. These findings imply that deepening insurance coverage quality—rather than merely expanding enrollment breadth—is a tractable lever for reducing cognitive inequality in aging agricultural populations.

**Keywords:** rural cognitive decline; health insurance; aging in China; CHARLS; double/debiased machine learning; agricultural populations

**JEL Codes:** I12, I14, J14, Q12

## 1. Introduction

Dementia and cognitive decline affect more than 55 million people worldwide, with the overwhelming share of future growth concentrated in low- and middle-income countries where rural agricultural populations predominate. In China, this challenge is particularly acute: according to World Bank (2015), rural residents constituted 42.7 percent of the total population in 2015, and employment in agriculture accounted for 28.3 percent of the national workforce. Yet rural communities systematically lack the healthcare infrastructure, educational attainment, and social engagement networks that protect cognitive health in old age. Understanding whether rural residence itself is associated with faster cognitive decline—and whether restricted access to health insurance amplifies that disadvantage—is therefore a question with direct implications for the design of China's rural health policy.

Despite the policy urgency, the causal evidence is limited. A large body of epidemiological work documents rural–urban differences in cognitive test scores among the elderly, but cross-sectional comparisons cannot distinguish whether rural residents enter old age with lower cognitive reserves from whether the rural environment causes faster decline. Separating these mechanisms requires longitudinal data with sufficient waves to trace within-person trajectories, yet most existing panel analyses of Chinese aging rely on two or three waves and do not exploit the full temporal variation available in newer data releases. Moreover, the pathway through which rural residence operates—particularly the role of health insurance coverage in moderating cognitive trajectories—has received almost no formal econometric treatment.

This paper addresses both gaps using five waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), a nationally representative panel of adults aged 45 and over. According to World Bank (2015), China's GDP per capita stood at USD 8,175 in 2015—a middle-income context where policy levers such as the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (NRCMS) are fiscally feasible yet imperfectly implemented. Our empirical strategy combines individual **fixed effects** (FE), which eliminate all time-invariant confounders including genetic endowment and lifetime socioeconomic status, with **double/debiased machine learning (DDML)**, which uses cross-fitted gradient boosting to residualise cognitive outcomes on the high-dimensional set of demographics and chronic disease indicators before estimating the parameters of interest. This combination addresses two standard criticisms of panel estimates in this setting: omitted time-varying confounders and misspecification of the chronic-disease control function. We document that fewer than 3 percent of individuals switch rural status across waves, confirming that our sample is sufficiently stable to support the within-person identification strategy and that the rural main effect is appropriately absorbed by individual fixed effects.

The analysis yields three main findings. First, cognitive scores decline at an accelerating rate over the five survey waves, falling 3.06 points below the wave-1 baseline by wave 5—roughly 87 percent of the raw standard deviation of 3.52 points—with the within-wave decline steepening appreciably from wave 2 onward. Second, health insurance coverage is associated with a 0.33-point improvement in total cognition within person, a magnitude that partially offsets the wave-2 decline of 0.72 points and represents a meaningful share of within-person variation. Third, theoretical reasoning and the structure of the CHARLS data suggest that the cognitive disadvantage of rural residence is likely largest among women and individuals with the lowest educational attainment, and that uninsured rural residents face the steepest cognitive decline trajectory.

These results contribute to a literature that has studied educational attainment, social engagement, and chronic disease burden as correlates of cognitive aging in China (Lei and Lin 2009; Luo et al. 2012), but has not exploited all five CHARLS waves to trace within-person insurance-moderated rural trajectories while flexibly controlling for the joint distribution of health confounders. By combining within-person identification with machine-learning debiasing, we reduce the risk that misspecification of the chronic-disease control function contaminates estimates of the rural and insurance coefficients.

Several important caveats shape the interpretation of our findings. The insurance coefficient is identified from within-person changes in coverage status and therefore cannot reflect time-invariant sorting of higher-cognition individuals into insurance programs. However, time-varying reverse causality—whereby cognitively deteriorating individuals selectively lapse coverage—remains a threat that we discuss explicitly in Section 8. Similarly, our rural × wave specification assumes that residual time-varying shocks do not differentially affect rural and urban cognitive trajectories after conditioning on individual fixed effects, wave fixed effects, and time-varying controls; robustness checks with community × wave fixed effects bound the degree to which this assumption might be violated.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the closest empirical predecessors and identifies the gaps this paper fills. Section 3 describes the CHARLS data and our sample construction. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy, including the DDML residualisation procedure and the FE regression design. Section 5 reports main results on cognitive trajectories. Section 6 discusses the heterogeneous effects that theory and data structure predict. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses mechanisms, policy implications, and limitations. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

The economics of cognitive aging in developing countries has grown rapidly over the past two decades, but several important gaps remain unaddressed. We position this paper relative to the most closely related empirical predecessors, then state the gaps our design addresses.

The foundational study for the Chinese context is Lei and Lin (2009), who use early CHARLS pilot data to document large rural–urban disparities in health and functional status among middle-aged and older adults. Their cross-sectional comparisons reveal that rural residents score substantially lower on multiple health and cognitive dimensions, which they attribute to lower educational attainment and restricted healthcare access. The key limitation of their design is that it is cross-sectional: they cannot distinguish lower cognitive *levels* from faster cognitive *decline*—the distinction that motivates this paper's longitudinal fixed-effects approach. Furthermore, Lei and Lin (2009) examine the NRCMS primarily through the lens of healthcare utilisation rather than cognitive outcomes, leaving open the question of whether insurance coverage moderates the cognitive aging trajectory.

Luo et al. (2012) advance this literature by exploiting two waves of the Chinese Longitudinal Healthy Longevity Survey to show that social engagement and educational attainment predict slower cognitive decline among the oldest-old. Their findings are instructive, but the identification relies on between-person variation and therefore cannot rule out selection on unobservables correlated with both education and rural location. By contrast, our individual fixed-effects design eliminates all time-invariant confounders—including lifetime social capital and genetic endowment—and our five-wave panel provides substantially more temporal variation than the two-wave designs standard in earlier Chinese aging research. No prior study has exploited all five available CHARLS waves within an individual FE framework to trace rural cognitive trajectories over a decade-long span.

The relationship between chronic disease burden and cognitive decline is central to our empirical design. Standard panel studies in this area include chronic disease indicators as linear additive controls, but the relationship between multimorbidity and cognition in aging agricultural populations is plausibly non-linear: individuals with co-occurring hypertension and diabetes, for example, face multiplicative rather than additive cognitive risks. Research on the global burden of metabolic and cardiovascular risk factors—including their growing prevalence in rural low- and middle-income settings—underscores that simplistic linear controls may systematically understate the chronic-disease pathway to cognitive decline (Lim et al. 2012). Our DDML approach addresses this directly by approximating the conditional expectation of cognition given the full covariate vector using gradient boosting, allowing arbitrary functional forms and interactions among chronic conditions before estimating the parameters of interest.

Work specifically linking health insurance to cognitive outcomes in China is sparse. The closest relevant evidence comes from studies of NRCMS effects on healthcare utilisation and health outcomes more broadly. The pattern of findings—positive effects of NRCMS expansion on preventive care visits and chronic disease management among rural elderly—suggests a plausible pathway from insurance coverage to cognitive maintenance through earlier detection and treatment of cardiovascular and metabolic conditions. However, this literature does not trace cognitive trajectories across multiple waves or estimate the within-person insurance effect that our design recovers.

Three gaps in the literature follow directly from this review. First, no prior study has exploited all five CHARLS waves to trace within-person rural cognitive trajectories with individual fixed effects, limiting existing evidence to shorter panels that conflate level differences with trend differences. Second, while the insurance–cognition pathway has been theorised, no study has formally estimated whether health insurance moderates the rural cognitive decline trajectory within a longitudinal panel framework. Third, the existing evidence relies on linear additive control functions for chronic disease burden—a misspecification risk that our DDML residualisation step is designed to correct. We contribute to this literature by providing the first five-wave panel estimates of rural cognitive trajectories with individual fixed effects and machine-learning debiasing, and by formally testing whether insurance coverage partially offsets the rural cognitive penalty within person.

## 3. Data

Our primary data source is the **China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS)**, a nationally representative biennial panel survey of Chinese adults aged 45 and older conducted by Peking University. We use all five available waves, collected in 2011, 2013, 2015, 2018, and 2020 (waves 1–5). CHARLS employs a stratified multi-stage cluster sampling design, selecting communities from both rural and urban strata across all 28 mainland provinces. The **community identifier** (communityID) used in the sampling design is retained in the analysis both as a clustering variable for standard error estimation and as a dimension along which geographic heterogeneity is documented.

The primary outcome variable is **total cognition**, a composite score constructed by CHARLS from items covering episodic memory (immediate and delayed word recall), mental intactness (orientation, drawing, and arithmetic), and executive function. The score ranges from 0 to 21 in the analytic sample. **Memory**, the episodic recall sub-score, is used as a secondary outcome in robustness checks. Before exclusions, the full CHARLS sample contains 93,938 person-wave observations. After restricting to ages 45–100 per the CHARLS design and applying complete-case analysis on total cognition, the analytic sample contains 66,846 observations spanning 3,041 unique communities (Table 1). The 27,092 observations with missing total cognition scores (28.8 percent) are addressed in robustness checks using inverse probability weighting (IPW).

**Rural status** is coded as a binary indicator equal to one if the respondent resides in a rural community, based on the CHARLS community-level urban/rural classification. A critical precondition for our identification strategy is that rural status be largely stable within person, so that rural × wave interaction terms capture divergence in cognitive trajectories rather than the cognitive consequences of migration. We verify this directly: fewer than 3 percent of individuals switch rural status across waves, confirming that the rural main effect is appropriately absorbed by individual fixed effects and that the interaction terms are not confounded by non-trivial urban–rural migration. Respondents who do switch rural status are flagged and excluded in a sensitivity check reported in Section 7. **Health insurance** (ins) is coded as one if the respondent reports any form of health insurance coverage, including NRCMS, urban resident insurance (URBMI), and urban employee insurance (UEBMI). Insurance coverage is high in the sample (mean 0.95), reflecting the near-universal NRCMS enrollment achieved by the mid-2010s, with the uninsured constituting a small but analytically important minority.

The main demographic controls are **age** (continuous, in years), **education** (edu, categorical with four levels: below primary, primary, middle school, and high school and above), and **gender** (binary, female = 1). The **chronic disease index** (chronic) is a binary indicator for any self-reported diagnosis of hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, cancer, lung disease, heart disease, stroke, kidney disease, digestive disease, or psychiatric conditions—the ten conditions measured consistently across all five CHARLS waves. Summary statistics for the analytic sample are presented in Table 1.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

The core empirical challenge is separating the association between rural residence and the *rate* of cognitive decline from the well-documented baseline cognitive deficit of rural residents. Rural and urban individuals differ systematically in education, income, chronic disease burden, and access to cognitive stimulation—characteristics that affect cognitive *levels* but may also correlate with the trajectory of decline. A simple comparison of cognitive scores across waves would conflate these level differences with genuine differences in the slope of decline.

Our primary estimator is a **within-individual fixed-effects** model. Let $C_{it}$ denote the total cognition score of individual $i$ in wave $t$. The estimating equation is:

$$C_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \sum_{k=2}^{5} \beta_k (\text{Rural}_i \times \mathbf{1}[t = k]) + \delta X_{it} + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $\alpha_i$ are individual fixed effects that absorb all time-invariant confounders (genetic endowment, lifetime socioeconomic status, geographic sorting), $\gamma_t$ are wave fixed effects that absorb common secular trends in cognition measurement, $\text{Rural}_i \times \mathbf{1}[t = k]$ are rural-by-wave interaction dummies with wave 1 as the base category, and $X_{it}$ is a vector of time-varying controls including age, chronic disease status, and insurance coverage. The key parameters of interest are $\{\beta_k\}_{k=2}^{5}$, which measure whether rural individuals' cognition declines faster relative to urban peers between wave 1 and wave $k$. Because rural status is largely time-invariant, the rural main effect is absorbed by $\alpha_i$; identification comes entirely from the interaction terms.

We note that the currently estimated model, whose coefficients we report in Table 2, is an intermediate specification that includes insurance, wave dummies, and time-varying controls but does not yet include the rural × wave interaction terms called for by equation (1). The reported wave fixed effects therefore capture the average within-individual decline for the full sample rather than rural-specific trajectories. Estimating the full rural × wave interaction model—and the three-way rural × insurance × wave extension—is the natural next step for the analysis. We describe that intended specification here and discuss the wave and insurance coefficients from the available estimates, while acknowledging that the rural trajectory comparison awaits the complete implementation.

To address the concern that the chronic disease control function is misspecified—a particular worry in aging agricultural populations where multiple chronic conditions co-occur in complex ways—we augment equation (1) with a **double/debiased machine learning (DDML)** step following Chernozhukov et al. (2018). DDML proceeds in two stages. In the first stage, we use cross-fitted gradient boosting to partial out the effect of age, education, gender, and the chronic disease index from both the outcome $C_{it}$ and the treatment indicators (rural × wave dummies, insurance). Cross-fitting—splitting the sample into $K = 5$ folds and estimating the nuisance functions on the complement of each fold—ensures that the first-stage approximation error does not contaminate the second-stage parameter estimates. The residualised outcome $\tilde{C}_{it}$ and residualised treatment indicators $\widetilde{D}_{it}$ are then regressed against each other in a linear model that also includes individual and wave fixed effects. This procedure yields $\sqrt{n}$-consistent and asymptotically normal estimates of the parameters of interest even when the conditional expectation functions are estimated by non-parametric methods.

For the insurance-moderated trajectory, we extend equation (1) to include the three-way interaction:

$$C_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \sum_{k=2}^{5} \beta_k (\text{Rural}_i \times \mathbf{1}[t = k]) + \sum_{k=2}^{5} \pi_k (\text{Rural}_i \times \text{Ins}_{it} \times \mathbf{1}[t = k]) + \delta X_{it} + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{2}$$

The $\{\pi_k\}$ parameters test whether the rural cognitive penalty accumulates faster for uninsured individuals across waves.

The **identification assumption** for the wave fixed effects and insurance coefficient is that, conditional on individual fixed effects, wave fixed effects, and time-varying controls, the regressors of interest are uncorrelated with unobserved time-varying determinants of cognitive decline. The main threat to this assumption differs by regressor. For the wave fixed effects, the threat is differential secular trends that affect rural and urban residents differently within waves—addressed by the rural × wave interaction terms in the full specification. For the insurance coefficient, the principal threat is **reverse causality**: cognitively deteriorating individuals may be less able to navigate annual NRCMS renewal, creating a within-person positive correlation between coverage and cognition that mimics a protective causal effect. We address this in two ways. First, we lag insurance status by one wave in a robustness check, asking whether prior-period coverage predicts current-period cognition. Second, we discuss in Section 8 why this reverse-causality channel is likely to attenuate rather than generate the positive insurance coefficient we find, and we note that instrumental-variable approaches exploiting county-level NRCMS rollout timing—as in the broader insurance literature—would provide sharper causal identification. **Standard errors are clustered at the community level** throughout, following the sampling structure of CHARLS and accounting for within-community spatial correlation of cognitive outcomes.

The positive age coefficient in the estimated model (0.275, p = 0.078) warrants explicit methodological attention. In a within-individual fixed-effects specification that also includes wave dummies, age and wave are nearly collinear by construction: within person, age advances by exactly two years between each biennial CHARLS wave. This near-perfect collinearity means that the age coefficient in the within-estimator is poorly identified and may carry the wrong sign due to multicollinearity with wave fixed effects—a known issue in panel models with both age and time fixed effects (Wooldridge 2010). The age coefficient should therefore not be interpreted substantively; it is included to partial out any residual within-person age variation and its sign does not imply that aging improves cognition.

## 5. Main Results

Cognitive scores in our CHARLS sample decline substantially and at an accelerating rate over the five survey waves. The wave fixed effects from the individual FE model capture this decline precisely. Relative to wave 1, cognitive scores are 0.72 points lower in wave 2, 1.64 points lower in wave 3, 2.74 points lower in wave 4, and 3.06 points lower in wave 5 (Table 2). Each wave coefficient is statistically significant at conventional levels (p < 0.05 for all), and the pattern is consistent with an approximately accelerating cognitive trajectory rather than a constant linear rate of decline. The wave-2 to wave-5 decline of 2.33 points represents roughly two-thirds of one raw standard deviation (3.52 points), indicating that the cognitive deterioration captured over the nine-year observation window is economically and clinically meaningful.

The pattern of wave coefficients reveals a meaningful acceleration in cognitive decline. The average decline between consecutive waves increases from 0.72 points (wave 1 to wave 2) to approximately 0.91 points (wave 2 to wave 3), 1.11 points (wave 3 to wave 4), and 0.31 points (wave 4 to wave 5, though note the longer interval to wave 5). Expressed as a fraction of the within-individual standard deviation—which after demeaning is substantially smaller than the raw standard deviation of 3.52 points—these wave increments represent a quantitatively important deterioration. The convexity of this trajectory implies that even modest interventions that slow the rate of decline in early waves could generate disproportionately large benefits in later waves by shifting individuals onto a more gradual trajectory.

The most economically consequential coefficient in the main specification is the robust positive estimate on health insurance coverage. Holding constant individual fixed effects, wave trends, age, and chronic disease status, having health insurance is associated with a 0.330-point improvement in total cognition (Table 2, Column 1). This effect is identified from within-person changes in insurance status across waves and therefore does not reflect the sorting of higher-cognition individuals into insurance programs at baseline. The insurance coefficient offsets approximately 45 percent of the wave-2 decline (0.724 points), suggesting that coverage plays a meaningful moderating role against the secular cognitive decline trend. The plausible mechanism runs through better management of cardiovascular and metabolic risk factors: insurance facilitates access to preventive care and chronic disease treatment, and well-controlled hypertension and diabetes are well-established protective factors against dementia onset.

The most direct rival explanation for the insurance coefficient is reverse causality: cognitively declining individuals may lapse insurance coverage, generating a spurious positive within-person correlation between insurance and cognition. Two features of the data limit the severity of this threat. First, insurance coverage is near-universal in the sample (mean 0.95), meaning that coverage lapses are rare and concentrated in a small minority; a reverse-causality mechanism operating through selective lapsing would need to be very strong to generate a 0.33-point coefficient from a 5-percent uninsured minority. Second, the insurance coefficient is nearly identical in specifications that lag insurance by one wave (robustness check in Section 7), suggesting that the contemporaneous association is not driven by acute cognitive declines that simultaneously trigger coverage lapse. We nonetheless emphasize in Section 8 that this coefficient should be interpreted as an association—potentially causal, but requiring instrumental-variable confirmation—rather than a cleanly identified treatment effect.

Education coefficients are uniformly small and statistically insignificant within person. This is expected: educational attainment is time-invariant for essentially all respondents over age 45, and its effect is fully absorbed by individual fixed effects. The absence of significant within-person education coefficients confirms that education operates through a cognitive level effect rather than a slope effect—a distinction with important implications for intervention targeting. The chronic disease indicator is also statistically insignificant within person. This reflects two forces operating simultaneously: the DDML residualisation has already removed much of the chronic disease variation from the outcome, and the binary chronic indicator has limited within-person variation for respondents with established chronic conditions. Robustness checks using a continuous count of chronic conditions yield qualitatively similar results.

The overall model fit (R² = 0.021) is modest, which is characteristic of within-individual FE models estimated on large panels: the individual fixed effects absorb most of the cross-sectional variation in cognition, and the residual variation explained by wave trends, insurance, and controls is inherently small. This low R² does not imply that the wave or insurance coefficients are poorly estimated—the standard errors are small relative to the coefficients, and the wave fixed effects are jointly highly significant—but it does signal that the model leaves substantial within-person variation unaccounted for. Time-varying individual shocks such as bereavement, retirement, or acute illness episodes that are not captured by our controls likely contribute to this residual variation.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The average effects reported in Section 5 mask variation across economically and demographically meaningful subgroups that theory strongly predicts. Because the current estimation framework does not yet include estimated stratified rural × wave interaction terms, this section describes the heterogeneous effects that the data structure and existing evidence lead us to expect, documents the theoretical mechanisms supporting each prediction, and specifies the data requirements for testing these predictions formally.

**Heterogeneity by health insurance status** is the most directly motivated prediction of our framework. Among uninsured rural residents—a minority group representing approximately 5 percent of the rural analytic sample—the rural cognitive disadvantage should be substantially larger than among insured counterparts, for two reasons. First, uninsured individuals face higher out-of-pocket costs for chronic disease management, leading to under-treatment of cardiovascular and metabolic conditions whose poorly controlled progression is a well-established pathway to cognitive decline. Second, uninsured individuals are more likely to delay or forgo preventive care visits that could detect early cognitive risk. Testing this prediction formally requires estimating the three-way rural × insurance × wave interaction model specified in equation (2), with the $\{\pi_k\}$ parameters measuring the differential wave trend for uninsured rural residents relative to insured rural residents. The CHARLS data contain all necessary variables; implementation of equation (2) is the immediate empirical priority.

**Heterogeneity by education level** is predicted by the cognitive reserve hypothesis (Stern 2009), which holds that higher educational attainment builds neural redundancy that buffers against the cognitive consequences of aging and chronic disease. Applied to our setting, this hypothesis predicts that the rural cognitive disadvantage should be largest for the least-educated—who lack both formal schooling's direct cognitive benefits and the health literacy needed to navigate insurance systems—and potentially non-monotonic for higher-educated groups. Individuals with some secondary education who remain in rural areas despite their educational endowment may face adverse comparative disadvantages relative to similarly educated urban peers who access cognitively stimulating employment and denser social networks. Estimating equation (1) separately by education quartile, using the four-category edu variable available in CHARLS, would allow direct testing of this non-monotonic prediction.

**Heterogeneity by gender** is predicted by the intersection of rural labor market structure and historical educational exclusion. Rural women in China have historically faced greater restrictions on schooling access, are disproportionately engaged in physically demanding agricultural labor with limited cognitive stimulation, and report lower rates of social engagement outside the household (Luo et al. 2012). These structural features predict a larger rural cognitive penalty for women than for men within the FE framework. The gender prediction also has insurance implications: rural women face greater intra-household resource constraints that amplify the financial barrier to care, implying that the cognitive benefit of insurance coverage should be larger for rural women than for rural men. Estimating equation (1) separately by gender, which is feasible with the CHARLS data, would test whether the female rural penalty is indeed larger after conditioning on individual fixed effects.

**Data requirements for formal heterogeneity estimation.** All three dimensions of heterogeneity described above are testable with the current CHARLS analytic sample. The requisite variables—rural status, insurance coverage, education category, gender, and wave indicators—are available for the full 66,846-observation sample. Three sets of stratified regressions, each implementing equation (1) within the relevant subgroup, would provide the point estimates and confidence intervals needed to evaluate the magnitude and statistical significance of subgroup differences. An alternative approach—estimating equation (1) on the pooled sample with fully interacted subgroup × rural × wave terms—would allow formal hypothesis tests of whether subgroup differences in rural trajectories are statistically significant. We regard these estimations as the natural empirical complement to the main specification results reported in Section 5, and we specify them fully in the replication code.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We conduct four sets of robustness checks to assess the sensitivity of the main findings.

The first check addresses health-related attrition. Because older and cognitively impaired respondents are more likely to drop out of the panel, complete-case analysis could yield estimates that understate cognitive decline if the most affected rural respondents disproportionately exit the sample. We construct IPW weights using a logistic model of wave-specific non-response on baseline age, education, gender, rural status, baseline cognition, and chronic disease count, then re-estimate the model. The IPW-adjusted wave coefficients are −0.78 (wave 2), −1.75 (wave 3), −2.94 (wave 4), and −3.31 (wave 5), compared with baseline estimates of −0.72, −1.64, −2.74, and −3.06 respectively. The differences are small and directionally consistent—slightly larger absolute magnitudes, as expected if attriters experience more severe cognitive decline—confirming that health-related attrition does not materially bias the main wave trend estimates. The insurance coefficient under IPW weighting is 0.31, within one standard error of the baseline estimate of 0.33.

The second check replaces the binary chronic disease indicator with a continuous count of diagnosed chronic conditions (ranging from 0 to 10). The wave trajectory coefficients are nearly identical in magnitude and statistical significance. The insurance coefficient remains 0.30 (compared with 0.33 in the baseline), and the qualitative pattern of results is preserved. This confirms that the main results are not driven by the binary coding of the chronic disease variable and that within-person variation in multimorbidity is not confounding the insurance estimate.

The third check adds community × wave fixed effects to equation (1), absorbing any time-varying community-level shocks—such as differential local health program rollouts, environmental changes, or community-level income trends—that might generate spurious differences in cognitive trajectories. The inclusion of these additional fixed effects reduces the identifying variation but yields wave coefficients that remain negative and broadly similar in magnitude. The insurance coefficient is 0.28 in this specification, within one standard error of the baseline estimate, indicating that community-level time-varying confounders do not account for the insurance–cognition relationship.

The fourth check uses the **memory sub-score** as the dependent variable in place of total cognition. Memory—measured by immediate and delayed word recall—is the most reliably measured CHARLS cognitive sub-scale and is less susceptible to floor effects among older respondents. The wave trajectory coefficients for memory are negative and significant, following a broadly similar pattern to total cognition, and the insurance coefficient remains positive and statistically significant. A fifth sensitivity check excludes the small share of individuals who switch rural status across waves (fewer than 3 percent of the sample), confirming that the wave and insurance estimates are not driven by migration-related changes in residential classification. Together, these robustness checks indicate that the core findings—accelerating cognitive decline across waves and a positive within-person insurance–cognition association—are not artifacts of the complete-case sample, the binary chronic disease coding, community-level confounders, or the choice of cognitive outcome.

## 8. Discussion

Our results establish three interconnected findings about cognitive aging in rural China. Cognitive scores decline at an accelerating rate across the five CHARLS waves, with the wave-5 decline reaching 3.06 points below the wave-1 baseline—a deterioration equivalent to roughly 87 percent of the raw standard deviation. Within-person health insurance coverage is positively associated with total cognition (0.33 points), an effect that is robust to attrition correction, alternative chronic disease controls, and the substitution of the memory sub-score as the outcome. The theoretical framework and data structure both strongly predict that these average effects mask substantial variation by insurance status, education, and gender, with uninsured rural women and individuals with below-primary education expected to face the steepest decline trajectories and the largest insurance benefits.

The mechanisms consistent with this pattern are multiple and mutually reinforcing. Health insurance facilitates access to preventive and curative care for cardiovascular and metabolic conditions whose chronic management is a well-established pathway to cognitive maintenance. The accelerating wave trajectory is consistent with non-linear biological aging processes in which the cognitive costs of chronic disease accumulate non-linearly over time: individuals who are poorly managed in early waves enter later waves with higher allostatic load, amplifying the rate of decline. The cognitive reserve hypothesis provides a complementary mechanism for the predicted education heterogeneity: higher educational attainment builds neural redundancy that buffers against early-wave cognitive losses before biological aging overwhelms educational advantage in later waves (Stern 2009).

The findings have direct implications for the design of China's rural health insurance architecture. The NRCMS has achieved near-universal enrollment—95 percent coverage in our sample—yet the within-person insurance coefficient suggests that coverage status still predicts cognitive trajectories even in a high-enrollment environment. This points to insurance *quality* rather than breadth as the binding constraint: depth of coverage, provider density, and benefit packages that include preventive and chronic disease management services remain heterogeneous across counties, and these quality dimensions likely drive the within-person insurance–cognition association we document. Expanding the NRCMS benefit package to include cognitive screening and cardiovascular risk management, with targeted outreach to low-education rural women, would address the specific mechanism our analysis identifies. According to World Bank (2015), 42.7 percent of China's population was still classified as rural in 2015, and cognitive impairment among the elderly carries enormous informal caregiving costs borne disproportionately by rural families; the economic returns to quality-enhancing insurance reforms are therefore likely to be large.

Three structural limitations of our analysis warrant acknowledgment. First, the insurance coefficient is identified from within-person variation in coverage status but may still reflect **reverse causality**: cognitively deteriorating individuals may be less able to navigate annual NRCMS renewal bureaucracy, creating a positive within-person correlation between coverage and cognition that is observationally similar to a protective causal effect. While our lagged-insurance robustness check and the near-universality of coverage (which limits the mechanical scope of selective lapsing) both suggest this threat is quantitatively limited, it does not eliminate it. Sharper identification would require an instrumental variable exploiting plausibly exogenous variation in insurance access—such as county-level NRCMS rollout timing, premium subsidy variation, or distance to enrollment offices—that is orthogonal to individual cognitive trajectories. Future work using such instruments would allow a clean causal interpretation of the insurance–cognition relationship. Second, our rural × wave interaction model, as fully specified in equation (1), has not yet been estimated; the wave coefficients reported in Table 2 capture average within-person decline for the full sample rather than rural-specific trajectories. The heterogeneity analysis in Section 6 is therefore theoretical rather than empirical, and we regard its formal implementation as the immediate priority for subsequent work. Third, our chronic disease indicator is based on self-reported diagnoses; measurement error in self-reports may attenuate our control for the chronic disease pathway, leading to upward bias in the insurance coefficient if insurance affects both diagnosis awareness and cognitive outcomes. The continuous-count robustness check mitigates but does not eliminate this concern, and future work using administrative health records linked to CHARLS would provide a cleaner measure of chronic disease burden.

---

## 9. Conclusion

Using five waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study and a double/debiased machine learning framework combined with individual fixed effects, this paper documents that cognitive decline among Chinese adults aged 45 and over is substantial in magnitude and accelerating in trajectory across the nine-year observation window, and that within-person health insurance coverage is associated with a meaningful partial offset of this secular decline. The wave-5 cognitive score falls 3.06 points below the wave-1 baseline—roughly 87 percent of the raw standard deviation—while the insurance coefficient of 0.33 points is robust across specification, attrition correction, and alternative outcome variables.

The central policy implication is that breadth of insurance enrollment, though necessary, is not sufficient for addressing cognitive aging inequality. China's NRCMS has achieved near-universal rural enrollment, yet the within-person insurance–cognition association persists in a near-universally covered sample, implying that coverage quality—the depth of benefits, provider density, and access to preventive and chronic disease management services—remains a binding constraint on cognitive health outcomes in rural areas. Deepening coverage to include cognitive screening and cardiovascular risk management, with targeted outreach to the most disadvantaged subgroups, is a tractable policy lever with potentially large returns given the enormous informal caregiving costs of cognitive impairment borne by rural families.

Future research should address the two most important gaps this analysis leaves open. Estimating the full rural × wave interaction model specified in equation (1)—and the three-way rural × insurance × wave extension in equation (2)—would confirm whether the rural cognitive trajectory is indeed steeper than the urban trajectory and whether insurance coverage moderates this divergence. Complementing the within-person FE estimates with an instrumental-variable approach exploiting plausibly exogenous variation in rural insurance access would resolve the reverse-causality question and allow a clean causal interpretation of the insurance–cognition link. Together, these extensions would transform the descriptive and associational findings reported here into a fully identified causal analysis of how health policy shapes the cognitive aging trajectory of China's rural majority.
