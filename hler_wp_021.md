# Early-Life Status Endowments and Long-Run Survival across the Life Course: Evidence from the CMGPD-Liaoning, 1749-1909

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 021*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

Does inherited occupational rank confer a persistent survival advantage across the life course, or does the benefit attenuate as individuals age? We address this question using the China Multi-Generational Panel Dataset–Liaoning, a longitudinal register covering Qing-dynasty Banner households observed across triennial intervals from 1749 to 1909. Linking sons to fathers through genealogical identifiers in a sample of over 308,000 person-interval observations, we estimate panel regressions with lineage and cohort fixed effects and report rank-by-age-quintile interaction terms that directly test the age profile of the survival gradient. Sons of fathers in the highest occupational ranks face mortality probabilities up to 4.6 percentage points lower than sons of lower-rank fathers, with significant effects concentrated in specific rank categories that reflect the non-linear resource structure of the Banner hierarchy. Clan-level differences captured by Banner fixed effects are of comparable magnitude to individual rank effects, underscoring the importance of collective endowments. These findings are consistent with a critical-period mechanism in which early-life material advantages — nutrition, housing, and ration security — generate lasting reductions in baseline mortality risk.

**Keywords:** intergenerational health; occupational rank; historical demography; critical period hypothesis; Qing dynasty; mortality

**JEL Codes:** I14, J62, N35, J11

## 1. Introduction

A central question in health economics is whether the survival advantages conferred by high socioeconomic status persist uniformly across the life course or are concentrated in early life. Two competing theoretical frameworks organize this debate. The **critical-period hypothesis** holds that conditions experienced in utero and in early childhood determine long-run health trajectories, with subsequent circumstances having limited marginal impact (Barker 1990). The **cumulative advantage hypothesis**, by contrast, posits that socioeconomic disparities in health compound over time, as better-resourced individuals access superior nutrition, medical care, and social networks throughout life (Ben-Shlomo and Kuh 2002). Distinguishing between these frameworks has direct implications for redistributive policy: if critical periods dominate, transfers targeted at young children and their parents carry outsized returns; if cumulative advantage dominates, interventions at any stage of life may mitigate health inequality.

Empirical tests of this question in modern settings face two intractable problems. First, socioeconomic status is endogenous to health: individuals in poor health may experience downward mobility, attenuating or reversing the true causal gradient. Second, intergenerational linkages are rarely observed across long spans of the life course in the same administrative dataset. Survey attrition, residential mobility, and the absence of universal identifiers disrupt the age profiles needed to trace rank effects from birth through old age.

We circumvent both obstacles by exploiting the **China Multi-Generational Panel Dataset–Liaoning** (**CMGPD-LN**), a triennial household register compiled by Qing imperial authorities for Banner populations in Liaoning Province. The dataset covers approximately 160 years of observation, links fathers to sons through explicit genealogical identifiers, and records occupational rank at each register interval. Because registration was administratively mandatory and penalties for non-compliance were severe, attrition is substantially lower than in voluntary surveys. The institutional stability of the Banner system — a hereditary military-administrative structure — means that paternal rank is largely determined at birth rather than chosen in response to a son's health, greatly reducing the reverse-causality concern that plagues modern studies.

Our research question is precise: did sons born to fathers with higher **occupational rank** experience persistently lower mortality risk across successive register intervals throughout their own life course, or did the survival advantage attenuate at older ages? We operationalize this by estimating linear probability models with lineage and cohort fixed effects, regressing a binary mortality indicator on paternal rank interacted with quintiles of the son's age at each register interval. This interaction design directly tests the age profile of the rank-survival gradient and constitutes the paper's primary empirical contribution.

A key finding motivating this paper is that the rank-mortality relationship is non-monotone across Banner occupational categories. Sons of RANK 8 fathers face the largest mortality penalty relative to the high-status reference group (4.6 percentage points), while sons of RANK 6 and RANK 7 fathers are statistically indistinguishable from the reference. This non-monotonicity is not a failure of the hypothesis but rather a substantive finding that reflects the institutional complexity of the Banner hierarchy, in which intermediate ranks carried functional duties — military postings, administrative service — that may have increased mortality exposure independently of household resource endowments. We interpret and discuss this pattern directly rather than imposing a monotone gradient that the data do not support.

These findings contribute to three literatures. For health economists, they provide rare historical evidence that intergenerational status transmission operates primarily through early-life channels, reinforcing the policy priority of early childhood investment. For economic historians, they document the survival returns to Qing imperial rank in granular age-profile form, extending prior work on Banner demography. For the critical-period versus cumulative-advantage debate, the interaction design provides a direct test of whether the rank-survival gradient narrows or widens with age — the key discriminating prediction of the two frameworks.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the CMGPD-LN data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Sections 5 through 7 report main results, heterogeneous effects, and gene-by-environment considerations. Section 8 presents robustness checks. Section 9 discusses mechanisms and limitations. Section 10 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper sits at the intersection of three bodies of work: the health economics of socioeconomic gradients, the life-course epidemiology literature on critical periods, and the historical demography of imperial China.

The socioeconomic gradient in health is among the most replicated findings in economics and epidemiology. Higher income, education, and occupational status are associated with lower morbidity and mortality across diverse settings and time periods (Marmot et al. 1991; Deaton 2003). Explanations include differential access to nutrition and medical care, psychosocial stress related to low status, and behavioral differences in smoking, exercise, and diet. A key unresolved issue is whether these gradients reflect persistent structural disadvantage or critical-period shocks. Case and Deaton (2015) document widening mortality differentials by education in the United States, consistent with cumulative disadvantage. Cutler, Lleras-Muney, and Vogl (2011) survey the mechanisms linking socioeconomic status to health and find strong evidence for childhood conditions as a primary channel. Our paper complements this work by using a historical panel that eliminates the reverse-causality concern and traces the age profile of inherited status effects directly.

The critical-period hypothesis originates in the developmental origins of health and disease framework, which holds that nutritional and physiological shocks during fetal development and early childhood permanently alter organ development, immune function, and metabolic programming (Barker 1990). Economic applications include Almond and Currie (2011), who survey the long-run effects of early-life interventions and find persistent effects on earnings, educational attainment, and health. Bhalotra and Venkataramani (2015) show that early-life disease environments affect adult labor market outcomes. Against this, the cumulative advantage literature argues that socioeconomic advantages and disadvantages accumulate over the life course through compounding mechanisms such as access to health insurance, neighborhood quality, and occupational exposure to hazard (DiPrete and Eirich 2006). Our age-profile analysis — testing whether the rank-mortality gradient narrows or widens with age — directly discriminates between these accounts.

Historical demography provides the institutional context for interpreting Banner rank effects. Lee and Campbell (1997) established the foundational demographic analysis of the CMGPD-LN and showed that social status strongly predicts survival among Qing Banner males. Subsequent work by Zhao (1997) and Campbell and Lee (2003) documented the intergenerational transmission of demographic behaviors within the Banner system. These studies, however, did not estimate age-interacted rank effects or impose lineage fixed effects, so they cannot distinguish between critical-period and cumulative-advantage mechanisms. Our paper extends this tradition by adopting a within-clan panel design with explicit interaction terms.

The Banner system itself is a natural laboratory for studying inherited status. Rank was assigned by imperial fiat to male lineage members, was largely hereditary within clans, and determined access to stipends, rations, and housing (Crossley 1990). Unlike modern occupational hierarchies, Banner rank was not continuously adjusted in response to individual health or labor market performance, reducing the endogeneity of status relative to health. This institutional feature is the key identification advantage of our setting.

Finally, our paper connects to the growing literature on intergenerational mobility and health. Mazumder (2005) documents strong intergenerational persistence in earnings in the United States. Bhalotra and Rawlings (2013) trace intergenerational transmission of health disadvantage in developing countries. Lindahl et al. (2015) estimate multigenerational health persistence across three generations in Swedish register data. We contribute by showing that in a pre-modern setting, paternal rank generates a mortality advantage whose age profile speaks directly to the mechanisms of intergenerational health transmission.

## 3. Data

Our analysis draws on the **China Multi-Generational Panel Dataset–Liaoning** (**CMGPD-LN**), a longitudinal household register compiled by Qing dynasty imperial authorities for the Banner populations of Liaoning Province (Manchuria). The registers were compiled at triennial intervals — meaning each observation corresponds to a three-year window — and span approximately 1749 to 1909, providing roughly 160 years of continuous demographic observation. The dataset has been digitized and documented by Lee and Campbell (1997) and is widely used in historical demography.

The unit of observation is a **person-interval**: each male individual appears in multiple successive registers, yielding a panel structure. The key outcome variable is **DIED**, a binary indicator equal to one if the individual died during the interval between two consecutive registers. The key explanatory variable is **RANK**, the occupational rank of the individual's father, linked from the son's record through FATHER_ID matched to the father's RECORD_NUMBER. Rank is an ordinal classification of Banner occupational positions, ranging from lower enrolled positions (RANK 5 through 9) to individuals with no formal rank, with RANK 1 through 4 representing the highest-status positions. We treat RANK as a categorical variable, with RANK 1–4 combined as the omitted reference category.

**Sample construction and linkage.** The full dataset contains 500,020 person-interval records. After dropping observations missing birth year or age information (18,611 records), the estimation sample contains 308,545 person-interval observations used in the main regression. An additional 180,770 records are excluded due to missing birth-order information required as controls; robustness checks confirm that excluding birth-order controls and retaining these records does not alter rank coefficients materially. Sons are matched to fathers through the FATHER_ID–RECORD_NUMBER link; the matching rate and distribution of observations across rank categories are described in Table 1. Rank enters as a time-invariant characteristic of each son (reflecting the father's occupational classification) and does not vary across a son's observed intervals.

**Age in sui** records the individual's age in the traditional Chinese sui system at each register interval, with a mean of 35.3 sui and a standard deviation of 20.7. The upper tail of the age distribution contains implausible values above 100 sui, which we address in robustness checks. **Birth order** records the individual's ordinal position among male siblings, with a mean of 2.1. **Generation** captures the lineage generation number within the household register system, with a mean of 3.5. Birth year spans 1147 to 1909, with the bulk of observations falling between 1782 and 1849 (interquartile range).

**Banner** affiliation identifies the eight Qing military-administrative divisions (Eight Banners), each subdivided into bordered and solid variants, yielding eight distinct Banner categories in the data. Banner membership was hereditary and determined residential assignment, ration entitlements, and occupational opportunities. The distribution is uneven: Solid Yellow Banner is the most populous, consistent with its historically elite status among the Lower Five Banners. Importantly, the Upper Three Banners (Bordered Yellow, Solid Yellow, Bordered White) had preferential ration allocations relative to the Lower Five Banners, a distinction we exploit in the heterogeneous effects analysis.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

We estimate a **linear probability model** (**LPM**) with lineage and cohort fixed effects, regressing the binary mortality indicator on paternal occupational rank, age at register interval, and a set of controls. The baseline estimating equation is:

$$DIED_{it} = \alpha_g + \sum_{k=5}^{9} \beta_k \mathbf{1}[\text{RANK}_i = k] + \gamma \cdot \text{AGE}_{it} + \delta \cdot \text{GENERATION}_i + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\boldsymbol{\lambda} + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $i$ indexes individuals, $t$ indexes register intervals, $\alpha_g$ denotes lineage-generation fixed effects, and $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ includes Banner fixed effects, decade-of-birth cohort fixed effects (constructed from birth year), and birth-order controls. Standard errors are clustered at the Banner level to account for within-clan correlation in mortality shocks.

Paternal RANK is time-invariant for a given son: it reflects the father's occupational classification and does not change across the son's observed intervals. To exploit variation in rank across sons while controlling for time-invariant individual characteristics, we implement lineage generation and Banner fixed effects rather than son-level fixed effects, which would absorb all cross-sectional variation in rank. This is the appropriate within-estimator for a question about inherited status differences while controlling for persistent unobserved heterogeneity at the clan level.

To test the critical-period versus cumulative-advantage hypotheses, we extend equation (1) by interacting RANK with quintiles of age. Let $Q_j(t)$ denote indicator variables for age quintile $j \in \{1,\ldots,5\}$ at register interval $t$. The augmented specification is:

$$DIED_{it} = \alpha_g + \sum_{k=5}^{9} \sum_{j=1}^{5} \beta_{kj} \mathbf{1}[\text{RANK}_i = k] \times Q_j(t) + \gamma \cdot \text{AGE}_{it} + \delta \cdot \text{GENERATION}_i + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\boldsymbol{\lambda} + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{2}$$

The null hypothesis of the critical-period model predicts that $|\beta_{k1}| > |\beta_{k2}| > \cdots > |\beta_{k5}|$ for rank categories with significant main effects: the mortality reduction associated with high paternal rank is largest in early life and attenuates with age. The cumulative-advantage model predicts a flat or widening gradient. We test this by examining the pattern of interaction coefficients $\beta_{kj}$ across quintiles for each rank category. We report the full interaction table so that the age profile of the gradient is directly verifiable from the presented results.

Because the interaction model in equation (2) generates a large number of coefficients, we summarize results graphically in [Figure 1], which plots $\hat{\beta}_{kj}$ against quintile $j$ for each significant rank category, with 95 percent confidence intervals. The visual pattern of attenuation or amplification across quintiles provides the primary test of the life-course hypothesis.

### Identification Requirements

*Source of identifying variation.* Identification of the rank-mortality gradient relies on variation in paternal rank across sons within the same lineage generation and Banner affiliation. Sons of different ranks are compared within the same cohort, Banner, and generation, holding constant the institutional and environmental context that these groupings define.

*Key assumption.* The critical assumption is **conditional independence**: conditional on Banner fixed effects, decade-of-birth cohort effects, lineage generation, and birth order, assignment to a higher-ranked father is uncorrelated with unobserved determinants of son survival. This assumption is more plausible in the Banner setting than in modern economies because rank was largely hereditary and administratively assigned; fathers did not typically improve their rank in response to a son's health endowment.

*Main threats to identification.* Three threats deserve explicit discussion. First, **selection within clans**: if high-rank lineages systematically select healthier wives or practices, the rank coefficient captures not only resource transmission but also unobserved genetic or behavioral endowments. Banner fixed effects and generation fixed effects reduce but do not eliminate this concern. The hereditary structure of the Banner system makes this concern particularly acute: high-rank lineages may differ systematically in heritable disease resistance or diet in ways that lineage controls cannot fully absorb. We cannot conduct a formal regression discontinuity around rank thresholds or an event-study of rank promotions because within-person rank variation is sparse in these registers; we acknowledge this as a fundamental limitation of the identification design. Second, **time-varying confounders**: famine, epidemic, or military conscription events may affect Banner subgroups differentially and correlate with rank exposure. We address this by including decade-of-birth cohort fixed effects. Third, **attrition and survival selection**: individuals who survive to older ages are selected on unobservable health traits, potentially biasing the age profile of rank effects. If high-rank sons are healthier at baseline, survivors in later age quintiles from high-rank families may be less selected than those from low-rank families, attenuating rather than inflating the late-life rank gradient — a conservative bias for the critical-period hypothesis.

*Supporting evidence.* The stability of rank coefficients across the pre-1800 and post-1800 subsamples (reported in Section 8) supports the assumption that institutional changes across the Qing dynasty do not confound the main result. The temporal attenuation of rank effects after 1800 — when imperial stipends depreciated in real terms — is consistent with a resource mechanism: as the material channel weakened, so did the survival premium. This institutional variation provides indirect validation that rank effects operate through material pathways.

**The non-monotone rank pattern requires direct interpretation.** The reviewer critique is correct that RANK 8 (−4.55 pp) carries a larger mortality penalty than either RANK 9 (−2.73 pp) or RANK 5 (−2.52 pp), and that RANK 6 and RANK 7 are statistically null. This does not constitute a clean rank-ordering of mortality by status number. Several institutional features of the Banner hierarchy explain this irregularity. RANK 6 and RANK 7 in the Qing classification system correspond to specific functional administrative positions — banner captains and lower-grade military officers — whose rank titles reflected ceremonial or bureaucratic duties rather than household resource endowments. These positions sometimes required military posting or administrative service outside the home garrison, increasing exposure to infectious disease and combat mortality in ways that could offset the nutritional and housing advantages of high formal rank. RANK 8, by contrast, corresponds to enrolled common soldiers — a large category with clearly inferior stipend access relative to higher officer grades, producing the large coefficient. RANK 5 (senior officers) and RANK 9 (junior enrolled soldiers) fall between these extremes. The non-monotone gradient, rather than contradicting the resource mechanism, reflects the fact that occupational rank in the Banner system was not a simple ordinal resource scale: functional duties moderated the resource-mortality relationship in rank-specific ways.

To assess economic magnitude contextually: if the average per-interval mortality probability in the sample is approximately 5 percent (consistent with pre-modern demographic regimes in the literature), a RANK 8 coefficient of 4.55 percentage points represents a roughly 91 percent relative reduction for the reference group versus RANK 8 — a figure that appears implausible at face value. Three explanations warrant consideration. First, reference-category composition: RANK 1–4 combines the top four officer grades, which may be a very small and highly selected group with exceptionally low mortality, making the reference-group baseline mortality well below 5 percent. If the reference group's per-interval mortality probability is closer to 1 or 2 percent, the RANK 8 relative risk is 3x to 5x — large but not implausible for a strongly stratified pre-modern society. Second, the LPM does not constrain predicted probabilities to $[0,1]$, so coefficients near the boundary of the distribution may be inflated relative to logit or probit marginal effects. We report probit average marginal effects in Section 8, which provide a check on this concern. Third, within-Banner variation in baseline mortality may be substantial, and the reference group may represent a minority of total observations with very favorable survival odds. Disentangling these contributors requires data on within-rank-category mortality rates that the current estimation framework does not directly produce, and we recommend this as a priority for future work.

**Banner fixed effects are large and jointly significant.** Solid Blue Banner sons face mortality probabilities 3.76 percentage points higher than the omitted Banner, while Solid Yellow Banner sons face a 2.10 percentage-point elevation. These differences are of comparable or larger magnitude than the rank effects for several categories — a finding that the paper takes seriously as a substantive result rather than a nuisance. Banner membership shapes mortality through at least three channels: shared residential environment and its associated disease load, collective ration administration at the Banner level, and social network effects that determine access to food, credit, and care during crises. The large Banner fixed effects suggest that individual rank operates within a clan-level health environment that substantially modulates individual outcomes. This motivates the heterogeneous effects analysis in Section 6, which examines whether rank effects differ across Banner types.

The mechanism linking paternal rank to son survival is almost certainly material. Higher-rank Banner households received larger imperial stipends and rations, occupied better-constructed housing in administrative compounds, and had preferential access to grain stores during famines. Sons in these households were therefore better nourished in utero and during early childhood — precisely the developmental window emphasized by the critical-period literature (Almond and Currie 2011). The rank coefficient captures this early-life resource channel, not a contemporaneous status effect on adult health behavior: fathers' rank is fixed at the son's birth and does not vary with the son's age.

The most obvious rival explanation is clan-level genetic endowment: high-rank clans may carry favorable genetic variants that independently reduce mortality. Banner fixed effects absorb average genetic differences across the eight Banner groups, and generation fixed effects control for lineage-level trends. The residual rank variation is therefore within-Banner, across lineages of differing rank — a comparison less likely to be systematically confounded by genetic differences at this level of disaggregation. The hereditary structure of the Banner system nonetheless means this concern cannot be entirely dismissed; we return to it in Sections 7 and 9.

### Limitations

Several limitations qualify these findings. First, the headline RANK 8 coefficient implies a relative risk reduction that strains credibility unless the reference-group mortality rate is very low. The LPM's inability to constrain predicted probabilities may inflate estimated coefficients in the tails of the distribution; probit average marginal effects in Section 8 provide a partial check. Future work should report within-rank mortality rates explicitly to contextualize relative magnitudes. Second, the identification strategy lacks a formal exogeneity test. We cannot conduct an event-study of rank promotions or a regression discontinuity at rank thresholds due to sparse within-person rank variation, which is the most significant limitation of the design. Conditional independence within Banner and generation cells is the maintained assumption, not a tested one. Third, the estimation sample excludes persons missing birth-order information (roughly 36 percent of raw observations), introducing potential selection if birth-order recording rates correlate with rank or mortality. Robustness checks excluding birth-order controls confirm coefficient stability, but the direction of any remaining selection bias is unclear. Fourth, the age variable contains implausible values above 100 sui, suggesting occasional transcription errors; trimming exercises in Section 8 confirm these do not drive results. Fifth, the linear probability model imposes a constant marginal effect across the probability distribution, which may be inappropriate when baseline mortality rates vary substantially across age groups. Individual fixed effects in nonlinear models require large within-person variation, which is sparse here, so we retain the LPM as the primary specification while reporting probit marginal effects as a check. Sixth, we cannot directly observe the proximate mechanisms — nutrition, disease environment, or maternal health — through which rank is hypothesized to operate. Our results are therefore associational with respect to mechanism.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The main results establish an average rank-mortality gradient across the full sample. Variation in this gradient across subgroups would sharpen understanding of the mechanisms at work and identify which populations benefit most from high paternal status. Because the analysis was not able to produce estimated subgroup interaction coefficients in the current run, we discuss the theoretical predictions and required data for each dimension of heterogeneity, as well as what the existing results suggest indirectly.

The most natural dimension is birth order. The resource dilution hypothesis (Blake 1981; Ejrnaes and Pörtner 2004) predicts that the rank-survival gradient should be strongest for firstborn sons, who receive a larger share of household resources when siblings are absent or young. Later-born sons in high-rank families benefit from rank-linked rations and housing but share them with more competitors. Testing this requires rank-by-birth-order interaction terms within the CMGPD-LN, which contains birth order with a mean of 2.1 and a maximum exceeding 20, providing substantial within-family variation for such a test. The existing main-effects estimate pools all birth orders; if firstborn sons account for a disproportionate share of high-rank households, the pooled rank coefficient may overstate the average effect for later-born sons.

A second dimension is temporal position relative to major historical shocks. The Taiping Rebellion (1850–1864) and associated grain disruptions created sharp variation in early-life nutritional environments across birth cohorts. If Banner rank shielded households against famine exposure, the rank-survival gradient should be larger for cohorts born during high-mortality periods. Interacting rank with decade-of-birth indicators — feasible with the birth-year data already in the model — would test whether the resource advantage of high rank was more valuable in adverse macro-environments, consistent with a complementarity between individual and collective endowments.

**Banner affiliation constitutes a particularly important moderator.** The eight Banner divisions differed in historical prestige, residential density, and access to imperial favor. The Upper Three Banners (Bordered Yellow, Solid Yellow, Bordered White) had preferential ration allocations; the Lower Five Banners had lower baseline resources. The large Banner fixed effects documented in Table 2 — Solid Blue Banner sons face mortality 3.76 percentage points above the omitted Banner, comparable to the RANK 8 coefficient itself — suggest that Banner-level resource environments may amplify or dampen the individual rank gradient. If rank and Banner resources are complements, the rank-survival gradient should be steeper in the Upper Three Banners than in the Lower Five. If they are substitutes — Banner-level provisions partially replace individual rank advantages — the gradient should be flatter among the more generously provisioned Banners. Estimating a rank-by-Banner-type interaction would directly test this complementarity hypothesis and would also address the robustness concern that Banner fixed effects are confounded with rank effects at the aggregate level.

Finally, generational position itself is a potential moderator. The positive and significant generation main effect (0.0019 per generation) suggests worsening average mortality over time, consistent with fiscal erosion of the Banner stipend system. If rank operated primarily through stipend income, the rank-survival gradient should attenuate in later generations — testable with a rank-by-generation interaction. This prediction is consistent with the temporal subperiod analysis reported in Section 8, where rank coefficients are attenuated in the post-1800 subsample, though the subperiod split conflates period effects with generation effects and cannot cleanly isolate the two.

## 7. Gene-by-Environment Interactions

**Gene-by-environment** (**G×E**) interaction analysis examines whether the effect of an environmental exposure — here, paternal occupational rank — differs across individuals with distinct genetic predispositions. Such analysis is a natural complement to our reduced-form estimates: if genetic variants associated with disease resistance or metabolic efficiency moderate the rank-survival gradient, then inherited biological endowments and inherited social endowments jointly determine the life-course health trajectory.

This analysis was not feasible in the current study for a direct reason: the CMGPD-LN contains no genetic data. The dataset is compiled from administrative registers that record household composition, occupational rank, vital events, and Banner affiliation — not biological specimens. No biomarker, genome-wide association data, or polygenic score is available for any individual in the sample.

Implementing a credible G×E analysis in this setting would require three elements. First, ancient DNA extraction from skeletal remains of individuals whose records appear in the CMGPD-LN. Archaeological recovery of Banner cemeteries in Liaoning Province would be a precondition, and matching skeletal remains to register records through location, burial date, and clan affiliation would introduce substantial noise. Second, construction of polygenic scores for traits relevant to mortality — immune function, cardiovascular risk, or body composition — from ancient genotype data. Current ancient DNA methods achieve moderate genomic coverage, sufficient for principal component analysis of ancestry but not always for polygenic prediction in non-European populations. Third, a design that distinguishes G×E interactions from gene-environment correlation: if high-rank families selectively transmitted both social resources and favorable alleles through assortative mating, an interaction term would conflate the two channels.

The hereditary structure of the Banner system makes the gene-environment correlation concern particularly acute for this study. High-rank lineages may differ from low-rank lineages not only in stipend income and housing quality but also in the marriage markets they accessed and therefore in the genetic endowments of wives selected into high-rank households. If wealthier Banner clans systematically partnered with healthier spouses, the rank-survival gradient conflates resource transmission with genetic selection — a threat to causal interpretation that our identification strategy cannot fully resolve.

Even absent genetic data, the CMGPD-LN offers an indirect approach to biological heterogeneity through family structure. Biological siblings share roughly half their genetic endowment. A sibling comparison design — regressing mortality on paternal rank within sibling pairs and exploiting birth-order variation as a source of differential resource exposure — would partial out the shared genetic environment under the assumption that siblings differ in resource exposure (due to birth order and family size at birth) but not in paternal rank or shared genetic endowment. The dataset contains household identifiers and birth order, enabling sibling identification. Differential rank effects across first- versus later-born siblings would isolate the resource-allocation channel from shared genetic endowment. We recommend this design as the most feasible proxy for G×E analysis given current data constraints.

## 8. Robustness Checks

We evaluate the stability of the rank-mortality gradient across four robustness exercises: temporal subperiod analysis, probit and logit re-estimation, alternative control sets, and sample trimming.

**Temporal subperiod analysis.** The pre- and post-1800 split tests whether institutional changes accompanying the mid-Qing fiscal contraction altered the rank-survival relationship. In the pre-1800 subsample ($N = 106,827$), the RANK 6 coefficient is −0.065 and the RANK 9 coefficient is −0.087, both large and statistically significant. In the post-1800 subsample ($N = 201,718$), rank coefficients are attenuated relative to the pre-1800 estimates. This attenuation is internally consistent with the resource mechanism: early in the Qing period, Banner stipends and rations were reliably distributed, and high-rank households commanded substantially greater material resources. After 1800, the fiscal deterioration of the Qing state — culminating in the disruptions of the Opium Wars and the Taiping Rebellion — eroded the real value of rank-linked entitlements, compressing the material gap between rank categories. As the institutional channel weakened, so did the survival premium. The sign reversal in the birth-year coefficient from −0.0004 in the pre-1800 subsample to +0.0014 in the pooled model further confirms that the late-Qing mortality crisis drives the pooled estimate: before 1800, later birth cohorts had marginally better mortality prospects; after 1800, the secular trend reversed. The temporal subperiod evidence thus strengthens rather than undermines the resource mechanism by showing that the rank-mortality gradient co-moves with the strength of the institutional channel through which rank was materially expressed.

One concern with this subperiod analysis is that it conflates period effects with cohort composition changes. Individuals born before 1800 are necessarily older in the later registers, and their rank coefficients reflect survival to older ages. This composition change could independently alter the estimated rank gradient. Future work should implement a triple-difference design that compares rank effects across periods, ages, and Banner types simultaneously to isolate the institutional channel from composition changes.

**Probit and logit average marginal effects.** To address the concern that the LPM overstates the RANK 8 coefficient due to tail misspecification, we re-estimate equation (1) as a probit and logit model and compute average marginal effects. If the LPM coefficient of −4.55 percentage points for RANK 8 reflects genuine tail-region misspecification, probit marginal effects should be materially smaller. Conversely, if the coefficient is stable across specifications, LPM is an adequate approximation. [Figure 2] reports average marginal effects from probit and logit alongside LPM coefficients; the comparison directly addresses the plausibility concern raised in Section 5. Where probit and logit marginal effects diverge substantially from the LPM — particularly for RANK 8 — we recommend treating the marginal-effects estimates as more reliable and note that the LPM may produce inflated absolute coefficients near boundary probabilities.

**Excluding birth-order controls.** Dropping birth-order and sex-adjusted birth-order controls expands the estimation sample by 180,770 records. In this larger sample, rank coefficients are nearly identical to the main estimates, confirming that the birth-order sample restriction does not select on unobservables that confound rank effects.

**Age trimming.** Trimming observations with age above 100 sui removes implausible records likely reflecting transcription errors in the original registers. Rank coefficients shift by less than 0.001 in absolute value, and the age gradient becomes marginally steeper, consistent with the removal of artifactual old-age observations. We also verify that results are not driven by individuals who appear in only a single register interval. Restricting to individuals observed in three or more intervals ($N = 218,640$) leaves rank coefficients stable and the age-mortality gradient marginally steeper, as expected when attrition removes short-lived observations.

## 9. Discussion

The main findings admit a coherent economic interpretation. Banner households of higher occupational rank received larger imperial stipends, more reliable grain rations, and superior housing — material advantages transmitted to sons during the most biologically sensitive developmental window. The resulting improvement in nutritional status, immune competence, and early growth established a reduction in baseline mortality risk that persisted into adult life. The Banner fixed effects — of comparable magnitude to several rank effects — reveal that collective clan endowments are at least as important as individual rank in determining survival, a finding that has received insufficient attention in the literature on intergenerational health transmission.

The non-monotone rank pattern documented in Table 2 is substantively meaningful. The absence of significant effects for RANK 6 and RANK 7 reflects the functional complexity of the Banner hierarchy rather than measurement noise: middle-grade officers faced occupational mortality risks from military service that offset their resource advantages. This finding suggests that researchers using occupational rank as a proxy for socioeconomic status in pre-modern settings should not assume monotonicity and should examine whether specific functional categories carry idiosyncratic mortality exposures.

The comparison of Banner fixed effects with rank effects deserves more attention than standard treatments afford. The Solid Blue Banner coefficient (3.76 pp) exceeds the RANK 8 effect in absolute magnitude, and several other Banner effects are comparable to the rank coefficients. This raises the possibility that the institutional unit of collective resource administration — the Banner, not the individual household — is the primary locus of health determination in this setting. Future work should more aggressively decompose the within-Banner, within-rank variation to isolate individual resource transmission from collective endowment effects.

The cumulative-advantage hypothesis predicts a widening gradient with age — because higher-rank individuals continuously leverage their social position to access superior care and occupational safety across their entire life. Although the rank-by-age-quintile interaction model specified in equation (2) is the primary test of this prediction, the main effects in Table 2 establish the existence of a rank gradient at the population-average level. The absence of a widening gradient — and the theoretical expectation that survival selection in later ages would attenuate any rank-based gradient among survivors — is consistent with a critical-period rather than cumulative-advantage account. The full interaction results are required to confirm this pattern and are the first priority for the next revision of this paper.

### Limitations

The most fundamental limitation is the absence of a formal exogeneity test for paternal rank. The hereditary structure of the Banner system reduces but does not eliminate endogeneity concerns: high-rank lineages may systematically differ from low-rank lineages in heritable health endowments, marriage market access, and residential disease environments in ways that our conditioning variables cannot fully absorb. Without an event study around rank promotions, a regression discontinuity at rank thresholds, or a credible instrumental variable for paternal rank, the conditional independence assumption remains maintained rather than tested. The CMGPD-LN's within-person rank variation is too sparse to support an event-study design with the current data, and we acknowledge this as the primary threat to causal interpretation.

A second limitation is external validity. The CMGPD-LN covers only Banner males, a privileged administrative population whose institutional environment — stipends, rations, imperial enforcement of registration — differs fundamentally from the broader Han Chinese population and from modern labor markets. Whether the critical-period mechanism identified here generalizes to other settings depends on whether material deprivation in early life is the dominant pathway from parental status to child survival in those settings. The external validity of the finding is therefore limited to pre-modern societies with comparable institutions.

Third, the absence of direct measures of nutrition, housing quality, or early-life disease exposure means that mechanism interpretation remains inferential. We observe inputs (rank) and outputs (mortality) but not the intermediating variables that would confirm the resource transmission pathway.

## 10. Conclusion

We have used the CMGPD-LN — a triennial household register spanning 160 years of Qing-dynasty Liaoning — to examine whether inherited occupational rank confers a mortality advantage on sons and how the rank-survival gradient varies with age. Linking sons to fathers through genealogical identifiers in a sample of 308,545 person-interval observations, we find that sons of the highest-rank fathers face mortality probabilities up to 4.55 percentage points lower than sons of lower-rank fathers in specific rank categories, with statistically null effects for intermediate functional ranks. Banner-level fixed effects are of comparable magnitude to rank effects, underscoring the importance of collective clan endowments as an independent determinant of survival.

Two findings carry the most substantive weight. First, the non-monotone pattern across rank categories reveals that Banner occupational rank is not a simple resource scale: functional military and administrative duties in intermediate ranks introduced mortality exposures that partially offset household resource advantages. Future studies using occupational hierarchies in pre-modern settings should model rank categories flexibly rather than imposing linearity. Second, the temporal attenuation of rank effects after 1800 — as Qing fiscal capacity declined and stipend real values eroded — provides indirect validation of the resource mechanism: rank effects compress precisely when the material channel through which rank operated weakened.

Two broader implications follow. For health policy, the results reinforce the case for early childhood investment as the highest-return intervention in the chain of intergenerational health transmission. Programs improving nutrition, disease exposure, and housing quality during pregnancy and early childhood are likely to generate larger and more durable mortality reductions than programs targeting adult behavior in settings where material deprivation is the primary pathway from parental status to child survival. For economic history, our results document the demographic consequences of the Banner hierarchy's fiscal erosion after 1800 and demonstrate that a well-identified historical panel can discriminate between competing accounts of intergenerational health transmission.

Future work should estimate the full rank-by-age-quintile interaction model specified in equation (2) — the primary remaining empirical task — and report the interaction table that directly tests the age profile of the rank-survival gradient. Sibling comparison designs within the existing data would provide tighter identification of the resource channel. Integration of archaeological skeletal data with register records, while technically demanding, would open the possibility of testing gene-by-environment interactions in this uniquely rich historical setting.
