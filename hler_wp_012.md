# Rural Health Insurance and Labor Market Earnings: Evidence from the China Health and Nutrition Survey

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 012*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

We estimate the effect of China's New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (NCMS) on individual labor market earnings using an event study design applied to six waves of the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS) spanning 1989–2009. Exploiting the 2003 NCMS launch, we compare earnings trajectories of rural residents before and after program exposure while controlling for individual and wave fixed effects. Pre-treatment coefficients are statistically indistinguishable from zero. Post-reform earnings rise sharply: by two survey periods after the policy cutoff, rural earnings increase by approximately 5,819 yuan in real terms relative to the reference period. We are candid that these estimates should not be interpreted as causal: the design collapses to a pre-post comparison of rural residents around a single 2003–2004 cutoff, and wave fixed effects cannot absorb concurrent rural policies—agricultural tax abolition, minimum wage increases—that differentially benefited rural workers over exactly this window. We accordingly reframe our contribution as a descriptive event study documenting earnings dynamics around the NCMS expansion. Heterogeneous effects favor male and more educated workers, consistent with differential capacity to translate insurance coverage into labor market transitions.

## 1. Introduction

Access to health insurance is widely regarded as one of the most consequential determinants of household welfare in developing economies. Beyond its direct effects on health outcomes, insurance coverage can fundamentally reshape labor supply decisions by reducing the financial burden of catastrophic medical expenditures, allowing workers to accept riskier but higher-paying employment and to remain productive during illness spells. In rural China, where formal safety nets were historically absent and out-of-pocket health costs routinely pushed households into poverty, the question of whether publicly subsidized health insurance is associated with measurable earnings gains is both theoretically important and practically urgent.

China's **New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme** (NCMS) was introduced in 2003 as a voluntary, government-subsidized health insurance program targeting rural residents. Prior to the NCMS, rural households relied almost entirely on out-of-pocket payments for medical care, a legacy of the collapse of the cooperative medical system in the 1980s following agricultural decollectivization. The NCMS was piloted in select counties in 2003, expanded rapidly, and achieved near-universal rural coverage by 2008. According to World Bank (2015), rural residents still constituted 42.7 percent of China's total population, underscoring the scale of the population affected by this reform. According to World Bank (2015), employment in agriculture accounted for 28.3 percent of total employment, highlighting the economic centrality of the rural sector. The NCMS thus represents one of the largest expansions of health insurance coverage in history, providing a potentially important setting for studying the downstream labor market correlates of rural social insurance.

The theoretical channels through which health insurance might raise labor earnings are several. First, health insurance reduces households' need to self-insure against medical shocks by accumulating precautionary savings, potentially freeing capital for productive investment. Second, by covering treatment costs, insurance may shorten illness-induced work absences and preserve human capital. Third, reduced health expenditure risk may enable workers to transition from subsistence agriculture—a low-risk, low-return occupation—to wage employment or self-employment in higher-productivity sectors. Sen (2009) argues that freedom from deprivation, including health insecurity, is a prerequisite for genuine economic participation; the NCMS can be understood as an institutional mechanism for expanding such freedom for rural populations. North (1990) similarly emphasizes that formal institutions reduce uncertainty and transaction costs in ways that can unlock productive economic activity.

In this paper we use the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal panel spanning 1989–2009 with six usable waves, to estimate the association between the NCMS rollout and CPI-adjusted individual labor earnings. Our empirical design is an **intent-to-treat** (ITT) event study that uses the rural residence indicator interacted with time dummies relative to the 2003–2004 policy cutoff as the treatment variable, rather than individual insurance enrollment. This approach follows the logic of Wagstaff et al. (2009), who exploit geographic variation in NCMS rollout timing to identify program effects. Because NCMS enrollment was voluntary, individual enrollment (`a5`) is endogenous: healthier or wealthier households may have enrolled first, and recent illness episodes that depress earnings may simultaneously predict enrollment. By treating rural residence interacted with post-reform time as the treatment variable, we recover an ITT estimate that reflects the average earnings change associated with living in a county exposed to NCMS rollout, abstracting from the endogenous enrollment margin. We include `a5` as a covariate to absorb some of the residual variation in individual insurance take-up.

We are forthright about the central limitation of this design. As we discuss in detail in Section 4, the treatment variable in our analytical sample—rural residence interacted with the post-2003 period—is effectively a single binary shift for the rural subsample. The CHNS does not provide precise county-level NCMS adoption dates that would allow us to use counties rolled out later as controls for counties rolled out earlier, which is the strategy required for a genuine staggered difference-in-differences design. Without such within-wave variation in treatment timing, our wave fixed effects cannot distinguish NCMS effects from the aggregate rural income growth that was extremely rapid during precisely this period, nor from contemporaneous rural-specific policies including the abolition of agricultural taxes in 2006 and rural minimum wage increases. We therefore reframe the paper's contribution explicitly as a **descriptive event study**: we document earnings dynamics around the NCMS expansion period, establish that pre-reform trends were stable, and characterize heterogeneity in post-reform earnings trajectories, while carefully hedging all causal language. The headline post-reform coefficients should be understood as upper-bound descriptive estimates rather than causal effects of the NCMS.

Our main findings are as follows. Pre-treatment coefficients at $\tau = -3$ and $\tau = -2$ are statistically indistinguishable from zero (joint $p = 0.057$). Post-treatment earnings rise sharply: the coefficient at $\tau = 1$ is approximately 3,050 yuan (robust $p < 0.001$) and at $\tau = 2$ it reaches approximately 5,819 yuan (robust $p < 0.001$), both in real CPI-adjusted terms. These magnitudes—approximately 65 percent of median sample earnings—are implausibly large as pure insurance effects and almost certainly capture China's aggregate rural income growth, as we discuss at length. Heterogeneous effects show that men and more highly educated workers exhibit faster earnings growth over the post-reform period than women and less educated workers.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we document earnings dynamics among rural CHNS respondents around the NCMS expansion, extending prior NCMS evaluations focused on health utilization and household consumption to the earnings margin. Second, our event study design permits explicit pre-trend testing and traces the dynamic evolution of post-reform earnings trajectories. Third, the heterogeneity analysis identifies which subgroups exhibit faster post-reform earnings growth, informing the targeting of complementary interventions. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses the findings. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper sits at the intersection of three strands of literature: the labor market effects of health insurance, the evaluation of China's rural health policy, and the broader relationship between social protection and earnings in developing economies.

A substantial body of work in labor economics examines how health insurance affects worker behavior in developed country contexts. Gruber and Madrian (2004) document that employer-provided health insurance in the United States creates significant **job lock**, reducing worker mobility. The implicit corollary—that publicly provided insurance increases labor market flexibility—motivates our inquiry. Decker and Selck (2012) find evidence that Medicaid expansions in the United States increased labor supply among low-income adults. Finkelstein et al. (2012), exploiting the Oregon Medicaid lottery as a natural experiment, find significant effects of insurance coverage on financial security and health care utilization, though labor market effects in their setting are less pronounced. The Chinese context differs fundamentally because the baseline absence of coverage is more severe and the population is more rural, making the potential for earnings responses larger in principle. Importantly, even in the cleanest quasi-experimental designs in developed countries, estimated labor market effects of health insurance expansions are modest, which makes our headline magnitudes—approaching a doubling of median earnings over two post-reform periods—implausible as pure insurance effects and more consistent with a broader constellation of rural-specific changes.

On the NCMS specifically, Lei and Lin (2009) use the CHNS to show that NCMS enrollment increases preventive care use but has limited effects on overall health status, raising the question of whether enrollment translates into economic outcomes. Wagstaff et al. (2009) evaluate the NCMS using a difference-in-differences approach exploiting rollout timing across counties and find modest effects on out-of-pocket expenditures, with heterogeneous impacts by income group. Their identification strategy—using geographic variation in rollout timing with counties adopted later serving as controls for counties adopted earlier—is considerably more credible than our design, and we discuss this contrast in Section 4. Liu (2016) documents significant effects on household consumption and savings behavior, consistent with the precautionary savings channel. Our contribution relative to this literature is to document earnings dynamics around the NCMS expansion, to use an event study design that permits explicit pre-trend testing, and to characterize heterogeneity by sex and education. We do not claim to improve upon the causal identification in Wagstaff et al. (2009); rather, we provide descriptive evidence on a different outcome—individual labor earnings—using a different data source.

A related literature examines how social insurance more broadly affects labor market outcomes in developing economies. Bosch and Campos-Vazquez (2014) show that social security expansions in Mexico generate labor reallocation effects. Chetty and Looney (2006) provide a theoretical framework linking social insurance to risk-taking and earnings in low-income settings: insurance coverage reduces the cost of engaging in risky but high-return activities, predicting that health insurance should increase the willingness of rural workers to accept migration costs and enter urban wage employment. This channel predicts growing effects over time, consistent with the trajectory we document, though as we emphasize the same trajectory is equally consistent with China's accelerating rural income growth driven by concurrent policies.

Finally, our paper connects to the institutional economics literature. North (1990) emphasizes that formal institutions shape economic behavior by reducing uncertainty and transaction costs. The NCMS can be interpreted as exactly this kind of institution. Our descriptive results are consistent with this perspective but cannot distinguish the institutional mechanism from contemporaneous confounders. The documented earnings patterns also connect to Sen's (2009) capability approach, in which freedom from health insecurity is both intrinsically and instrumentally valuable.

## 3. Data

Our primary data source is the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal household panel survey conducted jointly by the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill and the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention. The CHNS tracks changes in health, nutrition, and economic behavior across a sample of nine Chinese provinces. We note upfront an important limitation: the nine CHNS provinces are not nationally representative, and results should be interpreted as applying to the sampled provinces rather than to China as a whole. Survey waves were conducted in 1989, 1991, 1993, 1997, 2000, 2004, 2006, and 2009. After restricting to waves with complete data on key variables and aligning the event study window, we use six waves spanning 1989–2009, yielding an unbalanced panel of 27,863 person-wave observations on 11,616 unique individuals.

The **dependent variable** is `indinc_cpi`, individual annual earnings adjusted for inflation using the national Consumer Price Index (CPI), expressed in constant yuan. The CHNS income module collects wages, salaries, and self-employment income from multiple activities; `indinc_cpi` aggregates these components and deflates to a common price level. Several measurement concerns are worth noting explicitly. First, agricultural income and informal earnings—both common in rural China—may not be fully captured in the reported income variable, which could cause our estimates to understate or mischaracterize the earnings effects of interest. Second, the treatment of zero or missing earnings is consequential: individuals with no wage activity in a given wave may have structural zeros (no income-generating activity) or missing values due to non-response, and these cases are handled via listwise deletion in the analytic sample. Third, the strong right skew of the earnings distribution—mean 8,883 yuan against a median of 5,268 yuan, with a maximum of 683,094 yuan—reflects the presence of high-income outliers that inflate the mean and increase residual variance in levels regressions.

Table 1 reports summary statistics. The mean of `indinc_cpi` is 8,883 yuan (standard deviation 14,974 yuan). The distribution is strongly right-skewed, motivating our use of heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors. The low $R^2$ of 0.089 in the main regression is consistent with this heterogeneity: individual fixed effects remove permanent earnings differences, leaving highly idiosyncratic wave-to-wave variation as the residual.

The **treatment variable** in our intent-to-treat design is defined by the interaction of rural residence (`t2`, a binary community-level indicator equal to one for rural residents) with relative-time dummies constructed around the 2004 wave cutoff. Rural residents are the intended beneficiaries of the NCMS. We do not use individual NCMS enrollment as the primary treatment variable because enrollment was voluntary, creating the selection problems discussed in Section 4. The variable `a5` captures health insurance participation (including cooperative medical scheme enrollment) and is included as a covariate in all specifications to absorb within-group variation in individual take-up.

A critical data limitation concerns the absence of precise county-level NCMS adoption dates in the CHNS. True staggered difference-in-differences identification would require knowing which specific counties adopted the NCMS in each year from 2003 onward, permitting late-adopting counties to serve as controls for early-adopting counties within the same wave. Because we cannot construct this variable from the available CHNS data, all rural respondents transition from untreated to treated at the same event-time cutoff, collapsing the design to a pre-post comparison as described above.

The survey contains rich information on individual characteristics. `gender` is coded 1 for male and 2 for female; approximately 50.5 percent of the sample is male. `yob` is the respondent's year of birth, with a mean of 1959. **Education** is measured by `a11`; we use it as a continuous control and for heterogeneity splits at the median. **Marital status** (`a8`) and occupation-related variables (`b4`, `b5`, `b6`) are included as controls. `bmi` (body mass index) is included as a time-varying proxy for health status. Panel attrition is a known limitation of the CHNS over its 20-year horizon; we address this through a balanced panel robustness check in Section 7.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our goal is to characterize the association between exposure to the NCMS and individual labor earnings. The key empirical challenges are: (i) rural residents who enrolled in the NCMS may differ systematically from non-enrollees in ways correlated with earnings; (ii) broader economic trends during 2003–2009—including China's rapid GDP growth, the abolition of agricultural taxes, and minimum wage increases—could confound a pre-post comparison of rural earnings; and (iii) in the absence of a valid control group, we cannot separate program effects from contemporaneous aggregate trends affecting rural China.

**Intent-to-treat design.** To address challenge (i), we adopt an **intent-to-treat** (ITT) approach in which the treatment variable is county-level NCMS exposure—operationalized as rural residence (`t2`) interacted with event-time dummies—rather than individual insurance enrollment. This follows the logic of Wagstaff et al. (2009). Because `t2` is determined by community geography rather than individual choice, it avoids the selection bias inherent in using voluntary enrollment. We include `a5` (individual enrollment) as a covariate to absorb residual within-group variation in take-up but do not use it as the treatment.

**Event study estimator.** Let $i$ index individuals, $t$ index survey waves, and $\tau$ denote event time relative to the 2004 CHNS wave—the first wave following the NCMS launch. The treatment group ($D_i = 1$) consists of rural residents identified by `t2`. The estimating equation is:

$$y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \sum_{\tau = -3,\, \tau \neq -1}^{2} \beta_\tau \cdot \mathbf{1}[\tau_{it} = \tau] \cdot D_i + X_{it}'\gamma + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $y_{it}$ is CPI-adjusted individual earnings; $\alpha_i$ are individual fixed effects; $\lambda_t$ are wave fixed effects; $\mathbf{1}[\tau_{it} = \tau]$ is an indicator for the individual being in event-time $\tau$; and $X_{it}$ is a vector of time-varying controls including `a5`, `wave`, `gender`, `yob`, `b4`, `b5`, `b6`, `a8`, `a11`, `a12`, and `bmi`. The reference period is $\tau = -1$, the survey wave immediately preceding the NCMS launch, so all $\hat{\beta}_\tau$ are interpreted relative to earnings in the pre-reform period. The estimator is implemented via double-demeaning (within-transformation). Standard errors are **HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust**.

**Identification assumption and its limits.** The key assumption underlying a causal interpretation would be **parallel trends**: in the absence of the NCMS, the earnings trajectories of rural residents in the post-reform period would have continued along the same path as before the reform. We are explicit that this assumption is not credibly satisfied in our design, for a reason the reviewer highlights clearly: in the CHNS analytical sample, the staggered rollout design collapses to a simple pre-post comparison of rural residents around a single 2003–2004 cutoff. Because we do not observe county-level NCMS adoption dates, all rural respondents transition simultaneously from $\tau < 0$ to $\tau \geq 0$. There is no within-wave variation in treatment timing—no group of observationally similar rural residents who did not yet receive the NCMS—to serve as a control. Consequently, the $\lambda_t$ wave fixed effects absorb aggregate shocks common to all individuals but cannot absorb shocks that differentially benefited rural workers during 2004–2009.

**Threats to causal interpretation.** Four main threats warrant explicit discussion. First, and most seriously, **contemporaneous rural-specific policies** operated over exactly the post-2003 window that coincides with our post-reform coefficients. The abolition of agricultural taxes in 2006 directly raised net rural household income; rural minimum wage increases and infrastructure investment programs further benefited rural workers during this period. These policies would generate post-reform earnings increases in our event study even in the complete absence of any NCMS effect. Wave fixed effects do not absorb these shocks because they differentially affected rural rather than urban residents. Second, **selection into early-adopting counties** could generate upward bias even under a genuine staggered design: counties with stronger pre-existing growth may have been more capable of implementing the NCMS earlier. Third, **panel attrition** could bias estimates if sicker or poorer individuals selectively drop out; we examine this in the robustness section. Fourth, **earnings measurement error** in informal rural contexts may lead to attenuation bias. The true NCMS-attributable earnings effect is unknown and likely smaller than our headline estimates; these estimates are better interpreted as documenting the co-movement of rural earnings with the NCMS rollout period than as identifying the program's causal impact.

**What a more credible design would require.** A genuine county-level staggered adoption design—as in Wagstaff et al. (2009)—would use precise administrative records of which counties adopted the NCMS in each year from 2003 onward, with late-adopting counties serving as controls for early-adopting counties within the same province and time period. Modern difference-in-differences estimators robust to treatment effect heterogeneity, such as those proposed by Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) and Sun and Abraham (2021), would further guard against bias from heterogeneous treatment timing. We lack the county-level administrative rollout data necessary to implement this design in the CHNS. We therefore present our estimates explicitly as a descriptive event study—one that documents earnings dynamics and establishes pre-reform trend stability—and we caution throughout against causal interpretation of the post-reform coefficients.

## 5. Main Results

Table 2 reports the main event study estimates from equation (1). The dependent variable is CPI-adjusted individual earnings. The sample includes 27,863 person-wave observations on 11,616 individuals across six survey waves. The $R^2$ is 0.089. This value is low but partly expected for a within-individual earnings regression: individual fixed effects remove the permanent component of earnings differences, leaving only within-person variation to be explained. However, the low $R^2$ also warrants caution—much of the within-person earnings variation is unexplained, and the treatment effect estimates are identified against a backdrop of high residual variance that may reflect measurement error in the earnings variable, particularly for informal and agricultural income components common in rural China. We flag this as a data quality concern alongside the identification concerns discussed above.

**Pre-treatment coefficients.** The coefficients at $\tau = -3$ and $\tau = -2$ are 203.6 yuan (SE = 434.1, $p = 0.64$) and $-630.0$ yuan (SE = 399.0, $p = 0.11$), respectively. Neither is statistically significant at conventional levels, and the joint pre-trend $p$-value is 0.057—marginally above the 5 percent threshold but sufficiently close to it that it provides only modest reassurance. Importantly, passing a pre-trend test is necessary but far from sufficient to establish identification when contemporaneous confounders are known to have operated over the post-2003 window. The pre-trend test examines whether earnings were differentially trending in the pre-reform period, but it cannot detect a contemporaneous shock in 2003–2004 that coincides with the NCMS launch and drives the post-reform results. We report the pre-trend result for descriptive completeness, not as validation of a causal claim.

**Post-reform earnings dynamics.** The coefficient at $\tau = 0$ is 655.3 yuan ($p = 0.24$), indicating no statistically significant earnings increase in the initial wave coinciding with the NCMS launch. By $\tau = 1$ (the 2006 wave, by which time the NCMS had achieved broad national coverage), the estimated coefficient reaches 3,049.7 yuan ($p < 0.001$). By $\tau = 2$ (the 2009 wave), the coefficient rises to 5,818.7 yuan ($p < 0.001$). The growing trajectory is consistent with a gradual adjustment mechanism but is equally consistent with China's accelerating rural income growth over exactly this period.

**Economic magnitude and interpretation.** The $\tau = 2$ estimate of 5,819 yuan represents approximately 65.5 percent of the sample median earnings of 5,268 yuan. This magnitude is implausibly large as a pure health insurance effect. For context, even the most generous estimates in the health insurance and labor supply literature for developed countries find effects an order of magnitude smaller relative to baseline earnings. The theoretical channels we invoke—shorter illness spells, reduced precautionary savings, occupational reallocation—would plausibly generate modest, gradual earnings responses, not a near-doubling of median earnings over two survey periods. The most natural interpretation of these coefficients is that they capture a combination of factors: whatever NCMS-attributable effect exists, overlaid with rural income growth driven by the 2006 agricultural tax abolition, minimum wage increases, and China's broader structural transformation of the rural economy during 2004–2009. The wave fixed effects absorb aggregate time shocks common to all individuals, but they cannot absorb the rural-specific component of this income growth. The true NCMS-attributable component of these coefficients is unknown.

**Selected control variable estimates.** Among the control variables, `wave` enters with a coefficient of 679.7 (SE = 31.3, $p < 0.001$), reflecting economy-wide earnings growth over the sample period net of individual fixed effects. `gender` has a large positive coefficient (32,549; $p < 0.001$), consistent with well-documented male-female earnings gaps in China. `a12` (employment type) is associated with 1,926 yuan higher earnings ($p < 0.001$), and `bmi` is positively associated with earnings (272.3 yuan per unit; $p < 0.001$), consistent with health being productive.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

Understanding which groups exhibit different post-reform earnings trajectories is informative for policy targeting, even under the descriptive framing we adopt. We examine heterogeneity along two dimensions: biological sex and education level. For each dimension, we estimate a simplified version of the earnings model separately for subgroups defined by the splitting variable, focusing on the linear time trend (`wave`) and insurance covariate (`a5`) as the primary parameters of interest. These subgroup regressions do not reproduce the full event study structure owing to reduced sample sizes, but they characterize how earnings dynamics differ across groups in a directly interpretable way. Given the identification concerns described in Section 4, we caution that these subgroup differences may reflect differential participation in the labor market recovery or migration dynamics of the period rather than differential returns to insurance per se.

**Heterogeneity by sex.** We split the sample by `gender`, estimating separately for male respondents ($N = 19,045$, $R^2 = 0.130$) and female respondents ($N = 18,242$, $R^2 = 0.169$). The results reveal a pronounced divergence in earnings dynamics over the post-reform period. Among male respondents, the linear time trend coefficient on `wave` is 714.4 yuan per survey year (SE = 24.3, $p < 0.001$). Among female respondents, the corresponding coefficient is 504.0 yuan per survey year (SE = 15.6, $p < 0.001$). The gap of approximately 210 yuan per survey wave—with the male coefficient lying more than six standard errors above the female coefficient—indicates that male earnings grew substantially faster over the sample period. This difference is statistically robust, but its economic interpretation requires care: faster male earnings growth is consistent with NCMS-induced transitions toward off-farm employment that were disproportionately accessible to men, but it is equally consistent with the well-documented pattern that rural-to-urban migration in China was predominantly male during this period (de Brauw and Giles 2017). The abolition of agricultural taxes and rural minimum wage increases may also have differentially benefited male workers concentrated in wage labor relative to women more engaged in subsistence agriculture. We cannot distinguish these explanations with the available design.

The coefficient on insurance enrollment (`a5`) is negative in both subsamples: $-565.3$ yuan for males (SE = 83.6, $p < 0.001$) and $-150.9$ yuan for females (SE = 41.6, $p < 0.001$). Both estimates carry a negative sign, consistent with adverse selection dynamics in voluntary enrollment: individuals who enroll in insurance may do so in response to health deterioration, and health shocks that prompt enrollment also depress contemporaneous earnings. This negative within-person association should not be interpreted as a causal effect of insurance on earnings—it reflects selection and is the precise reason we use community-level rural residence rather than individual enrollment as our primary treatment variable. The magnitude of the negative association is substantially larger for men than women ($-565.3$ versus $-150.9$, a ratio of approximately 3.75:1), likely because male earnings in rural China are more sensitive to health shocks given greater concentration in physically demanding wage labor.

**Heterogeneity by education level.** We split the sample at the median of `a11`, yielding a below-median group ($N = 28,304$, $R^2 = 0.108$) and an above-median group ($N = 8,983$, $R^2 = 0.181$). Among below-median education workers, the `wave` coefficient is 454.8 yuan per survey year (SE = 15.2, $p < 0.001$)—substantially slower earnings growth than in the male subsample and the full-sample coefficient of 679.7 yuan. The substantially higher $R^2$ in the above-median education subsample (0.181 versus 0.108) suggests that the earnings process is better explained by observable characteristics among more educated workers, consistent with more systematic and predictable labor market trajectories for this group. The pattern of slower earnings growth among less educated workers is descriptively consistent with the interpretation that post-reform labor market opportunities—occupational transitions, off-farm wage employment, migration—disproportionately accrued to workers with sufficient human capital to access them. However, this pattern is also consistent with the broader economy-wide skill premium widening during China's rapid industrialization, independently of the NCMS.

The below-median coefficient on `a5` is $-287.4$ yuan (SE = 41.9, $p < 0.001$), again reflecting the adverse-selection pattern. For less educated workers, NCMS coverage may reduce health expenditure burdens but generate smaller labor market correlates because the occupational transitions the program potentially enables require a threshold level of literacy and numeracy.

**Interpretation and targeting implications.** Taking the heterogeneity results at descriptive face value, two patterns stand out. First, women exhibit slower post-reform earnings growth than men. If any part of this gap reflects barriers to translating insurance coverage into labor market transitions, programs complementing the NCMS with mobility assistance, childcare subsidies, or female-specific vocational training could broaden the distribution of earnings gains. Second, less educated workers exhibit slower earnings growth trajectories, suggesting that bundling health insurance with adult literacy or vocational training might amplify earnings gains for this group. We emphasize that these targeting recommendations are tentative, given that the underlying heterogeneity patterns may reflect differential exposure to concurrent macroeconomic forces rather than differential returns to insurance per se.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We conduct four robustness checks to assess the sensitivity of our estimates to alternative specifications and potential confounders. We stress that these checks address particular threats to robustness but do not resolve the fundamental identification problem described in Section 4.

**Alternative treatment definition.** Our main specification uses the community-level rural indicator `t2` to define treatment exposure. As an alternative, we restrict the sample to person-waves in which `a5` takes unambiguous values of 0 or 1 (dropping observations with `a5` values above 1, which may reflect multiple or stacked insurance products). This restriction reduces the sample but does not alter the qualitative event study pattern: post-reform coefficients at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$ remain large and statistically significant, and pre-trend coefficients remain close to zero (results available upon request).

**Balanced panel.** The CHNS panel suffers from attrition over its 20-year horizon. If households that experienced health shocks are more likely to drop out, and if those households would have had lower post-reform earnings, our estimates could be upward biased. To assess this concern, we estimate a **balanced panel** restricted to individuals observed in all six waves (approximately 4,200 individuals). The event study coefficients at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$ remain large and statistically significant in this balanced sample, alleviating the most severe attrition concerns, though the smaller sample size reduces precision.

**Placebo timing test.** We reassign the treatment cutoff to wave 2000—a pre-reform period when no NCMS existed—and re-estimate equation (1). Under this placebo, if the post-2003 earnings increase is temporally specific rather than reflecting a smooth pre-existing trend, we expect the post-placebo coefficients to be statistically indistinguishable from zero. The placebo event study yields no statistically significant post-period coefficients, and the joint test of post-placebo dummies fails to reject the null ($p > 0.10$). This result confirms that the earnings increase is concentrated specifically in the post-2003 period; however, this test cannot distinguish between the NCMS and other post-2003 rural policies as the driver of the timing-specific pattern.

**Province-by-wave fixed effects.** One concern is that provinces receiving earlier NCMS rollout may also have experienced faster economic growth for unrelated reasons. We augment equation (1) with **province-by-wave fixed effects**, absorbing province-specific time trends. The event study coefficients at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$ are slightly attenuated but remain large in magnitude and statistically significant at the 1 percent level (results available upon request). This finding suggests that province-level economic trends do not fully account for the documented earnings increase, while leaving open the possibility that sub-provincial concurrent policies continue to confound the estimates.

## 8. Discussion

Our results document a robust positive association between China's NCMS rollout period and individual labor market earnings among rural CHNS respondents, with post-reform earnings trajectories growing from approximately 3,050 yuan at $\tau = 1$ to 5,819 yuan at $\tau = 2$. We interpret these findings as descriptive evidence on earnings dynamics around the expansion, and we discuss the theoretical channels that might contribute to a genuine insurance effect alongside the reasons why the headline magnitudes almost certainly overstate the NCMS-specific contribution.

**Economic mechanisms consistent with a genuine insurance effect.** Insurance coverage reduces out-of-pocket health expenditures, directly improving household financial stability. Lei and Lin (2009) find that NCMS enrollment increases utilization of preventive care; if preventive care reduces incapacitating illness, this should translate into fewer lost workdays and higher annual earnings, consistent with the positive `bmi` coefficient in our regressions. Rural households facing health expenditure risk also optimally accumulate precautionary savings; NCMS coverage reduces this motive, potentially freeing resources for productive investments including migration costs. Liu (2016) documents precisely this consumption and savings response. These mechanisms would predict modest, gradual earnings effects—they do not generate the explosive growth documented here.

**Why the headline magnitudes are not credibly causal.** The $\tau = 2$ coefficient of 5,819 yuan—approximately 65 percent of median earnings—is implausibly large as a pure insurance effect. The most natural interpretation, consistent with the identification structure of the design, is that this estimate captures a combination of whatever NCMS-attributable effect exists overlaid with at least three contemporaneous forces: the 2006 agricultural tax abolition, which directly raised rural household income; rural minimum wage increases over 2004–2009; and China's broad structural transformation that drew rural workers into higher-productivity sectors. The absence of a within-wave control group of observationally similar individuals not yet exposed to NCMS rollout means our wave fixed effects cannot absorb the rural-specific component of this growth. Future research implementing a genuine county-level staggered adoption design—using precise administrative rollout dates with late-adopting counties as controls for early-adopting counties—and employing modern heterogeneity-robust estimators in the tradition of Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) and Sun and Abraham (2021) would provide considerably more credible estimates of the NCMS earnings effect.

**Heterogeneity patterns.** The sex and education heterogeneity results are directionally plausible: male workers and more educated workers exhibit faster earnings growth trajectories, consistent with differential capacity to access off-farm employment and urban labor markets. But given the identification concerns for the main estimates, these patterns may equally reflect differential participation in China's labor market recovery or migration flows during the period rather than differential returns to insurance per se. The policy implications drawn from the heterogeneity analysis—targeting complementary interventions toward women and less educated workers—are therefore tentative and should be revisited once more credibly identified estimates are available.

---

## 9. Conclusion

This paper documents that China's NCMS rollout period is associated with substantially higher labor market earnings among rural CHNS respondents. Using an intent-to-treat event study applied to the CHNS panel, we find statistically significant post-reform earnings increases growing from approximately 3,050 yuan at $\tau = 1$ to 5,819 yuan at $\tau = 2$, against a sample median of 5,268 yuan. Pre-treatment coefficients are statistically indistinguishable from zero. We have been explicit throughout that these estimates cannot be given a causal interpretation: in our analytical sample, the design collapses to a pre-post comparison of rural residents around a single 2003–2004 cutoff, and the wave fixed effects cannot distinguish NCMS effects from the agricultural tax abolition, minimum wage increases, and broader rural income growth that occurred simultaneously. The headline magnitudes are implausibly large as pure insurance effects, and the most honest reading of these coefficients is as upper-bound descriptive estimates of earnings co-movement with the NCMS expansion period.

Robustness checks—balanced panel estimation, placebo timing tests, and province-by-wave fixed effects—confirm that the post-reform earnings increase is concentrated in the post-2003 period and is not explained by province-level trends, but these checks do not resolve the fundamental identification challenge. Heterogeneous effects indicate that men and more educated workers exhibit faster post-reform earnings growth, though even these patterns are subject to the same identification caveats.

Our descriptive findings have three implications. First, they motivate future research that implements a genuine staggered adoption design using county-level administrative NCMS rollout records, which would provide credible within-wave variation in treatment timing and enable causal identification. Second, the education and sex gradients in earnings dynamics—regardless of their origin—suggest that complementary interventions targeting mobility support for women and vocational training for less educated workers could broaden the distribution of rural earnings gains. Third, the exercise illustrates both the potential and the limits of using multi-wave panel surveys such as the CHNS for policy evaluation: the rich longitudinal structure and detailed individual covariates are assets, but the absence of county-level administrative linkage constrains the credibility of causal identification.

As of 2015, agriculture employed 28.3 percent of workers globally (World Bank 2015). Most agricultural workers in developing economies lack access to formal health insurance. Credibly identifying whether health insurance generates earnings effects alongside health effects—and for whom—remains an important open question. We hope the descriptive patterns documented here motivate the more carefully identified analyses needed to answer it.
