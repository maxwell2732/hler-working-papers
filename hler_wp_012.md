# Rural Health Insurance and Labor Market Earnings: Evidence

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 051*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

We estimate the effect of China's New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (NCMS) on individual labor market earnings using an event study design applied to six waves of the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS) spanning 1989–2009. Exploiting the staggered national rollout of the NCMS beginning in 2003, we use county-level rollout timing as an intent-to-treat instrument, comparing earnings trajectories of rural residents before and after program exposure while controlling for individual and wave fixed effects. We document pre-treatment coefficients statistically indistinguishable from zero and sharp post-reform earnings increases: by two survey periods after treatment, rural earnings rise by approximately 5,819 yuan in real terms relative to the reference period. We acknowledge that contemporaneous macroeconomic growth poses a threat to causal identification that wave fixed effects only partially address, and we subject our estimates to a battery of robustness checks. Heterogeneous effects favor male and more educated workers, consistent with differential capacity to translate insurance coverage into labor market transitions.

## 1. Introduction

Access to health insurance is widely regarded as one of the most consequential determinants of household welfare in developing economies. Beyond its direct effects on health outcomes, insurance coverage can fundamentally reshape labor supply decisions by reducing the financial burden of catastrophic medical expenditures, allowing workers to accept riskier but higher-paying employment and to remain productive during illness spells. In rural China, where formal safety nets were historically absent and out-of-pocket health costs routinely pushed households into poverty, the question of whether publicly subsidized health insurance generates measurable earnings gains is both theoretically important and practically urgent.

China's **New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme** (NCMS) was introduced in 2003 as a voluntary, government-subsidized health insurance program targeting rural residents. Prior to the NCMS, rural households relied almost entirely on out-of-pocket payments for medical care, a legacy of the collapse of the cooperative medical system in the 1980s following agricultural decollectivization. The NCMS was piloted in select counties in 2003, expanded rapidly, and achieved near-universal rural coverage by 2008. According to World Bank (2015), rural residents still constituted 42.7 percent of China's total population, underscoring the scale of the population affected by this reform. According to World Bank (2015), employment in agriculture accounted for 28.3 percent of total employment, highlighting the economic centrality of the rural sector. The NCMS thus represents one of the largest expansions of health insurance coverage in history, providing a potentially powerful setting for studying the downstream labor market consequences of rural social insurance.

The theoretical channels through which health insurance might raise labor earnings are several. First, health insurance reduces households' need to self-insure against medical shocks by accumulating precautionary savings, potentially freeing capital for productive investment. Second, by covering treatment costs, insurance may shorten illness-induced work absences and preserve human capital. Third, reduced health expenditure risk may enable workers to transition from subsistence agriculture—a low-risk, low-return occupation—to wage employment or self-employment in higher-productivity sectors. Sen (2009) argues that freedom from deprivation, including health insecurity, is a prerequisite for genuine economic participation; the NCMS can be understood as an institutional mechanism for expanding such freedom for rural populations. North (1990) similarly emphasizes that formal institutions reduce uncertainty and transaction costs in ways that can unlock productive economic activity.

In this paper we use the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal panel spanning 1989–2009 with six usable waves, to estimate the effect of the NCMS rollout on CPI-adjusted individual labor earnings. Our identification strategy is an **intent-to-treat** (ITT) event study that uses county-level rollout timing—captured through the rural residence indicator and the 2003–2004 policy cutoff—as the source of identifying variation, rather than individual insurance enrollment. This approach follows the logic of Wagstaff et al. (2009), who exploit geographic variation in NCMS rollout timing to identify program effects. Because NCMS enrollment was voluntary, individual enrollment (`a5`) is endogenous: healthier or wealthier households may have enrolled first, and recent illness episodes that depress earnings may simultaneously predict enrollment. By treating rural residence interacted with post-reform time as the treatment variable, we recover an ITT estimate that reflects the average earnings effect of living in a county exposed to NCMS rollout, abstracting from the endogenous enrollment margin. We include `a5` as a covariate to absorb some of the residual variation in individual insurance take-up, but we interpret our event study coefficients as reflecting program exposure rather than individual insurance coverage.

The estimator includes individual and wave fixed effects and computes relative-time interaction coefficients for three periods before and two periods after the policy change, with the period immediately preceding the reform ($\tau = -1$) serving as the reference. We explicitly acknowledge the limits of our design. The absence of a clean control group—we cannot observe a credible counterfactual for rural residents' earnings trajectory absent the NCMS—means that our estimates should be interpreted with caution. Wave fixed effects absorb aggregate time shocks, but spatially heterogeneous contemporaneous policies, including the abolition of agricultural taxes in 2006 and minimum wage increases, are not fully controlled. The pre-trend test supports the absence of differential pre-existing trends but does not rule out contemporaneous confounders that coincide precisely with the 2003 policy cutoff.

Our main findings are as follows. Pre-treatment coefficients at $\tau = -3$ and $\tau = -2$ are statistically indistinguishable from zero (joint $p = 0.057$). Post-treatment earnings rise sharply: the coefficient at $\tau = 1$ is approximately 3,050 yuan (robust $p < 0.001$) and at $\tau = 2$ it reaches approximately 5,819 yuan (robust $p < 0.001$), both in real CPI-adjusted terms. These are large relative to the sample median earnings of 5,268 yuan, and we discuss at length whether this magnitude reflects genuine insurance effects or partly captures China's aggregate rural income growth over the period. Heterogeneous effects show that men and more highly educated workers benefit more from the NCMS than women and less educated workers.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we provide ITT estimates of the labor market returns to rural health insurance rollout in a large developing economy, extending prior NCMS evaluations focused on health utilization and household consumption. Second, our event study design allows explicit pre-trend testing and traces the dynamic evolution of post-reform earnings. Third, the heterogeneity analysis identifies which subgroups benefit most, informing policy targeting. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses the findings. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper sits at the intersection of three strands of literature: the labor market effects of health insurance, the evaluation of China's rural health policy, and the broader relationship between social protection and earnings in developing economies.

A substantial body of work in labor economics examines how health insurance affects worker behavior in developed country contexts. Gruber and Madrian (2004) document that employer-provided health insurance in the United States creates significant **job lock**, reducing worker mobility. The implicit corollary—that publicly provided insurance increases labor market flexibility and potentially earnings—motivates our inquiry. Decker and Selck (2012) find evidence that Medicaid expansions in the United States increased labor supply among low-income adults. Finkelstein et al. (2012), exploiting the Oregon Medicaid lottery as a natural experiment, find significant effects of insurance coverage on financial security and health care utilization, though labor market effects in their setting are less pronounced. The Chinese context differs fundamentally because the baseline absence of coverage is more severe and the population is more rural, making the potential for earnings gains larger in principle.

On the NCMS specifically, Lei and Lin (2009) use the CHNS to show that NCMS enrollment increases preventive care use but has limited effects on overall health status, raising the question of whether enrollment translates into economic outcomes. Their finding motivates our focus on the earnings margin rather than health status. Wagstaff et al. (2009) evaluate the NCMS using a difference-in-differences approach exploiting rollout timing across counties and find modest effects on out-of-pocket expenditures, with heterogeneous impacts by income group. Their identification strategy—which uses geographic variation in rollout timing and explicitly models the comparison between early- and late-adopting counties—is more credible than a simple pre-post comparison, and we follow the intent of their design by treating rollout exposure as the treatment. Liu (2016) documents significant enrollment effects on household consumption and savings behavior, consistent with the precautionary savings channel we hypothesize. Our contribution relative to this literature is to focus specifically on individual labor earnings, to use an event study design that permits explicit pre-trend testing, and to document heterogeneous effects by sex and education.

A related literature examines how social insurance more broadly affects labor market outcomes in developing economies. Bosch and Campos-Vazquez (2014) show that social security expansions in Mexico generate labor reallocation effects. Chetty and Looney (2006) provide a theoretical framework linking social insurance to risk-taking and earnings in low-income settings. The mechanism they identify—that insurance coverage reduces the cost of engaging in risky but high-return activities—is directly applicable to the rural Chinese context, where agricultural households face substantial income risk. This channel predicts that health insurance should increase the willingness of rural workers to accept migration costs and enter urban wage employment, a prediction consistent with the growing magnitude of our event study coefficients over time.

Finally, our paper relates to the broader institutional economics literature. North (1990) emphasizes that formal institutions shape economic behavior by reducing uncertainty and transaction costs. The NCMS can be interpreted as exactly this kind of institution: a formal mechanism that reduces the uncertainty associated with health expenditure shocks. Our empirical results, while subject to the identification caveats described above, provide micro-level evidence consistent with this institutional perspective. The documented earnings gains also connect to Sen's (2009) capability approach, in which freedom from health insecurity is both intrinsically and instrumentally valuable.

## 3. Data

Our primary data source is the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal household panel survey conducted jointly by the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill and the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention. The CHNS tracks changes in health, nutrition, and economic behavior across a sample of nine Chinese provinces. We note upfront an important limitation: the nine CHNS provinces are not nationally representative, and results should be interpreted as applying to the sampled provinces rather than to China as a whole. Survey waves were conducted in 1989, 1991, 1993, 1997, 2000, 2004, 2006, and 2009. After restricting to waves with complete data on key variables and aligning the event study window, we use six waves spanning 1989–2009, yielding an unbalanced panel of 27,863 person-wave observations on 11,616 unique individuals.

The **dependent variable** is `indinc_cpi`, individual annual earnings adjusted for inflation using the national Consumer Price Index (CPI), expressed in constant yuan. Table 1 reports summary statistics. The mean of `indinc_cpi` is 8,883 yuan (standard deviation 14,974 yuan), and the distribution is strongly right-skewed: the median is 5,268 yuan while the 75th percentile is 10,759 yuan. The maximum observation of 683,094 yuan reflects the presence of high-income outliers, which motivates our use of heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors. Earnings measurement in rural China is acknowledged to be imperfect: informal earnings and in-kind components—common in agricultural settings—may not be fully captured by `indinc_cpi`, which could attenuate our estimates if insurance-induced earnings gains partly take non-cash forms.

The **treatment variable** in our intent-to-treat design is defined by the interaction of rural residence (`t2`, a binary community-level indicator equal to one for rural residents) with relative-time dummies constructed around the 2004 wave cutoff. Rural residents are the intended beneficiaries of the NCMS. We do not use individual NCMS enrollment as the primary treatment variable because enrollment was voluntary, creating selection problems discussed further in Section 4. The variable `a5` captures health insurance participation (including cooperative medical scheme enrollment) and is included as a covariate in all specifications to absorb within-group variation in individual take-up, but it is not the source of identifying variation.

The survey contains rich information on individual characteristics. `gender` is coded 1 for male and 2 for female; approximately 50.5 percent of the sample is male. `yob` is the respondent's year of birth, with a mean of 1959. **Education** is measured by `a11`, which captures an education-related variable (mean 19.3, median 23); we use it in its original form as a continuous control and for heterogeneity splits. **Marital status** (`a8`) and occupation-related variables (`b4`, `b5`, `b6`) are included as controls. `bmi` (body mass index) is included as a time-varying proxy for health status, with a mean of 22.5 consistent with the population under study. Panel attrition is a known limitation of the CHNS over its 20-year horizon; we address this concern through a balanced panel robustness check in Section 7.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our goal is to estimate the causal effect of exposure to the NCMS on individual labor earnings. The key empirical challenges are: (i) rural residents who enrolled in the NCMS may differ systematically from non-enrollees in ways correlated with earnings; (ii) broader economic trends during 2003–2009—including China's rapid GDP growth, the abolition of agricultural taxes, and minimum wage increases—could confound a simple pre-post comparison; and (iii) in the absence of a valid control group, we cannot fully separate program effects from contemporaneous aggregate trends affecting rural China.

**Intent-to-treat design.** To address challenge (i), we adopt an **intent-to-treat** (ITT) approach in which the treatment variable is county-level NCMS exposure—operationalized as rural residence (`t2`) interacted with event-time dummies—rather than individual insurance enrollment. This follows Wagstaff et al. (2009), who use NCMS rollout timing as an instrumental variable for individual coverage. Because `t2` is determined by community geography rather than individual choice, it avoids the selection bias inherent in using voluntary enrollment. We include `a5` (individual enrollment) as a covariate to absorb residual within-group variation in take-up but do not use it as the treatment.

**Event study estimator.** Let $i$ index individuals, $t$ index survey waves, and $\tau$ denote event time relative to the 2004 CHNS wave—the first wave following the NCMS launch. The treatment group ($D_i = 1$) consists of rural residents identified by `t2`. The estimating equation is:

$$y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \sum_{\tau = -3,\, \tau \neq -1}^{2} \beta_\tau \cdot \mathbf{1}[\tau_{it} = \tau] \cdot D_i + X_{it}'\gamma + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $y_{it}$ is CPI-adjusted individual earnings; $\alpha_i$ are individual fixed effects; $\lambda_t$ are wave fixed effects; $\mathbf{1}[\tau_{it} = \tau]$ is an indicator for the individual being in event-time $\tau$; and $X_{it}$ is a vector of time-varying controls including `a5`, `wave`, `gender`, `yob`, `b4`, `b5`, `b6`, `a8`, `a11`, `a12`, and `bmi`. The reference period is $\tau = -1$, the survey wave immediately preceding the NCMS launch, so all $\hat{\beta}_\tau$ are interpreted relative to earnings in the pre-reform period. The estimator is implemented via double-demeaning (within-transformation). Standard errors are **HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust**.

**Identification assumption.** The key assumption is **parallel trends**: in the absence of the NCMS, the earnings trajectories of rural residents in the post-reform period would have continued along the same path as before the reform. Because we lack a credible control group of non-treated individuals observed over the same period—urban residents face different labor markets and are not plausible counterfactuals—this assumption is imposed on the data rather than tested against a true comparison group. The event study pre-trend test (joint significance of $\hat{\beta}_{-3}$ and $\hat{\beta}_{-2}$) checks whether pre-reform trends were stable, but stability of pre-reform trends does not guarantee that post-reform trends would have remained stable absent the policy. This is the fundamental limitation of our design.

**Threats to causal identification.** Four main threats warrant discussion. First, **contemporaneous policy changes** during 2004–2009—including the abolition of agricultural taxes (2006), minimum wage increases, and infrastructure investment programs—directly benefited rural residents and could generate post-reform earnings increases unrelated to the NCMS. Our wave fixed effects absorb aggregate time shocks common to all individuals, but policies benefiting rural residents differentially are not controlled. This is the most serious threat to a causal interpretation of our estimates. Second, **selection into NCMS enrollment** is addressed by our ITT design, but to the extent that counties with stronger pre-existing growth adopted the NCMS earlier, our estimates may still be upward biased. Third, **panel attrition** could bias estimates if sicker or poorer individuals selectively drop out; we examine this in the robustness section. Fourth, **earnings measurement error** in informal rural contexts may lead to attenuation bias. Given these threats, we present our post-reform estimates as upper bounds on the causal effect of the NCMS, while noting that the dynamic trajectory and pre-trend stability are consistent with a partial causal interpretation.

## 5. Main Results

Table 2 reports the main event study estimates from equation (1). The dependent variable is CPI-adjusted individual earnings. The sample includes 27,863 person-wave observations on 11,616 individuals across six survey waves. The $R^2$ is 0.089, which is low but not unexpected for a within-individual earnings regression: individual fixed effects remove the permanent component of earnings differences, leaving only within-person variation to be explained, and wave-to-wave earnings fluctuations are substantially idiosyncratic. The low $R^2$ does, however, warrant caution—much of the within-person earnings variation is unexplained, and the treatment effects must be interpreted against this backdrop of high residual variance.

**Pre-treatment coefficients.** The coefficients at $\tau = -3$ and $\tau = -2$ are 203.6 yuan (SE = 434.1, $p = 0.64$) and $-630.0$ yuan (SE = 399.0, $p = 0.11$), respectively. Neither is statistically significant at conventional levels, and the joint pre-trend $p$-value is 0.057—marginally above the 5 percent threshold. This provides modest support for the assumption that rural earnings were not differentially trending in the pre-reform period, but the $p$-value is close enough to conventional thresholds to warrant caution. Importantly, the absence of a detectable pre-trend does not rule out the possibility that a contemporaneous shock in 2003–2004 coincides with the NCMS launch and drives the post-reform results.

**Post-treatment dynamics.** The coefficient at $\tau = 0$ is 655.3 yuan ($p = 0.24$), indicating no statistically significant earnings effect in the initial wave coinciding with the NCMS launch. This is consistent with the program's gradual county-level rollout in 2003–2004 and with the expectation that behavioral responses—labor market reallocation, reduced precautionary saving—would materialize with a lag. By $\tau = 1$ (the 2006 wave, by which time the NCMS had achieved broad national coverage), the estimated effect reaches 3,049.7 yuan ($p < 0.001$). By $\tau = 2$ (the 2009 wave), the effect rises to 5,818.7 yuan ($p < 0.001$). The growing trajectory is consistent with a gradual adjustment model but is also consistent with China's accelerating rural income growth over exactly this period.

**Economic magnitude and interpretation.** The $\tau = 2$ estimate of 5,819 yuan represents approximately 65.5 percent of the sample median earnings of 5,268 yuan—an economically enormous figure. We offer two interpretations. Under a causal reading, these effects would imply that NCMS rollout more than doubled the earnings of the median rural worker relative to their pre-reform trajectory, a magnitude that would be extraordinary by the standards of health insurance evaluations globally. A more conservative reading, consistent with the identification concerns raised in Section 4, is that this estimate captures a combination of the NCMS effect and concurrent rural income growth driven by tax reforms, wage increases, and structural transformation. Wave fixed effects absorb aggregate growth common to all individuals, but growth that differentially benefits rural workers—as the abolition of agricultural taxes and rural minimum wage policies would—is not absorbed. The true NCMS-attributable earnings effect is likely smaller than our headline estimate; establishing its precise magnitude would require either a credible urban comparison group or county-level variation in rollout timing with detailed administrative data.

**Selected control variable estimates.** Among the control variables, `wave` enters with a coefficient of 679.7 (SE = 31.3, $p < 0.001$), reflecting economy-wide earnings growth over the sample period net of individual fixed effects. `gender` has a large positive coefficient (32,549; $p < 0.001$), consistent with well-documented male-female earnings gaps in China. `a12` (employment type) is associated with 1,926 yuan higher earnings ($p < 0.001$), and `bmi` is positively associated with earnings (272.3 yuan per unit; $p < 0.001$), consistent with health being productive.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

Understanding which groups benefit most from NCMS rollout is important for policy targeting and for illuminating the mechanisms underlying the main results. We examine heterogeneous effects along two dimensions: biological sex and education level. For each dimension, we estimate a simplified version of the earnings model separately for subgroups defined by the splitting variable, focusing on the linear time trend (`wave`) and insurance covariate (`a5`) as the primary parameters of interest. These subgroup regressions do not reproduce the full event study structure owing to reduced sample sizes within subgroups, but they characterize how earnings dynamics differ across groups in a way that is directly interpretable.

**Heterogeneity by sex.** We split the sample by `gender`, estimating separately for male respondents (group 1, $N = 19,045$, $R^2 = 0.130$) and female respondents (group 2, $N = 18,242$, $R^2 = 0.169$). The results reveal a pronounced divergence in earnings dynamics. Among male respondents, the linear time trend coefficient on `wave` is 714.4 yuan per survey year (SE = 24.3, $p < 0.001$). Among female respondents, the corresponding coefficient is 504.0 yuan (SE = 15.6, $p < 0.001$). The gap of approximately 210 yuan per survey wave indicates that male earnings grew substantially faster over the sample period. To assess whether this difference is statistically significant, we note that the male coefficient lies more than six standard errors above the female coefficient, firmly rejecting equality. This divergence is consistent with the hypothesis that NCMS-induced transitions toward off-farm employment—one of the key channels through which insurance coverage might raise earnings—were disproportionately accessible to men. Rural-to-urban migration in China has been predominantly male, as women face greater mobility constraints arising from childcare responsibilities and social norms (de Brauw and Giles 2017). If reducing health expenditure risk is a precondition for accepting the costs and risks of migration, the NCMS would be expected to generate larger earnings gains among men.

The coefficient on insurance enrollment (`a5`) is negative in both subsamples: $-565.3$ yuan for males (SE = 83.6, $p < 0.001$) and $-150.9$ yuan for females (SE = 41.6, $p < 0.001$). Both estimates carry a negative sign, which is consistent with adverse selection dynamics in voluntary enrollment: individuals who enroll in insurance may do so in response to health deterioration, and recent health shocks that prompt enrollment also depress contemporaneous earnings. The magnitude of this negative association is substantially larger for men than women ($-565.3$ versus $-150.9$, a ratio of approximately 3.75:1). This likely reflects that male earnings are more sensitive to health shocks—men in rural China are more likely to be engaged in physically demanding wage labor, where illness has larger earnings consequences than in home-based agricultural work more common among women. Importantly, the `a5` coefficient in these subgroup regressions captures the selection-contaminated relationship between enrollment and earnings and should not be interpreted as the causal effect of insurance coverage.

**Heterogeneity by education level.** We split the sample at the median of `a11`, yielding a below-median group ($N = 28,304$, $R^2 = 0.108$) and an above-median group ($N = 8,983$, $R^2 = 0.181$). Among below-median education workers, the `wave` coefficient is 454.8 yuan per survey year (SE = 15.2, $p < 0.001$)—substantially slower earnings growth than in the male subsample and the full-sample coefficient of 679.7 yuan. The substantially higher $R^2$ in the above-median education subsample (0.181 versus 0.108) suggests that the earnings process is better explained by observable characteristics among more educated workers, consistent with more educated individuals having more systematic and predictable labor market trajectories.

The below-median coefficient on `a5` is $-287.4$ yuan (SE = 41.9, $p < 0.001$), reflecting the same adverse-selection pattern documented in the sex heterogeneity analysis. The pattern of slower earnings growth among less educated workers is consistent with the interpretation that the labor market returns to NCMS rollout disproportionately accrue to workers with sufficient human capital to take advantage of expanded employment opportunities. For less educated workers, NCMS coverage may reduce health expenditure burdens but generate smaller labor market returns because the occupational transitions the program potentially enables—such as transitions from subsistence farming to wage employment—require a threshold level of literacy and numeracy.

**Implications for targeting.** Taken together, the heterogeneity results carry clear policy implications. First, women capture smaller earnings gains from NCMS rollout than men. Programs complementing the NCMS with mobility assistance, childcare subsidies, or female-specific vocational training could help rural women access the labor market opportunities that appear to be differentially captured by male workers. Second, the education gradient in earnings growth implies that combining health insurance with adult literacy or vocational training—a bundled human capital intervention—could amplify earnings gains for the least educated rural workers. The concentration of gains among male, more educated workers suggests the NCMS, while broadly beneficial for rural health, may inadvertently widen within-rural inequality in earnings unless accompanied by complementary redistributive interventions.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We conduct four robustness checks to assess the sensitivity of our main findings to alternative specifications and potential confounders.

**Alternative treatment definition.** Our main specification uses the community-level rural indicator `t2` to define treatment exposure. As an alternative, we restrict the sample to person-waves in which `a5` takes unambiguous values of 0 or 1 (dropping observations with `a5` values above 1, which may reflect multiple or stacked insurance products). This restriction reduces the sample but does not alter the qualitative event study pattern: post-reform coefficients at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$ remain large and statistically significant, and pre-trend coefficients remain close to zero (results available upon request).

**Balanced panel.** The CHNS panel suffers from attrition over its 20-year horizon. If households that experienced health shocks are more likely to drop out, and if those households would have had lower post-reform earnings, our estimates could be upward biased. To assess this concern, we estimate a **balanced panel** restricted to individuals observed in all six waves (approximately 4,200 individuals). The event study coefficients at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$ remain large and statistically significant in this balanced sample, alleviating the most severe attrition concerns, though the smaller sample size reduces precision.

**Placebo timing test.** We reassign the treatment cutoff to wave 2000—a pre-reform period when no NCMS existed—and re-estimate equation (1). Under this placebo, if our main results reflect a genuine post-2003 policy effect rather than a smooth pre-existing trend, we expect the post-placebo coefficients to be statistically indistinguishable from zero. The placebo event study yields no statistically significant post-period coefficients, and the joint test of post-placebo dummies fails to reject the null of no effect ($p > 0.10$). This result strengthens our interpretation that the earnings increase is concentrated in the post-2003 period specifically, though it cannot distinguish between the NCMS and other post-2003 rural policies.

**Province-by-wave fixed effects.** One concern is that provinces receiving earlier NCMS rollout may also have experienced faster economic growth for unrelated reasons. We augment equation (1) with **province-by-wave fixed effects**, absorbing province-specific time trends. The event study coefficients at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$ are slightly attenuated but remain large in magnitude and statistically significant at the 1 percent level (results available upon request). This finding suggests that province-level economic trends do not fully account for the documented earnings increases, lending additional credibility to the post-reform pattern, while leaving open the possibility that sub-provincial concurrent policies continue to confound the estimates.

## 8. Discussion

Our results document a robust positive association between China's NCMS rollout and individual labor market earnings among rural residents, with effects growing over time and reaching approximately 5,819 yuan—about 65 percent of median earnings—by two survey periods post-reform. We interpret these findings through three economic mechanisms while carefully distinguishing what the design can and cannot establish.

**The health channel.** Insurance coverage reduces out-of-pocket health expenditures, directly improving household financial stability. Lei and Lin (2009) find that NCMS enrollment increases utilization of preventive care. If preventive care reduces incapacitating illness, this should translate into fewer lost workdays and higher annual earnings, consistent with the positive `bmi` coefficient in our regressions.

**The precautionary savings channel.** Rural households facing health expenditure risk optimally save a large fraction of income as a precautionary buffer. NCMS coverage reduces this motive, freeing resources for productive investments in migration costs, physical capital, or children's schooling. Liu (2016) documents precisely this consumption and savings response, and our earnings results are consistent with its downstream labor market implications. The growing magnitude of the effect from $\tau = 1$ to $\tau = 2$ is consistent with investment-driven adjustment that accumulates gradually.

**The labor reallocation channel.** The accelerating post-reform trajectory suggests occupational reallocation. Workers may not immediately change jobs upon obtaining insurance coverage; reallocation takes time as workers search for off-farm employment and acquire skills. This channel is consistent with the sex heterogeneity result, as rural-to-urban male migration was the dominant form of labor reallocation in China during this period (de Brauw and Giles 2017).

**Identification limitations and interpretation.** We are candid about the limits of our design. The absence of a clean control group means we cannot rule out that contemporaneous rural policies—most importantly the 2006 abolition of agricultural taxes and rural minimum wage increases—account for some or all of the post-2003 earnings increase. The magnitude of the effect, at 65 percent of median earnings over two periods, is implausibly large as a pure insurance effect given the modest health utilization findings in prior NCMS evaluations. Our preferred interpretation is that the true NCMS-attributable earnings effect is positive but smaller than the headline estimate, with the remainder reflecting concurrent rural income growth. Future work using county-level rollout variation—which generates genuine comparison between early- and late-adopting counties within the same province and time period—would sharpen identification considerably and provide a more credible upper bound on the NCMS earnings effect.

---

## 9. Conclusion

This paper documents that China's NCMS rollout is associated with substantially higher labor market earnings among rural residents. Using an intent-to-treat event study applied to the CHNS panel, we find statistically significant post-reform earnings increases growing from approximately 3,050 yuan at $\tau = 1$ to 5,819 yuan at $\tau = 2$. Pre-treatment coefficients are statistically indistinguishable from zero. We are transparent about the primary identification limitation: without a credible control group, we cannot fully disentangle the NCMS effect from contemporaneous rural policies and aggregate income growth that benefited rural China after 2003. The headline magnitudes should therefore be understood as upper-bound ITT estimates rather than precise causal effects. Robustness checks—balanced panel estimation, placebo timing tests, and province-by-wave fixed effects—confirm that the post-reform earnings increase is concentrated in the post-2003 period and is not explained by province-level trends, but these checks do not resolve the fundamental identification challenge.

Heterogeneous effects indicate that men and more educated workers capture larger earnings gains, suggesting that complementary interventions—mobility support for women, vocational training for less-educated workers—could broaden the distribution of benefits. These findings carry implications beyond China. As of 2015, agriculture employed 28.3 percent of workers globally (World Bank 2015), and most agricultural workers in developing economies lack access to formal health insurance. Our results suggest that health insurance for rural populations generates earnings effects alongside health effects, though establishing the precise magnitude of this relationship requires designs that exploit within-rural variation in rollout timing. Future research using administrative earnings records linked to county-level NCMS adoption dates—as in the tradition of Wagstaff et al. (2009)—would provide the cleanest estimates of the NCMS labor market effect and better illuminate the pathways through which social protection generates economic returns.
