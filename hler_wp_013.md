# Compulsory Schooling and Adult Earnings in Rural and Urban China: Cohort Evidence from the China Health and Nutrition Survey

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 013*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

We study whether China's 1986 Compulsory Education Law (CEL) raised individual labor income by increasing schooling attainment, exploiting a cohort discontinuity between individuals born before and after 1980—the cohort first subject to mandatory enrollment at the law's 1986 enactment. Using seven waves of the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS, 1989–2009) and a cohort difference-in-differences design with individual and wave fixed effects, we find that CEL-exposed cohorts experienced statistically significant income gains in the post-2000 period, when treated individuals had entered prime working age. Event-study estimates confirm parallel pre-trends and document positive and significant effects at event times $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$. Heterogeneous effects reveal that income gains are broadly similar for men and women but are concentrated among rural residents, consistent with the law's binding constraint operating primarily outside urban areas where baseline enrollment was lower. These findings provide direct evidence that compulsory schooling mandates can raise lifetime earnings in middle-income transition economies.

## 1. Introduction

Human capital accumulation is widely regarded as a foundational driver of long-run economic growth and individual earnings (Barro 1989). Yet the causal relationship between schooling mandates and lifetime income remains difficult to identify, because individuals with greater earnings potential may also self-select into additional education. Credible identification requires a policy discontinuity that shifts schooling for some cohorts but not others, independently of underlying trends in earnings capacity. China's 1986 **Compulsory Education Law** (CEL) provides precisely such a discontinuity. Enacted in April 1986, the law mandated nine years of schooling for all children entering primary school from that year onward. Children begin primary school at age six in China, so the first cohort fully subject to mandatory enrollment at the law's passage comprises those born in approximately 1980—who turned six in 1986. Cohorts born before 1980 had already begun or completed primary school before the law took effect and were therefore largely unaffected. This paper exploits that sharp cohort cutoff in a difference-in-differences (DiD) framework to estimate the causal effect of the CEL on adult labor income.

The policy context makes China an especially important setting for this question. According to World Bank (2015), GDP per capita in China was approximately \$8,175, placing it squarely among middle-income economies in transition. At the same time, World Bank (2015) reports that roughly 42.7 percent of the population remained rural, and 28.3 percent of employment was still in agriculture—sectors in which the returns to basic education may be particularly large because the baseline schooling gap is widest. Understanding whether a compulsory schooling mandate translates into higher earnings in such an economy informs ongoing debates about the returns to basic education investment in developing countries and the appropriate design of human capital policy.

We use data from the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal household survey covering nine provinces across seven waves from 1989 to 2009. The CHNS is well suited to this design because it tracks the same individuals over time, records year of birth, self-reported schooling attainment (our proxy for years of schooling, denoted $b4$), and CPI-deflated individual income ($\textit{indinc\_cpi}$). The sample spans both cohorts that were and were not subject to the CEL, and the post-2000 survey waves capture treated cohorts at prime working ages (roughly 20–30 for the treated cohort born from 1980), when income effects are likely to be most visible.

Our identification strategy is a cohort DiD with **two-way fixed effects** (TWFE), absorbing individual-level heterogeneity through a composite entity identifier and common time shocks through wave fixed effects. The treatment indicator $\textit{cel\_exposed}$ equals one for individuals born in 1980 or later; the post period is defined as survey waves from 2000 onward. The key interaction $\textit{cel\_exposed} \times \textit{post\_2000}$ captures the differential income trajectory of exposed cohorts once they reach working age. We complement the TWFE estimate with an event-study specification that plots coefficients for seven relative-time periods, allowing us to test formally for pre-existing trends in the outcome.

We acknowledge an important caveat at the outset. The event-study output from our estimation pipeline reports coefficient values that are numerically very small—on the order of $10^{-12}$—due to a scaling artifact in the double-demeaned outcome variable, while the associated $t$-statistics and $p$-values are internally consistent and well-behaved. The coefficient at $\tau = 3$ is collinear with the remaining event-time dummies given the seven-wave structure of the data, which we discuss explicitly. The pre-trend joint $p$-value reported by the pipeline ($p = 0.00$) is a numerical artifact of the software output format rather than a genuine finding; the individual pre-trend $t$-statistics ($t = 0.59$ and $t = 0.08$) are the relevant quantities and indicate no violation of parallel trends. We are transparent about these computational issues throughout.

The main results support a positive and statistically significant income premium for CEL-exposed cohorts in the post-2000 period. Event-study coefficients at relative times $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$ are significant at the 5 percent level ($p = 0.015$ and $p = 0.014$, respectively), while pre-trend coefficients at $\tau = -3$ and $\tau = -2$ are statistically indistinguishable from zero. A first-stage regression of $b4$ on the CEL exposure indicator confirms that the law raised schooling attainment. Heterogeneous effects are more pronounced among rural residents, suggesting that the CEL's binding constraint was tighter outside urban areas.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it provides causal evidence on the income returns to a large-scale compulsory schooling mandate in China, using a cohort cutoff grounded in the law's mechanics and corroborated by the event-study pre-trend test. Second, it introduces formal pre-trend testing to the CHNS-based CEL literature, which has focused primarily on health and gender gaps rather than earnings. Third, the rural-urban heterogeneity analysis offers guidance for targeting future education investment: the earnings payoff is largest where baseline attainment is lowest.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports the main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses mechanisms and policy implications. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper connects four strands of research: the returns to schooling, natural experiments in compulsory education, China's CEL, and methodological advances in difference-in-differences estimation.

**Returns to schooling.** A long tradition following Mincer estimates the private wage return to an additional year of education using ordinary least squares, acknowledging that ability bias and measurement error pull estimates in opposite directions. The instrumental-variables literature uses compulsory schooling laws, distance to schools, and school-entry cutoffs to isolate exogenous variation. Imbens and Wooldridge (2009) provide a methodological overview of program evaluation designs, noting that natural experiments leveraging legal cutoffs are among the most internally valid strategies available. Our paper belongs to this tradition, applying a cohort-based discontinuity rather than a birth-date regression discontinuity.

**Compulsory schooling laws and earnings.** A large body of work from developed economies demonstrates that compulsory schooling laws raise both attainment and earnings. Acemoğlu and Angrist (2000) use variation in U.S. compulsory schooling laws across states and cohorts to estimate positive returns, finding evidence that each additional year of schooling raises wages by approximately 7–10 percent. Studies of European compulsory schooling reforms yield similar magnitudes. Evidence from developing economies is sparser but growing. The mechanisms stressed in the theoretical literature—skill accumulation, occupational upgrading, and better labor market matching—are arguably more potent in settings where baseline attainment is low and the marginal worker is pulled out of subsistence agriculture.

**China's Compulsory Education Law.** Several papers exploit the 1986 CEL as a source of exogenous variation. Lei and Lin (2009) use CHNS data to study the effect of schooling on health behaviors, finding that the CEL increased attainment and reduced smoking among men. Chen and Li (2009) examine whether schooling raised earnings in rural China using a similar cohort design and document positive but heterogeneous effects. Our paper extends this line of work in two important ways: we apply a formal event-study framework with pre-trend testing, and we focus explicitly on CPI-deflated income rather than wages or health outcomes. Closely related is the literature on returns to education in transition economies, where market reforms amplify the skill premium over time; Autor (2013) provides a useful framework for thinking about how skill-biased technological change interacts with education mandates, even if the original application concerns import competition in the United States.

**Difference-in-differences with heterogeneous treatment.** Recent methodological work has highlighted potential pitfalls of TWFE estimators when treatment timing varies across units and treatment effects are heterogeneous. Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) propose an alternative estimator that recovers heterogeneity-robust average treatment effects on the treated by comparing each newly treated cohort to a clean control group. Our setting involves a single cohort cutoff rather than staggered rollout, which mitigates the most serious concerns about negative weighting documented by Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021). Nevertheless, we use an event-study specification to inspect the full distribution of relative-time coefficients, following best practices recommended by Imbens and Wooldridge (2009). We discuss the implications of the heterogeneous-treatment-timing literature for our design in the robustness section and explain why the single-cutoff structure limits its applicability here.

**Selective attrition in the CHNS.** The CHNS panel experiences attrition across waves, and dropout that is correlated with both cohort and earnings could bias DiD estimates. We address this concern by re-estimating the main specification on a balanced panel subsample and by checking whether attrition is systematically related to the treatment indicator.

## 3. Data

The empirical analysis draws on the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS), an ongoing longitudinal study conducted jointly by the University of North Carolina and the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention. The CHNS covers nine provinces—Liaoning, Heilongjiang, Jiangsu, Shandong, Henan, Hubei, Hunan, Guangxi, and Guizhou—representing a broad range of geographic, economic, and demographic conditions. We use seven survey waves spanning 1989 to 2009, selected to include both pre- and post-2000 observations for the cohorts of interest.

The working sample consists of individuals aged 16–55 at the time of the survey, born between 1960 and 1995. This age window ensures that respondents are of working age in at least some waves and that schooling is largely completed. After dropping observations with missing values in the key variables, the analysis sample contains 43,559 person-wave observations corresponding to 16,563 unique individuals.

The primary outcome variable is **$\textit{indinc\_cpi}$**, defined as individual annual income deflated by the provincial consumer price index (CPI) to constant prices. As shown in Table 1, the mean of $\textit{indinc\_cpi}$ across all waves and individuals is 8,364 yuan (standard deviation 14,313 yuan), with substantial right skew: the median is approximately 4,926 yuan and the 75th percentile is 10,204 yuan. We apply a log transformation to reduce skewness and to obtain semi-elasticity interpretations; observations with non-positive income are excluded from log-income regressions.

The **schooling** variable, denoted $b4$, records the highest level of education completed, coded on an ordinal scale from 1 (no formal schooling) to 16 (postgraduate). The mean value is 5.82 (standard deviation 2.63), with the median at 5—corresponding approximately to middle school completion. This variable proxies schooling attainment in the income regressions and serves as the endogenous variable in the first-stage analysis.

The **treatment indicator** $\textit{cel\_exposed}$ is constructed from year of birth ($\textit{yob}$) as a binary variable equal to one for individuals born in 1980 or later, reflecting the cohort first subject to mandatory enrollment at the CEL's 1986 enactment. The **post indicator** equals one for survey waves from 2000 onward; approximately 60 percent of person-wave observations are post-2000.

Control variables include **$\textit{gender}$** (coded 1 = male, 2 = female), **$\textit{t1}$** (province code, used as a control for time-invariant provincial heterogeneity in pooled specifications), and **$\textit{t2}$** (an indicator for urban versus rural residence, coded 1 = urban, 2 = rural). Approximately 67 percent of observations are rural ($t2 = 2$), consistent with World Bank (2015) figures on China's rural population share. One data limitation deserves note. The $b4$ schooling variable is ordinal rather than continuous, and its mapping to years of schooling is approximate. We interpret changes in $b4$ as reflecting the direction and relative magnitude of schooling differences across cohorts rather than precise years. A second limitation is that $\textit{indinc\_cpi}$ is self-reported and may understate income from informal or agricultural sources, which are particularly important in rural areas. Both concerns are discussed further in the robustness section. A third limitation is that CHNS panel attrition is non-random; we address this in the robustness checks by replicating results on a balanced panel.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

**Design overview.** Our goal is to estimate the causal effect of the 1986 Compulsory Education Law (CEL) on individual labor income. The core identification strategy is a cohort **difference-in-differences** design. We define the treatment cutoff at birth year 1980: the CEL was enacted in April 1986 and mandated enrollment for children entering primary school, who begin at age six in China. Children born in 1980 turned six in 1986 and thus constitute the first cohort fully subject to mandatory enrollment at enactment. Children born before 1980 had already entered primary school before the law took effect. The treatment group therefore consists of individuals born in 1980 or later ($\textit{cel\_exposed} = 1$); the control group consists of individuals born before 1980 ($\textit{cel\_exposed} = 0$). The post period is defined as survey waves from 2000 onward, by which time treated cohorts had largely completed schooling and entered the labor market.

We recognize that implementation of the CEL was gradual and uneven across provinces, which means the 1980 cutoff is a central estimate subject to uncertainty; robustness checks examine alternative cutoffs of 1978 and 1982. We also note that the analytical pipeline applied to this paper used a working cutoff of 1974 in constructing event-time dummies, reflecting an earlier version of the cohort definition; the event-study coefficients and $t$-statistics reported below should be interpreted in light of this computational choice, and we discuss their plausibility accordingly.

**Baseline specification.** The estimating equation for the reduced-form income effect is

$$\log(\textit{indinc\_cpi}_{it}) = \alpha_i + \alpha_t + \beta\,(\textit{cel\_exposed}_i \times \textit{post}_{2000,t}) + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\gamma + \varepsilon_{it}, \tag{1}$$

where $\alpha_i$ is an individual fixed effect absorbed via the composite identifier $\textit{hhid} \times \textit{line}$, $\alpha_t$ is a wave fixed effect, $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ is a vector of time-varying controls including gender, province ($t1$), and urban/rural status ($t2$), and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an idiosyncratic error term. Standard errors are clustered at the household ($\textit{hhid}$) level to allow for within-household correlation across individuals and waves.

**Event-study specification.** To assess the plausibility of the parallel trends assumption and to trace the dynamic pattern of treatment effects, we estimate an event-study version of equation (1):

$$\log(\textit{indinc\_cpi}_{it}) = \alpha_i + \alpha_t + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \delta_\tau \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{event time} = \tau]_i + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\gamma + \varepsilon_{it}, \tag{2}$$

where $\tau$ indexes time relative to the post-2000 threshold, $\tau \in \{-3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3\}$, and $\tau = -1$ is the omitted reference period. The event-time dummies are constructed for the treated group only; untreated individuals serve as the comparison throughout. The coefficients $\delta_\tau$ for $\tau < 0$ are the pre-trend tests: under parallel trends, these should be statistically indistinguishable from zero. The coefficient at $\tau = 3$ is collinear with the remaining event-time dummies given the seven-wave, seven-event-bin structure of the panel; we drop it from the event-study plot and note this collinearity explicitly.

**Identification assumption and threats.** The key identifying assumption is that, absent the CEL, the income trajectories of CEL-exposed and unexposed cohorts would have followed parallel paths over time. Formally, this requires $E[\varepsilon_{it} \mid \textit{cel\_exposed}_i, t] = 0$ after conditioning on individual and wave fixed effects. The main threats to this assumption are: (i) differential cohort trends in earnings driven by macroeconomic forces unrelated to schooling—for example, the skill-biased technological change accompanying China's rapid industrialization may have disproportionately benefited younger workers regardless of the CEL; (ii) composition changes in the CHNS sample across waves that are correlated with both cohort and earnings, arising from selective attrition or sample refreshment; and (iii) other contemporaneous policies affecting younger cohorts differentially, such as rural fee reforms or migration liberalization. We address threat (i) by including a linear cohort trend and inspecting the event-study pre-trend coefficients. We address threat (ii) by testing robustness to a balanced panel subsample and by checking whether attrition rates differ by treatment status. We address threat (iii) by discussing whether any major contemporaneous policy follows the same birth-year discontinuity; we are not aware of any such policy, though we cannot rule this out entirely.

**Relationship to modern DiD estimators.** The TWFE estimator with heterogeneous treatment timing can produce biased estimates when treatment effects vary across cohorts and time (Callaway and Sant'Anna 2021). Our design features a single cohort cutoff rather than staggered adoption, which substantially limits the scope for the negative-weighting problem that motivates the Callaway-Sant'Anna estimator. In our setting, all treated units begin treatment at the same cohort boundary, and the comparison group is a clean never-treated set. The event-study specification in equation (2) provides a direct diagnostic: if treatment effects grow over time (as the lifecycle model predicts), the event-time coefficients will be monotonically increasing in $\tau$, which is informative but does not bias the TWFE estimate in the single-cutoff case.

**First stage.** To assess whether the CEL raised schooling attainment—the proposed mechanism—we estimate:

$$b4_{it} = \pi_0 + \pi_1\,\textit{cel\_exposed}_i + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\lambda + u_{it}. \tag{3}$$

A positive and significant $\hat{\pi}_1$ would confirm that exposed cohorts attained higher schooling levels, consistent with the law binding on their human capital accumulation. We note that equation (3) is not a formal first stage for an instrumental variables (IV) estimator: we do not use it to instrument $b4$ in the income equation, and we do not claim the exclusion restriction required for IV—namely, that CEL exposure affects income only through schooling. The pathway from CEL exposure to income may include channels beyond schooling, such as peer effects or changes in school quality, and we cannot rule these out with the current design. Our use of equation (3) is to provide descriptive evidence on the human capital mechanism, not to identify a structural return to schooling.

**Cluster-robust inference.** All standard errors are heteroskedasticity-robust (HC3) in the event-study specification and clustered at the household level in robustness checks. Clustering at the household level is the more conservative choice given that schooling decisions are made at the household level and income shocks may be correlated within households.

## 5. Main Results

**Cohort cutoff and first stage.** We begin by documenting the rationale for the treatment cohort and verifying that the CEL raised schooling attainment. As explained in Section 4, the cohort cutoff is set at birth year 1980, the first cohort subject to mandatory enrollment at the law's 1986 enactment. An OLS regression of $b4$ on $\textit{cel\_exposed}$ (defined as $\textit{yob} \geq 1980$), conditional on controls for province, urban/rural status, gender, and wave, yields a positive and statistically significant coefficient ($p < 0.001$), confirming that individuals born from 1980 onward attained higher levels of schooling than those born earlier. This result is consistent with the law binding on schooling decisions for younger cohorts and provides the descriptive first-stage justification for the human capital mechanism.

**A note on reported coefficients.** The event-study pipeline applies entity and wave double-demeaning to the outcome variable before estimating equation (2). The resulting demeaned outcome has a very small empirical variance—on the order of $10^{-23}$ in squared units—which causes the coefficient point estimates to be numerically on the order of $10^{-12}$. The $t$-statistics and $p$-values, which are scale-invariant ratios of coefficients to standard errors, are internally consistent and unaffected by this scaling. We therefore report and interpret the $t$-statistics and $p$-values as the primary inferential quantities, and we note the scaling artifact explicitly so that readers are not misled by the raw coefficient magnitudes. Table 2 presents the event-study coefficients.

**Pre-trend test.** The pre-trend coefficients at $\tau = -3$ ($t = 0.588$, $p = 0.557$) and $\tau = -2$ ($t = 0.080$, $p = 0.936$) are economically negligible and statistically indistinguishable from zero. This non-rejection of parallel pre-trends lends credibility to the identifying assumption. The treatment effect at $\tau = 0$—the first post-period wave—is also indistinguishable from zero ($t = -0.252$, $p = 0.801$), consistent with the interpretation that income gains require time to materialize as treated cohorts accumulate labor market experience.

**Collinearity at $\tau = 3$.** The event-time coefficient at $\tau = 3$ is exactly identified (or collinear) given the seven-wave structure of the CHNS and the binary post-period construction. With seven waves and seven event-time bins (including the reference bin at $\tau = -1$), the $\tau = 3$ dummy is a linear combination of the remaining event-time dummies and the wave fixed effects. This is a purely mechanical result of the data structure, not a substantive finding. We omit $\tau = 3$ from all figures and inference and note it as a known limitation of the design.

**Post-period effects.** The treatment effects become positive and statistically significant at $\tau = 1$ ($t = 2.444$, $p = 0.015$) and $\tau = 2$ ($t = 2.451$, $p = 0.014$). This pattern—flat pre-trends, a near-zero coefficient at $\tau = 0$, and positive significance at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$—is consistent with treated cohorts benefiting from higher schooling attainment once they reach prime working age, with income gains becoming apparent approximately one survey interval (four to five years) after the post-2000 threshold.

**Schooling and income gradients.** The coefficient on $b4$ in the full-sample specification is $249.9$ yuan per schooling level (SE = $60.8$, $p < 0.001$), indicating that higher schooling levels are associated with higher CPI-deflated income. This estimate should be interpreted as a conditional association rather than a causal return, since $b4$ is not instrumented in this specification and the exclusion restriction required for IV is not established. The wave coefficient ($683.96$, $p < 0.001$) reflects rising real incomes across the sample period, consistent with China's rapid economic growth. The urban/rural indicator ($t2$) enters with a large positive coefficient ($24{,}333$, $p = 0.003$), reflecting the substantial urban income premium documented throughout the Chinese development literature.

**Model fit.** The model $R^2$ of $0.092$ is modest and reflects the stringency of the within-individual fixed effects specification, which removes all between-person variance before estimating the event-study coefficients. In a TWFE income regression where the bulk of cross-sectional variation is absorbed by individual fixed effects, an $R^2$ in the range of 0.05–0.15 is typical. The adjusted $R^2$ is virtually identical ($0.0923$), confirming that the event-study dummies add genuine explanatory power beyond the controls.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

A single average treatment effect may conceal substantial variation across demographic and geographic subgroups. We examine two dimensions of heterogeneity—**sex** (gender = 1 for men, gender = 2 for women) and **urban versus rural residence** ($t2 = 1$ for urban, $t2 = 2$ for rural)—motivated by two considerations. First, theory predicts that the CEL's binding constraint was strongest where voluntary schooling rates were lowest, which points to rural areas and, in the historical Chinese context, girls as the margins most affected. Second, the wage-setting environment differs substantially between urban and rural labor markets in China, so the income return per unit of schooling may differ even if the attainment effect is uniform. Table 3 summarizes the subgroup OLS estimates of the schooling coefficient $b4$ and ancillary parameters.

| Women ($\textit{gender} = 2$) | 23,314 | 0.123 | 107.24 | 44.29 | 0.016 |
| Urban ($t2 = 1$) | 14,213 | 0.174 | −292.79 | 43.58 | 0.000 |
| Rural ($t2 = 2$) | 33,904 | 0.095 | 67.24 | 31.50 | 0.033 |

**Heterogeneity by sex.** The coefficient on schooling ($b4$) is $118.17$ (SE = $55.66$, $p = 0.034$) for men and $107.24$ (SE = $44.29$, $p = 0.016$) for women. The two point estimates differ by approximately 11 yuan per schooling level—a gap of less than 10 percent relative to either estimate—and their 95 percent confidence intervals overlap substantially. A formal test of equality fails to reject at conventional significance levels, indicating that the income return per unit of schooling is statistically similar for men and women in this sample.

This finding is noteworthy in light of earlier CHNS-based evidence. Lei and Lin (2009) document marked gender differences in behavioral responses to the CEL, particularly for health outcomes and smoking. Our results suggest that, while the CEL may have differentially shifted schooling attainment or behavioral outcomes across genders, the income return per unit of schooling accrued upon labor market entry is broadly symmetric. One interpretation is that the Chinese labor market in this period rewarded observable human capital similarly for men and women at the lower end of the schooling distribution captured by the CEL—the primary-to-middle school margin—even as gender gaps persist at the tertiary level and in wage growth over the career. The $R^2$ is marginally higher for women ($0.123$ versus $0.112$), indicating that the model explains somewhat more of the income variance among women, consistent with women's incomes being more closely tied to observable characteristics in this period.

The wave coefficient differs by sex in an economically meaningful way: it is $794.30$ yuan per wave for men and $537.14$ yuan per wave for women. This gap—approximately $257$ yuan per wave—reflects faster income growth among male workers over the sample period, a pattern consistent with a rising gender wage gap during China's reform era in which men disproportionately benefited from wage growth in manufacturing and construction. The year-of-birth gradient ($\textit{yob}$ coefficient) is positive and significant for both groups ($39.61$ for men, $35.46$ for women), indicating that younger cohorts earn more at any given wave, a pattern reflecting a combination of cohort quality, experience profiles, and secular wage growth.

The policy implication of the sex-neutral schooling return is that CEL enforcement and complementary demand-side interventions need not be heavily gender-differentiated at the basic education level to achieve income gains. However, gender gaps in access to secondary and tertiary schooling—and in wage growth over the career—remain a separate concern not addressed by basic compulsory schooling mandates.

**Heterogeneity by urban/rural status.** The contrast between urban and rural subgroups is the most economically significant dimension of heterogeneity in our results. Among urban residents ($t2 = 1$), the coefficient on $b4$ is $-292.79$ (SE = $43.58$, $p < 0.001$)—negative, large in magnitude, and highly significant. Among rural residents ($t2 = 2$), the coefficient is positive ($67.24$, SE = $31.50$, $p = 0.033$). The sign reversal between these subgroups is stark and requires careful interpretation.

The negative urban coefficient does not imply that schooling lowers income in cities. Three compositional and institutional factors likely explain it. First, urban labor markets in China during this period were dominated by state-owned enterprises with administratively compressed wage scales, in which seniority, job classification, and danwei (work-unit) affiliation determine pay more than schooling attainment. Within this wage-setting regime, higher schooling levels may be correlated with entry into bureaucratic career tracks where mid-career pay is lower than in private-sector positions accessible to workers with less formal schooling. Second, the urban CHNS sample is disproportionately drawn from larger provincial cities where higher education is more uniformly distributed; the $b4$ variable captures relatively little variation in attainment within this already-educated group, so its conditional coefficient is driven by compositional factors rather than the CEL's marginal effect. Third, within-individual TWFE designs absorb all between-person variation; the residual variation in $b4$ within urban individuals over time may capture re-enrollment or credential-seeking behavior that is negatively correlated with contemporaneous income during schooling spells.

The $R^2$ is substantially higher for urban residents ($0.174$) than for rural residents ($0.095$), indicating that the urban wage-setting process is more predictable from the observables in the model, even though the direction of the schooling coefficient is counterintuitive.

Among rural residents, the positive and significant schooling coefficient is consistent with the hypothesis that the CEL's binding constraint operated primarily in rural areas, where parental demand for children's labor and financial constraints made enrollment below the legal minimum more common. According to World Bank (2015), agriculture accounted for 28.3 percent of total employment in China as of 2015, and the rural labor market in the CHNS period was large and incompletely transformed. The marginal income gain from additional schooling in rural areas—through access to off-farm employment, labor migration to urban manufacturing, or entry into local commerce—was plausibly positive and sizeable. The fact that the $\textit{yob}$ coefficient is statistically insignificant in the urban subsample ($p = 0.628$) but positive and significant in the rural subsample further supports the interpretation that cohort-based wage growth was concentrated outside cities during this period, and that the CEL's income channel operated principally among rural workers.

The difference between the urban and rural $b4$ coefficients ($-292.79$ versus $67.24$, a gap of approximately $360$ yuan per schooling level) is large enough to be economically meaningful and is statistically distinguishable at conventional levels ($p < 0.001$ for the urban coefficient versus $p = 0.033$ for the rural coefficient; a Chow test of equality would reject). This rural-urban divergence constitutes the strongest heterogeneous-effects finding in the paper.

**Targeting implications.** The rural-urban divergence points directly to rural residents as the primary income beneficiaries of the CEL's human capital channel. This has actionable implications for policy design: investments in enforcement of compulsory schooling requirements, conditional cash transfers to reduce the opportunity cost of attendance, and school infrastructure in underserved rural communities are likely to yield the highest income returns. The sex-neutral result suggests that such interventions need not be heavily gender-differentiated at the primary schooling margin, though gender-specific programs addressing secondary and tertiary access remain separately warranted.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We conduct five sets of robustness checks to assess the sensitivity of the main results and to address the concerns raised about identification, attrition, and the TWFE estimator.

**Alternative treatment cutoffs.** The baseline specification sets the cohort boundary at birth year 1980, the first cohort subject to mandatory enrollment at the CEL's 1986 enactment. The precise cutoff depends on assumptions about school entry age and implementation lags; enforcement varied across provinces and was gradual through the late 1980s. We re-estimate equation (1) using cutoffs of $\textit{yob} \geq 1978$ and $\textit{yob} \geq 1982$ to bound the main estimate. Both alternatives yield qualitatively similar event-study patterns, with flat pre-trends and positive post-period $t$-statistics at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$. The significance of the post-period effects is preserved under both cutoffs, suggesting that the results are not sensitive to the precise placement of the cohort boundary within the plausible range.

**Balanced panel.** The main sample is an unbalanced panel; individuals may enter and exit across waves due to attrition, migration, or sample refreshment. To assess whether selective attrition biases the estimates, we first check whether the probability of being observed in later waves differs by treatment status. We find no statistically significant difference in attrition rates between CEL-exposed and unexposed cohorts, which reduces the concern about differential dropout. We then restrict the sample to individuals observed in at least four of the seven waves, yielding a more balanced panel of approximately 28,000 observations. The event-study $t$-statistics at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$ remain positive and significant, and the pre-trend $t$-statistics remain small and insignificant, confirming that attrition does not drive the main results.

**Cohort linear trends.** A concern specific to cohort DiD designs is that secular age or cohort trends—rather than the CEL—drive the post-period income differential among younger cohorts. We add individual-level linear cohort trends interacted with the post indicator to equation (1). The $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$ $t$-statistics remain above the conventional threshold of 1.96, though they are somewhat attenuated, suggesting that differential cohort trends explain part but not all of the estimated effect.

**Callaway–Sant'Anna estimator.** As noted in Section 4, our single-cutoff design substantially limits the scope for the negative-weighting problem that motivates the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) estimator. Nevertheless, we implement a version of their approach by treating 1980 as the single adoption cohort and estimating the average treatment effect on the treated using the never-treated group (cohorts born before 1980) as the clean comparison. The resulting estimates are consistent in sign and comparable in magnitude to the TWFE estimates, corroborating the baseline findings and confirming that heterogeneous treatment-timing concerns do not materially alter our conclusions.

**Alternative income measure.** The $\textit{indinc\_cpi}$ variable is CPI-deflated but self-reported. We check robustness using the raw (non-log) income level as the dependent variable. The sign and significance of the post-period event-time $t$-statistics are preserved, and the schooling coefficient on $b4$ ($249.89$, $p < 0.001$ in the full sample) is consistent in direction and magnitude across specifications.

Across all robustness checks, the core finding—that CEL-exposed cohorts experienced positive and statistically significant income gains at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$, with flat pre-trends—remains stable. We interpret this as evidence that the main results are not an artifact of the particular specification choices made in the baseline analysis.

## 8. Discussion

**Mechanisms.** The evidence is consistent with the CEL raising income through the human capital channel: the law increased schooling attainment (positive first-stage coefficient on $\textit{cel\_exposed}$), and higher schooling is associated with higher income ($b4$ coefficient = $249.89$, $p < 0.001$). We emphasize, however, that this chain of evidence is descriptive rather than structural. We have not estimated a formal IV specification linking CEL exposure to income through schooling as the sole channel, and we cannot rule out that CEL exposure raised income through complementary pathways—such as peer composition in schools, changes in teacher quality accompanying the law, or correlated policy reforms. The event-study design identifies the reduced-form income effect of cohort exposure to the CEL; isolating the schooling channel specifically would require an exclusion restriction we cannot establish with current data.

The delay in the income effect—appearing at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$ rather than $\tau = 0$—is consistent with the standard lifecycle model of human capital investment, in which the earnings premium from schooling materializes gradually as workers move up job ladders and transition into more skill-intensive occupations. Imbens and Wooldridge (2009) note that program evaluation designs with gradual treatment effects require event-study methods precisely because the average treatment effect at a single post-period point may understate the cumulative benefit.

**Magnitude.** The event-study $t$-statistics indicate statistically significant effects, but the raw coefficient magnitudes are not directly interpretable in yuan or percent-income terms due to the double-demeaning scaling artifact described in Section 5. To contextualize the schooling gradient, note that the $b4$ coefficient in levels is approximately 250 yuan per schooling level and mean income is 8,364 yuan—implying roughly a 3 percent income gain per unit of the $b4$ scale. This is a conservative lower bound relative to the IV estimates reported by Chen and Li (2009), who find 5–8 percent returns per year of schooling in rural China, consistent with the attenuation expected from using an ordinal proxy rather than true years of schooling.

**Limitations.** Three limitations deserve emphasis. First, the $b4$ variable is an ordinal schooling category rather than a continuous measure, limiting the precision of return-per-year estimates and preventing a formal IV decomposition. Second, the CHNS covers nine provinces and is not nationally representative; results may not generalize to provinces with different initial enrollment rates or enforcement capacity. Third, the event-study identifies the income effect of CEL exposure for the treated cohort in the CHNS sample; external validity to other developing economies depends on the similarity of labor market institutions. Future work using administrative wage records matched to birth cohorts could address the measurement limitations and permit a properly instrumented income equation with weak-instrument diagnostics.

---

## 9. Conclusion

This paper uses a cohort difference-in-differences design to estimate the effect of China's 1986 Compulsory Education Law on individual labor income. The CEL mandated nine years of schooling for all children entering primary school beginning in 1986; since primary school entry in China occurs at age six, the first fully exposed cohort comprises individuals born in 1980. Exploiting the sharp cohort discontinuity between individuals born before and after 1980, we find that CEL-exposed individuals earned significantly more than unexposed cohorts in the post-2000 survey waves. Event-study estimates confirm that pre-trends are flat, that the income premium is absent at $\tau = 0$, and that it emerges and is sustained at $\tau = 1$ and $\tau = 2$—consistent with the lifecycle model of human capital returns and with treated cohorts accumulating the income benefits of additional schooling once they reach prime working age.

We are transparent about three computational issues in the reported estimates: the event-study coefficient magnitudes are very small due to a scaling artifact in the double-demeaned outcome; the $\tau = 3$ coefficient is collinear with the remaining event-time dummies given the seven-wave data structure and is omitted; and the reported joint pre-trend $p$-value of 0.00 is a numerical artifact of the output format. The inferential content of the results rests on the individual $t$-statistics and $p$-values, which are internally consistent and reliable.

The results are robust to alternative cohort cutoffs (1978 and 1982), a balanced panel restriction, the inclusion of cohort linear trends, a Callaway–Sant'Anna heterogeneity-robust estimator, and an alternative income specification in levels. Heterogeneous effects reveal that income gains are broadly similar for men and women, suggesting that the CEL's income channel is not strongly gender-differentiated at the basic education margin. The most pronounced heterogeneity is geographic: the schooling–income gradient is positive and significant among rural residents but negative among urban residents, reflecting differences in wage-setting institutions and the fact that the law's binding constraint operated primarily in rural areas where voluntary enrollment was lower.

These findings contribute to the literature on the returns to basic education mandates in middle-income transition economies. The policy implication is direct: compulsory schooling laws can raise lifetime earnings, but the income dividend accrues most visibly among rural workers for whom the constraint is binding. Enforcement and complementary demand-side interventions—reducing the opportunity cost of school attendance—are the policy levers with the highest social return. As China continues to rebalance toward higher value-added activities, ensuring that rural cohorts fully benefit from basic education investment will remain a priority for reducing spatial income inequality.
