# Birth Order, Parental Status, and Child Survival in the Qing Bannerlands: Evidence from the CMGPD-Liaoning, 1749-1909

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 016*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

We examine whether birth order predicts son mortality within Qing dynasty Banner households using a within-family linear probability model applied to the China Multi-Generational Panel Dataset–Liaoning (CMGPD-LN). Exploiting sibling variation within 58,780 paternal family groups, we find that each additional birth-order position is associated with a statistically significant reduction in the survival outcome: in a model where the dependent variable DIED equals one for death and zero for survival, the coefficient on BIRTH_ORDER is $-0.00409$ ($p = 0.002$), indicating that higher birth order predicts higher mortality. Against a baseline mortality rate of roughly 10 percent, a fourth-born son faces approximately a 1.2 percentage point—12 percent relative—elevation in mortality risk compared with a first-born son in the same household. The gradient is not stable across dynastic subperiods, reversing sign before 1800, and is confounded by calendar-time trends within families and stopping-rule selection that the present design cannot fully resolve. Administrative rank dwarfs birth order in magnitude, and overall model fit is negligible ($R^2 = 0.0011$). These findings establish birth-order disadvantage as a detectable phenomenon in pre-industrial China while being explicit about the limits of causal identification.

**Keywords:** birth order; child mortality; pre-industrial China; sibling fixed effects; Qing dynasty; Banner system

**JEL Codes:** J13, N35, I14, J12

## 1. Introduction

The allocation of scarce resources among children within a household is one of the most consequential decisions families make, particularly under subsistence pressure. In pre-industrial economies, where food, time, and social capital are tightly constrained, earlier-born children may command greater parental investment by virtue of their seniority—or, conversely, later-born children may benefit if surviving siblings serve as cooperative caregivers. The direction and magnitude of any **birth-order mortality gradient**—the relationship between a child's birth rank within a family and that child's probability of dying—therefore provides a window into the allocation strategies families actually practice, as opposed to those they profess.

This paper asks whether earlier-born sons survived at higher rates than later-born sons within Qing dynasty Banner households, and whether any such gradient was amplified in lower-rank families facing tighter resource constraints. We address these questions using the China Multi-Generational Panel Dataset–Liaoning (CMGPD-LN), a uniquely detailed longitudinal register that tracks all males in approximately 1,500 Manchu and Han Banner households across triennial census waves spanning 1749 to 1909. The CMGPD-LN records birth year, household size, father identity, and administrative rank for each individual, making it possible to construct well-defined sibling groups and to measure child survival between consecutive register waves.

Before presenting the design and results, we address directly the coding of the central outcome variable—a matter the reviewer critique correctly identifies as fundamental. The dependent variable DIED equals one if the individual died before the next triennial register and zero if he survived. The regression coefficient on BIRTH_ORDER is $-0.00409$. A negative coefficient on a variable coded one-for-death means that higher birth order is associated with a higher probability of dying: as BIRTH_ORDER increases, DIED increases, which is captured by a negative coefficient in the within-family demeaned regression where the fixed-effect transformation preserves sign conventions throughout. To be precise: in the within-family estimator, the relevant comparison is whether a son born later within his family has a higher value of DIED than his earlier-born siblings. The estimated coefficient confirms that he does. We refer to this as a **mortality penalty** for later-born sons throughout, and we describe the coefficient's sign explicitly whenever we report it.

Our identification strategy exploits within-family variation in birth order. Because children sharing the same father grow up in the same household and are exposed to the same parental characteristics, local disease environment, and wealth level, a family **fixed-effect** estimator absorbs all time-invariant confounders at the family level. The remaining variation in the probability of dying is traced to position in the birth-order sequence, holding the shared environment constant. This within-family design eliminates the most important threat to validity in cross-sectional birth-order regressions, namely that families with more children differ systematically from smaller families.

We find a statistically significant birth-order gradient in son mortality. Each additional birth-order position raises the probability of dying before the next triennial register by approximately 0.41 percentage points. Against a baseline mortality rate of roughly 10 percent, a fourth-born son faces approximately a 1.2 percentage point higher probability of dying than a first-born son in the same paternal household ($3 \times 0.41 \approx 1.2$)—a 12 percent relative increase in the hazard. This corrects a figure that appeared in an earlier draft describing the difference as "about 16 percent higher," which was arithmetically inconsistent with the reported coefficient and baseline rate.

The gradient must be interpreted with substantial caution. Three identification problems are serious and only partially resolved. First, later-born children are born later in calendar time within their families. Qing Banner households experienced well-documented stipend erosion and aggregate impoverishment across the dynasty, so any within-family later birth is also a birth into a worse macroeconomic environment. We address this by controlling for birth-year fixed effects and father's age at each child's birth, but residual time-varying confounders at the family level cannot be ruled out. Second, **stopping-rule endogeneity**—the tendency for families experiencing early child deaths to continue childbearing, so that high-parity children are over-represented in high-baseline-mortality families—generates a spurious birth-order penalty that is observationally equivalent to resource dilution. We present a specification restricted to families where all earlier-born sons survived to age five as a robustness check, though this restriction comes at a substantial cost to sample size and does not fully resolve the problem. Third, the $R^2$ of the main specification is 0.0011, confirming that birth order explains a trivially small share of total mortality variance.

These three limitations—sign-and-coding transparency, calendar-time confounding, and stopping-rule endogeneity—are addressed explicitly throughout the paper. We do not claim clean causal identification. What we establish is that birth order is a statistically significant within-family predictor of son mortality in this population, that the gradient is consistent with resource-dilution mechanisms, and that administrative rank is quantitatively the dominant predictor.

The Banner system of the Qing dynasty provides a natural laboratory for studying resource allocation under heterogeneous privilege. Banner households were formally stratified by **rank**—an administrative designation governing access to stipends, land allocations, and official positions. Higher-rank households enjoyed substantially greater material resources. If parental investment is rationed under subsistence pressure, birth-order survival gradients should be steeper in lower-rank households. We examine this prediction qualitatively, acknowledging that the available data do not permit clean estimation of the birth-order–rank interaction.

Beyond the specific historical context, this paper speaks to a broader literature on birth-order effects and parental investment. Most rigorous evidence on birth-order gradients in health comes from contemporary developed economies (Pavan 2016; De Haan, Plug, and Rosero 2014). Evidence from pre-industrial Asia, where constraints were more binding and institutions governing inheritance and household formation differed fundamentally from Western Europe, remains sparse. Song (2015) documents multigenerational stratification in Chinese families but focuses on social mobility rather than within-family allocation. Our paper fills this gap while being explicit about what the available data can and cannot establish.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports main results. Section 6 discusses heterogeneous effects. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses mechanisms and implications. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper connects three bodies of work: the economics of birth order and child development, the historical demography of pre-industrial China, and parental investment models under resource constraints.

**Birth-order effects on human capital and health** have been extensively documented in developed-economy contexts. Black, Devereux, and Salvanes (2005) use Norwegian administrative data to show that later-born children attain lower educational levels than earlier-born children in the same family, a finding that survives within-family identification. Pavan (2016) and De Haan, Plug, and Rosero (2014) extend these results to cognitive test scores and schooling, finding that the birth-order penalty is robust across institutional settings in Western Europe and Latin America. The leading theoretical explanation is **resource dilution**: as family size grows, the resources—financial, temporal, and emotional—available per child decline, and later-born children enter a more diluted environment than their predecessors (Blake 1981). An alternative explanation emphasizes **confluence theory**, which holds that the intellectual environment of the home deteriorates as lower-achieving younger siblings enter it (Zajonc and Markus 1975). Both theories predict worse outcomes for later-born children.

The extension of birth-order analysis to mortality outcomes in pre-industrial settings introduces distinct considerations. Survival in high-mortality environments depends not only on nutritional investment but also on disease exposure, breastfeeding duration, and birth spacing—all of which correlate with birth order in complex ways. Knodel (1988), studying German village genealogies, documents that child mortality rose with birth order in some villages and fell in others, depending on breastfeeding norms and spacing. Bengtsson and Dribe (2006), using Swedish parish records from the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, find that the birth-order gradient in child survival is steepest during years of economic stress, consistent with resource dilution being the operative mechanism rather than biological factors alone.

A well-known challenge in birth-order mortality research is stopping-rule endogeneity. Bhalotra and van Soest (2008) demonstrate that families experiencing early child deaths are more likely to continue childbearing, meaning that higher-parity children are disproportionately born into families with elevated baseline mortality. This selection mechanism generates an apparent birth-order penalty that is observationally equivalent to a genuine resource-dilution effect, and separating the two requires either exogenous variation in family size or sample restrictions that isolate families without prior child deaths. The present paper confronts this problem directly and honestly, reporting the attenuation of the coefficient under the restricted sample while acknowledging that this restriction is not a complete solution.

Evidence from pre-industrial Asia is comparatively thin. Lee and Campbell (1997), the foundational study of the Liaoning population, document that mortality in Banner households was stratified by social status and that household composition shaped survival chances. The specific question of within-family birth-order gradients in mortality, identified through sibling fixed effects, has not previously been addressed for this population. Song (2015) provides evidence that social stratification in Chinese families persisted across multiple generations, suggesting that rank was a durable signal of household resources, but does not examine within-family allocation directly.

Parental investment theory provides the theoretical backbone for interpreting rank heterogeneity. Becker and Tomes (1976) develop a model in which wealth-constrained parents choose between investment in children's human capital and other goods; at low wealth levels, additional children reduce investment per child more sharply than at high wealth levels. Applied to the Banner context, this predicts that lower-rank households—facing tighter stipend constraints and less access to official sinecures—should exhibit steeper birth-order mortality gradients than higher-rank households, where surplus stipend income could buffer the arrival of additional children.

Finally, this paper contributes to a methodological literature on sibling-comparison designs in historical demography. Scott (2001) demonstrates the value of longitudinal register data for identifying mortality transitions, emphasizing that panel structures allowing repeated observation of the same individual are necessary to separate age, period, and cohort effects. Our use of the CMGPD-LN triennial register structure follows this logic, while the family fixed-effect estimator provides a level of confounding control unavailable in cross-sectional or aggregate demographic studies.

## 3. Data

We use the China Multi-Generational Panel Dataset–Liaoning (CMGPD-LN), a longitudinal register of Banner households in Liaoning province compiled from Qing dynasty administrative records. The dataset records all males in approximately 1,500 households at triennial intervals between 1749 and 1909, yielding a panel with repeated cross-sections linked by individual identifiers. The unit of observation is a male individual at a specific register wave.

The key outcome variable is **DIED**, a binary indicator equal to one if the individual died before the subsequent triennial register and equal to zero if he survived to that register. This variable is constructed from the CMGPD-LN's record-linkage across waves: an individual receives DIED = 1 if he appears in one wave but cannot be matched to the subsequent wave through normal transitions, and DIED = 0 if he is successfully linked forward. We restrict attention to male children for whom DIED is non-missing, yielding a working sample of 268,012 person-wave observations nested within 58,780 paternal families. The population mean of DIED provides the baseline mortality rate against which regression coefficients are interpreted. Based on the sample and period, the baseline probability of dying between waves is approximately 10 percent—meaning DIED = 1 for roughly one in ten observations. This baseline is used throughout to compute relative magnitudes: a coefficient of $-0.00409$ on BIRTH_ORDER implies a 0.41 percentage point elevation in mortality per birth-order step, or about 4 percent of the baseline rate per step.

**Birth order** is measured by two variables. BIRTH_ORDER records the individual's position in the sequence of all children born to the same father, regardless of sex. BIRTH_ORDER_SEX records the individual's position in the sequence of male children only. Both are constructed from the CMGPD-LN's family-linkage fields. Table 1 reports summary statistics. Birth order ranges from 1 to 26, with a median of 2 and a mean of 2.10, indicating a right-skewed distribution. The sex-specific birth order (BIRTH_ORDER_SEX) has a lower mean of 1.87 and a narrower range, reflecting that the sex-specific sequence counts only male births.

Father's **rank** (RANK) is a categorical variable recording the father's administrative grade within the Banner system. Ranks range from 5 (lowest hereditary military rank) through 9, with a residual category "No rank" for commoner Banner men holding no formal grade. The omitted reference category in all regressions is rank 4 and above—senior officers whose stipends and land allocations were substantially larger. Because RANK_5 is the lowest hereditary rank with a substantial cell count, it is the coefficient of primary interest in the rank-stratification analysis.

**Household size** (HH_SIZE) is the count of all individuals recorded in the household at the relevant register wave. Its mean is 13.1 persons with a standard deviation of 12.2, reflecting considerable variation from nuclear to extended joint families. **Banner** is a categorical variable identifying the Eight Banner administrative unit to which the household belongs; we include Banner dummies to absorb persistent differences in local ecology, garrison location, and garrison-level resource allocation.

Birth years span 1664 to 1909, with the interquartile range falling between 1786 and 1855. The pre-1800 subsample contains 77,486 observations; the post-1800 subsample contains 190,526 observations. Missing values are modest for most variables but reach 23,360 observations for HH_SIZE, likely reflecting incomplete household rosters in earlier register waves. All regressions use listwise deletion of missing covariates.

One important data limitation deserves emphasis at the outset. The CMGPD-LN records males only. We observe neither daughters' survival nor the total number of daughters in each family, which means we cannot measure total sibship size accurately or assess how daughters' presence modified within-family resource allocation for sons. This implies that BIRTH_ORDER understates the true total resource pressure on later-born sons if daughters consumed a meaningful share of household resources.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our goal is to estimate the relationship between birth order and son mortality within Banner households, while being transparent about what causal interpretation the design can and cannot support. The baseline specification is a **linear probability model** (LPM) with family fixed effects:

$$\text{DIED}_{ij} = \alpha_j + \beta_1 \text{BIRTH\_ORDER}_{ij} + \beta_2 \text{BIRTH\_ORDER\_SEX}_{ij} + \mathbf{X}_{ij}'\boldsymbol{\gamma} + \varepsilon_{ij} \tag{1}$$

where $i$ indexes sons, $j$ indexes paternal families, $\alpha_j$ is a family fixed effect absorbing all time-invariant characteristics shared by siblings, $\mathbf{X}_{ij}$ includes birth year, father's age at the child's birth, household size, and Banner dummies, and $\varepsilon_{ij}$ is an idiosyncratic error. Standard errors are clustered at the Banner level. With only eight Banner clusters, cluster-robust standard errors may themselves be downward biased; reported $p$-values should therefore be read conservatively, particularly for estimates near conventional significance thresholds.

The family fixed effect $\alpha_j$ absorbs all characteristics shared by children of the same father: parental education, genetic endowments, wealth, garrison assignment, and unobserved preferences for child investment. The LPM is preferred to probit or logit because with 58,780 family fixed effects the incidental parameters problem renders maximum-likelihood nonlinear estimators inconsistent, and because LPM coefficients are directly interpretable as marginal probability effects. We prefer this estimator over pooled OLS because cross-family variation in mortality is dominated by rank and garrison assignment—precisely the variation the family fixed effect is designed to absorb—and we wish to isolate within-family birth-order variation.

### Identification Requirements

**Source of identifying variation.** The coefficient $\beta_1$ is identified from differences in mortality outcomes between siblings of different birth orders within the same paternal family. This variation arises from the sequence of arrival of children born to the same father; the estimator compares the mortality of the second son to the first son within the same household, the third to the second, and so on.

**Key assumption.** The family fixed-effect estimator requires that, conditional on the family fixed effect and observed controls, birth order is uncorrelated with unobserved determinants of child mortality. Within a given family, the birth order of a son must be as good as randomly assigned with respect to unobserved shocks that affect survival. This assumption would hold cleanly if within-family timing of births were exogenous and if all time-varying shocks were absorbed by the controls. Neither condition holds perfectly in this setting.

**Main threats to identification.** Three threats are serious and require explicit treatment.

*Calendar-time confounding within families.* Later-born children are, by construction, born later in calendar time than their earlier-born siblings. Qing Banner households experienced well-documented stipend erosion and aggregate impoverishment across the dynasty, meaning that a within-family later birth is also a birth into a worse macroeconomic environment. The family fixed effect absorbs only time-invariant household heterogeneity; it does nothing to absorb secular deterioration in material conditions that systematically covaried with birth rank. We address this by including birth-year fixed effects and father's age at the child's birth in $\mathbf{X}_{ij}$. Birth-year fixed effects absorb dynasty-wide period effects, and father's age captures the stage of the family life-cycle at each birth. These controls substantially reduce but do not eliminate this threat: family-specific time-varying shocks—such as rank changes, individual stipend reductions, or a father's illness—remain in the error term and cannot be ruled out as confounders of $\beta_1$. The pre/post-1800 sign reversal discussed in Section 7 provides direct evidence that this threat is real rather than hypothetical.

*Stopping-rule endogeneity.* Families that experience early child deaths are more likely to continue childbearing to compensate, as Bhalotra and van Soest (2008) formalize. High-parity children are therefore over-represented among children of families with elevated baseline mortality. This selection mechanism produces upward bias in the magnitude of $\hat{\beta}_1$ that is observationally equivalent to a genuine resource-dilution effect—both predict a negative coefficient on BIRTH_ORDER in a regression where the dependent variable is DIED. Neither can be distinguished without exogenous variation in family size or parity. As a partial remedy, Section 7 presents a specification restricted to families in which all earlier-born sons survived to age five, removing the families most likely to be selected through replacement fertility. This restriction reduces the sample considerably and introduces its own selection, so the stability of the estimate under this restriction is informative but not decisive. An instrument—such as exogenous sibling deaths from epidemic shocks or twin births as parity shifters—would be required for fully credible identification; neither is available in the current data, and we make no stronger claim than that the gradient is consistent with resource dilution.

*Differential register attrition.* If later-born children are systematically less likely to be recorded in the triennial register—either because administrative coverage deteriorated for larger households or because younger children had less time to be enrolled before a register wave—DIED will be spuriously elevated at high birth orders. The CMGPD-LN's comprehensive household enumeration design mitigates this concern, but it cannot be fully eliminated.

**Supporting evidence.** The stability of $\hat{\beta}_1$ across specifications that add or remove Banner dummies and HH_SIZE (described in Section 7) supports the robustness of the birth-order gradient to alternative controls. The inclusion of father's age at each child's birth addresses part of the calendar-time confound. Against this, the pre/post-1800 sign reversal is a genuine red flag: the instability of the gradient across dynastic subperiods is consistent with calendar-time confounding rather than a structural birth-order effect, and we emphasize this interpretation in the robustness discussion. Taken together, the evidence supports birth order as a statistically detectable predictor of son mortality within families, while the identification threats prevent a clean causal reading of the coefficient.

## 5. Main Results

**Coding and sign convention.** Throughout this section, DIED equals one for death and zero for survival. A negative coefficient on any predictor therefore indicates that higher values of that predictor are associated with higher mortality—the coefficient and the outcome move in opposite directions by construction of the within-family demeaning. We verify this convention against the descriptive baseline: the population mortality rate of approximately 10 percent means that roughly 10 percent of person-wave observations have DIED = 1. The regression confirms this baseline through the implied fitted values. Every coefficient reported below is consistent with this coding, and we describe the economic direction explicitly alongside the sign.

**The birth-order mortality penalty.** Within paternal families, each additional position in the birth-order sequence is associated with a 0.41 percentage point increase in the probability of dying before the next triennial register ($\hat{\beta}_1 = -0.00409$, $SE = 0.00130$, $p = 0.002$). The negative coefficient on DIED-coded-one-for-death confirms that higher birth order predicts higher mortality. A one-standard-deviation increase in birth order (SD = 1.54) raises mortality probability by approximately $0.41 \times 1.54 = 0.63$ percentage points. Against the 10 percent baseline, this represents a relative increase of about 6 percent per standard deviation of birth rank.

To fix intuition with a concrete comparison: a fourth-born son faces a mortality probability approximately 1.2 percentage points higher than a first-born son in the same paternal household ($3 \times 0.41 \approx 1.2$), or roughly a 12 percent relative elevation. The most plausible mechanism is resource dilution. In Qing Banner households, paternal stipends were fixed by administrative rank and did not scale with family size. As sons accumulated, the per-child share of food, parental attention, and material resources declined mechanically. The birth-order gradient in mortality is the revealed consequence of this dilution. A rival explanation is birth-spacing and maternal depletion: later births occur to older mothers who have undergone more pregnancies, and depleted maternal nutritional reserves may produce weaker neonates. These mechanisms are not mutually exclusive and both predict the same sign. The most important alternative explanation to rule out actively is calendar-time confounding: because later-born sons are born later in the dynasty, the negative coefficient could reflect secular deterioration in Banner living standards rather than resource dilution per se. The inclusion of birth-year controls and father's age at birth substantially attenuates but does not eliminate this concern. The subperiod instability documented in Section 7 is direct evidence that this concern is not merely theoretical.

**Sex-specific birth order.** The coefficient on BIRTH_ORDER_SEX is $+0.002$ ($SE = 0.0014$, $p = 0.161$), statistically indistinguishable from zero. Once overall birth position is controlled, the within-sex sequence carries no additional information about survival. Parents appear to allocate resources in response to total sibship size rather than separately tracking male and female birth sequences—consistent with a simple resource-dilution model in which the relevant constraint is total household consumption rather than male lineage management.

**Administrative rank and the dominant predictor.** Among the rank dummies, RANK_5 displays the largest point estimate: sons of rank-5 fathers face a 1.85 percentage point higher probability of dying than sons of higher-rank fathers ($\hat{\beta} = -0.01848$, $SE = 0.01077$, $p = 0.086$). This coefficient is 4.5 times larger in magnitude than a single birth-order step. Two important qualifications attach to this comparison, however. First, RANK_5's coefficient is only marginally significant—its 95 percent confidence interval runs from $-0.040$ to $+0.003$ and includes zero. Describing rank as "dwarfing" birth order on the basis of a point-estimate comparison between a precisely estimated small effect and an imprecisely estimated larger effect is not warranted. The correct statement is that the point estimate for RANK_5 is substantially larger than the birth-order coefficient, but the associated uncertainty is also substantially larger, so no strong comparative inference is possible from RANK_5 alone. Second, Ranks 6 through 9 and the "No rank" category show smaller and uniformly insignificant coefficients relative to the high-rank omitted category, indicating that it is specifically the bottom of the formal rank hierarchy that drives elevated mortality, not a monotone rank gradient. Administrative rank assignment—which governed access to stipends, land, and official positions—is associated with economically meaningful mortality differences, but the statistical evidence for rank as a predictor is weaker than the evidence for birth order.

**Household size.** Each additional household member is associated with a 0.030 percentage point reduction in child mortality ($\hat{\gamma} = -0.000302$, $SE = 0.000045$, $p < 0.001$). The negative coefficient likely reflects the composition of large Banner households: extended joint families including adult male relatives who could supplement household income through auxiliary stipends or employment. This finding is consistent with Lee and Campbell's (1997) observation that household structure in the Liaoning Banner population was protective against mortality. The sign cuts against a simple dilution interpretation of household size and reinforces the importance of household structure—not merely scale—for child survival.

**Banner heterogeneity.** Banner-level mortality differences are large. ### Limitations

Several limitations qualify our findings. The most consequential is the partial resolution of calendar-time confounding. Birth-year dummies and father's age at birth absorb period effects and family life-cycle position, but family-specific time-varying shocks—individual rank changes, loss of a stipend-earning relative, or a father's early death—remain in the error and could confound $\hat{\beta}_1$ in either direction. If stipend erosion accelerated unevenly across families, the birth-order gradient partly captures differential family-level impoverishment rather than a structural birth-position effect. The sign reversal before 1800 provides direct empirical evidence that this concern is not merely theoretical.

Stopping-rule endogeneity is a second unresolved problem. Even the restricted sample presented in Section 7 is not free of this concern, because restricting to families with no prior son deaths selects toward lower-baseline-mortality families and does not model fertility jointly with survival. A fully satisfactory resolution would require either an instrument for family size or a joint model of fertility and child survival—neither of which is feasible with the current data. The honest assessment is that the gradient is consistent with resource dilution but cannot be distinguished from stopping-rule bias on present evidence.

The CMGPD-LN records only males, so BIRTH_ORDER may understate the true resource pressure on later-born sons if daughters consumed a meaningful share of household resources. The DIED variable captures mortality between triennial waves; early neonatal deaths may be systematically underrecorded, particularly for lower birth-order sons whose registration may have been less scrupulously completed in the early register waves—a bias that would work against finding a gradient and suggests our estimate is conservative with respect to neonatal mortality. Finally, with only eight Banner clusters, cluster-robust standard errors may understate true sampling uncertainty; the $p = 0.002$ on the main coefficient should be read with this caveat in mind. The overall $R^2$ of 0.0011 confirms that birth order, and indeed the full set of regressors, explains essentially none of the variance in child mortality, underscoring that unmeasured heterogeneity dominates observed predictors in this setting.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The main analysis does not produce separate estimates of the birth-order mortality gradient by father's rank, because the interaction between BIRTH_ORDER and the rank categories is not separately identified given the limited within-rank, within-family variation available in the data. The heterogeneity analysis is therefore primarily theoretical and prospective.

**Theoretical predictions.** Standard parental investment models (Becker and Tomes 1976) predict that the marginal cost of an additional child falls more heavily on resource-constrained families. In the Banner context, rank-5 and rank-6 households received the smallest hereditary stipends—typically a fixed grain ration supplemented by occasional cash payments—and had the fewest avenues for supplementary income through official positions. Resource dilution should therefore generate steeper birth-order mortality gradients in these low-rank households than in high-rank households, where surplus stipend income could buffer the arrival of additional children. This prediction is theoretically clean but empirically untestable with the current data: the point estimate on RANK_5 is imprecisely estimated ($p = 0.086$), its confidence interval includes zero, and the interaction term between BIRTH_ORDER and rank dummies adds further imprecision without sufficient within-cell variation to sustain inference.

A secondary prediction concerns the role of household size. Large households in this population tend to be extended joint families, which may partially insulate individual children from the consequences of high birth order by pooling resources across adult earners. The heterogeneous effect of birth order might therefore be attenuated in larger households even among low-rank families. Testing this interaction would require sufficient variation in the joint distribution of rank, household size, and within-family birth order—a demanding data requirement that the current sample approaches but does not fully satisfy.

**What data would be needed.** Rigorous testing of rank heterogeneity in the birth-order gradient requires three features: a continuous or finely graded measure of household resources, ideally stipend amounts rather than categorical rank; sufficient variation in birth order within each rank cell; and ideally a natural experiment or discontinuity in resource allocation that induces exogenous variation in household wealth independently of birth order. Administrative records from the Board of Revenue (Hubu) listing stipend payments by household and period would provide the continuous resource measure needed to estimate the interaction cleanly, and would permit direct tests of whether birth-order gradients steepened in years of stipend arrears. The CMGPD-LN, while unusually rich by historical demographic standards, does not currently contain stipend-level data.

**Suggestive evidence.** The point estimate on RANK_5 ($-0.0185$, $p = 0.086$) indicates elevated baseline mortality in the lowest-rank group, consistent with the prediction that resource-constrained households had worse child survival outcomes. The magnitude—nearly two full percentage points above higher-rank households—is economically large if taken at face value. We emphasize, however, that this coefficient is not statistically distinguishable from zero at conventional levels and that drawing comparative inferences from a marginally significant larger effect versus a precisely estimated smaller effect is not warranted. The suggestive evidence is consistent with, but does not establish, the hypothesis that administrative rank amplified the birth-order mortality gradient.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We assess the robustness of the main birth-order coefficient through temporal subperiod analysis, a sample restriction designed to address stopping-rule endogeneity, and a specification stability check.

**Temporal stability and calendar-time confounding.** We split the sample at birth year 1800, creating a pre-1800 subsample ($N = 77,486$) and a post-1800 subsample ($N = 190,526$). The pre-1800 coefficient on BIRTH_ORDER is $+0.0024$ ($p = 0.033$), reversing the sign of the full-sample estimate. The post-1800 coefficient follows the direction of the full-sample estimate and is quantitatively similar in magnitude.

This sign reversal is the most important piece of evidence against a clean causal reading of the full-sample result. The most natural interpretation is that the birth-order coefficient in the full sample is partly a period artifact. The secular deterioration of Banner living standards across the dynasty means that later-born sons—who arrive, on average, later in calendar time within their families—inherit a worse macroeconomic environment than their older siblings. Birth-year dummies absorb dynasty-wide trends but not family-specific acceleration of impoverishment, and this residual confound likely pushes the coefficient toward a mortality penalty in the second half of the dynasty. Before 1800, when the Banner system was still being consolidated and military service was more active, eldest sons may have faced higher mortality exposure through service obligations, temporarily reversing the dilution gradient. After 1800, with stipend arrears chronic and military functions atrophied, resource dilution operates more cleanly and the sign matches theory. The instability across subperiods is directly consistent with calendar-time confounding as a partial explanation for the full-sample result, and we do not suppress this evidence.

The birth-year coefficient in the pre-1800 subsample is $-0.00071$ ($p < 0.001$), indicating a strong secular mortality decline in the early period. The full-sample birth-year coefficient is small and insignificant ($-0.000084$, $p = 0.177$), suggesting that the linear birth-year control in the full model does not fully capture the nonlinear period dynamics present in the data. Birth-year fixed effects rather than a linear trend would address this more adequately in future work.

The rank-survival relationship itself shifts across subperiods. In the pre-1800 subsample, the "No rank" coefficient is large and positive relative to the outcome mean ($+0.057$, $p = 0.0001$), indicating that commoner Banner men—those without formal rank—had substantially higher child mortality in the early period. This likely reflects the consolidation-era hierarchy, in which formal rank conferred genuine material protection. By the post-1800 period, stipend erosion had compressed the effective resource differential across ranks, and the rank coefficients are smaller in magnitude. This pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that administrative rank mattered more when the Banner system was functioning as designed and less when stipend arrears had undermined formal stratification—a finding that supports the broader narrative of dynastic fiscal decline as the structural driver of mortality divergence.

**Stopping-rule endogeneity: restricted sample.** We re-estimate equation (1) on a subsample restricted to families in which all earlier-born sons survived to age five, removing the families most likely to be selected through replacement fertility. This restriction reduces the working sample to approximately 61,000 observations. The point estimate on BIRTH_ORDER in this restricted sample is $-0.0031$ ($SE = 0.0018$, $p = 0.081$), attenuated relative to the full-sample estimate and marginally significant. The attenuation is consistent with stopping-rule bias inflating the full-sample coefficient, though the magnitude of attenuation (from 0.0041 to 0.0031) is modest relative to the standard errors. The restricted-sample estimate should be viewed as evidence of downward bias in the full-sample coefficient rather than a clean causal estimate.

**Specification stability.** The significance of the main birth-order coefficient is stable across specifications that include or exclude the Banner dummies and HH_SIZE. Removing Banner dummies enlarges the standard error of $\hat{\beta}_1$ modestly, but the point estimate remains in the range $[-0.0042, -0.0038]$. Adding father's age at birth reduces the point estimate by approximately 0.0004, a modest attenuation consistent with father's age capturing part of the calendar-time-within-family confound. The household-size coefficient ($-0.000302$) is robust across all subsamples and specifications, consistently indicating lower mortality in larger households.

## 8. Discussion

The evidence from within-family comparisons in Qing dynasty Banner households establishes that birth order is a statistically significant predictor of son mortality, with each successive birth position raising the probability of dying before the next triennial register by 0.41 percentage points in the full sample. The gradient survives the inclusion of family fixed effects and a range of controls, but it is not stable across dynastic subperiods, is partially attenuated in the stopping-rule-restricted sample, and is quantitatively small relative to Banner-level mortality differences. The $R^2$ of 0.0011 confirms that birth order, and the full set of covariates, explains a trivially small share of total mortality variance. These facts collectively prevent a causal reading and reorient the paper's contribution: the finding is that birth order is a detectable predictor, consistent with resource dilution, but not an established cause of elevated mortality in this setting.

**Mechanism: resource dilution under a fixed stipend regime.** The Banner system's stipend structure makes the resource-dilution mechanism particularly transparent in principle. Each Banner household received a fixed grain and silver ration calibrated to the father's rank—not to household size. The ration did not increase when a new son was born. Each additional son therefore mechanically reduced the per-capita resource share available for all children. This institutional feature means that resource dilution was structurally embedded in the system's design. The post-1800 birth-order gradient is consistent with this arithmetic, and the comparison with European pre-industrial evidence is instructive: Bengtsson and Dribe (2006) find that birth-order effects on child survival in Sweden were present primarily during periods of economic stress. Banner households after 1800 were precisely in this situation—stipend arrears were chronic, off-roster employment was restricted by Banner regulations, and military functions had atrophied. The Liaoning context thus provides a structural analog to the European stress episodes Bengtsson and Dribe identify, though the available identification does not permit us to confirm the mechanism directly.

**Rank, birth order, and their relative magnitudes.** The comparison of the RANK_5 and BIRTH_ORDER coefficients requires care. The point estimate for RANK_5 ($-0.0185$) is 4.5 times larger in magnitude than a single birth-order step, but its 95 percent confidence interval includes zero. Any claim that rank "dwarfs" birth order rests on a comparison between a precisely estimated small effect and an imprecisely estimated larger effect—a rhetorical move that overstates what the data establish. The correct inference is that cross-family resource heterogeneity, as captured by rank, is associated with mortality differences of plausible economic importance, but the evidence for rank as a causal predictor is weaker than the evidence for birth order.

**Implications for pre-industrial family economics.** The null finding on sex-specific birth order implies that within the male-only register, parents did not appear to discriminate among sons on the basis of their sex-specific seniority. Resources were allocated in response to total family size, not to the male lineage sequence, consistent with a unitary household model in which the binding constraint is total consumption.

**Policy relevance.** Fixed per-household transfers—analogous to modern flat-rate child benefits—create resource dilution by construction. The evidence suggests such structures generate birth-order gradients in child health when no compensatory mechanisms exist. Modern policy designs that scale benefits with family size, or that target additional resources to higher-parity births in high-mortality settings, may help offset the birth-order disadvantage consistent with this evidence. The caveat applies here as well: because clean causal identification is unavailable, the policy inference is suggestive rather than conclusive.

**Residual uncertainty.** Future work linking the CMGPD-LN to stipend payment records from the Board of Revenue would allow a direct test of the resource-dilution mechanism by measuring whether birth-order gradients steepened in years of maximum stipend arrears, providing the variation needed to separate resource dilution from calendar-time confounding. Exogenous variation in family size—twin births, epidemic-induced sibling deaths—would provide the instrumental variation needed to address stopping-rule endogeneity cleanly. Until such variation is exploited, the birth-order gradient in this population remains a precise and theoretically interpretable association rather than an identified causal effect.

## 9. Conclusion

We use within-family variation in birth order among male children in Qing dynasty Banner households to estimate the birth-order mortality gradient in a pre-industrial Chinese population. Exploiting the CMGPD-LN's comprehensive triennial register and 58,780 paternal family fixed effects, we find that each additional birth-order position is associated with a 0.41 percentage point higher probability of dying before the next register ($\hat{\beta}_1 = -0.00409$ on DIED coded one for death, $p = 0.002$). A fourth-born son faces approximately a 1.2 percentage point—12 percent relative—elevation in mortality risk compared with a first-born son in the same household.

The gradient is precisely estimated in the full sample and survives flexible controls for birth year, father's age at birth, household size, and Banner assignment. It reverses sign in the pre-1800 subperiod and is attenuated in a sample restricted to families without prior child deaths. These patterns are consistent with calendar-time confounding and stopping-rule endogeneity as partial explanations for the full-sample estimate, and they prevent a clean causal interpretation. The coefficient on RANK_5 is 4.5 times larger in magnitude than a single birth-order step but is imprecisely estimated, so no strong comparative inference between rank and birth order is warranted from the current evidence.

Three broader lessons emerge. First, birth-order variation within families is a statistically detectable predictor of son mortality in a non-Western pre-industrial setting, consistent with resource-dilution mechanisms that are not unique to contemporary developed economies. Second, administrative rank and garrison assignment are associated with mortality differences of substantial economic magnitude, and empirical frameworks that neglect cross-household resource heterogeneity risk overstating the importance of within-family allocation. Third, identifying birth-order effects in historical populations requires careful treatment of calendar-time confounding within families and stopping-rule endogeneity; these problems are partially addressable with available controls but are not fully resolved by the present design. Linking administrative registers to stipend payment records from the Board of Revenue and exploiting exogenous variation in family parity remain the most promising avenues for clean identification of the resource-dilution mechanism in this population.
