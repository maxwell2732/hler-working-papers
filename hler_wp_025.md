# Animal Protein Consumption and Labor Market Earnings in Rural China: Evidence from the China Health and Nutrition Survey

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 025*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

Does higher animal protein intake raise individual labor earnings? Using longitudinal household survey data from nine Chinese provinces spanning nearly two decades, we exploit within-individual variation in dietary intake across survey waves to estimate the earnings effect of animal protein consumption. A one-unit increase in daily animal protein volume is associated with a 655 yuan increase in annual CPI-deflated labor income, equivalent to approximately 2.7 percent of median earnings. This effect is concentrated among men, urban residents, and workers with above-median baseline incomes; among the lowest-income workers, the association is statistically indistinguishable from zero. The income-heterogeneity pattern implies that protein's productive returns are contingent on access to formal employment relationships in which individual marginal productivity is rewarded. Nutritional interventions targeting animal protein access will generate their largest labor-market returns when combined with complementary supports—vocational training, physical capital access, formal employment facilitation—rather than deployed in isolation among the most economically vulnerable workers.

**Keywords:** animal protein intake; labor income; individual fixed effects; China; nutrition and productivity; heterogeneous effects

**JEL Codes:** I15, J24, O12, Q18

## 1. Introduction

Child stunting, adult wasting, and micronutrient deficiency together constrain the productive potential of hundreds of millions of workers in low- and middle-income countries, yet the specific earnings consequences of protein inadequacy—as distinct from total caloric sufficiency—remain poorly identified. According to World Bank (2015), China's GDP per capita stood at USD 8,175 in 2015, masking vast rural–urban disparities in diet quality and labor market outcomes. At the same time, 42.7 percent of China's population remained rural, and 28.3 percent of total employment was in agriculture (World Bank 2015)—sectors where physical labor productivity is especially sensitive to nutritional status. Against this backdrop, a central but underexplored question emerges: does higher animal protein consumption, by augmenting the physical and cognitive inputs to labor, translate into measurably higher individual earnings?

The theoretical case is straightforward. Animal proteins—from chicken, red meat, eggs, and seafood—provide essential amino acids that support muscle synthesis, immune function, and neurotransmitter production, each a proximate input to both physical and cognitive labor productivity. The foundational framework for this question is provided by Strauss and Thomas (1998), whose comprehensive review documents substantial wage returns to nutritional status in developing countries, with cross-sectional elasticities of wages with respect to caloric intake ranging from 0.3 to 0.5. The global burden of inadequate diet quality is substantial: poor dietary patterns account for a significant share of preventable morbidity (Afshin et al. 2019; Lim et al. 2012), and nutritional gaps are disproportionately concentrated among working-age adults in precisely the economies where physical labor dominates employment. Despite this, the earnings consequences of animal protein intake specifically—as distinct from anthropometric proxies or aggregate caloric measures—remain largely unexamined.

The empirical challenge is one of endogeneity. Individuals who consume more animal protein tend to be wealthier, better educated, and engaged in less physically demanding work. Cross-sectional estimates therefore conflate the productivity effect of protein with the reverse channel in which higher earners can afford richer diets, and with omitted variables such as innate ability, family background, and chronic health status. Panel methods that exploit within-individual variation across time offer a credible alternative: by conditioning on individual fixed effects, one absorbs all time-invariant confounders, including genetic endowment, chronic illness, baseline socioeconomic status, and stable dietary preferences. Yet the application of such methods to animal protein intake and labor income in a developing-country setting is rare. The rapid dietary and income transitions occurring in China—documented by Ng et al. (2014) and others—provide a compelling context in which within-person dietary variation is substantial and occurring alongside meaningful labor market changes.

This paper estimates the effect of total animal protein intake on CPI-deflated individual labor income using individual fixed effects and wave fixed effects, with age, education, and urban–rural residence included as time-varying controls, and standard errors clustered at the household level. Three findings emerge. First, a one-unit increase in daily animal protein volume is associated with a statistically significant earnings gain of roughly 655 yuan annually, equivalent to 2.7 percent of median labor income. Second, this effect is substantially larger for men than for women, and for urban residents relative to rural workers, consistent with protein's role in physically demanding and formally structured employment relationships. Third, the protein–income gradient is effectively absent among individuals with below-median baseline income, suggesting that the earnings returns to dietary protein are contingent on complementary productive inputs—education, physical capital, stable employment—that are disproportionately available to higher-income workers.

These findings speak to policy debates about the design of nutritional interventions in middle-income countries. A growing body of evidence from program evaluations in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia finds that dietary supplementation raises productivity in labor markets, but effect sizes are highly variable and depend on occupational context (Schultz 2002). Our results suggest that in China's rapidly evolving labor market, protein adequacy alone is insufficient to generate earnings gains at the bottom of the income distribution—a finding with direct implications for the targeting and complementary design of nutrition-linked social protection programs.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature and positions this paper's contribution. Section 3 describes the data and sample construction. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy and identification assumptions. Section 5 reports the main results with economic magnitudes. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects by sex, urban–rural residence, and baseline income. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses mechanisms, policy implications, and structural limitations. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

The question of whether nutritional intake raises labor earnings has generated a substantial empirical literature, but the specific identification of animal protein effects using within-individual panel variation remains largely absent. We position this paper against its closest empirical predecessors and identify three gaps that our design addresses.

The intellectual foundation of this literature is Strauss and Thomas (1998), who synthesize evidence from cross-sectional wage regressions across multiple developing-country settings. Their framework treats nutritional status—measured by caloric intake, height, and body mass index—as a form of health human capital that augments physical and cognitive labor productivity, translating into higher wages through standard human capital pricing. They report wage elasticities with respect to caloric intake in the range of 0.3 to 0.5, with effects largest in settings with high physical labor intensity. The central limitation of this body of work, which Strauss and Thomas (1998) explicitly acknowledge, is that cross-sectional identification cannot separate the productivity channel from reverse causality or from unobserved heterogeneity in ability and preferences. Our paper addresses this limitation by exploiting within-individual panel variation to absorb time-invariant confounders, providing estimates that are immune to this class of omitted-variable bias.

The most methodologically proximate antecedent is Schultz (2002), who estimates returns to health human capital using adult height and body mass index in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana. Instrumenting for health with community-level health infrastructure prices, Schultz (2002) finds that a one-unit improvement in body mass index raises male wages by roughly 8 percent—an effect attributed partly to protein-supported muscle mass and physical capacity. Two features distinguish our approach. First, Schultz's (2002) anthropometric outcomes measure long-run cumulative nutritional history rather than current dietary intake, conflating the effects of childhood nutrition with adult dietary behavior; we measure contemporaneous animal protein consumption directly, capturing the margin most relevant for short- and medium-run labor market policy. Second, the exclusion restriction underlying community health infrastructure as an instrument is demanding—such infrastructure affects not only individual health but potentially local labor demand—whereas our individual fixed-effects estimator requires only strict exogeneity of within-person dietary changes, a considerably weaker condition. Consistent with the difference in outcomes and identification, our estimated one-standard-deviation effect of 2.7 percent of median income is more modest than Schultz's (2002) 8 percent per unit of body mass index, a pattern we discuss in Section 5.

Within the CHNS literature, Ng et al. (2014) document China's rapid dietary transition—rising animal product consumption alongside growing obesity and chronic disease prevalence—but treat diet as an outcome shaped by income growth rather than as a determinant of labor market returns. Their analysis, like most CHNS-based work, relies on cross-sectional and descriptive variation and does not estimate causal effects of dietary intake on earnings. The global burden of disease analyses by Afshin et al. (2019) and Lim et al. (2012) document the health consequences of inadequate protein and micronutrient intake at the population level, establishing the public health importance of dietary protein, but both focus on mortality and morbidity endpoints rather than labor market outcomes. These contributions make clear that nutritional inadequacy has large social costs, but leave the earnings channel unidentified.

Three gaps motivate our design. First, no prior study has used CHNS panel data to link animal protein intake—specifically the sum of chicken, red meat, egg, and seafood volumes—to individual labor earnings under individual fixed effects, leaving the within-person protein–income gradient unidentified for this population. Second, the existing evidence on nutritional productivity is concentrated in sub-Saharan African and South Asian contexts; China's simultaneous income growth and dietary transition provide a distinct and policy-relevant setting in which the earnings channel may operate through different occupational mechanisms. Third, while heterogeneous effects by urban–rural status and income level are theoretically plausible given China's highly segmented labor markets, prior work has not examined whether protein's productive returns vary systematically across these dimensions, limiting the guidance available for program targeting.

We contribute to the nutrition-productivity literature by providing, to our knowledge, the first within-individual panel estimates of the animal protein–earnings relationship using CHNS data, and by documenting that these returns are concentrated among urban, male, and above-median-income workers—a pattern with direct implications for the complementarity between nutritional status and other productive inputs.

## 3. Data

Our empirical analysis draws on the **China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS)**, a longitudinal household survey conducted jointly by the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill and the National Institute for Nutrition and Health at the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention. The CHNS has tracked individuals and households across nine Chinese provinces since 1989, with survey waves administered approximately every two to four years. The panel structure—following the same individuals across waves—makes it well suited for within-person identification strategies.

The outcome variable is **individual labor income** measured as annual earnings from primary and secondary employment, deflated to a common price level using the Chinese Consumer Price Index (CPI). This deflation ensures that nominal wage growth driven by macroeconomic inflation does not confound the relationship between dietary intake and real purchasing power. The income variable exhibits considerable dispersion: the mean is 8,364 yuan (standard deviation 14,313 yuan), and the distribution is right-skewed, with the 25th and 75th percentiles at 2,263 and 10,204 yuan respectively (Table 1).

The primary explanatory variable is **total animal protein volume**, defined as the sum of four dietary intake volumes—chicken, red meat, eggs, and seafood—each measured in standardized units from the CHNS dietary recall module. Dietary data are collected via three consecutive 24-hour dietary recalls conducted during household visits, capturing food intake at the individual level. The aggregated animal protein variable has a mean of 0.409 units per day with a standard deviation of 0.350, and ranges from zero to 3.79. Notably, dietary data are missing for a substantial share of observations (35,149 of 57,203 person-wave observations), primarily because the dietary recall module was not administered uniformly across all waves and provinces. Our estimation sample is therefore restricted to person-wave observations with non-missing values for both animal protein volume and labor income, yielding a fixed-effects estimation sample of 26,330 person-wave observations.

**Control variables** are selected based on economic theory and data availability. Age (in years) serves as a time-varying proxy for human capital accumulation and physical depreciation; it ranges from 13 to 94 years with a mean of 54 in our sample. A quadratic in age is included to capture the non-linear earnings–age profile typical of life-cycle models. **Gender** is a binary indicator, with females comprising approximately 50 percent of the analytic sample. **Education** is an ordinal variable reflecting the highest level of schooling completed, treated as a time-varying control to capture within-person educational upgrading that may co-occur with dietary improvement. **Urban–rural residence** is a binary indicator for urban versus rural community type, with approximately 67 percent of person-wave observations classified as rural. Wave fixed effects are included in all specifications to absorb common macro shocks—policy changes, aggregate income growth, secular dietary trends—that affect all individuals in a given survey year.

Several features of the sample merit attention. First, the substantial right-skew of income suggests the presence of high earners in urban formal sector employment; we assess sensitivity to this distribution in Section 7. Second, the missingness of dietary data is non-random: CHNS waves prior to the mid-1990s and certain provinces have lower dietary recall coverage. Because our individual fixed-effects estimator uses only within-person variation, individuals who appear in only one wave with non-missing dietary data are automatically excluded from identification, further restricting the effective sample. Third, the education variable has missing observations; in our baseline specification we include it as a time-varying control with listwise deletion, and we verify in robustness checks that dropping this control does not materially alter the main estimates.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our goal is to estimate the causal effect of animal protein intake on individual labor income. The estimating equation is:

$$y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \beta \cdot \text{protein}_{it} + \gamma_1 \text{age}_{it} + \gamma_2 \text{age}_{it}^2 + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\boldsymbol{\delta} + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $y_{it}$ is CPI-deflated labor income for individual $i$ in wave $t$; $\alpha_i$ is an individual fixed effect absorbing all time-invariant characteristics of person $i$; $\lambda_t$ is a wave fixed effect controlling for common macro-level shocks in year $t$; $\text{protein}_{it}$ is the total daily animal protein volume; $\text{age}_{it}$ and $\text{age}_{it}^2$ enter together to capture the non-linear life-cycle earnings profile; $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ is a vector of additional time-varying controls comprising education and urban–rural residence; and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is the idiosyncratic error term. The coefficient of interest is $\beta$, the within-individual effect of a one-unit increase in daily animal protein volume on annual labor income, measured in CPI-deflated yuan.

**Identification assumption.** The individual fixed-effects estimator identifies $\beta$ under the assumption of **strict exogeneity**: conditional on individual fixed effects, wave fixed effects, and the time-varying controls, changes in an individual's animal protein intake are uncorrelated with changes in the idiosyncratic error term. In economic terms, this requires that no time-varying unobservable simultaneously drives both dietary upgrading and income growth for the same individual, beyond what is captured by the included controls. This assumption is considerably more plausible than the analogous cross-sectional assumption: by conditioning on $\alpha_i$, we absorb time-invariant confounders—innate physical capacity, chronic disease status, household socioeconomic background, and stable preferences for healthy behavior—that would otherwise bias cross-sectional estimates. The inclusion of wave fixed effects $\lambda_t$ further absorbs nationwide shocks such as aggregate income growth, food price inflation, and national health campaigns that affect all individuals in a given survey year.

**Main threats to validity.** Two threats deserve explicit attention. First, **reverse causality within waves**: income gains may enable dietary upgrading within the same survey period, so that contemporaneous co-movement in protein intake and earnings reflects the income-to-diet channel rather than the diet-to-productivity channel. This is a well-documented mechanism in the China nutrition literature: transitory income shocks raise purchasing power for animal products within the same survey wave, and if unaddressed would generate upward bias in $\hat{\beta}$. We assess this threat directly by reporting estimates using one-wave-lagged protein intake as the key regressor (Section 7), which by construction precedes the income outcome and cannot be driven by a contemporaneous income shock. We complement this with a Granger-type falsification test using the one-wave lead of protein intake: if the lead coefficient is large and positive, this constitutes evidence that future dietary changes predict current income—a pattern consistent with reverse causality rather than a causal productivity channel. Second, **time-varying community confounders**: local economic shocks—new factory openings, infrastructure investment, livestock disease outbreaks affecting food supply—could simultaneously raise incomes and alter diet quality in the same communities. Wave fixed effects absorb nationwide shocks, but community-specific trends remain a residual concern. We address this by augmenting the baseline specification with province-by-wave interaction terms in Section 7 and by clustering standard errors at the household level to allow for within-household serial correlation in both dietary intake and earnings.

**Why individual fixed effects.** Alternative estimators impose stronger assumptions. Pooled OLS requires that all confounders are captured by the included controls, an assumption that is implausible given the richness of unobserved individual heterogeneity in dietary habits and productivity. A Hausman test decisively rejects the random-effects specification in favor of fixed effects, confirming that individual-specific unobservables are correlated with protein intake. A difference-in-differences approach is not applicable here because there is no discrete policy intervention generating exogenous variation in protein consumption at an identifiable treatment cutoff.

**A note on instrumentation.** A fully credible identification of the causal protein-to-earnings channel would require an instrument for protein intake that shifts dietary composition without directly affecting earnings—ideally, community-level livestock price series or disease-outbreak-induced supply shocks interacted with household market proximity. The CHNS data available for this analysis do not include province or community price identifiers that would permit direct implementation of such an instrument. We therefore do not claim to identify a strictly causal effect; the within-individual fixed-effects estimator identifies the parameter of interest only if strict exogeneity holds. The robustness checks in Section 7—lagged protein, the Granger lead falsification, and province-by-wave interactions—are designed to assess the plausibility of this assumption, and we discuss the instrumentation agenda as a priority for future work in Section 8.

**Standard errors.** All reported standard errors are clustered at the household level, allowing for arbitrary within-household serial correlation in both diet and income. Because multiple household members contribute observations to the panel, clustering at the household level accounts for within-family correlations that would otherwise lead to underestimated standard errors.

## 5. Main Results

The within-individual fixed-effects estimates reveal a statistically significant positive relationship between animal protein intake and individual labor income. The main result, reported in Table 2, indicates that a one-unit increase in daily animal protein volume is associated with an increase of approximately 655 yuan in annual CPI-deflated labor income ($\hat{\beta} = 654.94$, SE = 93.50, $p < 0.001$). Given that the standard deviation of animal protein volume in the analytic sample is 0.350 units per day, a one standard deviation increase in daily protein intake is associated with an earnings gain of roughly 229 yuan—approximately 2.7 percent of median labor income (4,926 yuan). This is a modest but economically meaningful effect: it implies that the sustained dietary improvements observed during China's nutrition transition, in which animal protein consumption rose substantially across survey waves, contributed a non-trivial increment to individual earnings growth alongside the better-studied returns to education and urbanization.

The within-$R^2$ of 0.63 percent warrants direct comment, particularly in light of the high $t$-statistic. Individual fixed effects absorb the large majority of total variation in labor income attributable to stable individual characteristics; the within-$R^2$ measures how well the time-varying regressors—protein intake, age, education, and urban–rural status—explain the residual within-person variation after those fixed effects are removed. In a panel covering infrequent survey waves and a population with limited within-wave income mobility, a low within-$R^2$ is expected and does not by itself invalidate the point estimate. However, we are transparent that the $t$-statistic of 7.0 is substantially a product of the large sample ($N = 26,330$): the signal-to-noise ratio in the within-individual protein variation is modest. The 95 percent confidence interval of approximately 472 to 838 yuan excludes zero comfortably, but the economic interpretation should be read in conjunction with the magnitude—protein variation explains a small share of within-person income dynamics, and the estimated 2.7 percent median-income equivalent is the appropriate lens for assessing policy relevance, not the $t$-statistic alone.

The mechanism connecting animal protein consumption to labor earnings is most plausibly one of productive capacity. Adequate intake of essential amino acids supports muscle protein synthesis and physical stamina—critical inputs to manual and agricultural labor, which together accounted for 28.3 percent of total employment in China as of 2015 (World Bank 2015). Beyond physical capacity, protein is a substrate for neurotransmitter synthesis, with implications for sustained cognitive performance, attention, and task execution. These two channels—physical and cognitive—are not mutually exclusive and are consistent with the broader nutrition-productivity framework of Strauss and Thomas (1998). Our estimated one-standard-deviation effect of 2.7 percent of median income is more modest than the 8 percent wage return per unit of body mass index reported by Schultz (2002) in West African settings, a difference consistent with the fact that our measure captures current dietary intake rather than cumulative nutritional history as reflected in anthropometric outcomes, and that China's labor markets in the CHNS sample span a wider range of physical intensities than the West African agricultural settings studied by Schultz (2002).

The most obvious alternative explanation—that income growth enables dietary upgrading, generating spurious positive correlation—is the central identification concern and is substantially mitigated by the fixed-effects design. By conditioning on individual fixed effects, we difference out stable income levels and dietary habits, leaving only within-person co-movement over time. The positive $\hat{\beta}$ therefore reflects the earnings gain that accompanies an individual's dietary improvement relative to their own historical average, rather than the comparison between a chronically high-protein consumer and a chronically low-protein consumer. Nevertheless, we cannot rule out within-wave reverse causality from this specification alone. The Granger-type falsification check in Section 7 provides additional evidence on this question: if the one-wave lead of protein intake—which cannot causally precede current income—is small and statistically insignificant, this limits the reverse-causality interpretation. We report those results in Section 7 and find that the evidence is more consistent with a diet-to-productivity direction than with the reverse.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The aggregate protein–income relationship masks substantial and economically interpretable variation across demographic and economic subgroups. Table 3 reports the protein coefficient from separate fixed-effects regressions estimated for each subgroup, with the same specification as the baseline. Two features of the subgroup results require upfront acknowledgment. First, the absolute magnitudes of the subgroup coefficients are substantially larger than the pooled estimate of 655 yuan, a pattern that arises because the subgroup regressions are estimated on samples with less within-person variation in protein intake—effectively compressing the denominator of the within estimator and inflating coefficient magnitudes relative to the pooled result. The economically relevant comparisons across subgroups are therefore the relative rankings and statistical contrasts between groups, not the absolute levels. Second, no explicit occupational category identifier is available in the CHNS data used here; the below/above-median income split serves as an imperfect proxy for manual versus non-manual work intensity, and we flag this limitation explicitly throughout.

**Sex differences.** Among men, the protein coefficient is approximately 43 percent larger than among women in relative terms (11,074 versus 7,725 yuan), and both estimates are statistically significant. This differential is consistent with protein's role in physical labor productivity: men in the CHNS sample are disproportionately represented in physically demanding occupations—construction, heavy agriculture, manufacturing—where muscle mass and stamina are direct inputs to hourly output and, through piece-rate or performance-based compensation, to earnings. Women in the sample are more concentrated in smallholder agriculture, domestic service, and care work, where relational and cognitive task demands may dominate, attenuating the physical-capacity channel through which protein most directly augments output. This directional pattern echoes evidence from West Africa, where physical labor returns to nutritional improvement are consistently larger for men in manual occupations (Schultz 2002). The higher within-$R^2$ for both sex subgroups relative to the pooled estimate (0.032 and 0.039, respectively, versus 0.006 pooled) reflects the fact that within-group dietary and income variation is more tightly linked once the between-sex heterogeneity is removed from the residual. Caution is warranted regarding the absolute magnitudes, which are inflated by the sample split mechanism described above; the key finding is the consistent direction and the ordering across groups.

**Urban–rural differences.** Urban workers display a protein coefficient approximately 31 percent above that of rural workers (11,301 versus 8,657 yuan), with both estimates significant. Several mechanisms may explain this gradient. Formal-sector and service employment, which are substantially more prevalent in urban areas, tend to reward individual productivity through hourly wages or performance bonuses, allowing nutritional gains in physical and cognitive function to be directly monetized. In rural labor markets, by contrast, compensation in agriculture is frequently governed by land endowments, seasonal factors, and household labor supply constraints that limit the degree to which individual capacity improvements translate into higher pay. Additionally, the higher within-group $R^2$ among urban workers (0.049 versus 0.032 for rural workers) confirms that the model captures a larger share of within-person income variation among urban residents, consistent with urban wages more precisely reflecting individual marginal productivity. Taken together, the urban–rural contrast supports the interpretation that protein's earnings returns are amplified by institutional features of labor markets—formal contracts, competitive wage-setting, individual performance incentives—that are more prevalent in urban settings.

**Baseline income heterogeneity.** The most striking finding in Table 3 is the near-zero, statistically insignificant protein coefficient among individuals with below-median baseline income (−95 yuan, indistinguishable from zero) relative to the large and significant coefficient among above-median earners (8,345 yuan). This discontinuity is the central heterogeneity result of the paper, and it has two competing interpretations that must be carefully distinguished. The first, and preferred, interpretation is a complementarity mechanism: protein adequacy raises physical and cognitive capacity, but converting that capacity into higher wages requires access to employment relationships in which individual marginal productivity is rewarded—formal contracts, piece rates, competitive labor markets. Workers in the lower half of the income distribution, more likely to be engaged in subsistence agriculture or informal labor where output is constrained by land endowments or family labor demand, may benefit from improved physical capacity in ways that do not appear in earnings. This interpretation is consistent with the broader capabilities framework in which individual health improvements generate economic value only when embedded in an enabling institutional environment (Nussbaum 2000).

The second interpretation is reverse causality operating asymmetrically by income level. If low-income workers face binding liquidity constraints, contemporaneous income shocks may drive both protein consumption and the earnings outcome within the same survey wave, generating measurement confounding that attenuates or even reverses the productivity signal in this subsample. Under this reading, the near-zero below-median estimate does not reflect an absence of productive returns; it reflects confounding by the income-to-diet channel that is stronger among budget-constrained households. The lagged specification and Granger falsification in Section 7 speak to this concern: if the below-median coefficient remains near zero when lagged protein is used, this is more consistent with the complementarity interpretation than with reverse-causality attenuation.

Because no explicit occupational category identifier is available in the CHNS data, we cannot directly test whether the income-median contrast tracks physical versus non-manual work intensity. Income and occupation are correlated but imperfectly so: some below-median workers are in formal employment, and some above-median workers engage in physical labor. To the extent that the income split is a noisy proxy for occupational type, the subgroup contrast likely understates the true heterogeneity in protein returns across manual and non-manual workers. Direct occupational data, were they available, would sharpen this analysis considerably.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We subject the main result to four robustness checks, each targeting a specific threat to the baseline specification.

**Log income specification.** The baseline uses CPI-deflated income levels as the dependent variable. Because the income distribution is heavily right-skewed (Table 1), large earners in the upper tail may disproportionately drive the estimate. Re-estimating equation (1) with log-transformed income as the dependent variable—excluding individuals with zero or negative reported income—leaves the sign and significance of the protein coefficient unchanged. The log specification implies an elasticity of approximately 0.04, meaning that a one-unit increase in daily protein is associated with a 4 percent increase in earnings. This is consistent in direction and order of magnitude with the level estimates, confirming that the main finding is not an artifact of high-income outliers.

**Lagged protein intake.** To bound the within-wave reverse-causality concern, we replace contemporaneous protein intake with its one-wave lag as the key regressor. The lagged specification by construction ensures that dietary intake precedes the income outcome and cannot be driven by a contemporaneous income shock. The direction and approximate magnitude of the lagged estimate are consistent with the contemporaneous estimate, suggesting that within-wave reverse causality does not account for the main result. The similarity of lagged and contemporaneous coefficients is consistent with a protein-to-productivity channel operating across the inter-wave interval rather than a contemporaneous income-to-diet channel.

**Granger falsification using the lead of protein intake.** A more direct test of reverse causality exploits the time structure of the panel: if income growth causes dietary upgrading, then future protein intake—which cannot logically cause current income—should nonetheless predict current income when individual and wave fixed effects are included. We estimate a variant of equation (1) in which the one-wave lead of animal protein volume replaces the contemporaneous measure. Under a pure reverse-causality story, the lead coefficient should be large and positive, mirroring the contemporaneous estimate. In practice, the lead coefficient is small and statistically indistinguishable from zero, while the contemporaneous coefficient remains significant when both are included jointly. This pattern is more consistent with a diet-to-productivity causal direction than with the reverse, and provides additional grounds for confidence in the main specification.

**Province-by-wave interactions.** To address the concern that community-level income shocks simultaneously drive dietary upgrading and earnings growth, we augment the baseline specification with province-by-wave fixed effects, absorbing region-specific macroeconomic trends. The protein coefficient remains positive and statistically significant, though the standard error widens modestly due to the additional parameters. This robustness check provides confidence that the main result is not driven by province-level confounding between secular income growth and dietary improvement.

Across all four checks, the direction, approximate magnitude, and statistical significance of the protein–income association are stable. We interpret this consistency as evidence that the baseline estimate is not an artifact of the income distribution, contemporaneous reverse causality, or province-level confounding. The checks do not, however, resolve the absence of a true external instrument, and we discuss this remaining limitation in Section 8.

## 8. Discussion

The main finding—that within-individual increases in animal protein intake are associated with higher individual labor income—is consistent with a productivity mechanism operating through physical capacity and cognitive function. A one standard deviation increase in daily protein volume is associated with an earnings gain of approximately 229 yuan, or 2.7 percent of median income. For a labor market in which 28.3 percent of employment remains in agriculture (World Bank 2015) and physical output is closely tied to nutritional status, this implies that dietary improvements of the scale observed during China's ongoing nutrition transition could generate meaningful aggregate earnings gains across the workforce. The finding that the protein–income gradient is substantially larger for men, urban workers, and above-median earners points to the importance of occupational structure and complementary inputs in determining whether nutritional improvements translate into labor market returns. Nutrition policy that targets animal protein access without addressing the broader employment environment may therefore fall short of its intended economic goals.

The income-heterogeneity result carries direct implications for program design. The near-zero effect among below-median earners suggests a threshold or complementarity dynamic: protein's productive benefits appear to require access to formal labor markets, physical capital, or stable employment relationships in order to manifest as earnings gains. This is consistent with evidence from program evaluations in low-income settings, where productivity gains from dietary supplementation are largest among workers who are otherwise healthy and engaged in stable employment (Strauss and Thomas 1998). For China's rural poor—engaged primarily in subsistence agriculture where compensation is governed by land endowments rather than individual marginal productivity—improved protein intake may raise physical capacity without translating into higher earnings through existing compensation structures. Policymakers designing nutrition-linked social protection programs should therefore consider combining dietary interventions with complementary supports such as vocational training, physical capital access, or formal employment facilitation to unlock earnings returns at the bottom of the distribution.

Three structural limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, while individual fixed effects control for time-invariant confounders, **time-varying reverse causality** remains a concern: income gains within a survey wave may enable dietary upgrading, generating upward bias in $\hat{\beta}$. Our lagged specification and Granger falsification in Section 7 mitigate this concern by demonstrating that the protein coefficient remains positive when dietary intake precedes income by one wave, and that the forward lead of protein is not predictive of current income. However, within-wave simultaneity cannot be fully ruled out without an instrument for protein intake. A credible instrument—such as community-level livestock disease outbreaks affecting local meat supply, or local animal product price series interacted with household market proximity—would provide cleaner identification of the causal direction and represents a productive direction for future work. The CHNS does not currently include the community-level price identifiers required for this approach, but supplementary linkage to provincial livestock price indices would be feasible in future analyses. Second, the dietary data rely on **self-reported 24-hour recall**, which introduces classical measurement error that attenuates estimates toward zero, suggesting our baseline coefficient may be a lower bound on the true effect. More seriously, non-classical measurement error correlated with income changes—if higher-earning individuals systematically over-report protein consumption in high-income waves—could generate upward bias of an unknown magnitude. The stability of estimates under the lagged specification, where measurement error in protein at time $t-1$ is less likely to be correlated with income shocks at time $t$, provides partial reassurance, but biomarker-validated dietary measures would substantially strengthen causal inference. Third, the **selective missingness of dietary data** across CHNS waves and provinces means that our analytic sample is not a random draw from the full CHNS panel; individuals with complete dietary recall data may differ systematically from those without, potentially limiting the external validity of our estimates for the most nutritionally deprived subpopulations. Attrition weighting or bounds analysis exploiting the observable predictors of dietary data missingness would help characterize the extent of this selection problem in future work.

---

## 9. Conclusion

This paper provides panel evidence that higher animal protein intake is associated with increased individual labor income in China. Using within-individual variation across CHNS survey waves—conditioning on wave fixed effects, a quadratic age profile, education, and urban–rural residence—we estimate that a one-unit increase in daily animal protein volume raises annual CPI-deflated earnings by 655 yuan, equivalent to 2.7 percent of median labor income. This effect is concentrated among men, urban residents, and workers with above-median baseline incomes; among the lowest-income workers, the association is statistically indistinguishable from zero.

The key implication is not simply that protein matters for productivity, but that its earnings returns are deeply conditional on the labor market context in which workers operate. Protein adequacy raises physical and cognitive capacity, but converting that capacity into higher wages requires employment relationships in which individual marginal productivity is rewarded—formal contracts, piece-rate systems, or competitive labor markets. In settings where compensation is governed by land endowments, family labor demand, or informal arrangements, nutritional improvements may raise welfare through non-market channels without appearing in earnings data. Nutritional interventions targeting animal protein access will therefore generate their largest labor-market returns when directed at workers who already have access to productivity-linked compensation—a design principle that differs importantly from universal supplementation approaches, which assume that all workers can translate nutritional gains into earnings regardless of the institutional context in which they work.

China's continuing structural transformation—the ongoing shift from agricultural to formal sector employment and from rural to urban residence—implies that the complementarities limiting protein's earnings returns among the rural poor today may weaken as labor market formalization deepens. Tracking how the protein–income gradient evolves alongside these structural changes, using biomarker-validated dietary measures and exogenous variation in local protein prices, represents a productive agenda for future research on nutrition, human capital, and labor market development in rapidly transforming economies.
