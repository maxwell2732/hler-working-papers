# Retirement, Rural-Urban Divide, and Cognitive Aging: Evidence from CHARLS

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 031*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

China's rapid population aging raises urgent questions about whether retirement improves or harms cognitive function, and whether institutional context moderates this effect. Using five waves of a nationally representative Chinese panel spanning 2011 to 2020, we estimate within-person retirement effects on cognitive function, exploiting individual transitions into retirement while absorbing all time-invariant confounders. Retirement is associated with a modest improvement in total cognitive scores on average, but this masks pronounced heterogeneity by household registration status. Rural hukou holders exhibit a larger retirement-related cognitive gain than urban counterparts — consistent with rural workers experiencing a sharper reduction in physically taxing, cognitively unstimulating employment. To address reverse causality, we validate results against statutory retirement age thresholds as a robustness check. These findings challenge the developed-country consensus that retirement uniformly harms cognition and suggest that China's dual-track welfare system produces fundamentally different cognitive aging consequences. Expanding formal pension coverage to rural residents may therefore carry unrecognized cognitive health dividends alongside income-support benefits.

**Keywords:** retirement and cognition; cognitive aging; rural-urban inequality; hukou status; China; panel fixed effects

**JEL Codes:** I12, J26, J43, O15

## 1. Introduction

China's population is aging at a pace unmatched among large economies: by 2020, more than 260 million Chinese citizens were aged 60 or above, and projections suggest this figure will nearly double by 2050. At the same time, cognitive decline and dementia impose enormous welfare costs on individuals and on families who bear the burden of informal caregiving in a society with limited formal long-term care infrastructure. Understanding whether the transition from work to retirement accelerates or retards cognitive decline is therefore a question of first-order importance for social policy. Yet existing evidence is fragmented, with most studies conducted in high-income countries where retirement is accompanied by generous pension income, structured leisure activities, and robust social safety nets. Whether these findings generalize to a middle-income country like China — where the institutional experience of retirement differs sharply across urban and rural residents — remains an open question.

China offers a uniquely instructive setting for studying the retirement-cognition nexus because its **hukou** (household registration) system creates two legally and institutionally distinct populations living side by side. According to World Bank (2015), rural residents comprised approximately 42.7 percent of China's total population, and agricultural employment accounted for 28.3 percent of total employment that year. Urban workers covered by the formal pension system retire into income-replaced leisure, with access to community centers, social networks, and structured activities. Rural residents, by contrast, historically lacked formal pension coverage — the New Rural Social Pension Scheme, introduced in 2009, provides only modest transfers — and many continue informal agricultural or casual work well past any nominal retirement age. These institutional differences imply that the cognitive consequences of "retirement" may be fundamentally different for the two groups, yet existing panel estimates rarely permit identification of this heterogeneity.

The economic question motivating this paper is whether the within-person effect of retirement on cognitive function differs systematically between rural and urban hukou holders in China. Prior work has documented a broadly negative average effect of retirement on cognition in developed-country settings (Rohwedder and Willis 2010; Bonsang, Adam, and Perelman 2012), but the channels — reduced mental stimulation, loss of social interaction, or changes in physical health — operate differently depending on the pre-retirement job environment. Panel evidence suggests that the effect is heterogeneous across occupations and health status (Coe and Zamarro 2011; Celidoni, Dal Bianco, and Weber 2017), yet neither study considers the institutional duality that characterizes China's labor market. In the Chinese context, large rural-urban disparities in cognitive aging trajectories have been documented (Lei and Lin 2009), motivating the hypothesis that retirement interacts with hukou status to produce divergent outcomes. According to World Bank (2015), China's GDP per capita stood at approximately USD 8,175 in 2015 — a level at which institutional gaps between urban and rural welfare systems remain pronounced but narrowing — making this a particularly opportune moment to detect such heterogeneity.

We address this question using five waves of the **China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study** (CHARLS), covering 2011 to 2020 and encompassing roughly 68,600 usable individual-wave observations for cognitive analysis. Our identification strategy exploits within-person variation in retirement status through individual fixed effects, which absorb all time-invariant confounders — including innate ability, lifetime socioeconomic status, gender, education, and baseline health. Because hukou status is itself time-invariant and thus absorbed by the individual fixed effects, we identify the key heterogeneity parameter through a retirement-by-rural-hukou interaction, recovering whether the within-person retirement effect differs across the two institutional contexts. Wave dummies absorb common time trends, and age and age-squared capture nonlinear within-person aging trajectories. As a robustness check against reverse causality — the concern that cognitive deterioration may itself prompt retirement — we exploit China's statutory retirement age thresholds to validate the direction and magnitude of our estimates.

Our results confirm meaningful heterogeneity. Retirement is associated with improved cognitive scores overall, but the effect is larger for rural hukou holders, consistent with rural retirement representing a more discontinuous break from cognitively and physically taxing work conditions. These findings speak directly to ongoing debates about whether China's pension expansion to rural areas will improve cognitive health among older rural residents, and they contribute to a broader literature on how labor market institutions shape the cognitive consequences of aging.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature and positions our contribution. Section 3 describes the CHARLS data and variable construction. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy, including the fixed-effects specification and identification assumptions. Section 5 reports the main results for total cognitive function. Section 6 examines heterogeneity by hukou status. Section 7 presents robustness checks using cognitive subdomains and alternative samples. Section 8 discusses mechanisms, policy implications, and limitations. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

The relationship between retirement and cognitive function has attracted substantial empirical attention, yet evidence from institutional contexts resembling China's remains scarce. The closest empirical predecessors to this paper differ from our analysis in setting, identification, or the dimension of heterogeneity examined, and each leaves an identifiable gap that our study fills.

The most directly relevant evidence comes from Rohwedder and Willis (2010), who use cross-national data from the U.S. Health and Retirement Study, the English Longitudinal Study of Ageing, and analogous European surveys to document that earlier retirement leads to lower memory scores. Their identification strategy relies on cross-country variation in early retirement incentives as an instrument for retirement timing. They find substantial negative effects — up to 23 percent lower recall among those who retire earliest — and attribute this to the "use it or lose it" mechanism whereby formal employment provides cognitive stimulation that retirement removes. While compelling, this strategy identifies a local average treatment effect for individuals induced by pension rules in developed welfare states, where retirement universally means pension-financed leisure and full labor force exit. The mechanism they emphasize is equally applicable in China, but the institutional context — where rural workers often continue informal labor after nominal retirement and pension replacement rates are far lower — makes their average effect uninformative about the rural-urban heterogeneity we examine.

Bonsang, Adam, and Perelman (2012) extend this line of inquiry using the Survey of Health, Ageing and Retirement in Europe (SHARE), instrumenting retirement with country-specific pension eligibility rules. Their main finding is that retirement reduces cognitive function by roughly 5 percent after five years, with stronger effects for men who held physically demanding jobs. This specification is closer to ours in spirit — it acknowledges occupational heterogeneity as a moderator — but it treats occupational differences as a covariate rather than as a manifestation of a fundamental institutional bifurcation. Their data contain no analog to the Chinese hukou system's legally enforced separation of welfare entitlements, which produces genuinely distinct retirement experiences rather than merely differences in job characteristics. Celidoni, Dal Bianco, and Weber (2017), also using SHARE, find that the cognitive cost of retirement is concentrated among workers who engaged in more mentally stimulating occupations before retirement, while those in physically demanding roles show smaller or reversed effects. This heterogeneity result directly motivates our focus on the rural-urban distinction, since rural Chinese workers disproportionately occupy the physically demanding end of the occupational distribution, yet no prior study has mapped this occupational heterogeneity onto the hukou-based institutional divide.

Within the Chinese literature, Lei and Lin (2009) provide foundational evidence that rural Chinese elderly experience faster cognitive decline than their urban counterparts, attributable in part to lower education and reduced social engagement. Their analysis, however, relies on cross-sectional comparisons that cannot disentangle the within-person effect of retirement from the selection of particular worker types into retirement at particular ages. More recent work by Cheng, Liu, Zhang, and Zhao (2018) documents the expansion of China's New Rural Social Pension Scheme and its effects on household consumption and labor supply, but cognitive outcomes are not examined. This gap is consequential: if pension generosity shapes whether rural residents fully exit the labor force, then the cognitive consequences of pension policy reform are entirely unidentified in the existing Chinese literature.

Three gaps in this literature motivate the design of the present study. No prior study has exploited China's hukou-based institutional divide to test whether the cognitive consequences of retirement differ between formal and informal welfare regimes within the same country. The existing evidence is limited to settings where retirement implies a clean cessation of work and an income replacement; a key unanswered question is what happens to cognition when "retirement" is a partial or informal transition, as is common for rural Chinese workers. And while the event-study design with individual fixed effects has become standard in developed-country panel studies of retirement, it has rarely been applied to CHARLS with explicit rural-urban heterogeneity as the central focus.

We contribute to this literature by providing individual fixed-effects estimates of the retirement-cognition relationship that explicitly identify rural-urban heterogeneity within the Chinese institutional context, using a panel that spans nearly a decade of aging transitions. Our interaction design recovers whether the cognitive effect of retirement differs by hukou status — the institutional variable that most sharply divides the Chinese welfare system — and our robustness checks against statutory age thresholds address the reverse causality concern that prior China-specific analyses leave unexamined.

## 3. Data

Our analysis draws on five waves of the **China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study** (CHARLS), a nationally representative longitudinal survey of Chinese residents aged 45 and above, conducted by Peking University. The survey waves used in this paper were collected in 2011, 2013, 2015, 2018, and 2020, yielding a panel structure that spans nearly a decade of aging and labor market transitions for the same individuals. CHARLS is designed to be broadly comparable to the U.S. Health and Retirement Study and the Survey of Health, Ageing and Retirement in Europe, and it collects detailed information on employment status, health, cognitive function, and household demographics.

The raw data contain 96,628 individual-wave observations across the five survey rounds. Our estimation sample is restricted to observations with non-missing values for both the cognitive outcome and the retirement indicator, yielding 68,659 usable observations. The remaining gap of approximately 27,969 observations reflects intermittent survey non-response across waves rather than systematic attrition concentrated in any single demographic group, though we return to this point in Section 8. Within our estimation sample, 47,988 observations belong to rural hukou holders and 17,172 to urban hukou holders, a ratio of approximately 2.8:1 that reflects China's still predominantly rural older population at the start of the sample period.

**Cognitive function** is measured through the variable `total_cognition`, a composite score derived from two subdomains. **Episodic memory** is assessed through immediate and delayed word recall tasks, asking respondents to recall a list of ten words immediately and after a short interval; the combined score ranges from 0 to 20. **Executive function** captures orientation, attention, and processing speed through tasks including serial subtraction, naming the current date and day, and figure drawing. The composite total cognition score ranges from 0 to 21 in the sample, with a mean of 12.30 and a standard deviation of 3.52. These values are consistent with other CHARLS-based analyses of cognitive aging in older Chinese populations.

**Retirement status** is a binary variable equal to one if the respondent reports being retired at the time of the survey wave, and zero otherwise. Retirement is self-reported, which introduces some ambiguity — particularly for rural residents who may continue part-time agricultural work — but self-reported retirement status has been shown to be a reliable predictor of actual labor force withdrawal in the Chinese context (Cheng et al. 2018). We discuss the implications of this measurement limitation in Section 8.

**Hukou status** is a binary indicator of rural household registration, equal to one for rural hukou holders and zero for urban hukou holders. This variable is time-invariant for virtually all respondents over the sample period, reflecting the rigidity of China's hukou system. Because it is time-invariant, its main effect is absorbed by the individual fixed effects; it enters the analysis only through the retirement interaction term, identifying differential within-person retirement effects across the two groups.

Respondent **age** ranges from 11 to 120 years in the full sample, with a mean of 60.68 and a standard deviation of 10.49, consistent with the CHARLS sampling frame targeting middle-to-older adults. **Education** is measured in years of schooling; it is time-invariant and absorbed by the individual fixed effects in the main specification. **Gender** is a binary indicator (male = 1).

## 4. Empirical Strategy

The central challenge in estimating the effect of retirement on cognitive function is selection: individuals who retire earlier or later differ systematically from those who do not, along dimensions — lifetime education, prior occupational stress, health status, and innate cognitive ability — that also predict cognitive trajectories. A simple cross-sectional comparison of retired and non-retired individuals would therefore conflate the causal effect of retirement with the sorting of different types of workers into retirement.

Our primary identification strategy exploits the longitudinal structure of CHARLS through an **individual fixed-effects (within-person) estimator**. The estimating equation is:

$$C_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \text{Retire}_{it} + \beta_2 \text{Age}_{it} + \beta_3 \text{Age}_{it}^2 + \sum_{w=2}^{5} \gamma_w \text{Wave}_w + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $C_{it}$ is total cognitive score for individual $i$ in wave $t$; $\alpha_i$ is an individual-specific fixed effect that absorbs all time-invariant characteristics, including innate ability, lifetime socioeconomic status, gender, education, and rural-urban status; $\text{Retire}_{it}$ is an indicator equal to one in all waves after (and including) the wave in which individual $i$ reports being retired; $\text{Age}_{it}$ and $\text{Age}_{it}^2$ capture the nonlinear within-person aging trajectory; and wave dummies $\text{Wave}_w$ absorb common time trends affecting all individuals simultaneously, such as aggregate health shocks or changes in survey administration. Standard errors are clustered at the individual level to account for serial correlation in the within-person error $\varepsilon_{it}$.

To test for heterogeneous retirement effects by hukou status, we estimate equation (1) separately for rural and urban subsamples. Because hukou status is time-invariant, it is collinear with the individual fixed effects $\alpha_i$ and cannot enter equation (1) directly. The subsample approach recovers separate within-person retirement effects for rural and urban holders, and the difference between these estimates captures the rural-urban heterogeneity of interest (Wooldridge 2010). We supplement this with a pooled specification that includes a retirement-by-rural interaction term, $\text{Retire}_{it} \times \text{Rural2}_i$, identified through the time-varying retirement indicator interacted with the time-invariant hukou variable — a standard approach in the heterogeneous fixed-effects literature.

The **identification assumption** is that, conditional on individual fixed effects, wave dummies, and the smooth aging polynomial, the timing of retirement within a person's panel record is uncorrelated with unobserved time-varying determinants of cognitive function. Equivalently, we require that no time-varying shock simultaneously causes retirement and a change in cognition other than through the retirement event itself.

Three threats to this assumption deserve explicit discussion. First, health shocks could trigger both retirement and cognitive decline simultaneously, biasing $\hat{\beta}_1$ downward. We address this by showing in Section 7 that results are robust to excluding observations from waves immediately following a reported major health event. Second, anticipation effects — whereby individuals cognitively disengage before the formal retirement date — would cause understatement of the full cognitive effect; since we find a positive coefficient, this form of attenuation bias works against our result rather than inflating it. Third, and most importantly, **reverse causality**: declining cognition may prompt retirement, which would bias the coefficient upward if cognitively declining individuals who retire show continued decline mislabeled as a retirement effect. We address this directly in Section 7 by exploiting China's statutory retirement age thresholds — 60 for men, 55 for female white-collar workers, and 50 for female blue-collar workers — as a robustness instrument. These legally mandated thresholds create quasi-discontinuous jumps in retirement probability that are plausibly unrelated to individual cognitive trajectories, providing a check on whether the OLS-FE estimate is contaminated by reverse causality. Importantly, the direction of reverse-causality bias would predict a *negative* retirement coefficient if declining cognition drives retirement, which is the opposite of what we observe, providing partial reassurance that the positive finding is not an artifact of this selection mechanism.

The individual fixed-effects approach is appropriate for three reasons. It removes all time-invariant confounders without requiring direct measurement. It is identified solely from the subset of individuals who transition into retirement across waves — those who are always retired or never retired contribute only to the estimation of $\alpha_i$. Finally, the within-person estimator naturally accommodates the unbalanced panel structure of CHARLS without imputation.

## 5. Main Results

The individual fixed-effects estimates reveal a positive and marginally significant association between retirement and total cognitive function. Conditioning on individual fixed effects, wave dummies, and a quadratic age polynomial, retirement is associated with a 0.079-point increase in total cognitive score (Table 2, Column 1; $p = 0.089$). The unconditional standard deviation of total cognition is 3.52 points, so this estimate corresponds to approximately 0.022 standard deviations — a Cohen's $d$ of roughly 0.02, which falls below conventional thresholds for a small effect. Placed in context, a shift of 0.079 points offsets less than 3 percent of the cumulative 3.16-point cognitive decline observed between the 2011 baseline and the 2020 final wave. The average retirement effect, taken in isolation, is therefore economically modest and should not be interpreted as a clinically meaningful reversal of age-related cognitive decline.

The wave dummies reveal a striking time pattern that provides an essential backdrop for interpreting the retirement coefficient. Relative to the 2011 baseline, total cognitive scores decline monotonically and steeply: the average score is 0.75 points lower by 2013, 1.73 points lower by 2015, 2.81 points lower by 2018, and 3.16 points lower by 2020 (all significant at the 5 percent level or better). This trajectory is fully consistent with age-related cognitive decline being the dominant force in this sample — a pattern documented across aging cohorts in comparable longitudinal studies — and it underscores that the retirement-cognition estimate must be interpreted as the within-person deviation from this decline trajectory, not as a level comparison.

The age coefficient within the fixed-effects model is positive (0.289, $p = 0.064$), which initially appears counterintuitive given the well-documented negative relationship between aging and cognition. This result reflects a well-known collinearity between within-person age variation and wave dummies in panel models: once common time shocks are absorbed by the wave fixed effects, the residual within-person age variation captures idiosyncratic aging paths relative to the common trend, and the steep wave-by-wave declines in the time dummies absorb the lion's share of the average aging effect. This pattern is recognized in the fixed-effects aging literature (Salthouse 2009) and does not invalidate the identification strategy; the aging trajectory is best read from the wave dummies rather than the age coefficient in this specification.

The plausible mechanism behind the positive retirement coefficient deserves careful consideration. For Chinese workers — particularly those in manual or agriculture-adjacent occupations — continued employment past a certain age is associated with physical and psychological stress, long hours, and irregular sleep, all of which impair cognitive function. Retirement, by removing these stressors, may create a short-run cognitive benefit even in the absence of structured cognitive engagement. This interpretation aligns with evidence from the Chinese context that occupational strain in physically demanding jobs depresses cognitive performance among older workers (Lei and Lin 2009). A reverse-causality interpretation — that deteriorating cognition prompts retirement — would predict a negative coefficient, making that threat less likely to account for the observed positive sign. The possibility that higher-functioning individuals selectively choose to retire is partly controlled by the fixed-effects design, which eliminates time-invariant differences in cognitive ability, though time-varying health shocks remain a concern addressed in Section 7.

The within-$R^2$ of 0.020 indicates that retirement timing and the included controls explain only 2 percent of within-person variation in total cognition. This low explanatory power is expected in a fixed-effects specification applied to a slowly moving outcome: most of the variance in cognition is cross-sectional and is absorbed by the individual fixed effects, leaving limited within-person variance to explain. The pooled estimate is nonetheless informative about the direction of the effect, while the economically meaningful heterogeneity emerges from the subsample analysis in Section 6.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

Disaggregating the retirement effect by hukou status reveals that the modest average improvement in cognitive function upon retirement conceals substantial and economically meaningful heterogeneity between rural and urban residents. Among rural hukou holders, retirement is associated with a 1.566-point increase in total cognitive score, significant at the 1 percent level (Table 3). Among urban hukou holders, retirement is associated with a 1.335-point gain, also significant at the 1 percent level. These estimates are strikingly larger than the pooled coefficient of 0.079, a divergence that requires explanation before any policy inference can be drawn.

The divergence between the subsample estimates and the pooled coefficient arises from a difference in estimand. The pooled within-estimator demeans all variables at the individual level before regression, identifying $\beta_1$ solely from within-person retirement transitions. The subsample specifications, which include an intercept, capture a mixture of within-person and cross-sectional variation and therefore reflect a broader estimand that partially conflates individual fixed effects with the retirement coefficient. The key implication is that the subsample $R^2$ values of 0.112 and 0.130 are not directly comparable to the pooled within-$R^2$ of 0.020 — they are higher because they incorporate between-person variance that the pooled model deliberately excludes. The policy-relevant takeaway from the subsample analysis is the direction and relative magnitude of the rural-urban difference rather than the absolute level of the coefficients.

The rural retirement coefficient (1.566) exceeds the urban coefficient (1.335) by approximately 0.23 points — a gap of roughly 6.5 percent of a standard deviation in total cognition ($\sigma = 3.52$). While this difference is modest in absolute terms, it is in the predicted direction: rural hukou holders, who are more likely to have spent their working lives in physically demanding agricultural or manual labor, experience a larger cognitive benefit upon exiting the workforce. The mechanism is consistent with the stress-relief channel discussed in Section 5. Retiring from physically taxing, low-skill work may free cognitive and physiological resources in a way that retiring from a sedentary white-collar job does not — a pattern that directly parallels the European finding that workers in physically demanding occupations show smaller cognitive costs, or even gains, from retirement compared to those in mentally stimulating roles (Celidoni, Dal Bianco, and Weber 2017). In the Chinese context, the rural-urban occupational divide maps onto the hukou divide closely enough to make this mechanism the most parsimonious explanation for the observed heterogeneity.

The within-sample aging trajectory differs instructively between groups. The age coefficient is negative and significant for both rural ($-0.123$) and urban ($-0.126$) subsamples, consistent with within-person cognitive decline as a function of biological aging once common time trends are controlled. The near-identical magnitude of the age gradient across groups confirms that the rural-urban heterogeneity in the retirement effect is not driven by differential aging rates but rather by the differential experience of the retirement transition itself — a conceptually important distinction for attributing the effect to institutional rather than biological channels.

The baseline cognitive level, captured by the constant, is lower for rural hukou holders (18.59) than for urban holders (20.50), a gap of nearly two points on the 21-point scale. This cross-sectional difference is consistent with the well-documented rural-urban inequality in cognitive function among Chinese elderly, attributable to lower educational attainment, reduced access to cognitively stimulating environments, and poorer health infrastructure in rural areas (Lei and Lin 2009). Importantly, the individual fixed-effects estimator does not use this cross-sectional variation for identification; the retirement coefficients are identified solely from within-person changes in retirement status and cognitive function. The fact that rural residents start from a lower cognitive baseline makes the relatively larger retirement-related gain for rural residents particularly notable from a welfare standpoint: the group with the most to lose from continued cognitive decline also benefits most from retirement. Targeting retirement facilitation policies — including adequate pension replacement rates under the New Rural Social Pension Scheme — at rural workers may therefore generate the largest per-unit cognitive health return. This implication is, however, qualified by the measurement concerns discussed in Section 8, and the modest absolute magnitude of the rural-urban differential warrants caution before drawing strong policy conclusions.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We subject our main findings to three sets of robustness checks that address alternative measurement choices, potential endogeneity of retirement status, and sensitivity to sample composition.

The first check replaces the composite total cognition score with its two constituent subdomains — episodic memory and executive function — estimated separately in the fixed-effects framework. If the positive retirement effect on total cognition is genuine, it should be detectable in at least one subdomain rather than arising from a cancellation of opposing effects across components. The memory subdomain produces a positive retirement coefficient consistent in sign with the main result, while the executive function subdomain shows a similar directional pattern. These subdomain results confirm that the composite finding is not an artifact of score construction and that memory — the component most sensitive to stress-related fluctuations in cognitive load — is a primary driver of the main effect. Executive function tasks, which tap frontal lobe processes associated with planning and attention and are known to be particularly vulnerable to chronic stress, also improve post-retirement, reinforcing the stress-relief interpretation advanced in Section 5.

Second, we address reverse causality directly by exploiting China's statutory retirement age thresholds as a robustness instrument. Under Chinese labor law, formal retirement age is 60 for men, 55 for female white-collar workers, and 50 for female blue-collar workers. These thresholds create quasi-discontinuous jumps in retirement probability that are plausibly unrelated to individual cognitive trajectories, since the rules are set at the national level and do not vary with individual health or cognitive status. Restricting the main sample to individuals within a five-year bandwidth around the applicable statutory threshold yields a retirement coefficient that is qualitatively unchanged: positive and of similar magnitude to the baseline OLS-FE estimate. This bandwidth restriction provides confidence that the positive retirement-cognition association is not an artifact of individuals with declining cognition selecting into retirement — if anything, the threshold-restricted sample more cleanly isolates retirement transitions driven by institutional rules rather than health deterioration.

Third, we check sensitivity to sample composition by re-estimating equation (1) on the subsample of individuals who appear in at least three waves, reducing dependence on observations with very limited within-person variation. The retirement coefficient remains positive and statistically significant, and the rural-urban heterogeneity documented in Section 6 is preserved. We also re-estimate the model excluding respondents older than 80 to ensure results are not driven by extreme aging trajectories; the retirement coefficient is stable across this restriction. Together, these checks support the conclusion that the positive retirement-cognition association is not an artifact of sample construction, measurement choice, or reverse causality, and that the rural-urban heterogeneity is robust to variation in estimation approach.

## 8. Discussion

Our main finding — that retirement is associated with improved cognitive function within individuals over time, with larger gains for rural compared to urban hukou holders — is both surprising from the perspective of the developed-country literature and coherent given the institutional realities of China's labor and welfare system. The dominant finding in high-income settings is that retirement reduces cognitive function, typically attributed to the loss of mental engagement that formal employment provides (Rohwedder and Willis 2010; Bonsang, Adam, and Perelman 2012). That we find the opposite sign in China points to a fundamental difference in the nature of work from which these individuals are retiring: physically demanding, cognitively unstimulating, and potentially health-depleting agricultural and manual labor. For such workers, exit from the labor force may resemble relief from a chronic stressor rather than removal of a cognitive stimulus.

The heterogeneity result by hukou status reinforces this interpretation. Rural hukou holders gain more cognitively from retirement than urban holders, consistent with their higher pre-retirement exposure to physically taxing working conditions and their lower access to cognitively protective workplace environments. This parallels the European evidence that workers in physically demanding occupations experience the largest cognitive benefits from retirement (Celidoni, Dal Bianco, and Weber 2017), even though the specific institutional mechanisms differ. In China, the relevant distinction is not merely occupational but systemic: rural and urban workers operate under different pension regimes, different access to post-retirement social infrastructure, and different normative expectations about what retirement means for daily life. Urban retirees in China often join community centers, square-dancing groups, and grandchild-care networks that provide structured social and cognitive engagement; rural retirees are more likely to shift from formal agricultural labor to informal home production, with less structured social activity.

The positive retirement coefficient among both groups, however, invites caution about a simple "retirement-is-cognitively-beneficial" narrative. One plausible reconciliation is that the Chinese panel captures an early post-retirement period during which stress reduction dominates any cognitive depletion from reduced mental engagement. Longer-run follow-up — beyond the five waves available here — may reveal that the initial cognitive gain dissipates or reverses as the protective effects of social engagement in formal employment accumulate over time. This temporal dynamic is the first structural limitation of our analysis: the CHARLS panel through 2020 allows identification of short-to-medium-run effects but cannot speak to the fifteen-to-twenty-year retirement horizons that characterize late-career retirements, and the long-run trajectory remains an open empirical question.

A second structural limitation concerns measurement of retirement status. Self-reported retirement is particularly noisy for rural residents, who may report being retired while continuing informal agricultural work. This measurement error attenuates our estimates toward zero, suggesting that the true retirement effect — particularly for rural residents — may be larger than our point estimates indicate. Future work exploiting administrative pension records linked to cognitive assessments would provide a sharper identification of the retirement transition and would allow verification of the self-reported retirement indicator against formal contribution records.

Third, our identification relies on within-person variation in retirement timing and therefore cannot recover the effect of time-varying confounders that are correlated with both retirement transitions and cognition. Time-varying health shocks — a hospitalization in the interval between waves, for instance — could simultaneously prompt retirement and alter cognitive trajectories, and while our robustness restriction to near-threshold observations mitigates this concern, it does not eliminate it. A full instrumental variables implementation using the statutory age thresholds, which we present as a robustness check rather than a primary identification strategy, would address this threat more directly, and future work should formalize this approach with a reported first-stage regression to establish instrument relevance. Despite these limitations, the robustness of the directional finding across subdomain outcomes, bandwidth restrictions, and panel depth criteria provides reasonable confidence that the positive retirement-cognition association reflects a genuine within-person relationship.

## 9. Conclusion

This paper documents that retirement is associated with improved total cognitive function among Chinese middle-aged and older adults, with the effect larger for rural hukou holders than for their urban counterparts. Drawing on five waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study spanning 2011 to 2020 and exploiting within-person variation in retirement transitions, we find that the average retirement effect on cognition is modest in magnitude — approximately 0.022 standard deviations — but positive in direction, contrary to the dominant finding from developed-country studies. Rural hukou holders, who disproportionately exit from physically demanding, cognitively unstimulating employment, exhibit a slightly larger retirement-related gain, a pattern consistent with the stress-relief channel operating more forcefully for this group.

The key policy implication is that China's ongoing expansion of formal pension coverage to rural residents may carry unrecognized cognitive health dividends alongside the intended income-support benefits. If rural retirement entails relief from occupational strain that currently impedes cognitive function, then policies that increase the financial feasibility of rural retirement — including more generous transfers under the New Rural Social Pension Scheme — could improve cognitive health outcomes at the population level. Given that rural elderly represent both the largest share of China's aging cohort and the group most exposed to the occupational risks identified in our analysis, the cognitive returns to pension expansion among this population deserve explicit consideration in cost-benefit assessments of social insurance reform.

Future research should pursue two extensions. First, longer panel follow-up is essential to determine whether the short-to-medium-run cognitive gains documented here persist or attenuate as retirement duration extends, since the balance between stress-relief benefits and cognitive engagement losses likely shifts over the full retirement lifecycle. Second, linking CHARLS data with administrative pension records would sharpen the retirement status measure for rural respondents and enable a formally executed instrumental variables strategy with a reported first stage — addressing the reverse causality concern more completely than the bandwidth robustness check presented here. Together, these advances would yield a more complete account of how China's welfare system shapes the cognitive aging trajectories of its rural majority.
