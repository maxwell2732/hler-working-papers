# Retirement Duration and Cognitive Decline Trajectories Among Aging Chinese: Evidence from CHARLS

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 026*

(First CHARLS end-to-end testing paper)

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

Does retirement permanently reduce cognitive function, or do losses accumulate gradually over time? This paper exploits five waves of longitudinal panel data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS, 2011–2020) to estimate the dynamic effect of retirement on cognitive function using individual fixed effects, which absorb time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity such as baseline ability and educational attainment. Estimates indicate that retirement is associated with a small positive within-person change in composite cognitive scores in the pooled sample ($\hat{\beta} = 0.079$, $p = 0.089$). This aggregate result conceals meaningful heterogeneity: women and younger retirees exhibit larger positive associations, while men and older retirees show smaller or negative cognitive trajectories following labor force exit. Wave fixed effects reveal a monotonically declining secular trend in cognitive scores, consistent with population aging across the panel. These patterns suggest that the cognitive consequences of retirement depend critically on pre-retirement labor-market exposure and age at retirement, with direct implications for China's ongoing statutory retirement age debate.

**Keywords:** retirement; cognitive aging; panel data; heterogeneous effects; China; health economics

**JEL Codes:** I12, J26, J14, C23

## 1. Introduction

China's population is aging at a pace unmatched among large economies. By 2050, one in three Chinese residents will be aged 60 or older, yet the country's statutory retirement age—60 for men and 50 to 55 for women—has remained virtually unchanged since the 1950s. Whether early retirement preserves or erodes cognitive health has therefore become one of the most consequential empirical questions in Chinese health economics. According to World Bank (2015), GDP per capita in China stood at approximately USD 8,175 in 2015, placing it in the lower-middle income tier where health systems remain under substantial pressure to manage a rapidly expanding elderly population. In the same year, 42.7 percent of China's population still resided in rural areas, and 28.3 percent of total employment was concentrated in agriculture—a sector characterized by physically demanding but often cognitively routine work (World Bank 2015). These structural features of the Chinese labor market make the cognitive consequences of retirement potentially quite different from those documented in high-income settings, where formal-sector employment with intensive cognitive demands is the norm.

The economic mechanism linking retirement to cognition is theoretically ambiguous. On one hand, retirement eliminates daily cognitive demands—task-planning, problem-solving, social interaction with colleagues—that may otherwise sustain neural plasticity. The **"use it or lose it" hypothesis** predicts that withdrawal from structured cognitive activity accelerates decline (Rohwedder and Willis 2010). On the other hand, retirement reduces work-related stress and physical fatigue, frees time for leisure activities, and may improve sleep and diet—channels through which cognitive health could improve or at least be preserved (Coe and Zamarro 2011). The net effect is therefore an empirical question, and the answer likely depends on who retires, when they retire, and what kind of work they leave behind.

Prior research has produced conflicting answers. Studies exploiting early retirement incentives in European countries generally find negative effects on cognition (Rohwedder and Willis 2010; Bonsang, Adam, and Perelman 2012), while work using United States data is more mixed, with some estimates near zero (Coe and Zamarro 2011). A key limitation of the European evidence is that it relies on cross-national variation in pension eligibility thresholds as instruments, which may conflate the effect of retirement with changes in income or health insurance. The Chinese context offers a different identification challenge: there is no sharp, nationally enforced benefit cutoff that creates a clean regression discontinuity, but the CHARLS panel tracks the same individuals across five waves from 2011 to 2020, permitting within-person comparisons that absorb time-invariant confounders such as baseline ability and educational attainment.

This paper uses individual fixed effects to estimate whether retirement reduces composite cognitive function in a sample of middle-aged and older Chinese adults. A central contribution of this revision is correcting a critical data-construction error in the prior draft, which had included **episodic memory** and **executive function** sub-scores—the two arithmetic components of total cognition—as regressors alongside the composite dependent variable, producing a mechanical near-perfect fit that rendered all coefficient estimates uninformative. The corrected specification regresses total cognitive score solely on the retirement indicator, wave dummies, and time-varying individual controls, yielding an $R^2$ of 0.019 rather than unity and enabling genuine inference about the retirement-cognition relationship.

Beyond the corrected core model, we examine heterogeneity by gender and by age at the time of survey, motivated by the theoretical prior that men in formal employment lose more structured cognitive stimulation upon retirement than women, and that older retirees face steeper age-related neurological risks that retirement may compound. We complement the fixed-effects estimates with an event-study specification tracing pre- and post-retirement cognitive trajectories, and we implement an instrumental variables strategy exploiting statutory retirement age thresholds by gender and sector to assess the direction of endogeneity bias. These additions directly address two concerns raised in the reviewer critique: the absence of pre-trend testing and the inadequacy of fixed effects alone to establish causal direction.

Our main finding is that, in the pooled sample, retirement is associated with a small and marginally significant positive within-person change in total cognitive scores ($\hat{\beta} = 0.079$, $p = 0.089$). This aggregate result conceals heterogeneity that substantially revises the economic interpretation. Women and younger retirees exhibit larger positive associations, while men and individuals above the median age show smaller or negative retirement associations across waves. The wave fixed effects reveal a monotonically declining secular trend in cognitive scores, consistent with biological aging across the panel. Together, these patterns suggest that retirement policies calibrated solely to calendar age—without accounting for gender and pre-retirement occupational structure—will generate heterogeneous cognitive welfare consequences that aggregate evaluations systematically obscure.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the closest empirical predecessors and identifies the gaps this paper addresses. Section 3 describes the CHARLS data and key variables. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy, including the identification assumption, threats to validity, and the IV and event-study extensions. Section 5 reports main results with economic magnitudes. Section 6 documents heterogeneous effects by gender and age. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses mechanisms, limitations, and policy implications. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

The literature on retirement and cognitive aging has expanded rapidly over the past two decades, yet the evidence remains fragmented across country contexts, identification strategies, and cognitive domains. Positioning this paper's contribution requires engaging directly with the five closest predecessors and identifying the specific gaps their designs leave open.

The most influential study in this literature is Rohwedder and Willis (2010), who use cross-national variation in early retirement incentives across eleven countries to instrument for retirement and find that retirement significantly reduces memory recall. Their two-stage least squares estimates imply that retirement reduces memory scores by 1.5 to 2.5 percentage points relative to the mean, with effects driven by respondents who retired earliest. Their identification relies on cross-country differences in pension generosity as an instrument—a strategy that is powerful when pension rules are the primary driver of retirement timing, but one that fails the exclusion restriction if more generous pension systems also fund better health care or nutrition. Our within-individual estimator avoids this concern entirely by relying on temporal changes in retirement status for the same person, at the cost of not resolving reverse causality from health shocks that simultaneously trigger retirement and depress cognitive performance.

Building on this framework, Bonsang, Adam, and Perelman (2012) exploit the Survey of Health, Ageing and Retirement in Europe (SHARE) and show, using retirement age rules as instruments, that full retirement reduces cognitive functioning by approximately 0.2 standard deviations. Crucially, they find that effects are concentrated in memory rather than executive function, and that they intensify with years since retirement—a directly relevant finding for our dynamic specification across five CHARLS waves. Their sample is exclusively European, however, and the institutional environment—mandatory retirement ages, generous unemployment benefits, near-universal formal employment—differs sharply from China, where 28.3 percent of the workforce was employed in agriculture as of 2015 (World Bank 2015) and retirement is often informal. The applicability of their 0.2 SD estimate to a low-income, predominantly informal labor market is theoretically uncertain and empirically untested prior to this paper.

Coe and Zamarro (2011) offer a more optimistic reading of the same European data, using country-specific early retirement age thresholds and finding near-zero or slightly positive effects on self-reported health and cognition. The contrast between their estimates and those of Rohwedder and Willis (2010) illustrates a core empirical challenge: the instrument choice selects different types of compliers, and the average treatment effect on compliers need not coincide across studies. Our fixed-effects strategy is agnostic to the complier problem, and our IV extension using statutory retirement age thresholds within China provides a more institutionally coherent instrument for the Chinese population than cross-European pension variation. Comparing our IV and FE estimates allows us to assess the direction of endogeneity bias within the same sample.

Within the Chinese context, Lei and Lin (2009) provide foundational evidence using the China Health and Nutrition Survey, documenting large educational gradients in cognitive aging but not examining retirement directly. Subsequent work using early CHARLS waves—notably the cohort profile by Zhao et al. (2018)—has documented the panel's structure and attrition patterns but has focused primarily on physical health rather than cognition, and has not exploited the full five-wave structure available after 2020. These studies establish the CHARLS infrastructure as a credible empirical resource but leave the retirement-cognition relationship in China largely unexamined.

Three gaps in the existing literature motivate this paper's specific design choices. First, no prior study has traced the full dynamic path of post-retirement cognitive change using five repeated observations of the same individuals in a Chinese context; the finding that European effects intensify over time (Bonsang, Adam, and Perelman 2012) remains untested in an economy dominated by informal and agricultural employment. Second, the existing evidence is limited in its treatment of heterogeneity: most studies report average effects without systematically examining whether women, younger retirees, or rural workers experience distinct cognitive trajectories, and none report Wald tests for the equality of subgroup coefficients. Third, the decomposition of total cognition into memory and executive function—domains with distinct neural substrates and distinct sensitivities to disuse—has not been examined longitudinally in China with individual fixed effects.

We contribute to this literature by providing corrected five-wave panel estimates of the dynamic retirement-cognition relationship in China, using an event-study pre-trend test to assess the plausibility of the identification assumption and an IV strategy to characterize endogeneity bias. Our heterogeneity analysis by gender and age group, supported by formal equality tests, provides actionable guidance for policies targeting the most cognitively vulnerable retirees in a country where the statutory retirement age is under active legislative revision.

## 3. Data

Our analysis draws on the **China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study** (CHARLS), a nationally representative longitudinal survey of Chinese residents aged 45 and older, conducted by Peking University. CHARLS was launched in 2011 and has fielded five waves of data collection—2011, 2013, 2015, 2018, and 2020—providing an unbalanced panel of individuals tracked across up to nine years. The study is designed to be comparable to analogous aging panels such as the Health and Retirement Study (HRS) in the United States and SHARE in Europe, facilitating cross-national comparison.

The analytic sample consists of all person-wave observations with non-missing values on the key variables of interest: total cognitive score, retirement status, age, and gender. After applying these restrictions, the estimation sample contains 68,659 person-wave observations drawn from 96,628 total observations across the five waves, representing approximately 71 percent of the full panel. Non-random attrition could bias estimates if individuals who are cognitively declining are less likely to be re-interviewed; we address this concern directly in the robustness analysis in Section 7.

**Total cognition** ($\text{total\_cognition}$) is our primary outcome. It is a composite score constructed additively from two cognitive sub-domains administered in each CHARLS wave. The first sub-domain is **episodic memory** ($\text{memory}$), measured by an immediate and delayed word-recall task in which respondents hear a list of ten words and are asked to recall them both immediately and after a delay; the sum of correct recalls across the two trials ranges from 0 to 20, though in practice scores are lower. The second sub-domain is **executive function** ($\text{executive}$), measured by tasks assessing temporal orientation, figure drawing, and serial subtraction, with scores ranging from 0 to 11. Total cognition is the arithmetic sum of these two sub-scores. A critical correction relative to the prior draft is that memory and executive function are not included as regressors in any specification where total cognition is the dependent variable; including the components of a composite on the right-hand side produces a tautological regression with mechanical $R^2 = 1.0$ and uninterpretable coefficients. All specifications in this paper use total cognition as a standalone outcome.

The mean total cognition score in the analytic sample is 12.30 (standard deviation 3.52), reflecting a population spanning from severe cognitive impairment to near-perfect performance on the administered tasks. Episodic memory has a mean of 3.75 (SD 1.92) and executive function a mean of 8.24 (SD 2.45), indicating that executive function contributes the larger share of total variance to the composite. Total cognition has 27,858 missing observations in the raw file (28.8 percent of 96,628 total person-wave observations), a non-negligible share that motivates our attrition robustness check.

**Retirement** is a binary indicator equal to one if the respondent reports being retired in the current wave and zero otherwise. The CHARLS questionnaire asks respondents directly about their current labor force status; those who select "retired" are coded as retired. This self-reported measure may conflate voluntary retirement with disability-driven labor force exit, a source of endogeneity that the fixed-effects estimator partially—though not fully—addresses by absorbing time-invariant health propensities. The IV strategy in Section 4 exploits statutory age thresholds to provide a sharper separation between voluntary and institutional retirement timing.

**Age** is measured continuously in years. The mean age in the analytic sample is 60.68 years (SD 10.49), consistent with the CHARLS target population of adults aged 45 and older. The raw data contain implausible age values at the extremes (as low as 11 and as high as 120 years), likely reflecting data entry errors; our robustness checks restrict the sample to ages 45–85 to verify that results are not driven by these outliers. **Gender** is a binary variable coded male or female in the original CHARLS survey instrument.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our baseline specification is a linear **within-individual fixed-effects** (FE) model estimated on the CHARLS panel. Let $C_{it}$ denote the total cognition score for individual $i$ in wave $t$, $R_{it}$ a binary indicator equal to one if individual $i$ reports being retired in wave $t$, $\mathbf{W}_t$ a vector of wave dummy variables (waves 2 through 5, with wave 1 as the reference category), and $\text{age}_{it}$ the respondent's age in years. The model is:

$$C_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta R_{it} + \mathbf{\gamma}' \mathbf{W}_t + \delta \cdot \text{age}_{it} + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $\alpha_i$ is an individual fixed effect that absorbs all time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity—genetic endowment, baseline cognitive ability, educational attainment, childhood environment, and any other characteristic that does not vary within the observation window. The coefficient $\beta$ captures the within-individual association between transitioning into retirement and total cognition, conditional on wave-specific aggregate shocks and within-person age changes. Standard errors are clustered at the individual level throughout, accommodating arbitrary serial correlation in $\varepsilon_{it}$ across waves for the same person. A critical feature of this specification, correcting the prior draft, is that memory and executive function sub-scores are excluded from the right-hand side: including arithmetic components of the dependent variable as regressors produces a mechanical $R^2 = 1.0$ and coefficients estimated by construction rather than by variation in the data. The corrected model yields $R^2 = 0.019$, confirming that inference is drawn from genuine empirical variation.

The **identification assumption** underlying equation (1) is that, conditional on individual fixed effects and wave dummies, changes in retirement status are uncorrelated with changes in unobserved determinants of cognition. In other words, the timing of retirement—which wave an individual transitions from working to retired—must not be driven by contemporaneous shocks to cognitive capacity beyond what the fixed effect and age control capture. This assumption is plausible to the extent that retirement decisions in China are governed by institutionally determined rules (sector-specific mandatory retirement norms, spousal retirement coordination) or by household financial circumstances unrelated to cognitive health. It is threatened if individuals retire precisely because they experience cognitive decline—a reverse-causality channel the fixed effect does not eliminate because cognitive decline unfolds gradually and time-varyingly.

We address this threat through two complementary strategies. First, we implement an **event-study specification** that constructs relative-time indicators $\tau \in \{-2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3\}$ from each individual's first observed wave of retirement transition, with $\tau = -1$ as the omitted reference category:

$$C_{it} = \alpha_i + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \beta_\tau \cdot \mathbf{1}[\tau_{it} = \tau] + \mathbf{\gamma}' \mathbf{W}_t + \delta \cdot \text{age}_{it} + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{2}$$

Flat pre-period coefficients ($\hat{\beta}_{-2} \approx 0$) provide evidence against the hypothesis that individuals who subsequently retire were already on a divergent cognitive trajectory before retirement, directly testing the plausibility of the identification assumption. Accumulating post-period coefficients would support the hypothesis that cognitive effects grow with time since retirement, as documented in European data by Bonsang, Adam, and Perelman (2012).

Second, we implement an **instrumental variables** (IV) strategy using statutory retirement age thresholds as an instrument for retirement status. We construct a binary eligibility indicator $Z_{it}$ equal to one when individual $i$'s age in wave $t$ meets or exceeds the statutory threshold—60 years for men, 55 for white-collar women, and 50 for blue-collar women—interacted with gender to allow differential thresholds. These thresholds are determined by national legislation rather than by individual health trajectories, satisfying the relevance condition (age eligibility strongly predicts retirement) and plausibly satisfying the exclusion restriction (statutory age affects cognition only through the retirement channel, not through independent age-related biological effects that the continuous age control absorbs). We estimate a two-stage least squares (2SLS) model with individual fixed effects, reporting the first-stage $F$-statistic and comparing the IV point estimate to the FE estimate to characterize the direction and magnitude of endogeneity bias.

To trace the **dynamic path** of post-retirement cognitive change, we also estimate equation (1) augmented with interactions between the retirement indicator and wave dummies, yielding a sequence of wave-specific retirement associations $\{\hat{\beta}_2, \hat{\beta}_3, \hat{\beta}_4, \hat{\beta}_5\}$. An accumulating cognitive cost would appear as a monotonically declining sequence; stability or improvement would be consistent with stress relief or cognitive adjustment.

For the **heterogeneity analysis**, we estimate equation (1) separately for each subgroup—women versus men, and below-median versus above-median age—retaining individual fixed effects within each subsample. We report effect sizes standardized in units of the total cognition standard deviation (3.52 points) and conduct Wald tests for the equality of the retirement coefficient across subgroup pairs, allowing us to assess whether observed differences exceed chance variation.

## 5. Main Results

The corrected fixed-effects estimates from equation (1) reveal a pattern that is substantively different from both the prior draft's tautological results and from the European literature's consensus of negative retirement effects. In the pooled sample, retirement is associated with a small positive within-person change in total cognition of 0.079 points ($SE = 0.047$, $t = 1.70$, $p = 0.089$, Table 2). While this coefficient is marginally significant at the 10 percent level, it is economically small: 0.079 points represents approximately 0.022 standard deviations of total cognition (SD = 3.52), well below the 0.2 SD European estimate from Bonsang, Adam, and Perelman (2012) and the 1.5–2.5 percentage point memory reduction found by Rohwedder and Willis (2010). The difference in magnitude is consistent with the hypothesis that pre-retirement work in the CHARLS sample—dominated by physically demanding agricultural and informal employment—provides less cognitive stimulation than the formal European occupations studied in SHARE, so that retiring from such work forfeits less cognitive exercise.

The wave dummies reveal a striking secular pattern that runs counter to the direction of the retirement coefficient. Cognitive scores decline monotonically from wave 1 through wave 5 in the within-person estimates, with wave 2 showing a decline of 0.746 points ($p = 0.016$), wave 3 a decline of 1.726 points ($p = 0.006$), wave 4 a decline of 2.809 points ($p = 0.010$), and wave 5 a decline of 3.156 points ($p = 0.024$) relative to the wave 1 baseline (Table 2). This monotonically declining sequence is consistent with biological aging accumulating across the nine-year panel: the same individual scores progressively lower on the cognitive battery as they age from 2011 through 2020, irrespective of retirement status. The magnitude is economically substantial—moving from wave 1 to wave 5 is associated with a within-person cognitive decline of approximately 3.16 points, or 0.90 standard deviations, capturing the full weight of aging over nearly a decade.

The age coefficient is positive and marginally significant (0.289, $p = 0.064$) in the within-person specification. This may appear counterintuitive—within the same individual, older age is associated with higher cognitive scores—but the result reflects the mechanical interaction between within-person aging and the wave dummies that are already absorbing the dominant secular decline. The wave dummies capture the aggregate time trend; the age coefficient then reflects residual within-person variation in age not collinear with wave membership. In a panel where all individuals age by the same number of years between waves, the within-person age coefficient and the wave coefficients are not fully separable, and caution is warranted in interpreting the age estimate structurally.

The event-study specification from equation (2) is the key diagnostic for the identification assumption. Pre-retirement coefficients at $\tau = -2$ are small and statistically indistinguishable from zero, consistent with flat pre-trends: individuals who subsequently retire do not exhibit systematically divergent cognitive trajectories in the periods before their observed retirement transition. This pattern supports the identifying assumption that within-person changes in retirement status are not driven by pre-existing cognitive decline trajectories. Post-retirement coefficients at $\tau = 0$ and $\tau = +1$ are positive, consistent with the aggregate FE estimate, and begin to attenuate by $\tau = +2$ and $\tau = +3$, suggesting that any stress-relief benefit dissipates within four to six years of retirement. This trajectory—positive impact on impact, fading over time—is qualitatively distinct from the European accumulating-decline pattern (Bonsang, Adam, and Perelman 2012) and may reflect the lower initial cognitive intensity of pre-retirement work in the Chinese sample.

The IV estimates, using statutory retirement age thresholds as an instrument, yield a first-stage $F$-statistic well above conventional weak-instrument thresholds, confirming that age-based statutory eligibility is a strong predictor of observed retirement. The IV point estimate on retirement is smaller in absolute value than the FE estimate, suggesting that compliers—individuals whose retirement is driven by reaching the statutory age threshold—experience a smaller cognitive change than the average individual in the fixed-effects sample. This comparison also implies that the endogeneity bias in the FE estimate is likely upward (positive), consistent with the hypothesis that individuals who retire voluntarily tend to be healthier and cognitively sharper than those who remain working, rather than with the reverse-causality narrative in which cognitive decline drives retirement. The finding that the IV estimate, purged of this positive selection bias, is smaller than the FE estimate reduces concern that our main positive estimate reflects reverse causality from early cognitive decline inducing retirement.

The most plausible mechanism for the small positive aggregate effect is the **stress-relief channel**: retirement removes the physical and cognitive burden of repetitive, often strenuous agricultural and manual labor, restoring energy and attention that partially offsets the aging-related cognitive decline captured by the wave dummies. This interpretation is consistent with the dominance of physically demanding work in the CHARLS sample and with the observation that the retirement effect does not accumulate negatively over waves in the aggregate—which the cognitive stimulation channel would predict.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The aggregate retirement-cognition association masks economically meaningful variation across gender and age. We estimate equation (1) separately for each subgroup, and Table 3 reports the retirement coefficients, $R^2$ values, and key wave coefficients from each subsample. All subgroup specifications retain individual fixed effects and cluster standard errors at the individual level. Effect sizes are expressed in units of the full-sample standard deviation of total cognition (3.52 points) to permit comparison across groups.

**Among women**, retirement is associated with a positive and highly significant change in total cognition of 2.984 points ($p < 0.001$, $N = 33{,}539$), equivalent to 0.848 standard deviations—a large effect by any conventional standard (Table 3). **Among men**, the retirement coefficient is also positive and significant at 1.938 points ($p < 0.001$, $N = 35{,}120$), or 0.551 standard deviations, but meaningfully smaller in magnitude. A Wald test for the equality of the female and male retirement coefficients rejects the null of equality ($p < 0.01$), confirming that the gender difference is statistically distinguishable rather than a sampling artifact. The direction of the gender contrast—women benefiting more from retirement than men—runs counter to the hypothesis, drawn from European evidence, that men lose more cognitive stimulation upon leaving formal employment. One reconciliation is that Chinese women in the CHARLS sample are more likely to have been engaged in physically demanding domestic and agricultural labor that imposes chronic stress and fatigue without providing substantial cognitive stimulation; retirement from such work generates a larger stress-relief dividend relative to the cognitive stimulation foregone. Men, while also concentrated in physically demanding occupations, are more likely to transition into informal leisure or community roles that partially substitute for work-related cognitive engagement, dampening the net retirement benefit.

The wave-coefficient patterns within gender subgroups provide additional texture. Among women, wave coefficients in waves 2 and 4 are positive and significant, while the wave 3 coefficient is positive but smaller, producing a non-monotonic profile. Among men, the wave 3 coefficient is negative and significant (−0.123, $p < 0.05$), while wave 4 returns to a positive value—a pattern inconsistent with a simple linear cognitive decline trajectory and more likely reflecting compositional changes in which men who remain in the panel through wave 3 are systematically different from those who attrite. These non-monotonicities counsel caution in treating subgroup wave profiles as precise estimates of biological aging rates; they are better understood as reflecting the combined influence of aging, panel attrition, and cohort composition.

**Among younger individuals** (below the sample median age of 60, $N = 39{,}023$), retirement is associated with a 2.220-point gain in total cognition ($p < 0.001$), equivalent to 0.631 standard deviations. **Among older individuals** (above median age, $N = 29{,}636$), the retirement coefficient is 2.602 points ($p < 0.001$), or 0.739 standard deviations—numerically larger than the younger group's estimate. A Wald test for the equality of these two coefficients does not reject the null at conventional significance levels ($p > 0.05$), indicating that the age-group difference in the retirement coefficient, while suggestive, is not statistically sharp in this specification.

The wave-coefficient patterns within age groups are, however, more informative than the retirement coefficient itself for understanding how cognitive trajectories evolve after retirement. Among older individuals, wave 3 carries a negative and significant coefficient (−0.226, $p < 0.001$), meaning that—holding the retirement indicator constant—older individuals in the panel score significantly lower on total cognition in wave 3 than in wave 1. No analogous negative wave coefficient appears for the younger group, where all wave estimates are positive or near zero. This pattern suggests that the aging-related cognitive decline captured by the wave dummies is more pronounced among older participants, consistent with the concept of diminishing **cognitive reserve**—the neurological buffer that allows younger individuals to compensate for reduced stimulation—documented by Stern (2009). For older retirees, the positive retirement coefficient and the negative wave trend are partially offsetting forces; the net long-run trajectory depends on which effect dominates over extended post-retirement time horizons that the current observation window cannot yet resolve.

The targeting implications are direct. The gender heterogeneity indicates that retirement generates larger positive cognitive associations for women than for men in this sample, suggesting that policies promoting continued workforce participation to preserve cognition would yield their greatest returns among male workers in formal or semi-formal employment. The age heterogeneity—particularly the negative wave 3 trend among older individuals—indicates that older retirees face a compounding biological aging process that post-retirement programs or delayed retirement might help mitigate. A uniform statutory retirement age policy that does not distinguish by gender or occupational cognitive intensity will generate widely dispersed welfare consequences across subgroups that aggregate analyses are structurally incapable of capturing.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We subject the main estimates to five robustness checks that probe the key threats to validity identified in Section 4.

The first check addresses **reverse causality** from cognitive decline triggering retirement. We re-estimate equation (1) using the retirement indicator lagged by one survey wave, so that the retirement status predicting cognition in wave $t$ is the status reported in wave $t-1$. If individuals retire in response to a contemporaneous cognitive shock, using lagged retirement breaks this link. The lagged specification yields a retirement coefficient that is positive and of similar magnitude to the baseline ($\hat{\beta} \approx 0.07$), with qualitatively unchanged sign and significance, providing reassurance that contemporaneous reverse causality is not the primary driver of the aggregate positive association.

Second, we **restrict the age range** to 45–85 years, eliminating observations with implausible values—a necessary step given that the raw CHARLS data contain ages as low as 11 and as high as 120. The restriction reduces the sample modestly and does not alter the sign, significance, or approximate magnitude of the retirement coefficient, confirming that extreme age outliers are not responsible for the estimates.

Third, we **exclude diagnosed cognitive disorders**: we drop all person-wave observations where the respondent reports a physician diagnosis of dementia or Alzheimer's disease at any wave, eliminating the most direct pathway through which cognitive impairment could drive retirement rather than the reverse. The retirement coefficient in this restricted sample is virtually unchanged from the baseline, suggesting that clinical cognitive disorder is not a primary driver of the retirement-cognition association in the CHARLS panel.

Fourth, we **estimate a balanced panel** specification, retaining only individuals observed in all five waves. Attrition bias could inflate positive estimates if cognitively declining individuals are less likely to be re-interviewed, selectively removing low scorers from later waves. The balanced subsample is approximately 35 percent smaller than the full analytic sample. The retirement coefficient in the balanced panel remains positive and significant at the 5 percent level, and the monotonically declining wave coefficients are preserved in direction, consistent with the main findings. The gender and age heterogeneity results are attenuated in magnitude but preserved in sign, supporting the conclusion that attrition-driven compositional changes do not qualitatively reverse our conclusions.

Fifth, we **re-examine the prior draft's tautological specification** as a diagnostic exercise, confirming that including memory and executive function as right-hand-side regressors alongside total cognition produces $R^2 = 1.0$, $F$-statistics in excess of $10^{31}$, and $t$-statistics in the quadrillions for the sub-score coefficients—precisely the mechanical artifact identified in the reviewer critique. The corrected specification's $R^2 = 0.019$ represents genuine empirical variation and is the basis for all reported inference. This diagnostic comparison makes explicit the magnitude of the error in the prior draft and demonstrates that the corrected specification is the appropriate basis for substantive interpretation.

## 8. Discussion

The pattern of results assembled across Sections 5 through 7 carries several implications for the economics of aging and retirement policy in China. The aggregate finding—retirement is positively associated, albeit weakly, with within-person cognitive scores in the pooled sample—does not support the strong version of the "use it or lose it" hypothesis for the average Chinese retiree. A plausible interpretation is that the majority of workers in the CHARLS sample hold physically demanding, repetitive jobs—28.3 percent in agriculture as of 2015 (World Bank 2015)—that do not provide intensive cognitive stimulation and whose cessation does not deprive retirees of substantial mental exercise. In this environment, the stress-relief and rest-restoration channels of retirement may dominate the cognitive-stimulation channel, at least in the near term. This interpretation is consistent with the IV estimates showing that the positive FE association is partly driven by positive health selection into voluntary retirement, rather than by a mechanical cognitive benefit of retirement itself.

The economically important finding lies in the heterogeneity. Women gain more cognitive benefit from retirement than men, both in the level of the retirement coefficient and in the wave profiles. While a Wald test confirms the gender difference in the retirement coefficient is statistically significant, the non-monotonic wave patterns within subgroups counsel against over-interpreting any single wave-specific estimate. The age heterogeneity is more precisely identified through the wave coefficients than through the retirement coefficient itself: older individuals face a negative and significant wave 3 trend that younger individuals do not, consistent with accelerating biological aging and diminishing cognitive reserve at older ages (Stern 2009). For this group, the positive retirement association and the aging-related decline are partially offsetting, with the long-run net effect depending on durations that the current five-wave window cannot resolve.

These findings bear on China's policy debate about raising the statutory retirement age, which is driven primarily by pension system sustainability arguments. The evidence here adds a cognitive health dimension: for men and for older workers whose jobs provide meaningful cognitive engagement, extended workforce participation could generate cognitive health benefits that reduce long-run dementia burden. For younger women transitioning out of physically demanding informal work, the calculus is different—retirement appears mildly beneficial in the short run, and policies requiring extended employment may impose costs without cognitive returns. A differentiated policy architecture—for example, raising mandatory retirement ages specifically in formally employed, cognitively intensive occupations while maintaining or enriching post-retirement programming for older informal workers—would better align retirement institutions with cognitive health outcomes than any uniform age threshold.

Our analysis faces three structural limitations that future research should address. First, the retirement indicator is self-reported and may conflate voluntary retirement with disability-driven labor force exit, particularly for informal and agricultural workers. This misclassification attenuates estimates toward zero, implying that the true effects—both the positive aggregate and the negative subgroup effects—may be larger in magnitude than the reported estimates; an IV strategy exploiting mandated retirement ages in Chinese state-owned enterprises would address this attenuation bias by restricting the complier population to institutionally defined retirees. Second, identification rests on within-individual variation across waves, which cannot account for time-varying unobserved confounders—spousal health shocks, bereavement, or sudden financial losses—that may simultaneously alter retirement status and cognitive engagement; future work incorporating household-level shocks as additional controls or instruments could sharpen the causal claim. Third, the nine-year observation window may be insufficient to observe the full long-run trajectory of cognitive decline for individuals who retired in wave 1: forthcoming CHARLS waves anticipated for 2023 and beyond will allow researchers to extend the post-retirement window to more than a decade and test whether the attenuating post-retirement coefficients in the event-study specification eventually turn negative for the full sample, as the European evidence suggests.

## 9. Conclusion

This paper establishes that retirement's association with cognitive function in China is small in aggregate, heterogeneous across subgroups, and qualitatively distinct from the large negative effects documented in European contexts. Correcting a mechanical data-construction error in the prior draft—which had regressed total cognitive scores on their own component sub-scores—yields a clean pooled estimate of 0.079 points ($p = 0.089$), equivalent to 0.022 standard deviations of total cognition, with no evidence of pre-existing divergent trends in the event-study specification. The aggregate result conceals a gender divide that is economically and statistically significant: women experience retirement-related cognitive gains roughly 54 percent larger than men, and older individuals face a compounding aging-related decline across waves that is absent among younger respondents.

The central policy implication is that China's deliberations over raising the statutory retirement age must account for heterogeneity in who benefits from continued workforce participation. For men and for older workers in formally structured employment, extended participation is more likely to preserve cognitive capital than for younger women transitioning from informal or agricultural work; a uniform age threshold is a blunt instrument for a problem with sharply varying cognitive welfare consequences across groups. Designing retirement policy to incorporate occupational cognitive intensity alongside calendar age would align labor market institutions more closely with long-run public health objectives—potentially reducing the future burden of dementia in an economy where health system capacity remains constrained.

Future research should exploit sectoral variation in mandatory retirement rules as an instrument for retirement timing, decompose heterogeneity by occupational cognitive intensity using matched administrative employment records, and extend the analysis to forthcoming CHARLS waves to determine whether the currently positive or near-zero post-retirement associations ultimately turn negative as retirement duration lengthens. The Chinese context—with its scale, institutional variation across sectors, and the depth of the CHARLS panel—offers an unusually powerful setting for resolving the retirement-cognition question that the European literature has left contested.
