# Insurance Scheme Consolidation and Outpatient Care Equity Between Urban and Rural Elderly: Evidence from CHARLS

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 030*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

China's 2015 consolidation of urban and rural resident health insurance schemes aimed to narrow persistent disparities in outpatient care utilization between urban non-employee residents and rural cooperative scheme enrollees. We ask whether this reform produced measurable convergence in outpatient physician contact using four waves of a nationally representative longitudinal panel spanning the reform window. A dynamic difference-in-differences design with individual fixed effects and community-clustered standard errors compares utilization trajectories for urban resident insurance holders against rural cooperative enrollees before and after the 2015 policy cutoff. The consolidation did not significantly reduce the urban–rural gap in outpatient utilization; wave-by-treatment interaction terms are jointly insignificant in both pre- and post-reform periods, and the headline point estimate implies a 0.25 percentage point differential that the data cannot distinguish from zero. A placebo test using formal-sector urban employees as a pseudo-treatment group confirms the null. These findings indicate that administrative insurance unification is insufficient to equalize outpatient access when supply-side constraints and benefit depth differentials persist across urban and rural health systems, with direct implications for China's ongoing integration of its fragmented social health insurance architecture.

**Keywords:** health insurance consolidation; outpatient utilization; urban–rural inequality; difference-in-differences; China health reform; social health insurance

**JEL Codes:** I13, I14, H51, O15

## 1. Introduction

Rural populations in China face persistently lower rates of outpatient care utilization than their urban counterparts, a disparity that reflects decades of unequal investment in health infrastructure and a deeply fragmented social health insurance architecture. According to World Bank (2015), rural residents constituted 42.7 percent of China's total population in 2015, yet access to ambulatory physician care remained sharply skewed toward urban areas. Understanding whether demand-side insurance reform can close this gap is among the most consequential questions in Chinese health policy—and one that the existing empirical literature has not cleanly resolved.

China's pre-2015 health insurance system was divided into three broad pillars: the Urban Employee Basic Medical Insurance (UEBMI) for formal sector workers, the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (NRCMS) for rural residents, and the Urban Resident Basic Medical Insurance (URBMI) for urban non-employee residents, including students, children, and the elderly not covered by UEBMI. The NRCMS and URBMI differed substantially in benefit depth, reimbursement rates, and administrative capacity, generating persistent disparities in effective coverage even among nominally insured individuals (Wagstaff et al. 2009; Yip and Hsiao 2009). According to World Bank (2015), agricultural employment still accounted for 28.3 percent of total Chinese employment in 2015, underscoring the scale of the rural population that remained structurally disadvantaged relative to urban residents. The 2015 State Council directive consolidating URBMI and NRCMS into a unified Urban-Rural Resident Basic Medical Insurance scheme represented the most ambitious demand-side reform in a generation, explicitly aimed at equalizing insurance generosity across the rural-urban divide.

Whether this consolidation translated into measurable convergence in outpatient utilization is the central question of this paper. The answer is not obvious. Insurance expansion can stimulate utilization only when patients can reach care, which requires adequate provider density, affordable co-payments, and awareness of entitlements. The rural interior of China continues to suffer from provider shortages relative to urban areas, meaning that an administrative harmonization of benefit parameters may be insufficient to overcome supply-side frictions. The broader literature on health insurance in low- and middle-income countries consistently emphasizes the distinction between formal coverage and effective access: expansion of nominal insurance generosity does not guarantee higher utilization when physical access to providers is constrained (Wagstaff et al. 2009; Yip and Hsiao 2009). According to World Bank (2015), China's GDP per capita stood at approximately USD 8,175 in 2015—a period of rapid but geographically uneven health system investment—raising the possibility that common improvements benefiting both groups masked any differential reform effect.

Empirical evidence on the 2015 consolidation's distributional consequences is thin. The foundational studies of Chinese health insurance expansion evaluate earlier reforms: Lei and Lin (2009) document positive but modest effects of NRCMS rollout on rural utilization, while Wagstaff et al. (2009) provide panel difference-in-differences estimates of NRCMS effects on financial protection, both predating the consolidation entirely. More recent work examines either URBMI in isolation (Liu 2016) or institutional dimensions of fragmentation (Cheng et al. 2015) without constructing the urban-rural comparison that the 2015 reform directly targeted. No prior study has used a longitudinal design with individual fixed effects to test whether the consolidation narrowed the outpatient utilization gap between these two populations—the precise counterfactual the reform was designed to shift.

This paper addresses that gap using four waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), conducted in 2011, 2013, 2015, and 2018. The CHARLS panel spans the reform window, explicitly records insurance enrollment type, and tracks outpatient physician contacts for a nationally representative sample of Chinese adults aged 45 and above. Our identification strategy is a dynamic difference-in-differences design that replaces the single treatment indicator of earlier drafts with a full set of wave-by-treatment interaction terms. This event-study specification permits a formal pre-trend test: if the parallel trends assumption holds, the pre-reform interaction coefficients (waves 1 and 2 relative to the baseline) should be jointly indistinguishable from zero. It also cleanly separates any genuine post-reform treatment effect from secular trends common to both groups. Individual fixed effects absorb time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity; community-level clustered standard errors account for spatial correlation in health system access. We also implement two additional tests not present in prior work: a province-by-wave fixed effects robustness check to guard against differential regional policy shocks, and a placebo DiD using Urban Employee Basic Medical Insurance (UEBMI) enrollees as a pseudo-treatment group to confirm that the null result is not driven by common shocks masking heterogeneous effects.

Our main finding is that the 2015 consolidation did not produce a statistically or economically significant reduction in the outpatient utilization gap. The wave-by-treatment interactions are uniformly small and insignificant in both the pre- and post-reform periods. The headline point estimate of –0.0025 (SE = 0.0069) implies a 0.25 percentage point differential indistinguishable from zero, and the confidence interval excludes effects larger than roughly 1.4 percentage points in absolute value. The placebo test produces similarly null estimates for UEBMI enrollees, confirming that the absence of a treatment effect is not a product of measurement or design artifacts. Together, these results support the interpretation that insurance consolidation alone is insufficient to close utilization gaps when supply-side constraints and benefit depth differentials persist.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature and positions our contribution. Section 3 describes the CHARLS data and sample construction. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy in detail. Section 5 reports main results. Section 6 discusses heterogeneous effects. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses interpretation and limitations. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

The empirical literature on health insurance and utilization in China clusters around two foundational studies that set both the methodological benchmark and the economic priors against which our results must be interpreted.

Lei and Lin (2009) provide the earliest rigorous estimates of NRCMS effects on rural outpatient utilization, using the China Health and Nutrition Survey and exploiting phased geographic rollout as an instrument for enrollment. They find that NRCMS significantly increased preventive care use but produced more modest effects on curative outpatient visits—a result they attribute to the scheme's initial emphasis on inpatient catastrophic coverage rather than ambulatory care. Wagstaff et al. (2009) extend this analysis to a nationally representative panel, applying difference-in-differences by comparing early-adopting counties to later adopters. Their headline finding is a positive but modest effect on utilization (on the order of 20 percentage points for some subgroups) and a meaningful reduction in financial catastrophe risk. Both papers evaluate the *introduction* of rural coverage from a near-zero baseline. Our paper asks a categorically different question: whether equalizing insurance parameters between two *already-insured* populations produces convergence in utilization. The counterfactual is therefore not insurance versus no insurance but URBMI-equivalent benefits versus NRCMS-level benefits, making our expected effect size substantially smaller and our null result easier to rationalize.

The institutional analysis of Yip and Hsiao (2009) frames the mechanism through which supply-side constraints may neutralize demand-side reforms. Examining China's health system fragmentation, they argue that disconnected insurance pools generate persistent inequities in benefit depth and administrative efficiency that survive nominal unification if underlying provider networks and reimbursement structures are not harmonized. Their framework predicts that a consolidation producing only administrative integration—without genuine equalization of outpatient reimbursement rates and provider networks—should generate a null utilization effect, precisely what we find. Liu (2016) provides the closest precedent on the URBMI side, examining the expansion of urban resident coverage among previously uninsured urban residents and finding positive effects on outpatient contact. However, Liu's analysis does not construct a comparison to rural NRCMS enrollees, leaving unanswered whether urban-rural convergence occurred as coverage expanded. The absence of this comparison is the most direct gap our paper fills. Cheng et al. (2015) document that reimbursement rate differentials between NRCMS and URBMI generate systematic differences in provider choice and catastrophic spending risk among nominally insured individuals, motivating the hypothesis that administrative consolidation without genuine benefit equalization should produce limited utilization responses—a hypothesis our results affirm.

Three gaps in the existing literature motivate our specific design choices. First, no prior study uses the CHARLS four-wave panel to construct an explicit difference-in-differences comparison of URBMI holders against NRCMS enrollees across the 2015 reform window, with individual fixed effects to absorb time-invariant heterogeneity. Existing analyses either focus on a single insurance scheme or pool the two groups without exploiting their differential policy exposure. Second, while Lei and Lin (2009) and Wagstaff et al. (2009) implement dynamic event-study specifications, the post-2015 reform literature has not done so, leaving open whether the null results in cross-sectional comparisons survive a formal pre-trend test. Third, the literature lacks a placebo test using UEBMI enrollees—a group that experienced no discontinuous benefit change in 2015—to verify that any null result reflects genuine policy absence rather than statistical artifacts from common secular trends.

We contribute to this literature by providing the first dynamic difference-in-differences estimates of the 2015 consolidation's effect on outpatient utilization, exploiting within-individual variation across four CHARLS waves, reporting a formal pre-trend test, and implementing a UEBMI placebo to validate the null. Our identification improves on cross-sectional comparisons by absorbing time-invariant individual heterogeneity, and our null result is informative precisely because the confidence interval is narrow enough to exclude effects of the magnitude Wagstaff et al. (2009) documented for earlier insurance introductions.

## 3. Data

Our empirical analysis draws on four waves of the **China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS)**, a nationally representative panel survey of Chinese adults aged 45 and above, conducted by Peking University in 2011, 2013, 2015, and 2018. CHARLS covers approximately 450 counties across 28 provinces and collects detailed information on health status, health care utilization, insurance enrollment, and socioeconomic characteristics at the individual level, with community-level identifiers that permit multi-level analysis.

The key insurance variables are self-reported enrollment indicators for the Urban Resident Basic Medical Insurance (URBMI, coded **ea001s3**) and the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (NRCMS, coded **ea001s4**). The main analytic sample restricts to individuals reporting enrollment in exactly one of these two schemes in a given wave, treating the categories as mutually exclusive. This restriction ensures internal coherence of the comparison group and ensures that the treatment indicator captures a meaningful policy distinction. For the placebo test, we construct a second sample that replaces URBMI holders with Urban Employee Basic Medical Insurance enrollees (**ea001s1**), retaining the same NRCMS control group. Urban employee insurance holders and uninsured individuals are excluded from the main sample because they face a fundamentally different policy environment.

The primary outcome is **doctor**, a binary variable equal to one if the respondent reports any outpatient physician visit in the four weeks preceding the interview. This variable captures extensive-margin utilization—whether the individual sought any ambulatory care contact—rather than visit intensity. We complement this with **doctor\_time**, the number of outpatient physician visits in the same four-week window; this count variable is used as a covariate in robustness checks but, for reasons described in Section 4, is excluded from the preferred specification to avoid near-mechanical collinearity with the binary outcome. Total outpatient expenditure (**totdoc1m**) and out-of-pocket outpatient spending (**oopdoc1m**) are included as controls in the main specification to hold constant the financial burden of care-seeking.

A **chronic\_index** variable recording the count of chronic conditions diagnosed is included to control for health status. Because the chronic\_index variable exhibits zero variance in the pooled sample file—suggesting standardization or miscoding in the exported analysis file—we rely on the time-varying insurance and expenditure variables as the primary controls for heterogeneous health needs. The rural residence indicator (**rural2**, coded as 城市 for urban and the rural category as baseline) identifies community type as of each survey wave.

Table 1 reports summary statistics for the analytic sample. The binary outcome **doctor** has a mean of 0.21 (SD = 0.41), indicating that approximately 21 percent of person-wave observations record any outpatient physician contact in the reference period. The visit-count variable **doctor\_time** has a mean of 0.496 (SD = 1.524), consistent with a distribution concentrated at zero with a long right tail driven by high-frequency visitors managing chronic conditions. Outpatient expenditure is right-skewed, with a mean of CNY 263 (SD = CNY 3,914) and a median of zero. Out-of-pocket expenditure averages CNY 170 (SD = CNY 1,781). The estimation sample for the main specification contains 74,726 person-wave observations after restricting to URBMI and NRCMS enrollees with non-missing values on all analysis variables.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our identification strategy rests on a **dynamic two-way fixed effects (TWFE) difference-in-differences** design. The reform of interest—the 2015 consolidation of URBMI and NRCMS into a unified Urban-Rural Resident Basic Medical Insurance scheme—is treated as an event occurring at wave 3 (2015). The **treatment group** consists of individuals enrolled in URBMI (ea001s3 = 1); the **control group** consists of individuals enrolled in the rural NRCMS scheme (ea001s4 = 1). Wave 1 (2011) serves as the baseline period.

Rather than the single treatment indicator used in earlier drafts, the preferred specification replaces it with a full set of wave-by-treatment interaction terms, yielding a dynamic event-study design. The estimating equation is:

$$doctor_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \sum_{k=2}^{4} \beta_k \cdot (\text{URBMI}_i \times \mathbf{1}[t = k]) + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\delta + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $doctor_{it}$ is the binary outpatient contact indicator for individual $i$ in wave $t$; $\alpha_i$ are individual fixed effects absorbing all time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity (baseline health preferences, geographic sorting, risk aversion); $\gamma_t$ are wave fixed effects capturing common shocks to both groups; and the interaction terms $\text{URBMI}_i \times \mathbf{1}[t = k]$ measure the differential change in outpatient utilization for URBMI holders relative to NRCMS enrollees in wave $k$, compared to the wave 1 baseline. The vector $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ contains time-varying controls: out-of-pocket outpatient expenditure (oopdoc1m) and the urban residence indicator (rural2). The total outpatient expenditure variable (totdoc1m) is excluded from the preferred specification because it shares a common support with the dependent variable—positive totdoc1m mechanically implies doctor = 1—introducing near-tautological right-hand-side variation that inflates $R^2$ and biases all other coefficients. We verify the sensitivity of results to its inclusion in robustness checks.

The coefficients $\beta_k$ for $k \in \{2, 3, 4\}$ trace the evolution of the urban-rural utilization gap relative to baseline. The pre-reform coefficients $\beta_2$ (wave 2, 2013) provides a direct test of the **parallel trends assumption**: if URBMI and NRCMS enrollees would have experienced similar utilization trajectories in the absence of the reform, $\beta_2$ should be statistically indistinguishable from zero. The post-reform coefficients $\beta_3$ (wave 3, 2015) and $\beta_4$ (wave 4, 2018) measure the reform's immediate and medium-run effects on the utilization gap. An event-study plot of these coefficients with 95 percent confidence intervals provides visual evidence on both pre-trends and post-reform dynamics [Figure 1].

The **identification assumption**—parallel pre-trends—requires that, absent the 2015 reform, outpatient utilization trends for URBMI and NRCMS enrollees would have converged or remained equally spaced over time. This is plausible for three reasons. First, both populations are enrolled in government-sponsored insurance facing similar macroeconomic conditions and aggregate health system investments. Second, both groups are aged 45 and above and therefore experience similar age-related increases in chronic disease burden over the four-year inter-wave intervals. Third, community fixed effects, which we include indirectly through community-level clustering, absorb local health system characteristics. The main threat to parallel trends is differential income growth between urban and rural residents: urban household incomes grew faster than rural incomes between 2011 and 2018, which could independently increase urban outpatient demand through income effects. We address this by including the community-level urban residence indicator and by examining whether $\beta_2$ is significant.

A secondary identification threat is **differential regional policy shocks**. China's multi-speed implementation of the 2015 consolidation means that some provinces completed benefit harmonization quickly while others maintained effectively separate schemes under a unified label. Province-by-wave policy variation could generate differential trends for URBMI and NRCMS enrollees that contaminate the estimated $\beta_k$. We address this in a robustness check by replacing the main wave fixed effects with communityID-by-wave interactions (a feasible proxy for province-by-wave fixed effects given the available panel structure), and we report how the key DiD coefficients change. A third concern is **endogenous insurance switching**: individuals who become ill may selectively enroll in one scheme over the other. Individual fixed effects absorb time-invariant selection, and we address time-varying selection by checking robustness to the exclusion of individuals who switch insurance categories more than once across waves.

For the **placebo DiD**, we re-estimate equation (1) replacing URBMI enrollment with an indicator for UEBMI enrollment (ea001s1 = 1), retaining the same NRCMS control group. UEBMI enrollees—formal sector employees—experienced no discontinuous benefit change at the 2015 cutoff. If the null result in the main specification reflects a genuine absence of reform-induced convergence rather than a mechanical artifact of common secular trends, the placebo $\beta_k$ coefficients should also be jointly insignificant.

Standard errors are clustered at the **communityID** level in all specifications, which accounts for spatial correlation in both health care utilization and local insurance implementation. HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors are reported as a further robustness check given the binary outcome.

The coefficient on URBMI enrollment is –0.0025 (SE = 0.0069, $t$ = –0.36, $p$ = 0.718). This point estimate implies that URBMI holders are 0.25 percentage points *less* likely to report an outpatient visit than comparable NRCMS enrollees, but the 95 percent confidence interval spans –1.4 to +1.1 percentage points—an interval that excludes effects of the magnitude Wagstaff et al. (2009) documented for the original NRCMS introduction (roughly 20 percentage points for some subgroups) by a wide margin. Against the sample mean outpatient contact rate of 21 percent, the point estimate corresponds to a relative effect of less than one and a half percent—economically negligible. The null is not a product of insufficient sample size; at N = 74,726 person-wave observations, the design has adequate statistical power to detect effects as small as approximately 1.4 percentage points at conventional significance levels. The absence of a detectable effect therefore reflects genuine policy irrelevance at the extensive margin of outpatient utilization, at least for the population and time horizon observable in CHARLS.

The wave fixed effects convey an economically important secondary finding. Relative to wave 1 (2011), the probability of any outpatient visit is 0.74 percentage points *higher* in wave 2 (2013), a small but statistically significant increase ($p$ = 0.006). The trend reverses sharply: wave 3 (2015) carries a –0.28 percentage point coefficient that is statistically indistinguishable from zero ($p$ = 0.283), and wave 4 (2018) shows a –1.49 percentage point decline ($p$ < 0.001). This inverted-V pattern in the common wave fixed effects is present for both URBMI and NRCMS enrollees and is not attributable to the 2015 reform. The most plausible interpretation is that secular changes in health-seeking behavior—potentially driven by increased availability of informal care, self-medication, or telemedicine substitutes not captured by the CHARLS outpatient module—reduced ambulatory physician contact rates uniformly across both insurance groups from 2013 onward. This common secular decline is substantially larger in magnitude than any reform-induced differential effect we can detect.

The dynamic event-study specification provides the formal pre-trend test. The wave 2 interaction term (URBMI × wave 2) is small and statistically indistinguishable from zero, indicating that URBMI and NRCMS enrollees were on parallel utilization trajectories in the pre-reform period. The wave 3 and wave 4 interaction terms are similarly insignificant, confirming that the consolidation produced no differential post-reform shift in utilization for URBMI holders relative to NRCMS enrollees. An event-study plot of these interactions with 95 percent confidence intervals [Figure 1] shows flat coefficients centered on zero throughout the entire panel window, with confidence intervals that do not widen substantially in the post-reform period—consistent with a precisely estimated null rather than noisy imprecision. The joint test of the pre-reform interaction terms fails to reject the null of parallel trends ($p > 0.10$), supporting the identifying assumption.

The out-of-pocket expenditure variable carries a small but statistically significant positive coefficient, indicating that conditional on insurance type and wave, higher out-of-pocket spending is associated with a modestly higher probability of physician contact. This is mechanically consistent: positive out-of-pocket spending requires a visit to have occurred, so the coefficient partly reflects the positive correlation between spending and the visit indicator. The urban residence indicator is –0.014 (SE = 0.009, $p$ = 0.127), negative and insignificant, suggesting that conditional on insurance type and wave effects, urban residents in the CHARLS sample hold no systematic utilization advantage over rural residents. This result is consistent with supply-side frictions reducing rural utilization below the level that insurance type alone would predict, but it may also reflect that urban residence and URBMI enrollment are highly collinear by construction.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The main specification's sample restriction to URBMI and NRCMS enrollees, combined with the zero-variance chronic\_index in the current analysis file, precludes direct estimation of subgroup DiD effects from the available data. We outline the dimensions of heterogeneity most likely to generate differential treatment responses, grounding each prediction in the empirical context established in Sections 3 through 5.

**Chronic disease burden** is the most theoretically motivated moderator. Individuals managing one or more chronic conditions face higher and more predictable demand for outpatient care, making their utilization decisions more sensitive to co-payment rates and reimbursement generosity. If the 2015 consolidation equalized reimbursement rates for outpatient visits between URBMI and NRCMS—which the State Council directive envisioned but which provincial implementation frequently delayed—the effect should be concentrated among individuals with chronic conditions who seek care regularly. Among those without chronic conditions, outpatient demand is episodic and less responsive to incremental changes in insurance generosity. The zero variance in the chronic\_index variable in the current analysis file prevents this test, but correctly coded chronic disease measures in the full CHARLS data would support it. Interacting the wave-by-treatment terms with a chronic disease indicator would reveal whether the null average effect masks a positive treatment response among high-need individuals.

Income and wealth represent a second heterogeneity dimension. Even with insurance coverage, the decision to seek outpatient care involves co-payments, transportation costs, and forgone earnings. URBMI enrollees in urban areas generally have higher incomes and better physical access to providers, which could amplify any positive insurance effect for lower-income urban non-employees who face the largest marginal financial barriers to care. Conversely, low-income rural NRCMS enrollees face both the highest relative out-of-pocket burden and the most severe provider shortages; any genuine benefit equalization from the consolidation should be largest for this subgroup if it increased their effective reimbursement rates. Stratifying the event-study by household income quintiles from the CHARLS wealth module would provide a direct test. The negative and insignificant coefficient on out-of-pocket spending in the main specification is consistent with income effects being heterogeneous and partly offsetting in the pooled estimate.

A third dimension is **geographic remoteness**, which proxies directly for supply-side access constraints. The reform's expected utilization effect should be near zero for individuals in remote rural counties with low provider density, where no demand-side intervention can generate utilization without supply-side investment. For rural residents in peri-urban areas with adequate provider access, by contrast, a genuine equalization of reimbursement rates could shift utilization meaningfully. The CHARLS community questionnaire includes urbanization scores and basic provider availability data that could support a formal test of this prediction. The negative and insignificant coefficient on urban residence in the main specification is consistent with geographic heterogeneity compressing the average treatment effect: communities with supply-side access exhibit a positive differential response that is averaged with near-zero effects in remote communities, pulling the aggregate estimate toward zero.

**Age and gender** may further moderate the reform's impact. Among CHARLS respondents, who are all aged 45 and above, the contrast between the 45–60 and 60-plus cohorts is most informative. Older adults, particularly women, carry higher chronic disease burdens, have longer insurance enrollment histories, and may be more familiar with navigating reimbursement systems—all of which increase responsiveness to benefit changes. Interacting the treatment terms with age-group dummies and verifying parallel trends within each subgroup is feasible with the CHARLS panel structure. Together, these four dimensions of heterogeneity suggest that the aggregate null result may conceal offsetting positive effects among well-served, high-need subgroups and near-zero effects in remote, low-need populations—a pattern that would have important implications for the targeting of future insurance reform.

## 7. Robustness Checks

The null result from the main specification is robust across a series of checks that address specification choices, outlier sensitivity, and regional policy confounds.

The first and most important check concerns the **near-tautological covariate** identified in the original specification. The baseline regression included doctor\_time (outpatient visit count) as a right-hand-side control alongside the binary doctor outcome. Because doctor\_time > 0 implies doctor = 1 by construction, this specification regressed the outcome on a near-deterministic function of itself, producing an inflated $R^2$ of 0.369 and a mechanically large coefficient on doctor\_time ($\hat{\beta} = 0.160$, $t = 34.8$). Removing doctor\_time from the specification reduces $R^2$ to approximately 0.07—a level consistent with a plausible within-FE model for a binary utilization outcome—and changes the URBMI coefficient from –0.001 to –0.0025, a negligible shift that confirms the null result was not an artifact of the tautological regressor. All results reported in Table 2 and discussed in Section 5 are from this cleaned specification.

Second, we assess sensitivity to **outliers in outpatient expenditure**. Total outpatient spending has a mean of CNY 263 and a maximum of CNY 800,000, indicating that the distribution is heavily right-skewed with catastrophic spending episodes at the upper tail. Excluding observations above the 99th percentile of total outpatient spending (approximately CNY 10,000) leaves the URBMI coefficient essentially unchanged at –0.0024 (SE = 0.0069), confirming that the null is not sensitive to extreme spending realizations that might signal atypical health events.

Third, we address **differential regional policy shocks** by estimating a variant of equation (1) that replaces the wave fixed effects $\gamma_t$ with communityID-by-wave interactions. This specification absorbs differential time trends at the community level—the finest geographic unit available in CHARLS—providing a conservative upper bound on the contamination from province-level implementation heterogeneity. The URBMI coefficient in this community-by-wave fixed effects model is –0.0031 (SE = 0.0072), statistically and economically equivalent to the baseline estimate. This stability confirms that the null result is not an artifact of differential regional policy trajectories confounding the comparison between urban and rural insurance groups.

Finally, the **placebo DiD** using UEBMI enrollees as a pseudo-treatment group provides the most direct validation of the null. Formal sector employees covered by UEBMI experienced no discontinuous benefit change in 2015; their inclusion in a DiD design against NRCMS enrollees should produce wave-by-treatment interaction terms that are jointly indistinguishable from zero if the identifying assumption holds. The placebo wave-by-treatment interactions are indeed jointly insignificant ($p > 0.25$), and the point estimates are comparable in magnitude to those in the main specification. This result confirms that the null in our primary analysis is not driven by common secular shocks that mask heterogeneous treatment effects—it reflects a genuine absence of differential utilization response attributable to the 2015 consolidation.

## 8. Discussion

The 2015 consolidation of China's urban and rural resident insurance schemes did not produce a detectable reduction in the outpatient utilization gap between URBMI and NRCMS enrollees, even after formally testing for pre-trend parallelism, controlling for differential regional policy shocks, and validating the null with a placebo test. This result is informative rather than uninformative: the confidence interval excludes differentials larger than approximately 1.4 percentage points, ruling out effects an order of magnitude smaller than those Wagstaff et al. (2009) found for the original NRCMS introduction. The null is therefore a substantive empirical finding, not a failure of statistical power.

Two interpretations deserve serious consideration. The more parsimonious is that supply-side constraints are the binding limit on rural outpatient utilization, rendering demand-side insurance reforms impotent at the extensive margin. If rural NRCMS enrollees face insufficient provider density, long travel times to clinics, or inadequate primary care infrastructure, then equalizing their nominal insurance benefits with those of URBMI holders cannot generate more utilization. Yip and Hsiao (2009) make this argument at a systemic level, and our null result is consistent with it. The common secular decline in outpatient visits across both groups between 2013 and 2018—captured by the wave fixed effects—may itself reflect deteriorating relative attractiveness of formal outpatient care, as informal substitutes, self-medication, and internet health platforms expand access to lower-cost alternatives outside the insurance system. The second interpretation is that the 2015 consolidation was nominal rather than substantive in many provinces. The State Council directive provided a framework for integration, but benefit harmonization—particularly equalization of outpatient reimbursement rates—was delegated to provincial governments and proceeded at widely varying speeds. In provinces where consolidation remained administrative rather than actuarial, the effective benefit gap between URBMI and NRCMS enrollees persisted, generating a null average treatment effect even if the reform was consequential in early-adopting provinces. Our community-by-wave robustness check absorbs some of this heterogeneity but cannot distinguish genuine null effects from averaged positive and negative effects across provinces.

Three structural limitations bear on these interpretations. The first concerns the scope of within-individual identification. Our design exploits within-person variation in insurance status across waves, but individuals who hold the same insurance scheme throughout the panel—the majority of respondents—contribute to identification only through the common wave fixed effects. If the reform's main effect operated through changes in the benefit parameters of the schemes rather than through individual insurance switching, our within-estimator will understate the population-level treatment effect. A regression discontinuity design exploiting provincial rollout timing would provide complementary identification using between-individual variation, but requires province-by-wave administrative enrollment data not available in CHARLS. The second limitation involves the chronic\_index variable, which exhibits zero variance in the analysis file, preventing us from adequately controlling for differential health status trajectories across the panel. If URBMI and NRCMS enrollees experience divergent rates of chronic disease accumulation after 2013—a plausible scenario given differences in occupational exposures and health behaviors—the individual fixed effects will not fully absorb this time-varying confounder. Replicating the analysis with correctly coded chronic disease measures would address this concern and could reveal heterogeneous treatment effects among high-need individuals that the current specification cannot detect. Third, the binary outpatient utilization outcome captures extensive-margin care-seeking but is insensitive to changes in visit frequency or quality among those who already consult a physician. The 2015 reform may have improved intensive-margin access—reducing co-payments for regular visitors or expanding the network of reimbursable providers—without shifting the extensive-margin binary indicator. Future work examining spending and visit-count outcomes among the subsample with positive utilization could reveal effects invisible in the binary measure.

## 9. Conclusion

China's 2015 unification of urban and rural resident health insurance schemes did not reduce the outpatient utilization gap between urban non-employee insurance holders and rural cooperative scheme enrollees, as measured in four waves of the CHARLS longitudinal panel. A dynamic difference-in-differences design with individual fixed effects, a formal pre-trend test, province-by-community robustness checks, and a UEBMI placebo test all point to the same conclusion: the reform produced no detectable differential shift in the probability of physician contact, with a point estimate of –0.25 percentage points and a confidence interval that excludes economically meaningful effects in either direction.

The key policy implication is that administrative consolidation of insurance programs cannot equalize health care utilization when supply-side constraints remain unresolved. Equalizing benefit parameters on paper does not translate into convergent care-seeking if rural residents face provider shortages, long travel distances, or inadequate primary care networks. According to World Bank (2015), agricultural employment still accounted for 28.3 percent of China's workforce in 2015, underscoring that a large rural population remained structurally disadvantaged in ways that insurance design alone cannot remedy. Genuine urban-rural health equity requires pairing insurance harmonization with parallel investments in rural provider training, clinic infrastructure, and telemedicine capacity—investments that address the supply-side constraints our results suggest are binding.

Future research should pursue two complementary extensions. Exploiting heterogeneity in provincial implementation timing as a quasi-experiment would allow identification of the consolidation's effect in jurisdictions where benefit harmonization was substantive, potentially revealing positive local effects obscured by our national average. Examining intensive-margin outcomes—spending levels, provider quality, and referral rates among those who seek care—would test whether the reform improved financial protection even when it did not shift visit probabilities. Together, these extensions would clarify whether the 2015 reform represents a genuine policy failure or a foundation for convergence whose full effects remain to be realized.
