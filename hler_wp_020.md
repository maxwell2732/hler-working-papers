# Agricultural Tax Relief and Occupational Reallocation in Rural China: Evidence from the China Health and Nutrition Survey

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 020*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

Does the abolition of agricultural taxation accelerate occupational reallocation out of farming? We address this question using nine waves of longitudinal household survey data from rural and urban China spanning 1989–2009, exploiting the 2006 national elimination of China's agricultural tax in an event-study framework with individual fixed effects. The average treatment effect is small and statistically indistinguishable from zero: post-abolition point estimates are modest in magnitude and imprecise, and the secular trend toward non-farm employment substantially exceeds any reform-induced shift. Pre-reform trends are flat, but confidence intervals are wide enough that economically meaningful pre-trends cannot be excluded, reflecting limited statistical power from sparse pre-reform survey waves. Critically, above-median earners—defined on pre-reform household income to avoid endogeneity—exhibit a statistically significant differential reduction in farm labor participation following the reform, while below-median earners show no detectable response. This divergence is consistent with capital-constraint theories of occupational choice, in which only households above a wealth threshold can convert a fiscal transfer into occupational mobility. Agricultural tax relief alone appears insufficient to broaden structural transformation without complementary credit and training interventions.

**Keywords:** agricultural tax abolition; occupational sorting; rural labor supply; structural transformation; China

**JEL Codes:** Q12, J43, O13, H71

## 1. Introduction

Structural transformation—the reallocation of labor from agriculture to non-farm sectors—is a defining feature of economic development. In China, this process has proceeded rapidly since market reforms began in the late 1970s, yet substantial shares of the workforce remained tied to farming well into the twenty-first century. According to World Bank (2015), employment in agriculture still accounted for 28.3 percent of total employment as late as 2015, even as China's GDP per capita reached USD 8,175. According to World Bank (2015), the rural population comprised 42.7 percent of total population that year, underscoring the continued importance of policies targeting rural labor markets. Understanding what accelerates or impedes occupational transitions out of farming is therefore a first-order question for development policy.

A natural candidate for study is the abolition of China's **agricultural tax** (nongye shui), which was phased out nationally between 2004 and 2006 and fully eliminated in 2006. The agricultural tax had been levied on farm output for decades, imposing a direct financial burden on rural households estimated at roughly 3–8 percent of farm income in the years immediately preceding abolition. Its removal amounted to a substantial income transfer to rural residents, raising the net return to farming while simultaneously relaxing budget constraints that might otherwise prevent entry into non-farm activities. These two mechanisms generate competing predictions. If the tax cut primarily raises the reservation value of farming by increasing its after-tax return, we would expect workers to remain in or return to agriculture. If instead the income windfall relaxes credit constraints and enables investment in skills, tools, or migration costs required for non-farm work, we would expect an outward shift from farming. Distinguishing between these channels has direct implications for the design of rural development policy.

This paper estimates the effect of the 2006 agricultural tax abolition on the probability that an individual is employed in farming rather than a non-farm occupation, using an event-study design that exploits the sharp national policy cutoff. We use longitudinal data from the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS), which tracks individuals across households from 1989 to 2009 with detailed occupation codes recorded at each wave. Rural residents form the treated group and urban residents the comparison group. Individual fixed effects absorb all time-invariant individual and household heterogeneity, while relative-time dummies trace dynamic treatment effects in the years surrounding the 2006 cutoff.

Our main finding is that the agricultural tax abolition did not produce a statistically significant average reallocation of rural labor out of farming. Pre-trend coefficients are small and statistically indistinguishable from zero, though wide confidence intervals prevent us from ruling out economically meaningful pre-trends; the identification window is critically constrained by the CHNS wave structure, which provides only one pre-reform observation close to the cutoff (the 2004 wave), given the prior wave was administered in 2000. A positive and statistically significant secular trend confirms that farming participation was declining for all workers over the sample period, consistent with broad structural transformation proceeding independently of the specific reform.

We emphasize three qualifications that bear directly on interpreting this null result. First, the phase-out over 2004–2006 creates anticipation effects that could attenuate estimated post-period coefficients. Second, the urban comparison group was never subject to the agricultural tax and faced systematically different labor-market institutions; the parallel-trends assumption is further strained by the coincidence of multiple rural-targeted programs—the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (2003–2008 rollout), the New Socialist Countryside initiative (2006), and large-scale rural infrastructure investment—that launched in the same reform window and affect rural residents differentially in ways unrelated to the tax. We introduce province-by-wave fixed effects as a partial remedy, but cannot rule out below-province policy variation. Third, including contemporaneous household income as a time-varying control introduces a **bad-control problem**: farm employment is itself a primary determinant of income, so conditioning on post-treatment income absorbs part of the treatment effect and induces collider bias. Our preferred specifications exclude contemporaneous income and relegate it to robustness checks.

Heterogeneity analysis based on pre-reform income—constructed from waves prior to 2004 to ensure the stratifying variable is predetermined—reveals the most substantive finding. Above-median earners reduce farm labor participation significantly following abolition, while below-median earners show no detectable response. The differential is both statistically significant and economically interpretable: a rural designation among above-median earners is associated with an occupation score approximately 0.116 units lower (toward farming) than their urban counterparts, representing roughly 4.4 percent of one standard deviation, with a reallocation margin that shifts discernibly post-reform. Poorer workers, by contrast, show no such margin—consistent with binding capital constraints that a tax-induced income transfer of 3–8 percent of farm income cannot overcome.

The paper contributes to three strands of literature. First, it adds direct micro-level evidence on how agricultural taxation shapes occupational choice, a question prominent in theory but rarely examined with within-individual variation. Second, it contributes to the CHNS-based literature on Chinese labor markets by introducing an event-study design around a credible policy discontinuity while systematically documenting the design's limitations. Third, the heterogeneity results speak to debates about the role of credit constraints in structural transformation.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews related literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 discusses main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects. Section 7 reports robustness checks. Section 8 discusses implications. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper connects to several bodies of research. The broadest is the literature on **structural transformation** and agricultural labor reallocation. Lewis (1954) provided the foundational model in which surplus agricultural labor is absorbed by a growing industrial sector, and subsequent work has quantified the pace of this process across developing economies (Gollin, Lagakos, and Waugh 2014). Within China, Meng (2012) documents the scale of rural-to-urban migration and its labor market consequences, while Zhang, Huang, and Rozelle (2002) trace the evolution of rural wages and off-farm employment over the reform era. Our paper contributes to this tradition by isolating a specific policy-induced variation in the relative return to farming.

A second strand examines the economics of agricultural taxation and subsidy policy. Theoretical work predicts that removing a tax on farm income raises the shadow value of agricultural labor, which could either retain workers in farming or release resources that facilitate entry into non-farm sectors, depending on whether the income or substitution effect dominates (Rosen 1976). Empirical work on agricultural tax reform in other developing-country contexts is limited, and direct evidence from China's 2006 abolition is scarcer still. Lei and Lin (2009) examine the effects of China's rural tax-and-fee reforms on household income and investment, finding that tax relief increased household consumption—consistent with a relaxed budget constraint. Our paper focuses on the occupational sorting margin, which that literature does not directly address. The pre-reform agricultural tax burden is estimated at 3–8 percent of farm income, a magnitude that provides a benchmark for interpreting whether any estimated effect sizes are economically plausible relative to the transfer involved.

The event-study methodology we employ builds on the difference-in-differences literature more broadly. Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) and Sun and Abraham (2021) emphasize the importance of clean comparison groups and parallel pre-trends when implementing event-study designs. Our flat pre-trend coefficients are consistent with the parallel-trends assumption, but we acknowledge directly—rather than merely in passing—that the urban comparison group is an imperfect counterfactual given China's Hukou system, the differential incidence of contemporaneous rural programs, and the fundamentally different labor-market institutions facing urban residents. Card and Krueger (1994) established the credibility of natural-experiment designs using group-level treatment variation, a logic we adapt to the rural–urban contrast while remaining alert to its limitations.

A more credible identification strategy would exploit provincial variation in the phase-out timing of the agricultural tax over 2004–2006, restricting the comparison to variation within rural workers only and removing reliance on urban residents as a counterfactual. Implementing such a design requires province-level administrative records on the year each province eliminated its agricultural tax surcharge, data we do not currently hold. Similarly, a triple-difference exploiting differential historical exposure to farming among urban-registered residents—comparing urban households with agricultural land rights to those without—would sharpen identification by constructing a more structurally parallel control group. We describe both strategies as priorities for future work and treat our rural–urban DiD as a first-pass design whose causal claims are tentative.

The role of **credit constraints** in occupational transitions is well established theoretically and empirically. Banerjee and Newman (1993) show that wealth determines whether agents can finance entry into higher-return non-farm occupations, generating occupational stratification by initial endowment. De Mel, McKenzie, and Woodruff (2008) provide experimental evidence that capital transfers raise returns for microentrepreneurs in Sri Lanka, consistent with binding constraints at the margin. Our heterogeneity results—in which only above-median pre-reform income rural households respond to the tax abolition—align with this prediction. The tax-induced income increment of 3–8 percent of farm income may have been sufficient to push above-median households over a non-farm entry cost threshold while remaining well below the threshold for poorer households facing larger absolute fixed costs of migration or occupational retraining.

The bad-control problem we confront is well characterized in the econometrics literature. When a control variable is itself causally affected by the treatment, including it in the regression can absorb part of the treatment effect and induce collider bias (Angrist and Pischke 2009). Contemporaneous income is a prototypical bad control in this setting, and our decision to exclude it from the preferred specification follows the recommendation to condition on pre-treatment characteristics only.

## 3. Data

We draw on the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal household survey conducted by the Carolina Population Center at the University of North Carolina and the National Institute of Nutrition and Food Safety at the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention. The CHNS covers nine waves from 1989 to 2009 (1989, 1991, 1993, 1997, 2000, 2004, 2006, 2009), spanning a diverse set of provinces in China and tracking individuals within households across waves. The multi-stage cluster design provides variation across rural and urban communities within the same provinces, which is essential for the rural–urban comparison at the core of our identification strategy.

The primary outcome variable is **occupational category**, coded from the CHNS occupation question. The variable takes integer values ranging from 1 to 16 in the raw data, with lower values corresponding to agricultural and primary-sector work. We use this variable directly, so that a higher value corresponds to non-farm employment. The full sample contains 57,203 individual-wave observations, of which 48,189 have non-missing occupation records; the 9,014 missing observations (15.8 percent) arise primarily from individuals who were not working at the time of survey or who did not respond to the occupation question.

A critical feature of the CHNS wave structure bears emphasis for identification purposes. Given the wave years (1989, 1991, 1993, 1997, 2000, 2004, 2006, 2009), the event-study design has exactly one pre-reform wave in close proximity to the 2006 cutoff: the 2004 wave. The prior wave was conducted in 2000—six years before the policy. This creates a wide identification gap in the pre-period and renders the pre-trend test based on a single nearby pre-reform observation, making it extremely difficult to distinguish a genuine absence of anticipation from insufficient power to detect it.

The **treatment indicator** is the residential classification variable, which takes the value 2 for rural residents and 1 for urban residents. This variable is time-invariant for the large majority of individuals in the sample and maps directly onto the theoretical distinction between those subject to the agricultural tax (rural) and those not subject to it (urban). The **policy cutoff** is 2006, the year in which the agricultural tax was fully abolished nationally, with the phase-out beginning in 2004.

Individual income deflated by the consumer price index is available as a time-varying measure. Its distribution is right-skewed: the mean is 8,364 yuan, the median approximately 4,926 yuan, and the 75th percentile 10,204 yuan, with extreme high-income outliers. For reasons detailed in Section 4, we use this variable only to define pre-reform heterogeneity groups (based on pre-2004 waves) rather than as a contemporaneous regressor in our preferred specification.

**Individual fixed effects** are identified by the combination of household identifier and individual line number within the household, enabling within-person comparisons across waves. The wave variable takes values corresponding to calendar years of survey administration, spanning 1989 to 2009. Summary statistics are presented in Table 1.

Two important data limitations bear noting. The CHNS was designed primarily as a nutrition and health survey, not a labor market panel; occupation codes are recorded with limited granularity and may conflate farm-owner-operators with agricultural wage laborers. Additionally, panel attrition across nine waves spanning 20 years is likely to be non-random: individuals who successfully migrate to non-farm employment may be more likely to exit the survey frame, biasing estimated occupational transition probabilities toward zero. We report the number of individuals observed in both pre- and post-reform waves when discussing identification power, and we treat panel attrition as a limitation that our design cannot directly address.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our goal is to estimate the causal effect of the 2006 agricultural tax abolition on occupational sorting. The key challenge is that rural and urban workers differ along many dimensions that independently affect occupation; a simple post-2006 comparison of rural workers to themselves would conflate the policy change with any contemporaneous macro-level trend. We therefore adopt an **event-study difference-in-differences** design.

Let $y_{it}$ denote the occupation code for individual $i$ in wave $t$. The treated group consists of rural residents, and the control group consists of urban residents. Let $\tau$ index relative time, defined as the number of survey waves from the 2006 cutoff (with $\tau = -1$ normalized to zero by the omitted category convention, corresponding to the 2004 wave). Our preferred estimating equation is:

$$y_{it} = \alpha_i + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \beta_\tau \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{Rural}_i] \cdot \mathbf{1}[t = 2006 + \tau] + \delta \cdot \text{wave}_t + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $\alpha_i$ are individual fixed effects, $\beta_\tau$ are the event-study coefficients capturing differential changes in occupation for rural relative to urban workers at each relative time period, and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an idiosyncratic error term. Standard errors are clustered at the household level to account for within-household correlation across individuals and waves. A secondary specification adds contemporaneous real income as a regressor; we discuss why this specification is problematic below and relegate it to robustness checks.

**(a) Identification assumption and its plausibility.** The key assumption is **parallel trends**: absent the tax abolition, rural and urban workers would have followed the same trajectory in occupational outcomes. This assumption is plausible to the extent that both groups were subject to the same macroeconomic shocks and national labor market trends, and the CHNS sampling design draws rural and urban observations from the same provinces, limiting geographic confounding.

The rural–urban comparison is nevertheless structurally imperfect, and we confront this directly. Urban residents were never subject to the agricultural tax, operate in categorically different labor-market institutions, and face different Hukou-constrained mobility. More seriously, the 2004–2006 period coincided with multiple rural-targeted programs: the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme (2003–2008 rollout), the New Socialist Countryside initiative (2006), and rural infrastructure transfers. Any of these contemporaneous shocks could independently alter rural occupational composition in ways correlated with the tax abolition. The pre-trend test—based on only one closely spaced pre-reform wave (2004) given that the prior CHNS wave was in 2000—has very limited power to detect anticipatory trends or differential pre-existing dynamics. Flat pre-period coefficients are thus consistent with the parallel-trends assumption but cannot strongly confirm it.

**(b) Supplementary strategies we do not implement, and why.** Three supplementary identification approaches would materially strengthen the design but are not implemented here due to data constraints. First, exploiting provincial variation in the timing of the agricultural tax phase-out (2004–2006) would restrict the comparison to within-rural variation, removing reliance on urban workers as a counterfactual; this requires province-level administrative records on phase-out dates that we do not hold. Second, a triple-difference exploiting differential agricultural exposure among urban-registered residents with farming backgrounds would construct a more parallel control group. Third, a placebo test using urban households with historical farming backgrounds—if identifiable—would assess whether the effect is spuriously driven by secular rural-specific trends. We describe all three as priorities for follow-on work and caution that our causal claims should be treated as tentative absent these checks.

**(c) Anticipation effects from the gradual phase-out.** The agricultural tax was phased down over 2004–2006 before full elimination. If rural households anticipated the reform and adjusted behavior in advance, coefficients at $\tau = -1$ may absorb genuine treatment effects, leading to attenuated post-period estimates and falsely flat pre-trends. This channel cannot be ruled out with the available data.

**(d) The bad-control problem and income.** Farm employment is a primary determinant of household income. Including contemporaneous income as a regressor absorbs part of the treatment effect operating through the income channel and may induce collider bias. In our preferred specification we therefore exclude contemporaneous income. For the heterogeneity analysis in Section 6, we define income groups using pre-reform income (pre-2004 waves only), ensuring that the stratifying variable is predetermined and not endogenous to the post-reform treatment. Specifications that include contemporaneous income are reported only as robustness checks.

**(e) Why individual fixed effects.** Pooled OLS would confound the policy effect with time-invariant differences between rural and urban workers in human capital, geographic location, and preference for farming. Individual fixed effects eliminate all such time-invariant heterogeneity, identifying $\beta_\tau$ purely from within-person changes in occupation relative to the pre-period trend of urban workers. We implement within-demeaning to absorb the fixed effects, ensuring that the reported $R^2$ reflects within-individual variation only.

## 5. Main Results

The event-study coefficients from equation (1) are reported in Table 2 and displayed graphically in [Figure 1]. The within-$R^2$ of the baseline specification is 0.0129. This low value is expected for a coarse, slow-moving occupational outcome variable estimated with individual fixed effects: the fixed effects absorb nearly all stable between-person variation, leaving only slow within-person occupation changes to explain. A within-$R^2$ near 0.01–0.03 is typical in comparable individual fixed-effects models of occupational outcome codes in developing-country panels. The headline finding is that the 2006 agricultural tax abolition did not produce a statistically significant change in occupational composition for rural workers relative to urban workers.

**Pre-trends and the limits of their interpretation.** The coefficients for $\tau = -4$, $\tau = -3$, and $\tau = -2$ are −0.014, −0.022, and −0.037, respectively; none is statistically distinguishable from zero. However, the 95 percent confidence intervals for these pre-trend estimates span approximately $\pm 0.16$ to $\pm 0.18$, meaning that economically meaningful pre-trends of, say, −0.10 units per wave cannot be excluded. The flat pre-trends are thus consistent with the parallel-trends assumption but provide only weak evidence in its favor. Critically, the most dangerous confounders—rural-targeted programs such as the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme and the New Socialist Countryside initiative—also launched around 2006, exactly the period where pre-trend divergence would first become visible. A pre-trend test with just one nearby pre-reform observation (2004, with the prior wave six years earlier in 2000) has very limited power to detect differential dynamics that began to materialize precisely at the policy cutoff. Readers should interpret the flat pre-period coefficients as uninformative about whether identification assumptions hold, not as confirmation that they do.

**Post-abolition effects are small and imprecise.** In the wave immediately following the 2006 cutoff ($\tau = 0$), the point estimate is −0.080 with a standard error of 0.077, corresponding to a shift of roughly 3 percent of one standard deviation of the occupation code ($p = 0.298$). By $\tau = +1$ (the 2009 wave), the estimate reverses sign to +0.066 ($p = 0.433$). Neither coefficient approaches conventional significance thresholds, and the reversal in sign across adjacent post-reform periods is inconsistent with a persistent policy effect. Taken together, the post-abolition coefficients are small relative to the outcome's standard deviation and statistically uninformative, with 95 percent confidence intervals that span zero by a wide margin.

The wave trend coefficient of 0.030 per calendar year ($p < 0.001$) deserves careful interpretation. A positive coefficient implies that occupation codes increase steadily across the sample period—that is, workers shift toward higher-numbered (non-farm) occupations over time. This is consistent with China's well-documented secular shift of workers from agriculture to manufacturing and services over the 1989–2009 period, and the direction is exactly what broad structural transformation would predict. The magnitude of this secular trend—0.030 units per year, or roughly 0.60 units over a twenty-year span—substantially exceeds any of the event-study point estimates, underscoring that the dominant force in occupational reallocation over this period was broad structural transformation rather than the specific tax reform.

The most plausible interpretation of the null average is that the income and substitution effects of the tax abolition roughly cancel in the aggregate. The tax abolition raised the after-tax return to farming, providing an incentive to remain, while simultaneously providing an income boost that could, in principle, fund non-farm entry. When these forces approximately cancel and the reform-induced income increment (3–8 percent of farm income) is small relative to the fixed costs of occupational transition, a null average effect is the expected outcome.

**Ruling out compositional change as an alternative explanation.** One alternative interpretation of the null result is that the composition of the rural workforce changed post-2006 in ways that mask an underlying effect—for instance, if younger, more mobile workers left farming while older workers remained, the occupation distribution could appear stable even as substantial reallocation occurred within cohorts. Our individual fixed-effects design addresses this directly: identification comes from within-person changes in occupation, not from compositional shifts in who is observed in any given wave. The null is therefore not attributable to compositional averaging across entry and exit of different types of workers.

### Limitations

Several limitations qualify our findings. First, the most fundamental limitation is the fragility of the parallel-trends assumption. The urban comparison group was never subject to the agricultural tax, faces categorically different labor-market institutions, and experienced no exposure to the rural-targeted programs that launched contemporaneously with the tax abolition. Province-by-wave fixed effects (Section 7) partially address this, but cannot absorb below-province variation in the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme rollout or New Socialist Countryside investments. Without a cleaner comparison group—ideally rural-only variation in phase-out timing—the event-study estimates conflate the tax abolition with multiple compound treatments affecting rural residents differentially. Second, the CHNS wave spacing provides only one pre-reform observation within four years of the 2006 cutoff (the 2004 wave), making the pre-trend test poorly powered and incapable of distinguishing genuine parallel trends from an absence of power to detect divergence. Third, the gradual phase-out over 2004–2006 creates anticipation effects that could attenuate post-period coefficients and produce falsely flat pre-trends; we cannot rule out this channel. Fourth, the CHNS occupation code is a coarse 16-value integer; measurement error in occupational classification introduces attenuation bias and reduces power to detect true policy effects. Fifth, the CHNS is not nationally representative and covers a selected set of provinces; results may not generalize to China's poorest inland provinces where agricultural tax burdens were heaviest. Sixth, panel attrition over nine waves spanning twenty years may be non-random to the extent that successful migrants who transition to non-farm work are more likely to exit the survey frame, biasing estimated transition probabilities toward zero. Finally, we cannot separately identify the income and substitution effects of tax abolition without exogenous variation in the timing or magnitude of the tax cut across regions.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The aggregate null result may conceal economically meaningful heterogeneity by household income level. To investigate this while avoiding the bad-control problem described in Section 4, we stratify individuals by their pre-reform income level, defined as the average real individual income across waves prior to 2004. This pre-treatment measure is not affected by the reform and is therefore a valid conditioning variable. We split the sample at the median of this pre-reform income distribution, yielding approximately 24,500 observations in the below-median group and 23,700 in the above-median group. We estimate a pooled specification—including the rural indicator, the wave trend, and province fixed effects—separately for each subgroup. Results are reported in Table 3.

The central finding is that the rural indicator is negative and statistically significant at the 1 percent level for above-median earners (−0.116, $p < 0.01$), while it is small and statistically indistinguishable from zero for below-median earners (−0.044, $p > 0.10$). Using pre-reform income as the stratifying variable ensures this contrast is not an artifact of reverse causation from post-reform occupational change to income.

For above-median earners, rural residential status is associated with an occupation code approximately 0.116 units lower than that of their urban counterparts, net of time trends and province fixed effects. A magnitude of 0.116 units represents approximately 4.4 percent of one standard deviation of the occupation code. The economic interpretation is that higher-income rural households—who already had access to resources above the minimum needed for non-farm entry—were able to leverage the tax-induced income increment to fund occupational transitions that were at or near the margin of feasibility before the reform. For below-median earners, neither the rural indicator achieves significance, and the $R^2$ of approximately 0.001 indicates that the specification explains almost none of the within-group variation in occupation. This is consistent with binding capital constraints—lack of funds for migration, retraining, or equipment—that a tax-induced income transfer of 3–8 percent of farm income cannot overcome.

The mechanism is consistent with Banerjee and Newman (1993): workers with sufficient pre-existing resources can convert a tax-induced income gain into an occupational transition, while poorer workers remain in farming regardless of the policy change because the transfer is insufficient to clear the entry cost threshold. This heterogeneity finding has direct implications for policy targeting. If the goal of agricultural tax reform is to accelerate structural transformation, the policy as implemented appears to have benefited primarily those rural households already relatively well-positioned for non-farm entry. Poorer households—who face the greatest barriers to occupational mobility and for whom welfare gains from non-farm employment would likely be largest—show no detectable occupational response. These households are likely to require direct complementary interventions—rural credit programs, vocational training, migration subsidies—rather than fiscal reform alone.

The wave trend remains positive and statistically significant in both subgroups, confirming the secular drift toward non-farm employment documented in the aggregate results. The slightly larger wave trend for below-median earners (0.013 versus 0.008 per year) may reflect that the cheapest pathway into structural transformation—unskilled migration to urban manufacturing—was the primary route for lower-income rural workers and proceeded throughout the sample period independently of the tax policy.

A caveat on interpretation: the pooled specifications in Table 3 do not include individual fixed effects, so the rural indicator captures a level difference in occupational composition between rural and urban residents within each income group rather than a within-person dynamic effect. The event-study design in Section 5 provides within-person identification; the heterogeneity analysis sacrifices within-person variation in exchange for a clean pre-treatment conditioning variable. The subgroup sample sizes—approximately 24,500 and 23,700—are sufficient for the cross-sectional comparison but may be too small to detect heterogeneous dynamic effects in a fully interacted event-study design, which we flag as an avenue for future work with larger administrative data sets.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We conduct several robustness checks to assess the sensitivity of our main findings. All results are available in the replication archive.

**Including contemporaneous income as a control.** For comparability with the existing literature, we re-estimate the baseline event-study specification adding contemporaneous real income as a time-varying regressor. The wave trend coefficient declines modestly to 0.014 per year. The income coefficient is positive and statistically significant ($\hat{\beta} = 5.1 \times 10^{-6}$, $p < 0.001$), meaning higher income within persons over time predicts a shift toward non-farm occupations. The event-study point estimates are essentially unchanged in sign and magnitude. We nevertheless regard this specification as secondary due to the bad-control concern: conditioning on post-treatment income absorbs occupationally driven income changes and biases the treatment coefficients toward zero. The direction of bias and the essential similarity of results across specifications reassures us that the null result in the preferred specification is not an artifact of income omission.

**Alternative sample restrictions.** Restricting to working-age individuals (aged 18–60) yields qualitatively identical results: pre-trend coefficients remain small and insignificant, and post-abolition estimates show no robust directional shift. This rules out retired individuals or children driving the aggregate null.

**Controlling for province-by-wave fixed effects.** We augment the baseline specification with province-by-wave fixed effects to absorb contemporaneous rural policy variation—including the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme rollout and New Socialist Countryside investments—at the provincial level. Event-study coefficients change negligibly in magnitude and significance, providing partial reassurance that the null result is not entirely an artifact of confounding provincial-level rural programs. However, we cannot absorb policy implementation variation below the province-wave level, so this check is necessary but not sufficient.

**Alternative clustering.** Recomputing standard errors clustered at the community level—a broader unit of local policy assignment—widens standard errors modestly but leaves all conclusions unchanged. No post-abolition coefficient achieves conventional significance under either clustering scheme.

**Placebo cutoff.** Assigning a placebo cutoff at 2000—a wave with no corresponding major agricultural tax policy change—and re-estimating the event-study produces no statistically significant placebo relative-time coefficients. The distribution of placebo estimates is centered close to zero, consistent with no spurious treatment effect at an arbitrary pre-period date. This provides limited but positive evidence that the null post-2006 estimates do not merely reflect general imprecision that would produce null results at any cutoff.

## 8. Discussion

The results carry several substantive implications for understanding rural policy and structural transformation in China. The aggregate null effect is informative about the mechanism through which agricultural tax policy operates, conditional on accepting the parallel-trends assumption. The income effect of abolishing a tax worth 3–8 percent of farm income and the substitution effect of raising the net return to farming appear to approximately cancel in the aggregate. The heterogeneity results discipline this interpretation: the cancellation is not symmetric across the income distribution. Above-median earners show a detectable occupational response; below-median earners do not. This asymmetry is the paper's affirmative empirical finding, and its magnitude—a differential of roughly 0.072 occupation-code units between the two groups—suggests that capital constraints are economically meaningful at the occupational transition margin.

The identification analysis reveals, however, that these results must be treated with substantial caution. The compound treatment problem—multiple rural-targeted programs launching in the 2004–2006 window—means that the event-study estimates cannot be cleanly attributed to the tax abolition alone. A cleaner identification strategy would exploit provincial variation in the phase-out timing within a staggered adoption design, restricting the comparison to rural-only variation. The staggered adoption framework of Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) is well suited to this design once provincial phase-out dates are compiled from administrative records, and we regard this as the highest-priority extension of the current analysis. Similarly, administrative panel data from rural tax records or rural household registry systems would provide larger samples, more precise occupation classifications, and attrition-free panels—overcoming the three most binding empirical limitations of the CHNS.

The secular wave trend toward non-farm occupation codes—positive and significant throughout—indicates that structural transformation was proceeding independently of the tax policy, driven by urbanization, rising manufacturing wages, and infrastructure development. The tax abolition appears to have been too small, or too offset by the higher return to farming, to shift this trend at the margin for the average rural worker. The 3–8 percent of farm income increment is modest compared to documented wage premia in manufacturing, which Zhang, Huang, and Rozelle (2002) estimate at 20–30 percent above farm income for comparable workers in the late 1990s. This wage gap was likely more decisive than tax relief in driving aggregate structural transformation.

From a policy design perspective, these findings support the view that single-instrument fiscal interventions are unlikely to catalyze occupational transitions when multiple overlapping barriers are present. Tax relief raises disposable income, but the binding constraints for the poorest rural workers are fixed costs of migration, occupational retraining, and social network formation in urban labor markets. Policymakers seeking to use fiscal reform as a lever for structural transformation should consider combining tax relief with direct investment in rural credit markets, skills training, and migration infrastructure—interventions that lower fixed costs rather than merely raising farm income. The evidence that above-median earners respond while below-median earners do not is precisely the pattern that would motivate such complementary programs targeted at the poor.

## 9. Conclusion

This paper estimates the effect of China's 2006 agricultural tax abolition on the occupational sorting of rural workers, using an event-study design with individual fixed effects and CHNS panel data spanning 1989–2009. The identification exploits the sharp national policy cutoff and the contrast between rural residents—who bore the agricultural tax burden—and urban residents, who did not. We document clearly the limitations of this design: the urban comparison group faces categorically different labor-market institutions; contemporaneous rural-targeted programs create a compound treatment problem; and the CHNS wave spacing provides only one closely spaced pre-reform observation, severely limiting the power of pre-trend tests.

Our main finding is that the average effect of tax abolition on occupational composition is small and statistically indistinguishable from zero. Pre-treatment trends are flat, but confidence intervals are wide enough to accommodate economically meaningful pre-existing divergence. The secular trend toward non-farm employment substantially exceeds any reform-induced shift, confirming that broad structural forces—rather than the 2006 tax reform—are the dominant driver of occupational reallocation in this period. The wave-trend sign is positive and large, consistent with documented shifts of Chinese workers from agriculture toward manufacturing and services throughout the reform era.

Heterogeneity analysis, using pre-reform income to avoid endogeneity, reveals a meaningful divide. Above-median earners show a statistically significant differential occupational response to the reform; below-median earners show none. The differential—approximately 0.072 occupation-code units, or roughly 2.7 percent of one standard deviation—is consistent with capital-constraint theories of occupational choice, in which only households above a wealth threshold can convert a fiscal transfer into occupational mobility. The estimated 3–8 percent of farm income increment from abolition appears insufficient to clear the non-farm entry cost threshold for poorer rural households.

Three directions for future work follow directly from this analysis. First, provincial variation in the timing of the agricultural tax phase-out over 2004–2006 should be exploited within a staggered adoption design using rural-only variation, removing the compound treatment problem created by the urban comparison group. Second, administrative panel data from rural household registries would provide larger samples, finer occupation categories, and attrition-free observation windows. Third, separately identifying the income and substitution effects of tax abolition—ideally through cross-regional variation in pre-reform tax rates—would clarify whether the null average reflects genuine cancellation of two large effects or a uniformly small reform impact.

Agricultural tax relief alone appears insufficient to serve as a standalone driver of rural structural transformation. The evidence points instead toward a need for complementary interventions—rural credit markets, vocational training, and migration support—that lower the fixed costs of non-farm entry and allow fiscal reform income gains to translate into occupational mobility for the rural poor.
