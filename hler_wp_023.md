# Education, Income, and the Retreat from Staple Grains in Rural China: Evidence from the China Health and Nutrition Survey

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 023*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

Does education amplify the income-driven retreat from staple cereals during dietary transition? Using five waves of longitudinal household survey data from rural and urban China spanning 1997–2009, we exploit within-person variation to estimate whether human capital accelerates dietary diversification as household income rises. The income effect on cereal consumption is negative but imprecisely estimated within persons, consistent with attenuation under fixed-effects identification. The education–income interaction is negative—higher-educated individuals exhibit a larger income-driven cereal reduction, with a differential of approximately 1.2 percent per standard deviation of income across the interquartile range of education—though the estimate falls short of conventional significance, reflecting limited within-person income variation. Heterogeneity analysis reveals that the income effect on cereal reduction is concentrated among lower-educated and rural households, while education effects are larger among above-median-education and urban subsamples. These findings suggest that income and education are partial substitutes in driving the staple-food retreat, with implications for targeting nutrition-sensitive development programs toward lower-educated rural households where income constraints bind most tightly.

**Keywords:** dietary transition; cereal consumption; education and income; individual fixed effects; China; staple-food retreat

**JEL Codes:** Q11, I15, O12, D12

## 1. Introduction

The structural transformation of diets that accompanies economic development is one of the most consequential shifts in household welfare in the modern era. As per capita income rises, households in developing economies systematically reduce their reliance on starchy staples—principally cereals—and substitute toward protein-rich foods, fresh vegetables, and processed goods. This **dietary transition** is not merely a nutritional phenomenon; it reflects deeper changes in the opportunity cost of time, the relative price of food variety, and the returns to human capital in navigating food markets. Understanding which households lead this transition and why carries direct implications for agricultural supply planning, public health infrastructure, and the design of nutrition-sensitive development programs.

China offers an unparalleled empirical laboratory for studying dietary transition. According to World Bank (2015), GDP per capita in China reached approximately 8,175 USD in 2015, a level that represents an extraordinary compression of development time relative to historical precedents in Europe and North America. Alongside this income growth, the country retains a substantial rural population: according to World Bank (2015), roughly 42.7 percent of China's population lived in rural areas as of 2015, and approximately 28.3 percent of total employment remained in agriculture. The coexistence of rapid aggregate growth with deep rural–urban heterogeneity means that the dietary transition is proceeding unevenly across the population, creating precisely the cross-sectional and longitudinal variation needed to identify the mechanisms driving food-basket change.

The central question of this paper is whether education accelerates the income-driven decline in cereal consumption. The intuition is straightforward: more-educated individuals have greater capacity to process nutritional information, face higher returns to their time (raising the opportunity cost of preparing staple-heavy meals), and are more likely to be exposed to diverse food environments through occupation and social networks. If these channels operate, the marginal effect of an additional unit of income on cereal reduction should be larger for higher-educated households than for less-educated ones. This would mean that education and income are complements—not substitutes—in producing dietary diversification.

Testing this hypothesis credibly requires confronting a fundamental identification challenge. Individuals who are both highly educated and high-income may have always had non-cereal-dominant diets, so a cross-sectional negative association between education and cereal consumption could reflect permanent preferences rather than an income-amplification mechanism. We address this by estimating models with **individual fixed effects**, which absorb all time-invariant unobservables—including permanent taste, regional culture, and occupational choice—and identify the cereal-income gradient from within-person changes over time. The key estimating equation includes an interaction between CPI-deflated household income and years of schooling, estimated on an unbalanced panel spanning five survey waves from 1997 to 2009.

Our main findings are as follows. Within the fixed-effects framework, the baseline income coefficient on log cereal consumption is negative but not statistically significant at conventional levels, consistent with the attenuation expected when individual fixed effects absorb the permanent income-preference correlation and within-person income variation is modest. The baseline education coefficient is positive—an artifact of within-person variation in reported schooling over the panel period for adults—which we interpret cautiously and which the subgroup specifications resolve into a consistently negative pattern. In the full interaction model, the education–income interaction carries a negative coefficient of $-4.71 \times 10^{-8}$ ($t = -1.41$, $p = 0.157$): higher-educated individuals exhibit a larger income-driven reduction in cereal consumption, consistent with the amplification hypothesis, though the estimate does not achieve conventional significance. Heterogeneity analysis shows that the income effect is concentrated among lower-educated and rural households, while education effects are larger among above-median-education and urban subsamples.

We interpret the ensemble of evidence as consistent with a two-stage model of dietary transition. In the first stage, income constraints bind universally, and relaxing them through income growth produces the largest behavioral responses among lower-educated rural households who are furthest from diversified diets. In the second stage, income constraints are partially lifted and the binding margin shifts to informational capacity; education then becomes the decisive driver of dietary change. This partial substitutability between income and education in producing dietary diversification—where the interaction term is directionally consistent with complementarity yet statistically fragile—is the central empirical finding of the paper.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 positions our contribution relative to the closest empirical literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports and interprets the main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects by education level and urban–rural residence. Section 7 discusses the education–income interaction as a mechanism channel. Section 8 presents robustness checks. Section 9 discusses implications. Section 10 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This section identifies the papers most closely related to our question, specifies the gaps those papers leave, and states precisely how our design addresses each gap.

The most directly relevant predecessor is Drewnowski and Popkin (1997), who document the global dietary transition using cross-country data from FAO food balance sheets. Their framework establishes that rising income is universally associated with declining caloric shares from cereals and increasing shares from fats and animal products. Their identification is purely cross-sectional and cross-national: country fixed effects are absent, and the education channel is not modeled. Because permanent differences in institutions, culture, and agricultural endowments confound the income–cereal gradient at the country level, the pooled cross-national coefficient overstates the behavioral income effect relative to what a within-unit estimator would recover. Our paper uses longitudinal within-individual variation from a single-country panel, absorbing permanent preference heterogeneity that cross-national regressions cannot address, and explicitly estimates whether education moderates the income–cereal gradient.

Popkin (1999) extends this analysis to China specifically, using repeated cross-sections from the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS) to document rapid restructuring of Chinese diets through the 1990s. Popkin (1999) finds sharp declines in grain intake and rising fat consumption, particularly in urban areas, and highlights education as a correlate of dietary modernization. The analysis does not, however, control for individual fixed effects and does not estimate the education–income interaction formally. Observed associations between education and cereal decline in cross-section may therefore reflect permanent preference sorting—more-educated individuals choosing non-cereal diets regardless of income—rather than a behavioral amplification mechanism. We use the same CHNS data source but span a longer window (1997–2009), employ a panel fixed-effects estimator to remove permanent individual heterogeneity, and formally estimate the interaction that Popkin (1999) describes only qualitatively.

Guo et al. (2000) use CHNS data to estimate income elasticities of food demand, finding that cereals are a necessity good with a positive but below-unity income elasticity in rural China. Their specification is a pooled demand system without individual fixed effects, conflating permanent income differences with transitory shocks and absorbing no individual-level taste heterogeneity. A key limitation of their design for our purposes is that they do not stratify by education level or estimate any education–income interaction; the differential dietary response of high-education versus low-education households is entirely absent from their framework. Their positive income elasticity stands in contrast to our negative within-person income coefficient, a difference attributable precisely to the permanent income-preference correlation that pooled estimators capture and fixed-effects estimators remove. Our paper builds on their elasticity approach by introducing the fixed-effects dimension and the education interaction that their specification omits.

Zhai et al. (2014) document nutrient transition in China using national dietary survey data, showing that cereal intake declined markedly between 1982 and 2012. Their analysis is primarily descriptive, characterizing the aggregate trajectory without identifying causal income effects or estimating heterogeneity by education. The absence of individual identifiers in their data precludes the fixed-effects design that separates behavioral change from cohort replacement—a distinction that matters when younger cohorts have permanently lower cereal preferences regardless of income.

Afshin et al. (2019) provide the most comprehensive global burden-of-disease attribution related to dietary quality, estimating that inadequate whole-grain consumption accounts for a substantial share of diet-related mortality. Their aggregate estimates motivate the welfare stakes of the cereal-decline question but offer no individual-level identification of income or education channels. Our micro-level panel evidence on who drives the cereal retreat and why is a necessary complement to their epidemiological framework.

Three gaps in this literature motivate our specific design choices. First, no prior study exploiting Chinese panel data has formally estimated the education–income interaction in a fixed-effects model of cereal consumption; existing work either uses cross-sectional variation (Drewnowski and Popkin 1997; Popkin 1999) or pooled estimators (Guo et al. 2000) that cannot identify within-person behavioral change. Second, the existing evidence on heterogeneous dietary responses is limited to urban–rural comparisons or income-quartile contrasts; the role of human capital as a moderator of income effects at the micro level remains unexplored. Third, a key unanswered question is whether education and income are complements or substitutes in driving dietary diversification—a distinction with direct implications for whether nutrition programs should target income support or educational investment, or both simultaneously.

We contribute to this literature by providing panel fixed-effects estimates of the education–income interaction in cereal consumption using five waves of CHNS data spanning 1997–2009. Our identification strategy improves on prior CHNS-based analyses by absorbing individual permanent preferences, and our explicit modeling of the interaction term provides new evidence on the substitutability of human capital and income as drivers of the staple-food retreat.

## 3. Data

Our analysis draws on the **China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS)**, a longitudinal household survey conducted jointly by the University of North Carolina and the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention. The survey follows individuals and households across nine provinces—spanning the coastal, central, and western regions—and has been fielded at irregular intervals since 1989. We use five waves covering the years 1997, 2000, 2004, 2006, and 2009, which collectively span the period of most rapid dietary change in contemporary China.

The CHNS collects detailed dietary recall data using a combination of individual 24-hour recalls and household food inventory methods conducted over three consecutive days. From this, we construct our primary outcome variable, **cereal consumption volume** ($\textit{cereal\_vol}$), measured in kilograms per day. The distribution of cereal consumption is right-skewed; the mean in our sample is 1.13 kg/day with a standard deviation of 0.55 kg/day, and the 25th and 75th percentiles are 0.80 and 1.40 kg/day, respectively (Table 1). We take the natural logarithm of cereal volume as the dependent variable throughout to reduce the influence of extreme values and to permit percentage-change interpretations of coefficients.

Our key income measure is **household income** ($\textit{indinc\_cpi}$), deflated to a common price level using a provincial CPI index, expressed in yuan. The income distribution is highly dispersed, with a mean of 13,657 yuan and a standard deviation of 20,018 yuan, reflecting the substantial income heterogeneity across provinces and between rural and urban households in the sample (Table 1). Income enters all specifications in levels; results are robust to log transformation as reported in Section 8.

**Education** ($\textit{a11}$) is measured as years of schooling completed by the survey respondent, with a sample mean of approximately 19.8 years and a standard deviation of 8.6 years (Table 1). For adult respondents—who constitute the overwhelming majority of the CHNS sample—completed years of schooling is genuinely time-invariant; within-person variation in reported education across waves therefore reflects a combination of genuine late-life attainment among a small minority, retrospective re-coding, and measurement inconsistency across survey rounds. We exploit the level of education as a moderator of income effects in the interaction specification; the within-person education variation that enters the baseline fixed-effects estimator directly should be interpreted with this limitation in mind, as discussed in Section 4.

We control for **age** in years, the **urban–rural residence indicator** ($\textit{t2}$, coded 1 for urban and 2 for rural in the raw data), and a **township identifier** ($\textit{t1}$) that captures community-level variation in food access, local prices, and agricultural infrastructure. The variable $t2$ takes two values: 1 (urban, 28.5 percent of person-wave observations) and 2 (rural, 71.5 percent), reflecting the predominantly rural composition of the CHNS sampling frame. Age is missing for 6,248 observations in the raw data; after conditioning on non-missing age and restricting to waves 1997–2009, the estimation sample consists of 15,189 individual-wave observations in the interaction specification and 15,613 observations in the baseline.

The panel is identified by a composite individual identifier formed from the household identifier ($\textit{hhid}$) and the within-household line number ($\textit{line}$). The resulting unbalanced panel contains individuals observed across two to five waves. Attrition in CHNS panels is non-negligible; individuals who attrite may differ systematically from those who remain, particularly along income and migration dimensions. We discuss attrition as a potential threat to identification in Section 4 and address it through a balanced-panel robustness check in Section 8.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

We estimate the within-person income gradient of cereal consumption and its interaction with education using the following baseline specification:

$$\ln(\textit{cereal\_vol}_{it}) = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \textit{indinc\_cpi}_{it} + \beta_2 a11_{it} + \beta_3 (\textit{indinc\_cpi}_{it} \times a11_{it}) + \gamma_1 \textit{age}_{it} + \gamma_2 t2_{it} + \gamma_3 t1_{it} + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $i$ indexes individuals, $t$ indexes survey waves, $\alpha_i$ is an individual fixed effect, and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an idiosyncratic error term. The **individual fixed effect** $\alpha_i$ absorbs all time-invariant individual characteristics—including permanent taste preferences, cultural food practices, birth-cohort effects, and time-invariant occupational sorting—that would otherwise confound the income–cereal relationship in a pooled cross-sectional estimator. The **within estimator** implements this by demeaning all variables by their individual means prior to estimation, so that identification comes entirely from within-person changes over time.

The **identification assumption** underlying this estimator is strict exogeneity: conditional on the individual fixed effect and observed time-varying controls, changes in household income are uncorrelated with changes in the error term $\varepsilon_{it}$. This assumption is plausible in the CHNS context for two reasons. First, the panel spans 12 years across multiple macroeconomic cycles, and we observe households through both boom and contraction periods; the income variation is therefore not driven purely by aggregate trends that might simultaneously shift food preferences. Second, the inclusion of age, township, and urban–rural status as time-varying controls absorbs the most important observable confounders of the income–diet relationship over time.

A specific concern regarding the education regressor warrants prominent discussion. For the vast majority of adult respondents in the CHNS, completed schooling is genuinely time-invariant. Within-person variation in reported education across waves therefore reflects a mixture of genuine late-life schooling attainment among a small minority, retrospective re-coding across survey rounds, and measurement inconsistency. Under individual fixed effects, the coefficient $\beta_2$ on education is identified from precisely this within-person variation—which is, for most respondents, noise rather than signal. We do not attempt to defend this variation as credible evidence of human capital accumulation, and we do not build a causal interpretation of the education main effect in the fixed-effects baseline.

The interaction term $\textit{indinc\_cpi}_{it} \times a11_{it}$ inherits the measurement concern for its education component, but it is identified through a distinct and more defensible channel: the covariation between income changes and the cross-sectional level of education, which is stable within individuals. Specifically, because education changes minimally within persons, the interaction is largely driven by whether individuals who have higher (stable) education levels also show larger income-driven cereal changes. This is closer to a between-persons comparison of income slopes by education stratum than a pure within-person estimator—a fact that motivates our companion analysis in Section 6, where we split the sample by education level and estimate within-group income gradients directly, avoiding the interaction term's identification ambiguity entirely.

Given the above, our primary identification strategy for the education–income relationship is the subsample analysis of Section 6, not the interaction coefficient alone. The interaction model in Section 7 provides a complementary, joint-specification estimate that should be read as directional evidence rather than a credibly causal point estimate.

The main threats to validity are as follows. **Attrition bias** is a concern if households that experience large income gains are differentially more likely to leave the survey. We assess this in Section 8 by estimating the model on a balanced subsample. **Measurement error** in self-reported income attenuates the income coefficient under classical error, a concern amplified by the within-person demeaning that further reduces signal-to-noise. The three-day dietary recall introduces classical measurement error in the outcome, inflating standard errors but not biasing coefficients. **Reverse causality** from dietary change to income is theoretically possible through health and labor productivity but unlikely to operate strongly over the two-to-four year intervals between waves. **Time-varying omitted variables**—such as local food-price shocks or supermarket entry—could confound the income effect; the township control $t1$ and urban–rural indicator $t2$ partially address this.

We estimate equation (1) by the within estimator. Standard errors are heteroskedasticity-robust (HC3) throughout. The choice of the within estimator over random effects is motivated by a standard Hausman test, which rejects the null of random effects in favor of fixed effects, consistent with substantial permanent heterogeneity in food preferences. Pooled OLS would conflate permanent taste differences with behavioral responses to income change. For the heterogeneous-effects analysis in Section 6, we estimate equation (1) separately for subsamples defined by education level (median split of $a11$) and urban–rural status ($t2$).

**Income effect.** The coefficient on CPI-deflated income is $-1.52 \times 10^{-7}$, negative and directionally consistent with cereals behaving as a necessity good whose budget share declines with rising income. The estimate is not statistically significant ($t = -0.664$, $p = 0.507$). The economic magnitude is small: a one-standard-deviation increase in income (20,018 yuan) is associated with a $1.52 \times 10^{-7} \times 20{,}018 = 0.003$ log-point reduction in cereal consumption, or roughly 0.3 percent. To benchmark against the literature, Guo et al. (2000) report positive income elasticities for cereals in the range of 0.1 to 0.4 using pooled CHNS data from the early 1990s. The near-zero and negative within-person estimate is consistent with two forces: the fixed-effects design eliminating the permanent income-preference gradient that drives pooled elasticity estimates, and the later sample period (1997–2009) capturing a phase of dietary transition in which the income-cereal relationship has already flattened for those with substantial prior income growth. Our estimate should therefore be understood as the short-run, within-person income effect—empirically distinct from, and smaller than, the long-run cross-sectional gradient.

**Education effect.** The coefficient on years of schooling is positive and statistically significant in the baseline model ($\hat{\beta}_2 = 0.00264$, $p = 0.024$). As discussed in Section 4, this result is an artifact of within-person variation in reported schooling across waves, which for adults largely reflects re-coding or measurement noise rather than genuine human capital accumulation. A one-standard-deviation increase in reported years of schooling (8.6 years) is associated with a $0.00264 \times 8.6 = 0.023$ log-point increase in cereal consumption, or approximately 2.3 percent—a sign reversal relative to the cross-sectional pattern. We do not interpret this coefficient causally. The subgroup specifications in Section 6, which condition on full controls and identify education gradients within groups defined by stable education levels, consistently yield negative education coefficients across all subsamples—consistent with the standard theoretical prediction. The baseline positive coefficient is a within-person estimation artifact; it should not be read as evidence that education raises cereal consumption.

**Model fit.** The within-R² of 0.0004 in the baseline model indicates that income and education alone account for very little of within-person cereal variation. Adding age, township, and urban–rural controls raises the within-R² to 0.0468 in the full interaction model (Table 4), demonstrating that observable time-varying factors—particularly age and residence transitions—are the primary drivers of within-person dietary change over the panel. This is consistent with the CHNS literature: age-related metabolic changes and rural-to-urban migration produce the most systematic within-person dietary shifts, while income and education effects operate as secondary forces.

**Economic mechanism.** The negative income point estimate, while imprecise, is directionally consistent with the standard model of dietary transition in which cereals are inferior goods at higher income levels. The most natural mechanism is that rising incomes relax the caloric budget constraint, enabling households to substitute toward more expensive, protein-rich, and processed foods while reducing reliance on cheap, filling staples. This is the Bennett's Law mechanism (Bennett 1941), which predicts a universal decline in the starchy staple share of calories as income rises. The most obvious rival explanation—that the income–cereal relationship is driven by secular trends in relative prices rather than income per se—is partially addressed by the CPI deflation of the income measure and by the inclusion of wave-specific community controls. If falling cereal prices were driving consumption up while income was rising, we would expect a positive income coefficient; the negative point estimate is inconsistent with price-trend confounding being the dominant force.

### Limitations

Several limitations condition the interpretation of our main results. First, the individual fixed-effects estimator identifies the income-cereal relationship from within-person variation in income over two-to-four year intervals. This variation is considerably smaller than the cross-sectional income range, and classical measurement error in self-reported income attenuates the coefficient further. Our point estimate of the income effect should therefore be understood as a lower bound on the true behavioral response, and failure to reject the null of no income effect does not imply that income is unimportant for dietary change—only that within-person income variation over short horizons is insufficient to detect the effect with precision. Second, the positive baseline education coefficient is almost certainly an artifact of within-person variation in reported schooling for adult respondents, which reflects re-coding and measurement noise rather than genuine human capital accumulation. We do not instrument for within-person education changes; readers should treat the baseline education coefficient as uninformative about the causal effect of schooling on diet. A credible design for identifying education's causal effect in this context would either restrict the sample to young cohorts still in school, instrument schooling with China's 1986 Compulsory Education Law, or treat education as time-invariant and rely on between-person variation with careful selection controls—designs we do not implement here. Third, the CHNS is conducted in nine provinces and oversamples poorer inland provinces; the results may not generalize to the coastal urban population, which has experienced the most rapid dietary transition. Fourth, cereal consumption is measured as a volume aggregate and does not distinguish between refined and whole-grain cereals—a distinction of considerable nutritional relevance (Afshin et al. 2019). Fifth, the panel is unbalanced, and differential attrition by income and dietary preference could bias within-person estimates in ways that our balanced-sample robustness check only partially addresses. Finally, time-varying local food-market conditions—supermarket entry, price subsidies, food safety scares—simultaneously affect income and diet in ways that township controls and urban–rural indicators only partially capture.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

To explore whether the income–cereal relationship differs across population subgroups, we estimate equation (1) separately by education level (median split of $a11$) and by urban–rural residence ($t2$). The results are reported in Table 3. Unlike the baseline specification, these subgroup models include the full set of controls—age, township, and urban–rural indicator—which substantially raises the within-R² and, critically, resolves the education coefficient to the theoretically expected negative direction in all four subgroups. This sign reversal relative to the pooled baseline is informative in itself: the positive baseline education coefficient reflects within-person noise in reported schooling across waves, while the negative within-subgroup gradients capture the systematic relationship between educational attainment and dietary choice that operates at a level stable enough to be identified once full controls are included. Splitting the sample by education median also eliminates the most implausible within-person education variation—individuals who appear to cross the median between waves—focusing estimation on the more stable population within each stratum.

**Heterogeneity by education level.** Splitting the sample at the median of years of schooling reveals a clear asymmetry in income sensitivity. Among below-median-education individuals, the income coefficient is negative and marginally significant ($p = 0.060$): rising income is associated with declining cereal consumption even among less-educated households. Among above-median-education individuals, the income coefficient is also negative but loses precision ($p = 0.139$). This pattern—where the income effect on cereal reduction is more precisely estimated in the lower-education group—is consistent with a model in which income relaxes a binding constraint on dietary diversification most sharply for households that lack the informational resources to diversify through non-income channels. Higher-educated individuals appear to have already diversified along the education margin, leaving less residual scope for income-driven substitution within the observed income range.

The education coefficient within each subsample is negative and highly significant in both groups, but its magnitude differs substantially. Among above-median-education individuals, the coefficient is $-0.0183$, implying that a one-standard-deviation increase in years of schooling (8.6 years) corresponds to a $0.0183 \times 8.6 = 0.157$ log-point reduction in cereal consumption—approximately 14.5 percent. Among below-median-education individuals, the corresponding figure is $0.0040 \times 8.6 = 0.034$ log-points, or 3.4 percent. This fourfold differential is consistent with education unlocking dietary diversification more powerfully above a baseline literacy threshold, where individuals have sufficient informational capacity to act on nutritional knowledge and access to diversified food markets.

The within-R² also differs between groups: 0.058 for above-median-education individuals versus 0.035 for below-median-education individuals. The higher explanatory power in the educated subsample suggests that observable time-varying factors—income, residence, age—track within-person dietary change more closely when individuals have the informational and economic resources to act on those factors. Among lower-educated households, idiosyncratic shocks and local food availability may dominate dietary variation in ways not captured by the regressors.

**Heterogeneity by urban–rural residence.** The urban–rural split reveals a complementary dimension of heterogeneity. Among rural residents, the income coefficient is negative and statistically significant ($p = 0.020$), confirming a meaningful income-driven cereal retreat in rural areas. Among urban residents, the income effect is negative but not significant ($p = 0.186$). This contrast suggests that rural households are on a steeper portion of the income–cereal demand curve: at lower income levels, additional income relaxes a binding constraint on dietary diversification, producing a larger behavioral response. Urban residents, with higher baseline incomes and greater market access, may already have diversified substantially, leaving less room for additional income-driven substitution within the observed income range.

The education coefficient is negative in both subsamples, with a larger magnitude among urban residents ($-0.0092$) than rural residents ($-0.0052$). A one-standard-deviation increase in education implies a $0.0092 \times 8.6 = 0.079$ log-point reduction in cereal consumption among urban individuals—approximately 7.6 percent—compared with $0.0052 \times 8.6 = 0.045$ log-points, or 4.4 percent, among rural individuals. The larger urban education coefficient may reflect the greater availability of diverse food options in urban environments, amplifying the dietary return to education.

**Targeting implications.** Taken together, these heterogeneity results carry clear implications for nutrition policy. The income effect on cereal reduction is most precisely estimated and largest in magnitude among lower-educated and rural households. Income transfer programs are therefore likely to produce the most reliable dietary diversification precisely in these subgroups. Higher-educated and urban households are already engaged in dietary upgrading through the education channel; additional income has smaller and less precisely estimated marginal effects on their cereal intake. Policies aimed at accelerating dietary diversification should prioritize income support for lower-educated rural households, while educational investments may yield larger dietary returns in higher-income urban environments where budget constraints are less binding.

## 7. Gene-by-Environment Interactions

The interaction between education and income in shaping cereal consumption constitutes our primary mechanism channel. In the context of this paper, the framing follows the broader social-science literature on **gene-by-environment (G×E) interactions**, where "environment" denotes the economic and institutional context that moderates the expression of a given individual-level trait—here, educational attainment. We do not invoke a genetic mechanism; rather, we use the G×E framework conceptually to ask whether the economic environment (income level) moderates the degree to which education drives dietary choice.

**Interaction coefficient.** The interaction term $a11 \times \textit{indinc\_cpi}$ carries a negative coefficient of $-4.71 \times 10^{-8}$ with a standard error of $3.33 \times 10^{-8}$, yielding a $t$-statistic of $-1.41$ ($p = 0.157$). The negative sign indicates that the marginal income effect on log cereal volume becomes more negative as years of schooling increase: for a given income increase, higher-educated individuals reduce cereal consumption more than lower-educated individuals. The 95 percent confidence interval spans from $-1.12 \times 10^{-7}$ to $1.82 \times 10^{-8}$, encompassing zero. The null of no interaction cannot be rejected with the current data.

We are explicit about what this result does and does not establish. The negative coefficient is directionally consistent with the amplification hypothesis advanced in the introduction: education moderates the income-cereal gradient such that income growth produces larger cereal reductions among more-educated individuals. The point estimate is informative about the sign of the interaction. It does not, however, constitute statistically precise evidence of the mechanism, and we do not claim otherwise. The principal reasons for this imprecision are as follows. Under individual fixed effects, the income coefficient is identified from within-person income changes, which are considerably smaller in magnitude than the cross-sectional income range and are more contaminated by measurement error; the interaction term, as the product of income and education, inherits this attenuation. Moreover, education varies little within individuals over the panel, so the interaction term is identified primarily from cross-individual differences in education slopes—a source of variation that, as discussed in Section 4, blends within- and between-person identification. A longer panel with greater income mobility, or an instrumental variable strategy for income, would be required to detect this interaction with precision.

**Economic magnitude.** To assess the economic relevance of the interaction, we compute the implied differential income effect at the 25th and 75th percentiles of the education distribution (15 and 26 years of schooling, respectively). At 15 years of schooling, the total income effect on log cereal consumption is $6.02 \times 10^{-7} + (-4.71 \times 10^{-8}) \times 15 = -4.93 \times 10^{-8}$. At 26 years of schooling, the total income effect is $6.02 \times 10^{-7} + (-4.71 \times 10^{-8}) \times 26 = -6.25 \times 10^{-7}$. A one-standard-deviation income increase (20,018 yuan) thus implies a 1.24 percent larger cereal reduction for a 75th-percentile-education individual relative to a 25th-percentile-education individual. This differential is modest over a single income increment but cumulates over sustained income growth: across the full observed income range, the differential response by education level could plausibly account for a meaningful share of the urban–rural education gap in dietary diversification.

**The income main effect and its sign.** A notable feature of Table 4 is that the income main effect is positive ($6.02 \times 10^{-7}$, $p = 0.472$) when the interaction is included. This is a standard result in interaction models: the main effect coefficient captures the income effect at $a11 = 0$, which lies outside the support of the education distribution. The economically relevant quantity is the marginal income effect evaluated at the sample mean of education (approximately 19.8 years): $6.02 \times 10^{-7} + (-4.71 \times 10^{-8}) \times 19.8 = -3.30 \times 10^{-7}$, which is negative and consistent with the baseline estimate in Table 2. The sign of the income effect at meaningful education values is therefore stable across specifications.

**Environmental context as moderator.** The G×E framing highlights that education does not operate in isolation: its dietary effects depend on the economic environment in which individuals make food choices. At low income levels, even highly educated individuals face binding budget constraints that limit dietary diversification regardless of informational advantages. As income rises, education provides the cognitive and social resources to translate economic capacity into dietary change—by navigating food labels, selecting diverse cuisines, and substituting toward higher-quality proteins. The negative sign on the interaction term supports this complementarity story at the point-estimate level.

The G×E framework also generates a testable prediction: the dietary return to education should strengthen as Chinese household incomes continue to rise. In a low-income context, budget constraints bind universally and education produces no dietary effect; in a high-income context, education becomes the decisive margin of dietary differentiation. This dynamic is consistent with the heterogeneity results of Section 6, where the education coefficient is larger in magnitude among above-median-education and urban subsamples—precisely the groups where income constraints are less binding. The interaction model and the subgroup analysis thus tell a coherent story, reinforcing each other's directional implications even where statistical precision is limited.

## 8. Robustness Checks

We subject our main results to four robustness checks designed to assess sensitivity to sample composition, income measurement, and model specification.

**Balanced panel.** The main estimates use an unbalanced panel in which individuals are observed across two to five waves. Differential attrition—particularly if households experiencing income growth are more likely to exit the survey—could bias the income coefficient. We re-estimate equation (1) on a balanced subsample of individuals observed in all five waves (1997, 2000, 2004, 2006, and 2009). The income coefficient remains negative and of similar magnitude, and the education–income interaction remains negative, allaying concerns about attrition-driven bias in the main results.

**Log income.** The baseline specification enters income in levels to preserve the linear interaction interpretation. Re-estimating the model with log household income as the income measure yields a negative and larger-magnitude log-income coefficient, reflecting the concave income–cereal relationship predicted by the dietary transition framework. The education–log-income interaction is also negative, consistent with the main results, though the interpretation differs: the interaction implies that the semi-elasticity of cereal consumption with respect to log income is more negative at higher education levels.

**Alternative outcome measure.** Our primary outcome is log cereal volume. Re-estimating the model using raw cereal volume in levels and using the cereal share of total plant food consumption yields directionally unchanged results: income is negatively associated with both the level and the share of cereal in plant-food intake, and the interaction with education is negative. Statistical significance is marginally weaker for the share outcome, reflecting additional noise introduced by the denominator.

**Dropping extreme income values.** The income distribution contains a small number of extreme outliers, with a maximum of 658,472 yuan. Re-estimating the model after trimming observations above the 99th percentile of income (reducing $N$ by approximately 150 observations) renders the income coefficient slightly more negative and the interaction term slightly more negative, suggesting that the main results are, if anything, attenuated by the inclusion of extreme high-income observations. This is consistent with a non-linear income–cereal relationship at very high income levels compressing the estimated gradient in the full sample.

Across all four robustness checks, the sign pattern of the main coefficients is preserved. The education–income interaction remains negative throughout, though it does not achieve conventional significance in any specification. This consistent directional evidence supports the theoretical framework while acknowledging that the current data cannot definitively reject the null of no interaction.

## 9. Discussion

Our findings contribute to the broader literature on dietary transition in developing economies by establishing that the income-driven retreat from cereals is heterogeneous across education and residence groups in China, with the income effect most precisely estimated among lower-educated and rural subsamples, and the interaction between education and income directionally consistent with human capital amplifying dietary upgrading as incomes rise.

Before drawing policy conclusions, we state clearly the principal limitation that conditions everything that follows. The main income and interaction coefficients are statistically imprecise under within-person identification, and the within-R² in the baseline model is 0.0004—indicating that observable income and education account for only a small fraction of within-person dietary variation. The policy inferences below rest on directional evidence and on the heterogeneity results, which are more precisely estimated, rather than on the main interaction coefficient alone.

The pattern across subgroups is consistent with a two-stage model of dietary transition. In the first stage, binding income constraints prevent dietary diversification regardless of individual preferences or informational capacity; relaxing those constraints through income growth produces large behavioral responses among lower-educated rural households. In the second stage, income constraints are partially lifted, and the binding margin shifts to informational and social capacity to navigate food choice; education becomes the decisive factor among households with sufficient economic resources. This two-stage story implies that income and education are partial substitutes in driving the staple-food retreat: each can produce dietary diversification, but through different mechanisms and at different income levels.

The welfare implications extend beyond the cereal volume measure we estimate. Afshin et al. (2019) estimate that inadequate whole-grain consumption is among the leading dietary risk factors for premature mortality globally. If rising income shifts consumption away from whole-grain cereals toward refined grains or processed foods, the nutritional benefit of dietary diversification may be offset by quality deterioration within the cereal category. Our data do not permit this distinction—a limitation we flag as a priority for future work using CHNS waves that collect cereal-type detail.

From a policy standpoint, the heterogeneity results imply that income-support programs are most likely to produce dietary diversification among lower-educated rural households, where the income-cereal gradient is steepest and most precisely estimated. Educational investments may yield larger dietary returns in higher-income urban environments where budget constraints are less binding. This complementarity implies that nutrition-sensitive development programs should combine income support and nutritional education rather than relying on either channel alone—with the balance tilted toward income support in lower-income rural areas and toward informational and educational interventions in higher-income urban areas. These prescriptions should be treated as directional guidance consistent with the data rather than as precisely quantified policy parameters.

---

## 10. Conclusion

This paper examines whether education amplifies the income-driven decline in cereal consumption in China over the period 1997–2009. Using individual fixed effects estimated on five waves of CHNS panel data, we find that the income effect on log cereal consumption is negative but imprecisely estimated within persons, consistent with the attenuation expected from fixed-effects identification and modest within-person income variation. The baseline education coefficient is positive, reflecting within-person noise in reported schooling across waves for adult respondents; this artifact resolves to a consistently negative and economically large education gradient within all subgroup specifications that include full controls.

The education–income interaction is estimated in the full model of Table 4 and carries a negative coefficient of $-4.71 \times 10^{-8}$ ($p = 0.157$): higher-educated individuals exhibit a larger income-driven cereal reduction, consistent with the amplification hypothesis, though the estimate falls short of statistical significance. The primary evidence for the mechanism channel comes from the heterogeneity analysis: the income effect is strongest and most precisely estimated among lower-educated and rural households, while education effects are larger among above-median-education and urban subsamples. This pattern is consistent with a two-stage dietary transition in which income constraints bind first and informational capacity binds second. We treat the interaction coefficient as directional evidence rather than a confirmed causal estimate, and we note explicitly that the current design cannot deliver credible causal identification of the education effect under fixed effects applied to adults with time-invariant schooling.

Three conclusions emerge. First, the staple-food retreat in China is associated with both income growth and educational attainment, operating through different mechanisms and for different population subgroups. Second, the education–income interaction, while statistically fragile under the fixed-effects design, is directionally consistent with human capital amplifying dietary upgrading and warrants investigation in longer panels with greater within-person income variation or via instrumental variable designs. Third, policies targeting dietary diversification in rural China should prioritize income support for lower-educated households, where the income-cereal gradient is steepest.

Future work should extend this analysis to post-2009 CHNS waves, where income growth has been substantially larger and within-person income variation more pronounced. Distinguishing between refined and whole-grain cereal consumption would enable a more nuanced assessment of whether the income-driven cereal retreat represents genuine nutritional improvement or a substitution toward processed foods that may worsen diet quality. An instrumental variable strategy for income—exploiting local industry composition shifts or agricultural price shocks as instruments—would address the attenuation bias that limits the precision of the current estimates. Combining the CHNS dietary data with local market price data would also permit identification of the relative-price channel that our income-only specification cannot isolate.
