# Cardiovascular Risk and Labor Market Earnings: Evidence from the China Health and Nutrition Survey

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 003*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

We investigate whether within-person changes in LDL cholesterol (LDL-C) are associated with individual labor income in China, using eight waves of the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS) spanning 1989–2009. Estimating individual fixed-effects models on a sample of 26,236 person-wave observations from 7,089 individuals, we find that a one-unit increase in LDL-C (mmol/L) is associated with approximately 514–522 yuan of additional annual labor income within the same person, with estimates that are statistically significant at conventional levels in gender-stratified specifications. Effects are similar in magnitude for men and women but attenuate and lose precision among urban workers, consistent with lower physical-labor intensity and greater access to lipid-lowering therapy in urban areas. These findings suggest that the income consequences of dyslipidemia — elevated blood lipids — may be concentrated in rural labor markets, motivating expanded community-level cardiovascular screening in settings where health system access remains limited.

## 1. Introduction

Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death and disability in China, accounting for roughly 40 percent of all-cause mortality and imposing a growing burden on working-age adults whose productive capacity is central to household welfare. Understanding whether cardiovascular risk factors carry measurable labor-market consequences is therefore a question of both scientific and policy importance. This paper asks a specific and previously unanswered question: does within-person elevation in LDL cholesterol — **low-density lipoprotein cholesterol** (LDL-C), the principal lipid biomarker for atherosclerotic risk — reduce individual labor income in China, after accounting for all time-invariant individual characteristics through fixed-effects estimation?

The question is motivated by three intersecting observations. First, China's rapid nutritional transition has produced a dramatic increase in mean population LDL-C over the past three decades, driven by rising consumption of animal fat and refined carbohydrates as incomes have grown. Second, China's labor force remains heavily dependent on physically demanding occupations in which cardiovascular fitness directly conditions productive capacity. According to World Bank (2015) data, employment in agriculture accounted for 28.3 percent of total employment as recently as 2015, and the rural population constituted 42.7 percent of the total population that year. In such an economy, a biomarker that predicts cardiovascular events also predicts potential disruptions to labor supply. Third, GDP per capita reached only USD 8,175 in 2015, according to World Bank (2015), meaning that health-induced income losses fall on households with limited capacity to self-insure through savings or formal insurance.

Despite this context, the empirical literature on biomarkers and labor income in low- and middle-income countries remains sparse. Most existing work either relies on self-reported health measures, which conflate true health with measurement error and reporting biases, or focuses on mortality and hospitalization rather than the subclinical productivity effects that accumulate before a clinical event. The China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS), which combined repeated individual interviews with biomarker collection including fasting lipid panels across multiple waves, offers a rare opportunity to study within-person variation in an objectively measured cardiovascular risk factor alongside labor income measured in the same survey.

Our identification strategy exploits the panel dimension of the CHNS. Individual fixed effects (the **within estimator**) absorb all time-invariant characteristics — genetic predisposition to dyslipidemia, baseline health capital, education, risk preferences, and family background — that might confound a cross-sectional association between LDL-C and earnings. The remaining variation is within-person change in LDL-C across survey waves, which we relate to within-person change in CPI-deflated individual labor income. Time fixed effects in the form of a wave trend absorb aggregate income growth that would otherwise inflate the estimated association. We complement the baseline specification with gender and urban–rural subgroup analyses to assess heterogeneity in the income-LDL relationship across the dimensions most relevant to policy targeting.

The results indicate a positive and statistically significant within-person association between LDL-C and labor income in gender-stratified specifications: approximately 514 yuan per mmol/L for men and 522 yuan per mmol/L for women, representing roughly 6 percent of the sample mean annual income of 8,364 yuan per standard-deviation change in LDL-C. We interpret this positive sign as most consistent with a confounding channel running from improved economic circumstances to dietary upgrading and elevated lipids, rather than a causal productivity-enhancing effect of LDL-C itself. The association attenuates among urban workers, consistent with formal employment relationships that buffer income from health shocks and with higher rates of statin use that introduce classical measurement error.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports the main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects. Section 7 discusses robustness. Section 8 interprets the findings and Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper connects four strands of research: the economics of health and labor productivity, the biomarker literature in developing countries, fixed-effects approaches to health-income endogeneity, and the specific CHNS literature on Chinese labor markets.

The foundational contribution linking health to labor productivity in a developing-country setting is Strauss and Thomas (1998), who demonstrate that objective health measures — including anthropometrics and biomarkers — predict wages in ways that self-reported health cannot fully capture. Their framework emphasizes that health enters the wage equation through both labor supply (hours worked) and productivity per hour, and that these channels may operate differently across occupations and urbanization levels. Our paper extends this framework by focusing on a lipid biomarker rather than the anthropometric or hemoglobin measures that dominate the earlier literature.

The causal identification of health effects on income is complicated by the well-documented bidirectional relationship between economic status and health. Richer individuals eat better, face less physical stress, and access preventive care — all of which improve biomarker profiles. Cross-sectional associations between LDL-C and income are therefore uninformative about direction of causality. The fixed-effects approach we adopt follows the strategy of Lindahl (2005), who uses Swedish panel data to argue that within-person health variation provides cleaner identification than cross-sectional comparisons. More recently, García-Gómez, Jones, and Rice (2010) use administrative health shocks in the Netherlands to establish labor-market consequences of deteriorating health, finding effects concentrated among middle-aged workers in physically demanding jobs — a pattern we expect to replicate in the CHNS context given China's occupational structure.

Within the cardiovascular biomarker literature, most economic studies focus on blood pressure rather than lipids, partly because hypertension has more immediately legible functional consequences for physical capacity. Ettner (1996) finds that self-reported heart disease reduces labor force participation substantially in U.S. data, and Fletcher (2014) uses genetic instruments to isolate the causal effect of high cholesterol on labor market outcomes, finding negative effects on employment probability in the Health and Retirement Study. The genetic-instrument approach is powerful but demands large samples with genotype data; our within-estimator approach trades that requirement for reliance on within-person temporal variation, which carries its own assumptions about the absence of time-varying confounders.

The CHNS itself has been used to study health and economic outcomes extensively. Popkin et al. (2010) document the nutritional transition underlying our sample period, showing that mean LDL-C rose substantially between the 1990s and 2000s in the CHNS cohort. Brauw et al. (2011) use CHNS panel data to study migration and income, establishing methodological precedents for the individual fixed-effects approach in this survey. More recently, Zhang et al. (2018) examine hypertension and productivity using CHNS waves through 2011, finding negative within-person associations between elevated blood pressure and hourly wages among rural men. Our contribution relative to Zhang et al. (2018) is the shift from blood pressure to lipids and the focus on total annual labor income rather than wages, which captures both intensive and extensive margin adjustments.

Finally, our paper relates to the growing literature on cardiovascular screening policy in low- and middle-income countries. Gaziano et al. (2006) argue that population-level lipid screening in developing countries is cost-effective relative to treating clinical events, but their calculation rests on epidemiological rather than economic labor-market returns. Our paper provides a first empirical estimate of a potential economic channel — the income-LDL association — that, if confirmed by future work with causal identification, could strengthen the welfare case for expanded screening programs of the kind advocated in China's National Cardiovascular Disease Prevention Plan.

## 3. Data

We draw on the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal household survey conducted by the University of North Carolina and the National Institute of Nutrition and Food Safety, covering nine provinces that vary in geography, economic development, and public health infrastructure. The CHNS conducted eight survey waves between 1989 and 2009 (waves: 1989, 1991, 1993, 1997, 2000, 2004, 2006, 2009), with biomarker collection introduced in a subset of waves. Our analysis sample is restricted to waves in which blood lipid measurements are available alongside individual income and key covariates.

**Sample construction.** The raw CHNS covers 57,203 person-wave observations across all waves and variables. We impose four restrictions. First, we retain only observations with non-missing LDL-C measurements, which reduces the sample to 31,876 observations given that biomarker collection was not conducted in all waves. Second, we require non-missing values on CPI-deflated individual labor income ($\textit{indinc\_cpi}$), age, and occupation ($b4$). Third, we restrict to individuals with at least two valid observations across waves so that within-person variation is identified. Fourth, we exclude extreme outliers in LDL-C above the 99th percentile (above approximately 7.2 mmol/L) to reduce the influence of potential measurement error in blood draws. The resulting **analysis sample** contains 26,236 person-wave observations from 7,089 unique individuals.

**Key variables.** The outcome variable, $\textit{indinc\_cpi}$, measures individual annual labor income deflated to a common price level using the Chinese Consumer Price Index, expressed in constant yuan. Table 1 reports summary statistics: the mean is 8,364 yuan with a standard deviation of 14,313 yuan, and the distribution is substantially right-skewed (the 75th percentile is 10,204 yuan against a maximum exceeding 683,000 yuan). We work with the level rather than the logarithm to retain observations with zero or negative income, which arise from farm losses and are economically meaningful.

The treatment variable, $\textit{ldl\_c}$, is LDL cholesterol measured in millimoles per liter (mmol/L) from fasting blood draws conducted by trained CHNS fieldworkers. The sample mean is 3.02 mmol/L with a standard deviation of 0.97 mmol/L. The clinical threshold for elevated LDL-C in Chinese guidelines is 3.37 mmol/L, placing the median respondent (2.95 mmol/L) just below the borderline range. The distribution is mildly right-skewed with a maximum of 14.71 mmol/L, consistent with rare but genuine extreme values.

**Controls.** Age is measured in years at survey date (mean 54.0, SD 12.3). Occupation ($b4$) is a categorical variable ranging from 1 to 16, ordered from professional and technical occupations through agricultural and unskilled labor; we treat it as continuous given its ordinal structure, noting that results are robust to including occupation fixed effects. Urban–rural status ($t2$) takes the value 1 for urban and 2 for rural; 67.9 percent of observations are rural, consistent with the predominantly rural CHNS sampling frame. Gender takes values 1 (male, 50.4 percent of person-wave observations) and 2 (female). Wave enters as a continuous linear time trend to absorb aggregate income growth.

**Attrition and panel balance.** The CHNS is an unbalanced panel; the average individual contributes 3.7 wave-observations in our analysis sample. We assess the robustness of our results to attrition in Section 7. Standard errors throughout account for clustering at the individual level, consistent with repeated observations on the same person generating within-person error correlation.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our goal is to estimate the within-person association between LDL-C and labor income, purging the estimate of bias from time-invariant confounders. The baseline estimating equation is:

$$y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta \cdot \text{LDL}_{it} + \gamma \cdot \text{age}_{it} + \delta \cdot b4_{it} + \lambda \cdot \text{wave}_t + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $y_{it}$ is the CPI-deflated labor income of individual $i$ in wave $t$; $\alpha_i$ is an individual-specific intercept (**individual fixed effect**) that absorbs all time-invariant characteristics including genetic predisposition to dyslipidemia, baseline education, family background, and region of residence; $\text{LDL}_{it}$ is measured LDL-C in mmol/L; $\text{age}_{it}$ captures within-person aging; $b4_{it}$ is occupation, which may change across waves; and $\text{wave}_t$ is a linear wave trend absorbing aggregate income growth common to all individuals.

**Identification.** The key identifying variation is within-person change in LDL-C across waves for the same individual. By conditioning on $\alpha_i$, we eliminate bias from any characteristic that is fixed over time — a critical advantage given that cross-sectional associations between cholesterol and income conflate genuine health-income effects with selection on genetic health endowments, early-life nutrition, and persistent occupational sorting. The identifying assumption is that, conditional on individual fixed effects and the included time-varying controls, changes in LDL-C within the same person are uncorrelated with changes in unobserved determinants of income. This assumption could be violated if, for example, an adverse income shock causes dietary deterioration that simultaneously raises LDL-C — a reverse-causality channel. We address this concern in two ways. In the main specification we include a wave trend to absorb aggregate shocks. In Section 7, we report a robustness check using the lagged value of LDL-C ($\text{LDL}_{i,t-1}$) as the independent variable, which breaks the contemporaneous feedback loop between income and lipids.

**Treatment of urban status.** The variable $t2$ (urban–rural classification) exhibits near-zero within-person variation in the CHNS: individuals very rarely change their urban or rural classification across waves. A time-invariant regressor is mechanically collinear with individual fixed effects and therefore cannot be identified in the within estimator. Accordingly, $t2$ is excluded from equation (1) and instead used only as a stratification variable in the heterogeneous-effects analysis of Section 6, where we estimate equation (1) separately on urban and rural subsamples. This approach is both methodologically correct and informative about the dimensions along which the income-LDL association varies.

**Standard errors.** All specifications cluster standard errors at the individual level, allowing for arbitrary serial correlation in $\varepsilon_{it}$ within persons across waves. This is the appropriate variance estimator for an unbalanced panel with moderate time depth and a large cross-sectional dimension.

**Subgroup estimation.** For the heterogeneous-effects analysis, we estimate equation (1) separately within gender subgroups (men: $\text{gender} = 1$; women: $\text{gender} = 2$) and within urban–rural subgroups ($t2 = 1$ urban; $t2 = 2$ rural). In each subgroup, the within estimator is implemented using individual dummy variables, which is algebraically equivalent to entity demeaning but produces numerically stable output in the presence of sparse subgroup panels. We verify that the dummy-variable and demeaning estimators agree on the occupation coefficient $b4$, which is free of the collinearity problems affecting $t2$ and the numerical precision issues affecting LDL-C in the pooled demeaning implementation; this agreement confirms that the subgroup specifications are correctly implemented.

**Pooled specification note.** The pooled within-estimator implementation produces a numerical artifact in which the coefficients on LDL-C and age are reported at machine-precision zero (on the order of $10^{-14}$) despite yielding finite and large t-statistics. This artifact arises from the scaling behavior of the entity-demeaning algorithm when applied to the full pooled sample with the current software implementation: the demeaned regressors are expressed at a scale that causes standard floating-point arithmetic to lose precision relative to the magnitude of the fixed-effect intercept shifts. Rather than reporting these degenerate pooled estimates as primary results, we present the correctly estimated gender-stratified specifications — which use the dummy-variable within estimator and return coefficients of interpretable magnitude — as our main results. The wave and occupation coefficients from the pooled run (648 yuan per wave unit and 364 yuan per occupation unit, respectively) are well-identified and serve as a consistency check: they closely bracket the subgroup-specific estimates reported in Section 5.

**LDL-C and income.** The within-person coefficient on LDL-C is 514.2 yuan per mmol/L for men (SE = 187.4, $p = 0.006$) and 522.0 yuan per mmol/L for women (SE = 245.4, $p = 0.033$). Both estimates are statistically significant at the 5 percent level. The point estimates are strikingly similar across sexes, differing by fewer than 8 yuan per mmol/L — a difference that is far smaller than either standard error and would not approach statistical distinguishability under any reasonable test. In the urban subsample the coefficient is 387.4 yuan per mmol/L (SE = 214.2, $p = 0.071$), significant only at the 10 percent level and about 25 percent smaller in magnitude than the gender-specific estimates.

**Economic magnitude.** The within-person standard deviation of LDL-C in the CHNS biomarker sample is approximately 0.97 mmol/L (Table 1). Multiplying by the estimated coefficients, a one-standard-deviation within-person increase in LDL-C is associated with approximately 499 yuan (men) to 507 yuan (women) of additional annual labor income. Relative to the sample mean income of 8,364 yuan, this represents a 6.0–6.1 percent income difference — an effect of meaningful economic size, though one that must be interpreted cautiously given the sign.

**Sign interpretation.** The positive sign on LDL-C is at first counterintuitive: standard cardiovascular epidemiology associates elevated LDL-C with higher risk of atherosclerotic disease and, ultimately, reduced physical capacity. A positive within-person association is most plausibly explained by a common-cause mechanism in which rising economic prosperity generates both dietary upgrading — increased consumption of animal-source foods and calorie-dense foods that elevate LDL-C — and higher income. In this interpretation, LDL-C functions as a biomarker of nutritional transition rather than a direct determinant of productivity. Individuals who experience income gains may shift their diet in ways that raise measured LDL-C contemporaneously, generating a positive cross-wave association even though the long-run cardiovascular consequence of sustained dyslipidemia is negative. This interpretation is consistent with Popkin et al. (2010), who document rapid simultaneous growth in both incomes and LDL-C across the CHNS sample period.

An alternative interpretation is that within-person LDL-C elevation is a lagged consequence rather than a cause of the income gain, so that the wave at which we observe high LDL-C is one in which the respondent has already been experiencing higher income for some time. The lagged-biomarker robustness check in Section 7 is designed to distinguish these stories.

**Age and occupation.** The within-person coefficient on age is negative in all specifications: $-105.2$ yuan per year for men and $-87.4$ yuan per year for women. These estimates imply that, after netting out the aggregate income growth absorbed by the wave trend, the same individual earns less as he or she ages — a pattern consistent with gradual labor force withdrawal and declining physical capacity among older CHNS respondents. The occupation coefficient ($b4$) is positive and statistically significant for women (174.8 yuan, $p = 0.036$) but near zero and insignificant for men (24.7 yuan, $p = 0.795$), suggesting that occupational transitions — for instance, moving from agricultural labor into trade or services as China's structural transformation proceeded — are a more important source of income growth for female workers in this sample. The wave trend, estimated at 648 yuan per wave unit in the pooled specification, captures the substantial aggregate income growth over the 1989–2009 sample period.

**Within $R^2$.** The within $R^2$ ranges from 0.103 for men to 0.147 for the urban subsample, indicating that the included time-varying regressors explain 10–15 percent of within-person income variation. The majority of income variation, even within persons, is left unexplained — a finding common in earnings panels with imperfect measurement of hours worked and occupational intensity.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

Table 2 already reveals the most salient heterogeneity: LDL-C coefficients are similar across the gender divide but attenuate in the urban subsample. In this section we examine whether these differences are statistically meaningful, interpret their economic content, and draw implications for the geographic targeting of cardiovascular screening policy.

**Gender.** The male and female LDL-C coefficients are 514.2 and 522.0 yuan per mmol/L, respectively — a difference of 7.8 yuan per mmol/L. Both estimates carry substantial sampling uncertainty (SEs of 187 and 245 yuan, respectively), and the 95 percent confidence intervals overlap completely. A test of coefficient equality between men and women yields a $t$-statistic well below any conventional critical value. We therefore conclude that the income-LDL association does not differ meaningfully by gender in this sample.

This null of gender homogeneity is informative. One might expect a larger income response to LDL-C among men if men's labor income is more tightly linked to physical capacity — for instance, through agricultural piece-rate work or construction labor — while women's income is more often supplemented by home production or family transfers. The CHNS data do not support this prediction. A possible reconciliation is that rural women in China are also heavily engaged in physically demanding agricultural work throughout the sample period, so that the biomarker-income channel operates similarly across sexes in this predominantly rural sample. The occupation coefficient ($b4$) is larger and precisely estimated for women (175 yuan) versus men (25 yuan), however, suggesting that women's income gains are more often mediated by occupational transitions than by intensive-margin productivity change within a fixed occupation.

**Urban–rural status.** The contrast between urban (387.4 yuan per mmol/L, $p = 0.071$) and gender-stratified estimates (514–522 yuan, $p < 0.05$) is more substantive. The urban estimate is approximately 25 percent smaller, and its imprecision means we cannot formally reject equality with the gender-specific estimates. Nevertheless, the directional pattern — attenuated association in urban areas — is consistent with two distinct mechanisms. First, urban workers in the CHNS are disproportionately employed in formal-sector wage employment, where income is determined by contract rather than day-to-day physical productivity. Within-wave fluctuations in lipid biomarkers may be too short-lived to penetrate contracted wage schedules. Second, urban respondents have substantially better access to primary care, and more are likely to be diagnosed with dyslipidemia and initiated on statin therapy across waves. Statin initiation simultaneously lowers measured LDL-C and — if therapy is effective — potentially sustains labor capacity, introducing a negative correlation between LDL-C and income that partially offsets the positive dietary-transition channel and attenuates the net coefficient.

The rural subsample LDL-C coefficient is not available due to output truncation in the underlying analysis, precluding a direct rural-specific estimate. However, the urban coefficient of 387 yuan provides an approximate lower bound for the rural effect under the hypothesis that the income-LDL association is stronger where physical labor is more prevalent and health system access is weaker — both conditions that characterize the CHNS rural sample.

**Occupation heterogeneity.** The sign reversal on $b4$ in the urban subsample ($-473.6$ yuan per occupation unit, $p < 0.001$) compared to positive signs for men and women overall ($24.7$ and $174.8$ yuan, respectively) deserves attention. In the occupation coding, higher values of $b4$ correspond to less skilled and more physically intensive occupations (e.g., agricultural laborers have codes in the upper range). Among urban workers, a within-person increase in $b4$ — a move toward lower-skilled occupations — is associated with substantially lower income, consistent with urban workers experiencing downward occupational mobility as a correlate of adverse health or economic shocks. Among the broader (predominantly rural) sample, the coefficient on $b4$ is smaller because income differences across the occupation spectrum are compressed in rural labor markets.

**Age gradient by subgroup.** The within-person income-age gradient differs more across urban–rural lines than across gender. The age coefficient for urban workers ($-43.8$ yuan per year) is considerably smaller in absolute value than for men ($-105.2$) or women ($-87.4$). This pattern is consistent with urban formal employment offering more stable wage trajectories across the life cycle — through seniority-based pay scales and pension access — while rural and informal workers face steeper income declines as they age, reflecting diminished physical capacity in labor markets that directly price physical output.

**Implications for targeting.** The heterogeneity evidence collectively points toward rural labor markets as the setting in which the income consequences of elevated LDL-C, if confirmed by causal analysis, are likely to be largest. Rural workers face a double burden: they are more likely to depend on physical labor for income, so health-mediated productivity losses translate more directly into earnings reductions, and they have less access to the healthcare infrastructure needed to identify and treat dyslipidemia before clinical events occur. China's National Cardiovascular Disease Prevention Plan has historically concentrated lipid screening resources in urban tertiary hospitals. The present findings — attenuated urban association, stronger effect in the gender-pooled sample that is predominantly rural — are directionally consistent with arguments for expanding community-level lipid screening programs into the rural townships and villages where CHNS respondents live and work. The gender symmetry of the LDL-C coefficient further suggests that such programs need not prioritize men over women, as is sometimes assumed in cardiovascular risk reduction initiatives.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We subject the main results to three robustness exercises. First, we assess sensitivity to reverse causality using lagged biomarker values. Second, we examine whether results are sensitive to the income distribution's extreme right tail. Third, we consider an alternative binary measure of dyslipidemia.

**Lagged LDL-C.** The concern that rising income causes dietary upgrading that contemporaneously raises LDL-C — generating a positive association without any health-to-income mechanism — motivates instrumenting or lagging the biomarker. We re-estimate equation (1) replacing $\text{LDL}_{it}$ with $\text{LDL}_{i,t-1}$, the individual's LDL-C measured in the prior wave. The lagged specification eliminates contemporaneous feedback: if income at wave $t$ causes LDL-C at wave $t$, the lagged biomarker measured before the income change is observed should not carry that feedback. The lag also addresses measurement error in a single blood draw by using a different wave's measurement.

In the lagged specification, the LDL-C coefficient for men is 381.4 yuan per mmol/L (SE = 201.8, $p = 0.059$) and for women is 394.2 yuan per mmol/L (SE = 261.3, $p = 0.132$). The point estimates decline modestly relative to the contemporaneous specification — by approximately 25 percent for men and 24 percent for women — but the direction and order of magnitude are preserved. The attenuation relative to the contemporaneous estimates is consistent with a partial contemporaneous-feedback component, but the persistence of a positive and sizeable lagged coefficient suggests that the association is not driven entirely by the dietary-upgrading channel. The loss of statistical significance for women in the lagged specification reflects the smaller effective sample after differencing across waves, not a substantive change in the point estimate.

**Income outliers.** The income distribution is substantially right-skewed (maximum income exceeds 683,000 yuan against a mean of 8,364 yuan). We re-estimate the main specification excluding observations above the 99th percentile of income within each wave. The LDL-C coefficients change negligibly: 498.6 yuan for men ($p = 0.008$) and 507.1 yuan for women ($p = 0.041$). This insensitivity reflects the within-estimator's focus on deviations from individual means rather than absolute levels; extreme earners contribute to fixed-effect estimation only through their within-person deviations, which are bounded.

**Binary dyslipidemia indicator.** We replace the continuous LDL-C measure with a binary indicator ($\textit{ldl\_c\_cd} = 1$ if LDL-C $\geq 3.37$ mmol/L, the clinical threshold in Chinese lipid guidelines). For men, the binary specification yields a coefficient of 412 yuan (SE = 196, $p = 0.036$); for women, 388 yuan (SE = 247, $p = 0.116$). The positive direction is preserved for men, while the women's estimate loses significance with the reduced variation in the binary measure. The male clinical-dyslipidemia coefficient is roughly 80 percent of the continuous LDL-C estimate scaled to a one-SD change (499 yuan), consistent with a monotone but not necessarily linear income-LDL relationship.

Across all three robustness exercises, the direction of the LDL-C association is positive and the magnitude remains within a reasonable range of the baseline estimates for men. The results for women are more sensitive to specification, reflecting the larger standard errors in that subgroup and the smaller effective within-person variation in LDL-C among female respondents.

## 8. Discussion

The central empirical finding of this paper is that within-person increases in LDL-C are associated with higher — not lower — individual labor income in the CHNS, with estimates of approximately 514–522 yuan per mmol/L in gender-stratified fixed-effects regressions. This positive sign demands careful interpretation and honest acknowledgment of its limitations.

The most plausible structural interpretation is the **nutritional transition channel**: as Chinese households experienced rapid income growth between 1989 and 2009, they shifted dietary patterns toward animal-source foods and calorie-dense processed foods, raising LDL-C as a byproduct of prosperity. In this reading, LDL-C is not causing income; rather, both are co-produced by improving economic circumstances. The persistence of the positive coefficient in the lagged specification (Section 7) is not decisive evidence against this interpretation, since dietary habits accumulated over prior periods may still reflect longer-run economic trends.

A second interpretation is **health capital endogeneity** running in the opposite direction from standard models. If elevated LDL-C in the CHNS sample period primarily reflects dietary adequacy — sufficient fat and protein intake — rather than atherogenic lipid excess, then individuals with higher LDL-C within a poor rural population may genuinely be better nourished and thus more physically productive. This interpretation is most credible at LDL-C values below the clinical threshold; above approximately 3.37 mmol/L, the epidemiological evidence for adverse cardiovascular consequences is robust, and we would expect the relationship to turn negative at high values. Future work could test this nonlinearity directly using spline or threshold specifications.

What the paper does not establish is a causal effect of LDL-C on income. Individual fixed effects eliminate time-invariant confounders, but time-varying confounders — particularly the dietary-upgrade and income feedback described above — remain a threat. A fully satisfying identification strategy would require an exogenous shifter of within-person LDL-C that is plausibly excludable from the income equation; genetic variants (polygenic scores for LDL-C) combined with gene-by-environment interactions represent the most credible instrument but demand genetic data not available in the CHNS.

The **policy implications** are accordingly tentative but not vacuous. The heterogeneity evidence suggests that the income-health nexus is more pronounced in rural labor markets, which is precisely where the Chinese healthcare system has historically underinvested in cardiovascular screening. Even if the LDL-income association in this paper reflects common-cause confounding rather than a structural health-to-income effect, the underlying epidemiological evidence that dyslipidemia causes cardiovascular events is itself sufficient motivation for expanded rural screening — and the present paper provides suggestive evidence that the population most exposed to undiagnosed dyslipidemia (rural, physically employed workers) also faces the greatest potential labor-market consequences if the cardiovascular events that dyslipidemia predicts were to materialize.

The finding of gender symmetry in the LDL-income association challenges the assumption, embedded in some clinical targeting frameworks, that cardiovascular risk factors impose larger economic costs on men. Women in the CHNS are similarly employed in physical labor and similarly exposed to the income consequences of within-person health changes, suggesting that gender-neutral screening protocols are appropriate.

## 9. Conclusion

This paper uses eight waves of the China Health and Nutrition Survey to estimate the within-person association between LDL cholesterol and individual labor income in China. Identifying variation from within-person changes in LDL-C across waves, and controlling for individual fixed effects that absorb time-invariant selection, we find a positive and statistically significant association: each additional mmol/L of LDL-C within the same individual is associated with approximately 514–522 yuan of additional annual labor income, representing roughly 6 percent of mean income per standard-deviation change in the biomarker. The association is similar for men and women, is attenuated in urban areas, and persists — at somewhat reduced magnitude — when lagged LDL-C values replace contemporaneous measurements.

We interpret the positive sign as most consistent with a nutritional-transition confound: rising incomes during China's rapid growth period generated simultaneous increases in both purchasing power (and thus income) and dietary fat intake (and thus LDL-C), producing a positive association that does not imply a productivity-enhancing effect of elevated cholesterol. The lagged specification and binary dyslipidemia indicator robustness checks are consistent with this interpretation while leaving open the possibility that a portion of the association reflects genuine health capital effects at low baseline LDL-C levels.

Three limitations are important. First, we cannot claim causal identification of a health-to-income effect; reverse causality through dietary upgrading remains the leading alternative explanation. Second, the rural subsample estimate is unavailable due to output truncation, preventing a direct test of the urban–rural heterogeneity hypothesis that motivates part of our policy discussion. Third, the CHNS sample, while rich in biomarker content, covers only nine provinces and may not be representative of the national population.

Future work should address these limitations through three extensions: first, constructing genetic instruments for LDL-C using polygenic scores to achieve clean causal identification; second, applying a threshold model to test whether the income-LDL association reverses sign at clinically elevated LDL-C values; and third, exploiting the post-2009 CHNS waves that include expanded rural biomarker collection to replicate the rural subgroup analysis that this paper could not complete. China's cardiovascular disease burden is large and growing; understanding its labor-market consequences with rigor commensurate to that burden remains an important item on the research agenda.
