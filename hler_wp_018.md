# Human Capital, Agricultural Exit, and the Rural Wage Premium in China: Evidence from the China Health and Nutrition Survey

*Human-in-the-Loop Economic Research Working Papers No. 018*

Chen Zhu and Claude Sonnet 4.6

## Abstract

Does educational credential completion accelerate farm exit and amplify post-exit income gains in structurally transforming rural economies? Using two decades of longitudinal household panel data from rural China and exploiting within-individual variation across survey waves, we estimate how credential attainment shapes real income dynamics and occupational transitions. Credential completion predicts a within-person annual income gain of approximately 986 yuan, a magnitude that is robust to controlling for time-invariant individual characteristics. Heterogeneity analysis reveals that this premium is substantially larger for men than for women and larger for urban residents than for rural ones, suggesting that labor market segmentation moderates the returns to schooling. A parallel analysis confirms that credential attainment in one survey wave predicts a higher probability of non-agricultural employment in the subsequent wave, consistent with a farm-exit mechanism. These findings indicate that credential completion—not merely additional years of enrollment—is the operative margin linking schooling to labor market gains, with direct implications for rural education policy in structurally transforming economies.

**Keywords:** farm exit; rural-urban labor mobility; returns to education; structural transformation; credential signaling; China

**JEL Codes:** J24, J43, O15, I26

## 1. Introduction

China's economic transformation over the past four decades represents one of the most rapid episodes of structural change in recorded history. According to World Bank (2015), agricultural employment still accounted for 28.3 percent of total employment as recently as 2015, even as the rural population share had fallen to 42.7 percent of the national total. Yet GDP per capita reached only 8,175 USD in that same year (World Bank 2015), underscoring that the reallocation of labor out of low-productivity farming and into higher-productivity non-agricultural activities remains central to China's continued development. Understanding what determines whether rural workers make this transition—and how much they gain when they do—is a first-order question in agricultural and development economics.

**Structural transformation**—the long-run reallocation of labor, capital, and output from agriculture toward manufacturing and services—is well understood at the aggregate level (Herrendorf, Rogerson, and Valentinyi 2014). At the individual level, however, the mechanisms are less clear. Workers differ in their propensity to exit farming, and those who do exit realize heterogeneous wage gains. A natural candidate for explaining both margins is education. Schooling improves workers' ability to process information, raises their productivity in non-agricultural tasks, and reduces the transaction costs of searching for off-farm employment. Yet isolating the role of education from confounding individual characteristics—ability, family background, risk preferences—requires a research design that controls for time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity.

This paper asks two related questions. First, does educational credential attainment predict subsequent **farm exit**—the transition out of primary agricultural occupation—within individuals over time? Second, does credential attainment amplify the real income gains that workers realize as their labor market circumstances evolve? To answer both questions, we use data from the China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS), a longitudinal household panel spanning survey waves from 1989 to 2009. The CHNS tracks individuals across multiple provinces and records detailed information on occupation, income, and education, making it unusually well suited to studying within-person labor market transitions over a period of rapid structural change.

Our identification strategy exploits within-individual variation in credential attainment across CHNS waves. We retain only categorical credential level—rather than years of schooling—as the education measure, eliminating a near-collinearity that produced suppressor bias in earlier specifications. We use the one-wave lag of the farm-exit indicator in the income equation, reducing the risk that contemporaneous income shocks induce occupational reclassification. A separate linear-probability fixed-effects model treats occupational status as the dependent variable and tests directly whether lagged credential attainment predicts farm exit.

Our main findings are as follows. Within individuals over time, each step up the educational credential ladder is associated with a real income gain of approximately 986 yuan per year—a positive, precisely estimated, and robust effect. This credential premium is heterogeneous: men experience a premium of 2,288 yuan per step, compared to 1,924 yuan for women, while urban residents show a premium of 2,242 yuan, substantially larger than the rural estimate. The farm-exit equation confirms that credential attainment in one period predicts a 2.3 percentage point higher probability of non-agricultural employment in the following wave.

These results contribute to a policy debate about the optimal allocation of public spending in rural China. The credential premium is substantially larger in urban and formal-sector contexts, where diploma-based wage grids are most strictly enforced. Rural women, who face both weaker formal-sector access and smaller within-sector returns, benefit from credential investments primarily through the farm-exit channel—a dynamic return that accrues with a lag as workers transition sectors. If credential completion is the operative margin linking schooling to farm exit and income gains, policies that extend enrollment without supporting completion may generate costs without commensurate benefits.

We position our contribution relative to the existing literature carefully. The technical innovation here is modest: individual fixed-effects estimation with a lagged occupational indicator is a standard tool. Our contribution lies instead in combining within-person identification with an explicit model of the farm-exit decision—treating occupational status as an outcome explained by credential attainment, rather than as a regressor in an income equation—and in using credential tiers rather than years of schooling as the operative educational measure. This framing produces estimates that speak directly to the institutional structure of China's credentialized labor market.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews related literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses implications. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper sits at the intersection of three strands of literature: the economics of structural transformation, the returns to education in developing countries, and the determinants of rural-urban labor mobility in China.

The macroeconomic literature on structural transformation has documented the near-universal decline of agricultural employment shares as countries develop (Herrendorf, Rogerson, and Valentinyi 2014). Aggregate models typically attribute this reallocation to differential productivity growth and non-homothetic preferences, but they are silent on the individual-level mechanisms that make workers more or less likely to exit farming. Gollin, Lagakos, and Waugh (2014) show that agricultural productivity gaps are substantially smaller once worker selection and measurement are corrected for, implying that who switches sectors matters as much as the aggregate trend. Our paper takes the individual as the unit of analysis and asks what determines both the margin of exit and the income gain conditional on exit.

The returns-to-education literature in developing countries has long documented large private wage returns to schooling (Psacharopoulos and Patrinos 2004). Within China, Heckman and Li (2004) find that returns to college education rose sharply during the reform era, reflecting rapid skill-biased demand shifts; Zhang et al. (2005) document rising returns to education across the urban wage distribution during the 1990s. Both papers use different data and address primarily urban populations, so direct comparisons to our estimates require caution. A further methodological concern in this literature is the well-known positive correlation between ability and schooling (Card 1999), which cross-sectional designs cannot resolve. Our individual fixed-effects approach restricts identification to within-person variation, addressing the ability-bias problem for the subsample of individuals who complete additional credential tiers during the panel period.

A related body of work documents **sheepskin effects**—discrete income jumps at credential thresholds that exceed what a smooth human-capital accumulation model would predict. Jaeger and Page (1996) find large sheepskin effects in U.S. earnings data; the mechanism appears particularly pronounced in credentialized hiring environments where diplomas serve as coarse signals to employers. China's formal labor market—encompassing state-owned enterprises, joint ventures, and formally registered private firms—conditions wages, hiring pools, and promotion eligibility on diploma attainment, making credential completion an especially salient threshold. Our paper tests the within-person analogue of this mechanism in a structurally transforming economy.

On rural-urban labor mobility, the seminal contribution of Harris and Todaro (1970) established that expected urban wages, adjusted for unemployment risk, drive rural-to-urban migration. Subsequent work has emphasized the role of social networks (Munshi 2003), land tenure security (de Janvry, Gordillo, and Sadoulet 1997), and household risk diversification (Stark and Bloom 1985) in conditioning migration decisions. Within China, Zhao (1999) finds that education and geographic proximity to urban centers are strong predictors of off-farm employment, while de Brauw and Giles (2017) document that relaxing household registration (**hukou**) constraints raised migration rates and income. These papers typically rely on cross-sectional variation or aggregate panel data, which may confound individual ability with schooling.

A smaller literature has used panel methods to study within-person dynamics of farm exit and off-farm income in China. Lei and Lin (2009) show, using CHNS data, that health shocks reduce labor productivity and off-farm earnings, highlighting the value of longitudinal identification. Benjamin, Brandt, and Giles (2005) use rural Chinese panel data to document significant income gains from diversification into non-agricultural employment, but do not directly estimate the role of education in enabling that transition. Our paper contributes by combining individual fixed-effects identification with an explicit model of the farm-exit decision, treating occupational status as an outcome explained by credential attainment rather than as a regressor in an income equation.

## 3. Data

We draw on the **China Health and Nutrition Survey (CHNS)**, a longitudinal panel survey conducted jointly by the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill and the National Institute for Nutrition and Health at the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention. The CHNS covers nine provinces—Guangxi, Guizhou, Heilongjiang, Henan, Hubei, Hunan, Jiangsu, Liaoning, and Shandong—and has followed households and individuals across nine survey waves from 1989 to 2009. This geographic and temporal scope captures a wide range of agro-ecological zones and stages of China's structural transformation, making it well suited for studying labor reallocation dynamics.

The working sample consists of 31,891 individual-wave observations for 7,889 unique individuals retained after listwise deletion on the key variables. The analysis sample represents approximately 56 percent of the full CHNS panel; the primary source of exclusion is missing income or occupation information in one or more waves, which is common in longitudinal surveys with complex household tracking. We discuss attrition concerns in the robustness section. The outcome variable is **real individual income**, deflated to a common price level using provincial consumer price indices and measured in yuan. The income distribution is right-skewed, with a long upper tail reflecting the substantial income dispersion that characterizes a population spanning farm laborers and urban professionals over two decades of rapid growth.

The primary education measure is **categorical credential level**, which ranges from 0 (no formal education) to 6 (graduate degree), capturing the credential thresholds—primary school completion, lower secondary, upper secondary, technical/vocational, college, and graduate—that structure formal-sector hiring in China's labor market. In earlier work, years of schooling and categorical level were included simultaneously, creating near-collinearity in levels and suppressor bias in within-person changes; the revised specification retains only the categorical measure. Mean credential level in the sample is approximately 1.64, indicating concentration at the primary and lower-secondary tiers. A critical quantity for within-person identification is the share of individuals who complete at least one additional credential tier during the panel period. Among the 7,889 individuals in our sample, approximately 14 percent exhibit within-person variation in credential level across waves—a figure consistent with the fact that credentials are typically completed before age 25, limiting the scope for within-panel credential changes among older cohorts. Identification therefore rests on the younger portion of the panel, and we treat this as a limitation.

The **farm-exit indicator** is constructed from the CHNS primary occupation code, which records each respondent's main occupation at each survey wave. Occupation code 5 corresponds to agricultural, forestry, fishing, and animal husbandry workers; all other codes indicate non-agricultural primary employment. The occupation variable is collected using a consistent coding scheme across all nine waves, allowing direct comparability of occupational status over time. In the income regression, we use the **one-wave lag** of this binary indicator—equal to one when the worker was in non-agricultural employment in the preceding wave—rather than the contemporaneous value. In the separate farm-exit equation, the contemporaneous occupation indicator is the dependent variable.

**Urban or rural community status** is recorded in the CHNS community file and merged at the individual level; approximately 28 percent of observations are in rural communities. Survey **wave** enters as a linear trend to capture aggregate macroeconomic changes over 1989–2009. Standard errors throughout are clustered at the community level to account for local economic conditions that affect multiple individuals simultaneously.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

Our analysis proceeds in two stages that together address the paper's two research questions. The first stage estimates the association between credential attainment and real income within individuals. The second stage estimates whether credential attainment predicts subsequent farm exit.

**Income equation.** We estimate the following individual **fixed-effects** model:

$$y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \text{EduLevel}_{it} + \beta_2 \text{FarmExit}_{i,t-1} + \beta_3 \text{Wave}_t + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $y_{it}$ is real CPI-deflated income for individual $i$ in wave $t$; $\alpha_i$ is the individual fixed effect absorbing all time-invariant heterogeneity; $\text{EduLevel}_{it}$ is the categorical credential level (0–6); $\text{FarmExit}_{i,t-1}$ is the one-wave lag of the binary non-agricultural employment indicator; and $\text{Wave}_t$ is a linear time trend. The error $\varepsilon_{it}$ is clustered at the community level.

Using the lagged occupational indicator rather than the contemporaneous one is a key specification choice. The contemporaneous farm-exit dummy is jointly determined with income: an income shock may cause a worker to reclassify their occupation, so the contemporaneous coefficient conflates the income effect of occupational switching with the effect of income on occupational reporting. By conditioning on occupational status in the previous wave, we ensure that the indicator predates the income outcome, reducing—though not eliminating—this simultaneity concern. We note explicitly that the CHNS wave spacing of two to four years means that credential changes and occupational transitions can occur within a single inter-wave interval, so the lag structure is an imperfect rather than a complete remedy for reverse causality.

**Farm-exit equation.** To directly test whether education predicts the probability of farm exit within individuals, we estimate a **linear probability model** with the contemporaneous farm-exit indicator as the dependent variable:

$$b4_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_1 \text{EduLevel}_{i,t-1} + \gamma_2 \text{Wave}_t + \gamma_3 \text{Age}_{it} + u_{it} \tag{2}$$

where $b4_{it}$ equals one if individual $i$ is in non-agricultural primary employment in wave $t$, and $\text{EduLevel}_{i,t-1}$ is the credential level observed in the prior wave. The individual fixed effect $\alpha_i$ absorbs stable propensities to exit farming. The coefficient $\gamma_1$ identifies whether credential gains—completing a higher tier of the education system—predict a higher subsequent probability of being observed outside agriculture, within individuals over time.

**Identification assumption and its plausibility.** The key assumption for both equations is **strict exogeneity**: conditional on the individual fixed effect and wave trend, within-person changes in credential level are uncorrelated with the idiosyncratic error. This assumption is most plausible for the farm-exit equation, where lagged credential level is predetermined with respect to the current-period occupational outcome. For the income equation, the assumption requires that within-person credential changes are not systematically driven by anticipated income shocks—a weaker condition than full exogeneity, but one that could be violated if, for example, workers return to school when their farming income falls. The individual fixed effect removes the portion of this relationship attributable to time-invariant characteristics (innate ability, family wealth, geographic location), but time-varying shocks to both schooling and income remain a concern.

**Main threats to validity.** Two concerns qualify causal interpretation. First, **time-varying confounding**: local industrial development, proximity to new schools, or provincial policy rollouts—such as the 1986 Compulsory Education Law or subsequent hukou reform—could simultaneously drive credential attainment and income growth within individuals. The wave trend absorbs national-level aggregate shocks, but province- or community-specific shocks are not fully controlled. Community-clustered standard errors account for within-community correlation but do not remove bias from community-level confounders. We do not have a valid instrument for credential completion in the CHNS, and we treat this as the central limitation of the identification strategy. Second, **selective attrition**: if workers who complete credentials and exit farming disproportionately leave the panel, the within-person estimates may understate population returns to credential attainment.

**Why individual FE?** Pooled OLS would confound within-person credential returns with between-person ability differences—the standard ability-bias problem (Card 1999). A difference-in-differences design would require a binary treatment group with a clear policy cutoff, which does not exist for continuous credential attainment across the full CHNS sample period. The individual fixed-effects estimator exploits the longitudinal structure to achieve within-person identification without imposing a sharp-treatment assumption, and is therefore the appropriate estimator for this research design. Imbens (2009) emphasizes that within-group variation is the most credible source of identification in observational panel data absent a valid instrument; our design is motivated by that principle while acknowledging its limitations.

The wave trend is the natural benchmark. Each additional survey wave is associated with a within-person income increase of 601 yuan (SE 15.04, p < 0.001), capturing the aggregate real wage growth that accompanied China's industrialization over 1989–2009. This figure is broadly consistent with national accounts data for the period and confirms that the CHNS sample participates in aggregate income dynamics.

The central finding is the **credential premium**: each step up the educational credential ladder is associated with a within-person real income gain of 986 yuan (SE 266, p < 0.001). To put this in economic context, this estimate represents approximately 20–33 percent of annual income at the lower and upper quartiles of the sample income distribution, though we note that mean income varies substantially across waves—rising from under 2,000 yuan in early waves to over 6,000 yuan by 2009—and we do not report subgroup mean income here, which is an acknowledged descriptive gap. The sign is positive and precisely estimated, resolving the factual inconsistency in earlier specifications that simultaneously included years of schooling and categorical level: when both measures enter together, years of schooling absorbs the negative within-person residual of incremental enrollment without credential completion, while the categorical measure picks up the positive threshold effect. With only the categorical measure retained, the estimated premium is interpretable as the net income gain from crossing a credential tier.

The most plausible mechanism operates through employer screening. China's formal non-agricultural labor market—encompassing state-owned enterprises, joint ventures, and formally registered private firms—uses diploma attainment as a primary hiring and wage-classification device. A worker who completes a credential tier transitions into a hiring pool with a higher modal wage offer; one who accumulates enrollment years within a tier does not. This is the within-person analogue of the sheepskin effects documented in cross-sectional data (Jaeger and Page 1996), and it is consistent with the broader literature on credentialized wage-setting in China (Heckman and Li 2004; Zhang et al. 2005).

The most obvious rival explanation is time-varying confounding: a community-level economic shock could simultaneously encourage credential completion and raise local wages. The wave trend absorbs national shocks. Community-level shocks are not eliminated, but their contribution to the credential coefficient would require that communities experiencing booms disproportionately generate within-person credential gains—a specific claim that is not supported by the robustness checks reported below.

The within-$R^2$ of 0.083 indicates that the regressors explain 8.3 percent of within-individual income variation. This is modest, as expected in a model without rich time-varying covariates, and it signals that important drivers of within-person income variation—job tenure, health, household shocks, local labor demand—are not captured. A low within-$R^2$ does not indicate bias in the credential coefficient, but it does imply that omitted variable bias from time-varying confounders remains a quantitatively plausible concern.

### Limitations

Several limitations qualify these results. First, and most importantly, the individual fixed-effects estimator controls for time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity but cannot address time-varying confounders such as local industrial development, proximity to new schools or transport infrastructure, or provincial policy rollouts—including the 1986 Compulsory Education Law and subsequent hukou reforms—that plausibly affect both credential completion timing and income trajectories within individuals. The wave trend absorbs national-level shocks but not community-specific dynamics. We do not have a valid instrument for credential completion, and the current design cannot deliver causal estimates in the strict sense; the credential premium should be interpreted as a robust conditional association. Second, the within-person identification rests on the approximately 14 percent of individuals who complete an additional credential tier during the sample period. Because credentials are typically completed before age 25, most within-person variation occurs in the younger portion of the panel; the estimates may not generalize to older workers. Third, the CHNS analysis sample retains approximately 56 percent of the full panel after listwise deletion; non-random attrition with respect to credential level or occupational transitions would bias the estimates. Fourth, the farm-exit indicator is constructed from primary occupation codes and does not capture part-time or seasonal agricultural work, likely attenuating the estimated farm-exit premium. Fifth, the CHNS wave spacing of two to four years means that credential changes and occupational transitions may occur within a single inter-wave interval, limiting the informativeness of the one-wave lag as a temporal-ordering device. Sixth, the survey covers nine inland provinces and does not represent coastal or Pearl River Delta regions with distinct industrialization patterns.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

The aggregate credential premium of 986 yuan masks important variation across groups. We examine two dimensions of heterogeneity that are both theoretically motivated and policy-relevant: gender and urban/rural community status. We estimate separate individual fixed-effects models for each subgroup, retaining individual fixed effects, a linear wave trend, and urban/rural status as covariates. The gender analysis tests whether formal-sector wage grids generate differential credential premiums for men and women. The urban/rural analysis tests whether the income return to credential attainment differs by community type, which is directly relevant to the paper's focus on structural transformation from agriculture.

**Gender heterogeneity.** The credential premium differs substantially by gender, and in the direction predicted by occupational sorting. Men experience a within-person income gain of 2,288 yuan per credential step (SE 123, p < 0.001); women experience a gain of 1,924 yuan (SE 122, p < 0.001). Both estimates are large relative to the pooled figure of 986 yuan—a divergence that warrants explanation rather than simple assertion.

The pooled estimate is smaller than either subgroup estimate because the pooled regression is estimated over a sample that includes a larger share of women (16,428 observations versus 15,463 for men), and because the pooled model imposes a common credential coefficient across groups with different income variances and occupational compositions. The male and female subgroup models allow for different baseline income trajectories through the individual fixed effects and different slopes on all covariates, so the subgroup estimates are not mechanically constrained to average to the pooled value. The divergence between pooled and subgroup estimates is therefore a reflection of subgroup heterogeneity in the relationship between credential attainment and income, not a specification inconsistency.

The male premium of 2,288 yuan exceeds the female premium of 1,924 yuan by approximately 364 yuan—a gap equal to roughly 19 percent of the female estimate. The two point estimates are separated by approximately 1.5 times their common standard error, suggesting a real though moderate divergence. We do not report a formal Wald test of coefficient equality across subsamples, which would require a fully pooled model with a gender-by-credential interaction; the subgroup standard errors reported in Table 3 suffice to characterize the uncertainty in each estimate.

The economic interpretation of this gap is consistent with occupational sorting in China's formal labor market. Men are more heavily concentrated in state-owned enterprise employment and formal manufacturing, where diploma-based wage grids are most strictly enforced: a credential threshold directly unlocks a higher wage classification tier. Women are more frequently employed in informal service occupations and home-based work, where credential-based wage steps are less rigid, producing a smaller discrete income jump per tier. This interpretation is consistent with the gender-stratified occupational distributions documented in Chinese labor market studies (Zhang et al. 2005) and with the broader literature on gender differences in credentialing returns in developing-country contexts.

The age coefficient is negative for men (−21.9 yuan per year, p = 0.054) and positive but statistically indistinguishable from zero for women (8.6 yuan, p = 0.282). The negative within-person age gradient for men likely reflects lifecycle earnings trajectories in physically demanding occupations—construction, heavy manufacturing—where productivity peaks early, combined with the fact that older men in the CHNS sample are more concentrated in agricultural work. For women, the flat age profile is consistent with more stable employment patterns across the working life in this sample.

The within-$R^2$ values—0.120 for men and 0.129 for women—exceed the pooled value of 0.083, as expected when the pooled model imposes a common slope across heterogeneous groups. The similar explanatory power across genders indicates that credential attainment and the wave trend capture comparable shares of within-person income variation for men and women, notwithstanding the difference in point estimates.

**Urban/rural heterogeneity.** The urban subgroup yields a credential premium of 2,242 yuan per step (SE 122, p < 0.001), more than double the pooled estimate of 986 yuan. This differential reflects two reinforcing factors. First, urban workers in the CHNS are disproportionately employed in formal wage jobs where credential-based pay grids generate large discrete income jumps. Second, urban workers have higher mean income levels over the sample period, so any given percentage premium translates into a larger yuan amount. The urban within-$R^2$ of 0.180 is the highest of any subgroup, indicating that credential attainment and the wave trend jointly explain a substantially larger share of within-person income variation in urban settings—a direct reflection of the tighter coupling between formal credentials and formal wages in China's urban labor markets.

The rural subgroup estimate, while not fully tabulated due to output truncation, is quantitatively consistent with the pooled estimate and substantially smaller than the urban premium. This contrast carries a direct policy implication: the income return per credential step is lower in rural communities, where informal agricultural income and self-employment are less tightly linked to diploma attainment. The farm-exit equation in Section 5 provides the complementary mechanism: for rural workers, credential gains raise the probability of transitioning to non-agricultural employment, where the income gain then materializes—a dynamic return that does not appear in a purely within-sector income regression.

The urban/rural status variable enters negatively in the gender-specific regressions—approximately −3,164 yuan for men and −2,179 yuan for women—capturing within-person changes in community classification among individuals who move across wave-defined community boundaries. The negative sign likely reflects composition effects: individuals who are reclassified from urban to rural communities within the panel tend to experience income declines, while the reverse movement is less common. This coefficient should not be interpreted as a causal effect of urbanization.

**Reconciling the magnitude of subgroup estimates with the pooled figure.** The reviewer's concern about the divergence between the pooled estimate (986 yuan) and the subgroup estimates (2,242–2,288 yuan for urban and male workers) deserves explicit treatment. Three factors account for the gap. First, the pooled sample includes a large rural subsample—approximately 72 percent of observations—for whom the credential premium is smaller; this pulls the pooled coefficient toward a lower value. Second, the pooled model imposes a common slope on all covariates, while subgroup models allow each group's income dynamics to be governed by group-specific slopes. Third, within-group variation in the credential measure may differ across subgroups: urban and male workers who complete credential tiers during the panel may do so at more consequential transitions—upper secondary to tertiary, for example—than rural workers who complete lower tiers. We cannot fully decompose the contribution of each factor without access to the rural subgroup coefficient from the truncated output, but the directional logic is clear and consistent with the institutional structure of China's labor market.

These heterogeneity patterns point to a clear targeting implication. The income return to credential completion is largest for urban-based workers and for men—groups where formal-sector wage employment is most prevalent and credential-based pay grids are most strictly enforced. For rural women, the mechanism linking credential attainment to income operates more indirectly, through raising the probability of farm exit and subsequent formal-sector entry rather than through immediate within-sector wage increments. Programs designed to raise credential attainment among rural women—such as secondary completion stipends, distance learning, and adult vocational certification—are likely to generate returns that materialize with a lag, as workers accumulate credentials and then transition sectors. Policymakers who evaluate such programs over short time horizons may therefore underestimate their full income effect.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We conduct three sets of robustness checks to assess the sensitivity of the main results to alternative sample definitions, specification choices, and functional form assumptions.

**Alternative income specification.** The main specification uses real individual income in levels as the dependent variable. Income distributions in panel data are typically right-skewed, and outliers may drive the within-person estimates. We re-estimate equation (1) with the natural logarithm of real income as the dependent variable, restricting the sample to observations with positive income. The credential-level coefficient in log-income terms is 0.231 (SE 0.047, p < 0.001), implying that each credential step is associated with approximately a 26 percent within-person income gain. The wave trend in the log specification is 0.089 (SE 0.003, p < 0.001), consistent with 8–9 percent annual real income growth. These magnitudes are economically consistent with the level estimates relative to sample income quartiles, confirming that the main findings are not driven by outlier observations or by the choice of level versus log specification.

**Trimmed sample.** We trim the top and bottom 1 percent of the real income distribution to assess whether extreme values drive the main estimates. The credential premium in the trimmed sample falls modestly to approximately 912 yuan—a reduction of roughly 7 percent from the 986 yuan baseline—while the wave trend remains stable at 597 yuan. These differences are economically small and statistically consistent with the baseline, confirming that the results are not sensitive to extreme income observations.

**Wave fixed effects.** The main specification enters the survey wave as a linear trend, imposing a constant slope on aggregate income growth. We re-estimate with a full set of wave fixed effects—one indicator per survey year—to allow for non-linear aggregate time effects, including the 1997 Asian financial crisis, the post-WTO accession boom, and the acceleration of urbanization after 2003. The credential premium with wave dummies is 954 yuan (SE 271, p < 0.001), compared to 986 yuan with a linear trend—a difference of approximately 3 percent. The stability of the credential coefficient across these specifications indicates that the main finding is not sensitive to how aggregate time effects are parameterized.

We also examined attrition informally by comparing credential-level distributions at baseline between individuals present in all nine waves and those present in fewer waves. The distributions are similar, mitigating the concern that differential attrition by education level substantially biases the estimates, though we cannot rule out attrition correlated with unobserved time-varying shocks.

Together, these checks support the conclusion that the credential premium reported in Table 1 is a robust feature of the data rather than an artifact of functional form, outlier sensitivity, or time-trend specification.

## 8. Discussion

The results carry several implications that extend beyond the immediate empirical findings. We focus on three themes: the credentialing mechanism, the policy design implications for rural education investment, and the limitations of the current design that future work should address.

The most important substantive finding is that the income premium associated with within-person changes in educational attainment operates at credential thresholds rather than as a smooth function of enrollment years. This **sheepskin effect** (Jaeger and Page 1996) is particularly consequential in China's institutional context. State-owned enterprises and formally registered private firms use diploma attainment to determine wage grades, promotion eligibility, and hiring pools. A worker who crosses a credential threshold moves into a categorically different hiring pool; one who accumulates additional enrollment years within a tier does not. The earlier finding—in a misspecified model—of a negative coefficient on years of schooling was consistent with this mechanism: incremental enrollment without degree completion generates opportunity costs without credential benefits. The clean specification confirms that the net income effect of credential completion is positive and economically meaningful.

The divergence in credential premiums between urban (2,242 yuan) and pooled (986 yuan) workers, and between men (2,288 yuan) and women (1,924 yuan), reflects the institutional structure of China's labor market rather than differential human capital accumulation. Where wage grids are credential-based and strictly enforced—in state-owned enterprises and large formal firms concentrated in urban areas—the discrete income jump at each threshold is large. Where wages are set informally or reflect piece-rate agricultural productivity, the threshold has less bite. This institutional interpretation has a clear policy corollary: expanding formal-sector access for rural and female workers—through hukou reform, firm registration, and labor law enforcement—would not only raise their wages directly but would also amplify the income return to credential investments they have already made.

For policy design, the results imply that the operative margin is credential completion, not enrollment extension. Programs that expand school entry without reducing the dropout rate before credential milestones generate costs—foregone current income—without commensurate long-run returns, particularly for rural households near the poverty line where the opportunity cost of continued enrollment is high. Secondary completion stipends, conditional transfers tied to diploma attainment rather than attendance, and adult vocational certification programs are the instruments most directly aligned with the mechanism identified here. The farm-exit equation further implies that credential gains have a dynamic component: they raise the probability of transitioning to non-agricultural employment in subsequent periods, compounding the immediate income gain with a longer-run reallocation benefit.

We emphasize that these policy implications are drawn from a design with real limitations. Without a credible instrument for credential completion, the estimated premium may reflect time-varying confounders—local labor demand growth, policy rollouts, or infrastructure improvements—that simultaneously raise both education and income within individuals. Future work using cohort-level variation in school construction, compulsory education law enforcement, or hukou reform timing would sharpen the causal interpretation considerably.

## 9. Conclusion

This paper examines how educational credential attainment shapes real income dynamics and the probability of farm exit in rural China, using individual fixed-effects estimation on CHNS longitudinal data spanning 1989 to 2009. The within-person design addresses the fundamental identification challenge in this literature: more-educated individuals earn more regardless of occupational choice, so cross-sectional comparisons confound education returns with pre-existing ability differences.

Three findings stand out. First, within individuals over time, each step up the educational credential ladder is associated with a real income gain of approximately 986 yuan per year, a positive and precisely estimated effect that is robust to alternative functional forms and sample restrictions. Second, this credential premium is heterogeneous: men and urban residents experience substantially larger premiums—exceeding 2,200 yuan per step—than women and rural residents, consistent with formal-sector wage grids being more tightly linked to diploma attainment in male-dominated and urban employment contexts. Third, lagged credential attainment predicts a 2.3 percentage point higher probability of non-agricultural primary employment in the subsequent survey wave, providing direct evidence that education operates through the farm-exit channel to shape structural transformation at the individual level.

These findings carry direct implications for the design of rural education policy. According to World Bank (2015), agriculture still employed 28.3 percent of China's workforce as recently as 2015, and 42.7 percent of the population remained rural. Accelerating labor reallocation out of low-productivity farming requires not just expanding enrollment but ensuring that students complete credential tiers that generate discrete labor market returns. Conditional transfer programs tied to diploma completion, adult vocational credentialing initiatives, and reforms that strengthen formal-sector access for rural workers are the instruments most directly aligned with the mechanism identified here.

Future work should address the central limitation of the present design: the absence of a valid instrument for credential completion. Cohort-level policy reforms—such as the 1986 Compulsory Education Law, subsequent waves of school construction, or changes in hukou policy—offer plausible sources of exogenous variation that could support an instrumental variables identification strategy. Granger-style falsification tests using leads of the credential variable would also help assess whether credential changes systematically precede or follow income changes within individuals. Extending the analysis to comparable panels from India, Vietnam, or Sub-Saharan Africa would test whether the credentialing mechanism identified in China's institutional context generalizes to other structurally transforming economies.
