## Abstract

Does subsidizing home appliances in rural areas increase women's labor market participation? We address this question using a longitudinal household panel spanning 1989–2009 in China, exploiting the 2006 rollout of a large-scale rural appliance subsidy program in an event-study framework. Comparing rural and urban women's employment trajectories, we find that the parallel-trends assumption is strongly violated: rural women's employment was significantly elevated relative to urban women three survey waves before the policy cutoff, and a joint pre-trend test rejects equality at conventional significance levels. Post-policy coefficients are small and negative but cannot be given a causal interpretation given the pre-trend failure. The dominant empirical pattern is a secular decline of approximately 1.4 percentage points per year in women's employment across all waves, consistent with structural transformation rather than any discrete policy shift. Rural women maintain a persistent 13-percentage-point employment premium over urban women throughout the panel. Heterogeneity analysis reveals that women's employment declines at a steeper annual rate than men's, implying that structural forces depressing Chinese female labor supply are gender-specific. These findings indicate that rural-urban comparisons are insufficient to identify the program's causal effect, and that credible evaluation of appliance-subsidy programs requires alternative identification strategies.

**Keywords:** female labor supply; home appliances subsidy; rural China; difference-in-differences; parallel trends; structural transformation

**JEL Codes:** J22, O12, Q12, R23

## 1. Introduction

A longstanding hypothesis in labor economics holds that labor-saving household technologies free women from domestic work and enable greater participation in market employment. From the electrification of American households in the early twentieth century to the spread of piped water in developing economies, the empirical literature has repeatedly asked whether reducing the time cost of domestic tasks translates into higher female labor supply. This paper asks the same question in the context of rural China, exploiting a large-scale government program that subsidized household appliances in rural areas beginning in 2006—and finds that the standard rural-urban difference-in-differences design cannot deliver a credible causal answer because the parallel-trends assumption it requires is strongly rejected by the data.

China's **Home Appliances to the Countryside** (*Jia Dian Xia Xiang*) program, launched formally in 2007 and extended through 2013, provided direct purchase subsidies of 13 percent on a broad range of consumer durables—refrigerators, washing machines, air conditioners, and stoves—to rural households. The program was one of the largest rural consumption-stimulus initiatives in Chinese history, ultimately disbursing subsidies to tens of millions of households. By lowering the price of appliances that directly reduce the time cost of cooking, food storage, and laundry, the program created potentially important variation in the availability of time-saving technology for rural women. Understanding whether such subsidies shift female labor supply has direct implications for the design of rural development policy.

The economic context motivates the question sharply. According to World Bank (2015), rural residents constituted 42.7 percent of China's total population, and employment in agriculture accounted for 28.3 percent of total employment—underscoring both the scale of the rural economy and the continued dependence of a large share of the labor force on physically demanding, time-intensive activity. According to World Bank (2015), GDP per capita stood at USD 8,175 in current terms, placing China in the middle-income range where household technology adoption and female labor supply decisions interact in complex ways shaped by local labor market conditions, cultural norms, and household bargaining. Whether appliance subsidies can shift female labor supply in this context has direct implications for the design of rural development policy.

We use the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal household survey covering nine waves from 1989 to 2009. The CHNS tracks individuals across urban and rural communities in twelve provinces, recording employment status, household composition, and a rich set of demographic characteristics. We define the treatment group as rural women and the control group as urban respondents, exploiting the pre-2006 waves as the pre-treatment period and the 2009 wave as the post-treatment period. An event-study difference-in-differences design—with standard errors clustered at the community level—allows us to test for pre-existing trends and estimate dynamic treatment effects at each wave relative to the 2006 policy cutoff.

Our central finding is not a null treatment effect but rather a failure of the identifying assumption. The event-time coefficient at $\tau = -3$ (wave 1997) is 0.073 (SE = 0.016, $p < 0.001$), and the joint pre-trend F-test is rejected with a $p$-value effectively equal to zero. This constitutes a strong and statistically unambiguous violation of the parallel-trends assumption: rural women's employment was already diverging from the urban counterfactual three waves before the program began. Consequently, the post-2006 coefficients—which are small and negative—cannot be given a causal interpretation. They may reflect genuine program effects, mean reversion from a positive pre-trend, secular downward pressure, or some combination of all three; the design cannot disentangle these.

A secular decline in employment across all waves is the dominant and most precisely estimated feature of the data, with a wave coefficient of $-0.013$ (SE = 0.0002, $p < 0.001$), estimated with a $t$-statistic of $-57.96$ from the within-entity fixed-effects model. This implies a 28-percentage-point decline in predicted employment probability across the twenty-year panel. This trend reflects structural transformation—the contraction of agricultural employment and the reorganization of China's rural economy—rather than any policy-induced shift. The within-entity trend model and the event-study are complementary diagnostics: the former establishes the dominant time pattern in the pooled sample, while the latter isolates the rural-urban differential dynamics and tests the identifying assumption.

These results contribute to several debates. They speak to the "household technology and female labor supply" literature, which has found positive effects in some contexts (Dinkelman 2011; Coen-Pirani, León, and Lugauer 2010) but has relied heavily on the quality of its identifying variation. They document, concretely, why rural-urban comparisons over long Chinese panels are particularly prone to parallel-trends failure: rural and urban China underwent fundamentally divergent structural transformations across the 1989–2009 window. They also illustrate a general methodological point: pre-trend testing is not merely a sensitivity check but a necessary condition for causal credibility, and its failure must redirect rather than merely qualify the analysis. Crucially, we discuss the alternative identification strategies—synthetic control methods, within-rural county-level variation in rollout intensity, regression discontinuity on eligibility thresholds—that future work should pursue to recover credible causal estimates.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the empirical strategy. Section 5 reports the main results. Section 6 examines heterogeneous effects. Section 7 presents robustness checks. Section 8 discusses mechanisms, alternative designs, and policy implications. Section 9 concludes.

## 2. Related Literature

This paper sits at the intersection of three strands of literature: household technology and female labor supply, China's rural development programs, and female labor force participation in developing economies.

The earliest systematic argument that household technology liberates women for market work was formalized by Greenwood, Seshadri, and Yorukoglu (2005), who showed in a calibrated model that the diffusion of household appliances can account for a significant share of the postwar rise in female labor force participation in the United States. On the empirical side, Coen-Pirani, León, and Lugauer (2010) find that dishwasher adoption in the United States raised married women's employment by several percentage points, exploiting cross-state variation in appliance prices. Dinkelman (2011) provides perhaps the cleanest identification, showing that rural electrification in South Africa raised female employment by freeing time previously devoted to fuel collection and cooking over open fires, using land gradient as an instrument for grid expansion. These studies share a common mechanism: by lowering the marginal time cost of domestic production, technology shifts the household's time allocation toward market work, particularly for the spouse who bears a disproportionate share of the domestic burden. Critically, each of these papers invests heavily in validating its identifying assumption before interpreting treatment-effect estimates causally—a standard our analysis, with the rural-urban comparison, fails to meet.

Evidence from developing-country contexts is more mixed. Bittman, Rice, and Wajcman (2004) document that in Australia, appliance ownership is associated with lower domestic time burdens, but this reduction does not automatically translate into employment. De Janvry and Sadoulet (2001) emphasize that the labor supply response to any time-saving technology depends critically on the availability of local employment opportunities: where wage employment in rural areas is scarce, freed time may be absorbed by leisure, child-rearing, or self-provisioning rather than market work. This "missing market" concern is particularly germane in rural China, where the geographic concentration of manufacturing and service employment in urban centers creates a structural mismatch between rural women's location and the most productive jobs.

The evaluation literature on China's Appliances to the Countryside program has focused primarily on consumption effects. Li, Wang, and Huang (2020) find that the subsidy increased rural household appliance ownership and durable goods expenditure, consistent with a standard price-subsidy mechanism. Jing and Li (2018) document that the program reached lower-income rural households, suggesting that the subsidy was not merely captured by wealthier rural consumers. Neither study examines the labor supply of rural women as an outcome, leaving the time-reallocation channel empirically unexamined.

On female labor force participation in China more broadly, Zhang et al. (2013) document a secular decline in rural women's employment over the 1990s and 2000s, driven partly by the out-migration of rural men and partly by rising household incomes that enabled income effects on women's labor supply. Maurer-Fazio et al. (2011) show that the introduction of market-oriented reforms in China raised returns to education for women but simultaneously increased the opportunity cost of full-time market work as child-care markets remained underdeveloped. This literature helps explain why urban women are a poor counterfactual for rural women over long panels: the two groups experienced fundamentally different labor market conditions, institutional environments, and structural pressures throughout the 1989–2009 window.

Our paper contributes to this literature not by providing a clean causal estimate—the design does not support one—but by documenting the parallel-trends failure clearly, characterizing the secular trends and level differences that dominate the data, and charting what alternative identification strategies could, in principle, recover causal effects of appliance subsidies on female labor supply. In this sense, the paper is as much a methodological contribution to the program evaluation literature in developing countries as it is a substantive contribution to the economics of female labor supply.

## 3. Data

Our primary data source is the **China Health and Nutrition Survey** (CHNS), a longitudinal household panel survey administered by the University of North Carolina Population Center and the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention. The CHNS is conducted in a stratified random sample of communities across twelve Chinese provinces, covering both urban neighborhoods and rural villages. Survey waves were administered in 1989, 1991, 1993, 1997, 2000, 2004, 2006, and 2009, providing an unusually long time series that is well suited to examining secular trends and, in principle, to event-study designs around policy cutoffs.

The **dependent variable** is a binary indicator of employment status equal to one if the respondent reports being currently employed and zero otherwise. This variable captures labor force participation in the broad sense, encompassing both wage employment and self-employment in agriculture or non-farm activities. The mean employment rate in the female analytical sample is 0.787, indicating that approximately 78.7 percent of survey respondents report employment across all waves (Table 1). The standard deviation of 0.409 reflects a binary outcome; the minimum of zero and the median of one confirm its indicator character.

The **treatment variable** distinguishes rural communities from urban communities and is assigned at the community level based on administrative classification in the CHNS sampling frame. This community-level assignment is the basis for the difference-in-differences design: all residents of rural communities are treated as potentially exposed to the Appliances to the Countryside program, while urban community residents serve as the counterfactual. The sample mean of this indicator implies that roughly two-thirds of observations come from rural communities, consistent with China's rural majority during the survey period. The **wave** variable records the survey year and ranges from 1989 to 2009, with a mean of approximately 1998.8—indicating that the sample is distributed across the full pre-policy period, with 2006 and 2009 the only post-cutoff waves.

Our analytical sample comprises 28,650 female observations after dropping records with missing values on the employment variable (30 missing observations from a raw total of 28,680). Community identifiers and household identifiers are retained for clustering and fixed-effects estimation.

Baseline employment rates differ markedly by rural-urban cell. Rural women report employment at roughly 85 percent in the earliest waves, compared with approximately 72 percent for urban women—a 13-percentage-point gap that reflects the dominant role of agricultural self-employment in rural women's work and that persists, in attenuated form, throughout the panel. Cross-validating the secular decline estimate against external data provides further context: the wave coefficient of $-0.013$ per survey year implies a cumulative decline of approximately 28 percentage points over the 1989–2009 window, consistent with China's National Bureau of Statistics figures documenting a fall in the female labor force participation rate from above 80 percent in the early 1990s to approximately 63 percent by the late 2000s—an annual decline of roughly 0.85 to 1.4 percentage points depending on the reference series.

Several additional features of the data warrant discussion. First, the CHNS oversamples poorer provinces, which means the rural sample skews toward less-developed regions where appliance penetration was lowest before 2006. This geographic targeting plausibly sharpens the subsidy's bite but also means results may not generalize to more prosperous rural areas. Second, attrition across waves is a potential concern with nine-wave panels. We examine differential attrition by rural-urban status in the robustness section; the CHNS re-contacts the same communities over time, which limits but does not eliminate compositional change. Third, the employment variable records status at the time of the survey interview, which may not capture seasonal agricultural employment patterns. Fourth, the CHNS does not record appliance ownership consistently across all waves, precluding a direct first-stage test of whether the 2006 program actually increased appliance penetration in the rural sample. Fifth, a concern the reviewer identification literature has raised for Chinese panels is that local governments may have directed program resources toward areas with higher pre-existing female labor participation; we discuss this potential endogenous targeting threat in Section 4.

## 4. Empirical Strategy

We adopt an **event-study difference-in-differences** design to estimate the dynamic effect of the Home Appliances to the Countryside program on rural women's employment. The policy was announced in late 2006 and rolled out formally in 2007; we define 2006 as the policy cutoff and normalize event time $\tau$ relative to this year.

The CHNS waves nearest to the cutoff map to the following event-time indices: 1997 corresponds to $\tau = -3$, 2000 to $\tau = -2$, 2004 to $\tau = -1$ (omitted baseline), 2006 to $\tau = 0$, and 2009 to $\tau = +1$. The event-study regression is:

$$\text{Emp}_{it} = \alpha + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \beta_\tau \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{Rural}_i] \cdot \mathbf{1}[t = \tau] + \gamma \cdot \text{wave}_t + \delta_i + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{1}$$

where $\text{Emp}_{it}$ is employment status for individual $i$ at wave $t$, $\mathbf{1}[\text{Rural}_i]$ is an indicator equal to one for rural community residents, $\beta_\tau$ are the event-time coefficients of primary interest, $\gamma$ captures the common linear time trend, $\delta_i$ denotes individual fixed effects, and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an idiosyncratic error term. Standard errors are clustered at the community level to account for within-community correlation in employment outcomes and program exposure.

We also report a complementary within-entity fixed-effects trend model:

$$\text{Emp}_{it} = \alpha + \gamma \cdot \text{wave}_t + \delta_i + \varepsilon_{it} \tag{2}$$

Equation (2) is a bivariate panel regression that estimates the average within-person secular trend in employment across the full sample, using HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors. This model is not an alternative identification strategy for the program's causal effect—it does not exploit the rural-urban contrast or the 2006 cutoff—but it provides a precise estimate of the aggregate secular employment decline that dominates the data-generating process. The two models serve distinct purposes: equation (2) characterizes the trend, while equation (1) tests the parallel-trends assumption and estimates differential rural-urban dynamics. We harmonize standard error reporting by noting that the clustering in equation (1) is appropriate given community-level treatment assignment, while equation (2)'s HC3 correction is appropriate for the pooled sample without a natural clustering unit for the secular trend; both choices are conservative relative to conventional OLS standard errors.

**Identification assumption.** The key assumption is **parallel trends**: in the absence of the policy, rural and urban women's employment rates would have followed the same trend over time. This assumption would be plausible if both groups faced similar macroeconomic forces and if the rural-urban gap in employment levels were stable before 2006. The geographic stratification of the CHNS—rural and urban observations drawn from the same provinces—limits the scope for differential regional trends driven by province-specific factors. Pre-treatment wave coefficients statistically indistinguishable from zero would constitute direct empirical support.

**Threats to validity.** Four main threats merit explicit discussion. First, **structural divergence between rural and urban China**: across the 1989–2009 panel window, rural and urban areas underwent fundamentally different economic transformations. Urban sector expansion, rural agricultural contraction, hukou-system restrictions on mobility, and differential selectivity of migration all operated simultaneously. Urban women were never eligible for the program, faced different labor markets, had different pre-existing appliance stocks, and operated under different social norms governing market work. These categorical differences make urban women a potentially poor counterfactual for rural women even conditional on province effects and a common linear trend. Second, **endogenous program targeting**: the parallel-trends pre-test cannot distinguish structural divergence from a scenario in which local governments directed subsidies toward provinces or counties with higher female labor participation, inducing reverse causality in treatment assignment. If the program was targeted toward areas where rural women's employment was already rising relative to urban women, the positive $\tau = -3$ coefficient could partly reflect anticipatory effects or selection into treatment rather than structural transformation alone. We cannot fully resolve this concern without administrative data on local rollout decisions. Third, **concurrent policy changes**: the mid-2000s saw several rural support programs enacted simultaneously—the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme, abolition of agricultural taxes, and others. If these changes differentially affected rural women's employment, our estimates would confound multiple interventions. Fourth, **selective migration**: if the program induced differential out-migration from rural communities, the composition of the rural sample would change after 2006, biasing employment comparisons.

**Why this estimator is appropriate—and what its limitations are.** The event-study design is appropriate in principle because it (a) allows pre-trend testing rather than imposing the parallel-trends assumption, (b) estimates dynamic rather than average effects, and (c) can accommodate the staggered multi-wave structure of the CHNS. However, the design's validity depends entirely on the pre-trend test passing. As we document in Section 5, it does not: the pre-trend violation is statistically strong, not marginal, and the causal interpretation of post-2006 coefficients is therefore invalidated. The honest role of this design in our paper is diagnostic—it demonstrates that the standard rural-urban comparison cannot support causal inference here—rather than estimative. Community-level clustering is conservative relative to robust standard errors and is standard practice in village-level program evaluations (Imbens and Wooldridge 2009). In Section 8, we discuss alternative identification strategies that could, in principle, support causal inference where this design cannot.

**Pre-trend failure.** The most important result in Table 2 is not the post-policy coefficients but the pre-policy ones. The coefficient at $\tau = -3$ (wave 1997) is 0.073 (SE = 0.016, $p < 0.001$), a strongly statistically significant deviation from zero—precisely what parallel trends forbids. This is not a borderline or marginal result: at a $t$-statistic of approximately 4.45, the 1997 pre-trend coefficient is among the most precisely estimated figures in the event-study table, more significant than any post-policy estimate. The coefficient at $\tau = -2$ (wave 2000) is 0.028 (SE = 0.016, $p = 0.084$), marginally significant. A joint F-test on both pre-treatment coefficients rejects the null of zero at a $p$-value effectively equal to zero. The identifying assumption of the event-study design—parallel trends—is unambiguously violated. This invalidates any causal reading of the post-2006 estimates.

The economic interpretation of this pre-trend is instructive. In 1997, rural women's employment was approximately 7.3 percentage points higher, relative to the 2004 baseline, than the parallel-trends assumption predicts given the urban counterfactual. This most likely reflects the state-owned enterprise (SOE) restructuring of the late 1990s, which shed tens of millions of urban workers—disproportionately women—from formal employment, temporarily compressing urban employment rates while rural women remained in agricultural self-employment. By 2000, as the urban labor market absorbed displaced SOE workers and new service-sector jobs expanded, this differential had partially closed. The pattern describes a genuine structural divergence between rural and urban employment trajectories, not noise or a minor anomaly.

That said, the pre-trend test alone cannot distinguish this structural-transformation channel from a scenario of endogenous program targeting. If provincial or county governments that later participated most intensively in the appliance subsidy program had pre-existing trends of rising rural female employment relative to urban women, the positive $\tau = -3$ coefficient could partly reflect selection into the program rather than autonomous structural change. Resolving this ambiguity would require administrative data on local rollout decisions linked to pre-treatment community-level employment trends—data not available in the CHNS. Both channels imply the same conclusion for identification: the parallel-trends assumption fails and the rural-urban comparison is insufficient for causal inference.

**Post-policy coefficients.** With the parallel-trends assumption rejected, the post-2006 event-study coefficients cannot be given a causal interpretation. We report them for completeness. The contemporaneous coefficient at $\tau = 0$ (wave 2006) is 0.001 (SE = 0.017, $p = 0.951$), indistinguishable from zero. The coefficient at $\tau = +1$ (wave 2009) is $-0.033$ (SE = 0.018, $p = 0.071$), small and marginally significant in the negative direction. These estimates could reflect a true null or negative program effect, mean reversion from the elevated 1997 pre-trend, continuing secular decline, or some combination—the design cannot separate these channels. A credible estimate of the program's effect would require a different identification strategy.

**Secular trend.** The wave coefficient of $-0.0133$ (SE = 0.0002, $p < 0.001$, $t = -57.96$) reported in Table 3 is the most precisely estimated parameter across all specifications and is robust across all robustness checks. Each additional survey year is associated with a 1.33-percentage-point decline in the probability of employment. Across the twenty-year panel from 1989 to 2009, this cumulates to a 27-percentage-point reduction in predicted employment probability. A one-standard-deviation increase in the wave variable (approximately 6.7 years) is associated with a $6.7 \times 0.0133 = 8.9$-percentage-point decline in employment probability, equal to approximately 11 percent of the sample mean employment rate of 78.7 percent. This secular trend reflects structural transformation: as China's economy industrialized and urbanized, women in both rural and urban areas shifted out of agricultural and informal employment. The program's potential signal, if any, is swamped by this structural force.

**Rival explanations.** The most obvious rival explanation for the near-zero post-2006 finding is genuine program inefficacy: the subsidy may not have increased appliance ownership sufficiently to reduce domestic time burdens meaningfully, or labor demand constraints may have prevented any freed time from translating into market employment. Jing and Li (2018) document that appliance ownership did rise following the program, suggesting take-up was substantial—but the labor supply channel remains unidentified by our design. A second rival is that the employment variable captures a narrow slice of activity, missing reallocation of domestic time into agricultural self-employment or home production. We cannot distinguish between these explanations precisely because the post-2006 coefficients may themselves be biased by the pre-trend failure.

### Limitations

Several limitations constrain interpretation of these results, beyond the pre-trend failure already discussed. First, the event-study model's $R^2$ of 0.019 indicates that the specification explains essentially none of the within-person variation in employment, raising the concern that important confounders—agricultural cycles, household composition changes, local labor market conditions—are excluded. Second, the CHNS samples only twelve provinces and oversamples poorer regions; results cannot be generalized to provinces where the appliance subsidy had lower bite or where local labor markets were more developed. Third, the event-study specification does not allow for province-specific trends; if urbanizing provinces experienced faster employment declines, the rural-urban differential may be confounded by differential within-province urbanization rates. Fourth, the employment variable does not distinguish between part-time and full-time work, nor between agricultural and non-agricultural employment, limiting our ability to characterize the margins along which any reallocation of domestic time might have occurred. Fifth, the CHNS does not record appliance ownership consistently, so the mechanism linking the policy to an employment response remains inferred rather than measured. Sixth, the absence of variation in program timing across communities eliminates one natural source of identifying variation—staggered rollout—that could in principle support a more credible design. Seventh, differential panel attrition by rural-urban status, if present, could itself generate spurious pre-trends; we assess this in the robustness section, though the CHNS's community-based recontact design mitigates the concern.

## 6. Heterogeneous Effects

To examine whether employment patterns and secular trends vary systematically across subgroups, we estimate the pooled cross-sectional specification—regressing employment on the rural indicator, the linear wave trend, and a constant—separately for men and women (Table 4). Because the pre-trend failure documented in Section 5 prevents causal attribution of any estimate to the program, this section characterizes descriptive heterogeneity in employment patterns across groups rather than estimating heterogeneous treatment effects. The coefficients are nonetheless economically meaningful: they describe the structure of China's rural-urban employment gap and the gender-differentiated pace of secular employment decline.

**Rural employment premium.** Both men and women exhibit a positive and statistically significant rural employment premium, indicating that rural residents are more likely to be employed than their urban counterparts conditional on wave. For women, this premium is 0.130—13 percentage points—compared with 0.115, or 11.5 percentage points, for men. The difference of 1.5 percentage points is small in absolute terms but reflects a meaningful structural asymmetry: rural women engage heavily in agricultural self-employment, which is recorded as employment in the CHNS, while urban women are more likely to report non-participation during career interruptions associated with childbearing or family care (Maurer-Fazio et al. 2011). The larger rural premium for women thus reflects not a differential response to the appliance subsidy but a pre-existing structural feature of how women's work is organized across China's rural-urban divide. Critically, the persistence of the rural premium throughout the full sample period—covering waves both before and after 2006—confirms that the 13-percentage-point gap is a long-run feature of the data rather than a program-induced shift.

**Secular trend heterogeneity.** The more economically significant difference between the two groups lies in the secular trend. Women's employment declines at 1.41 percentage points per survey year, compared with 0.94 percentage points for men—a difference of 0.47 percentage points annually. Over the twenty-year panel, this differential cumulates to a roughly 9-percentage-point larger decline in women's employment relative to men's. A one-standard-deviation increase in the wave variable (approximately 6.7 years) is associated with a $6.7 \times 0.0141 = 9.5$-percentage-point decline in women's employment, compared with a $6.7 \times 0.0094 = 6.3$-percentage-point decline for men—a ratio of approximately 1.5 to 1. This finding is consistent with Zhang et al. (2013), who document that women bore a disproportionate share of the secular decline in Chinese labor force participation over this period, driven by a combination of rising household incomes (which raised women's reservation wages), inadequate childcare infrastructure, and the contraction of agricultural self-employment that had historically constituted a large share of rural women's work. The steeper secular trend for women implies that the structural forces driving Chinese female labor supply downward are genuinely gender-specific and are not reducible to aggregate labor market shocks that affect men and women symmetrically.

**Model fit and interpretation.** Women's employment is somewhat better explained by the pooled specification ($R^2 = 0.076$) than men's ($R^2 = 0.051$). This difference reflects the greater importance of the rural indicator and the wave trend in predicting women's employment outcomes—consistent with the argument that women's labor supply is more responsive to structural changes in rural-urban conditions than men's, whose employment tends to be higher and more uniform across contexts.

**Implications for policy targeting.** The heterogeneity results carry a direct implication for the design of rural development programs. The persistent rural employment premium and the steeper secular decline for women together suggest that the constraint on rural women's market labor supply is not primarily a contemporaneous supply-side bottleneck—rural women are already working at rates 13 percentage points above urban women—but reflects longer-run structural forces driving all employment downward, more severely for women than for men. A subsidy that reduces the time cost of domestic production may be insufficient to counteract these forces unless it is accompanied by demand-side interventions that create local employment opportunities. This finding echoes Nussbaum (2000) in arguing that women's capability to participate in economic life depends not only on their own time availability but on the presence of supportive institutional structures, including access to childcare, credit, and non-discriminatory labor markets. Time-saving technology is a necessary but not sufficient condition for female labor market entry, and its marginal contribution is likely smaller where structural decline is the dominant force.

## 7. Robustness Checks

We assess the robustness of our findings along four dimensions: sensitivity to the inclusion of the 1997 wave, alternative trend specifications, sample stability across waves, and alternative clustering.

**Dropping the 1997 wave.** The $\tau = -3$ coefficient is the primary driver of the pre-trend rejection. To assess whether it is an outlier driven by the late-1990s SOE restructuring rather than a sustained violation, we re-estimate equation (1) dropping the 1997 wave entirely, restricting the pre-trend test to the 2000 versus 2004 comparison. The wave coefficient and post-policy coefficients are qualitatively unchanged. The $\tau = -2$ coefficient shrinks toward zero when the 1997 wave is excluded, and the joint pre-trend test based on the 2000 wave alone is no longer rejected at the 5 percent level. This result is informative but does not rehabilitate the design: the 1997 pre-trend violation was real, and a shorter pre-period that passes a weaker test with fewer degrees of freedom does not constitute parallel-trends validation. The 1997 divergence may have partially mean-reverted by 2000, but the underlying structural reasons for rural-urban non-comparability—differential exposure to SOE restructuring, different agricultural and industrial labor markets—remain.

**Nonlinear wave trend.** The linear wave specification in equation (1) imposes structure on the common trend. We re-estimate adding a quadratic wave term to allow for a decelerating or accelerating secular trend. The post-policy event-study coefficients remain small and statistically insignificant. The pre-trend coefficient at $\tau = -3$ diminishes in magnitude when the quadratic term is included, suggesting that part of the 1997 anomaly is absorbed by the flexible trend. However, the need for a nonlinear trend to make the pre-period appear parallel is itself a signal that the linear trend misspecifies the data-generating process in ways that could bias post-policy estimates.

**Differential attrition and sample stability.** Selective panel attrition could generate spurious pre-trends if rural and urban respondents exit the CHNS at differential rates across waves. We assess this by computing the rural share of the analytical sample in each wave and testing for a discontinuous change at 2006. The CHNS re-contacts the same communities over time, so compositional change is limited by design. The rural share in the analytical sample is stable across waves, providing no evidence of systematic selective attrition. We also verify that baseline employment rates within the rural and urban cells are stable across early waves before the structural divergence period, consistent with a genuine 1997 shock rather than accumulating attrition bias.

**Alternative clustering.** Our main specification clusters at the community level. As an alternative, we cluster at the county level, which is more conservative given that the appliance subsidy was administered at the county level in some provinces. Post-policy coefficients remain statistically insignificant under this alternative clustering, confirming that the null post-policy result is not an artifact of overly tight standard error estimation. The pre-trend coefficient at $\tau = -3$ also remains significant under county-level clustering, confirming that the parallel-trends failure is not a consequence of within-community error correlation that community-level clustering already accounts for.

## 8. Discussion

The main finding of this paper—that we cannot credibly estimate the causal effect of the Home Appliances to the Countryside program on rural women's employment because the parallel-trends assumption is strongly violated—warrants careful interpretation before drawing conclusions. Three issues deserve extended attention: the failure of the rural-urban comparison, alternative designs that could in principle succeed, and broader lessons for program evaluation in China.

**The failure of the rural-urban comparison.** Urban women are a categorically poor counterfactual for rural women over the 1989–2009 panel. They were never eligible for the program, faced structurally different labor markets, had different pre-existing appliance stocks, and experienced the SOE restructuring shock of the late 1990s with far greater intensity. The significant pre-trend at $\tau = -3$ is the empirical fingerprint of this divergence, and the possibility of endogenous program targeting adds a further layer of confounding that the rural-urban design cannot resolve. These are not incidental failures of a particular dataset—they are inherent features of the rural-urban contrast in a rapidly transforming economy. Any researcher using a nationally representative Chinese panel to compare rural and urban employment trends across the 1989–2009 window must confront the same issues.

**Alternative identification strategies.** Documenting a design's failure is a first step, not a final answer. Three alternative approaches could, in principle, recover credible causal estimates. First, **synthetic control methods** (Abadie, Diamond, and Hainmueller 2010) reweight urban communities to match the rural pre-treatment employment trajectory, constructing a data-driven counterfactual rather than relying on the assumption of parallel linear trends. Applied to the CHNS, this approach would require identifying a weighted combination of urban communities whose 1989–2004 employment path closely tracks the rural trend, then estimating the post-2006 divergence. Second, a **within-rural design** exploiting county-level or province-wave variation in program rollout intensity would avoid the rural-urban comparability problem entirely. Administrative data on subsidy take-up rates, appliance sales volumes, or county-level program entry dates—available from China's Ministry of Commerce records—could support a differences-in-differences or instrumental variables design comparing early- and late-adopting rural counties. Third, a **regression discontinuity** on the household income eligibility threshold for the subsidy, if such a threshold was consistently enforced, would identify local average treatment effects for households near the eligibility margin. Each of these alternatives requires data beyond the CHNS as configured, but each is feasible with appropriate administrative linkage.

**Broader implications for program evaluation in China.** The mid-2000s witnessed a proliferation of rural support programs in China—agricultural tax abolition, the New Rural Cooperative Medical Scheme, and appliance subsidies, among others—implemented simultaneously across similar geographic units. This stacking of concurrent interventions makes clean identification of any single program's effects difficult using standard difference-in-differences methods applied to nationally representative surveys. The lesson of this paper is methodological as much as substantive: parallel-trends testing is a binding constraint rather than a formality, and its failure should redirect rather than merely qualify the analysis. Researchers evaluating Chinese rural programs should invest in administrative data with precise geographic rollout timing and should pre-register their identification strategies, including explicit parallel-trends tests, before examining post-policy outcomes.

## 9. Conclusion

This paper examines whether China's Home Appliances to the Countryside program—a large-scale subsidy for refrigerators, washing machines, and stoves in rural areas—increased rural women's labor market participation. Using an event-study difference-in-differences design with data from the China Health and Nutrition Survey spanning 1989 to 2009, we compare rural and urban women's employment trajectories around the 2006 policy cutoff.

The central finding is that the event-study design's identifying assumption—parallel trends between rural and urban women's employment—is strongly and unambiguously violated. The pre-treatment coefficient at three waves before the policy cutoff is 0.073 (SE = 0.016, $p < 0.001$), and the joint pre-trend test is rejected at a $p$-value effectively equal to zero. This pre-trend most plausibly reflects the state-owned enterprise restructuring of the late 1990s, which differentially depressed urban women's employment—though we cannot rule out that endogenous program targeting contributed to the rural-urban divergence. Because the identifying assumption fails, post-2006 event-study coefficients—which are small and slightly negative—cannot be interpreted as causal estimates of the program's effect.

Two descriptive patterns are nonetheless robust and economically meaningful. A secular decline in employment of approximately 1.3 percentage points per survey year dominates the data, estimated with a $t$-statistic of $-57.96$ in the within-entity trend model and consistent with China's aggregate labor force statistics. Women's employment falls at a steeper rate than men's—1.4 versus 0.9 percentage points per year—and rural women maintain a persistent 13-percentage-point employment premium over urban women throughout the panel.

We interpret these findings as pointing toward two conclusions. Substantively, the rural employment premium is driven primarily by agricultural self-employment, and the secular decline reflects the contraction of that sector—forces that likely swamp any supply-side shift from appliance subsidization. Methodologically, the parallel-trends failure illustrates that long Chinese panels spanning the 1989–2009 structural transformation are ill-suited to standard difference-in-differences designs using the rural-urban contrast. Future evaluation of appliance-subsidy programs requires designs with tighter geographic variation in rollout timing—synthetic control reweighting, within-rural county comparisons, or regression discontinuity on eligibility thresholds—and control groups matched explicitly on pre-treatment employment trajectories. The original research question—whether labor-saving appliances increase rural women's market work—remains empirically open and policy-relevant; it simply requires better data and a more credible design than a nationally representative rural-urban comparison can provide.
